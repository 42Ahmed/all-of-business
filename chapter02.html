2  Measure What Matters

2.1 Executive Summary

“Measure What Matters” by John Doerr introduces Objectives and Key Results (OKRs) as a powerful, transformative goal-setting methodology. Proven effective in leading companies like Intel, Google, Amazon, and Uber, OKRs provide a framework for organizations to define ambitious objectives and measure their success through specific, measurable key results. The book highlights four “superpowers” of OKRs: Focus and Commit to Priorities, Align and Connect for Teamwork, Track for Accountability, and Stretch for Amazing. Beyond goal setting, the book also emphasizes the importance of Continuous Performance Management (CPM) through Conversations, Feedback, and Recognition (CFRs) and the critical role of organizational culture in the successful implementation of OKRs. The ultimate message is that “Ideas are easy; execution is everything,” and OKRs provide the necessary structure to achieve extraordinary outcomes.

2.2 Key Themes and Most Important Ideas/Facts

2.2.1 1. The Core Concept of OKRs: Objectives and Key Results

  • Definition: OKRs are a “management methodology that helps to ensure that the company focuses efforts on the same important issues throughout the organization.”

  • Objective (WHAT): “An OBJECTIVE… is simply WHAT is to be achieved, no more and no less. By definition, objectives are significant, concrete, action oriented, and (ideally) inspirational. When properly designed and deployed, they’re a vaccine against fuzzy thinking and fuzzy execution.”

  • Key Results (HOW): “KEY RESULTS benchmark and monitor HOW we get to the objective. Effective KRs are specific and time-bound, aggressive yet realistic. Most of all, they are measurable and verifiable. (As prize pupil Marissa Mayer would say, ‘It’s not a key result unless it has a number.’)”

  • Relationship: “Where an objective can be long-lived, rolled over for a year or longer, key results evolve as the work progresses. Once they are all completed, the objective is necessarily achieved. (And if it isn’t, the OKR was poorly designed in the first place.)”

  • Origin: John Doerr introduced OKRs to Google in 1999, inspired by his time at Intel under Andy Grove, who is considered “The Father of OKRs.” Grove emphasized “the importance of execution over mere knowledge,” establishing a system for goal-setting that distinguished between objectives and measurable milestones.

  • Contrast with MBOs: OKRs differ from traditional Management by Objectives (MBOs) in that OKRs are “short-term, flexible, and designed to foster teamwork and accountability without directly tying them to pay,” unlike MBOs which were often long-term, top-down, and tied to compensation, leading to “goals gone wild.”

2.2.2 2. The Four “Superpowers” of OKRs

Doerr identifies four key benefits that OKRs bring to an organization:

  • Superpower #1: Focus and Commit to Priorities

  • Principle: “Successful organizations prioritize key initiatives that can significantly impact their outcomes over the upcoming months.” It’s crucial to “distill a list of goals down to the most vital, as no organization can pursue all tasks simultaneously.”

  • Implementation: Leaders are responsible for “establishing organization-level OKRs, starting with a clear vision,” and communicating “both the ‘what’ and ‘why’ behind objectives to foster motivation and understanding.” Key results must be “specific, challenging, and primarily numerical.”

  • “Less is More”: “Limit significant objectives to increase focus on impactful initiatives.” A quarterly cadence is often most effective for review and adaptation.

  • Example (Remind Story): Brett Kopf of Remind, an educational communication platform, “emphasizes the critical need for focus in setting organizational goals.” OKRs helped them “prioritize effectively, ensuring that they could focus on impactful objectives rather than getting bogged down in less crucial tasks.”

  • Critical Thinking: While focus is important, it can “risk overlooking potentially valuable opportunities that may not align perfectly with the immediate goals.”

  • Superpower #2: Align and Connect for Teamwork

  • Principle: “Transparency and collaboration foster better team performance and innovation.” Publicly shared goals are “more likely to be achieved.”

  • Implementation: An OKR system makes “everyone’s goals” accessible, “thus fostering meritocracy and collaboration.” This helps “clarify what needs to be prioritized amid various tasks.”

  • Bottom-Up & Cross-functional: Moving beyond “top-down” cascading, OKRs encourage “bottom-up goal setting” where “employees contribute their own objectives,” fostering “engagement and motivation.” They also break down silos, enabling “cross-functional connectivity” and quicker responses to challenges.

  • Example (MyFitnessPal Story): “OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) are pivotal in aligning efforts across teams and enhancing organizational effectiveness.” MyFitnessPal used OKRs for “syncing their team’s goals with the company’s overall objectives, enhancing focus and coordination across different teams and functions.”

  • Critical Thinking: Complete transparency “may not always lead to optimal outcomes,” potentially causing “information overload and the sensitive nature of certain objectives,” or even “anxiety and competition rather than collaboration.”

  • Superpower #3: Track for Accountability

  • Principle: “The ability to track goals and revise them as needed is crucial.” OKRs are “dynamic and can adjust based on circumstances.”

  • Implementation: Requires “suitable tools” like “Cloud-based OKR management software” for enhanced “visibility, engagement, and connectivity.” Designating “OKR shepherds ensures that all team members remain accountable.”

  • Regular Check-ins: “Regular check-ins on OKRs… are essential to maintain focus,” allowing for timely “updates, new objectives, or halting those that are no longer relevant.” Post-implementation evaluation through “Scoring objectives provides insights into performance, while self-assessment fosters reflection.”

  • Example (Gates Foundation Story): The Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation adopted OKRs to “move beyond incremental thinking and focus on significant global health challenges.” OKRs enabled them to “monitor and adjust strategies for health initiatives, ensuring effective resource allocation and goal achievement.” Bill Gates emphasized “the need for realistic yet ambitious goals, distinguishing between specific objectives and broader missions to maintain accountability in philanthropy.”

  • Critical Thinking: The “hyper-focus on measurable outcomes can lead to a culture where employees might prioritize goal completion over authentic engagement,” and raise “concerns about data privacy and the potential for misinterpretation of performance metrics.”

  • Superpower #4: Stretch for Amazing

  • Principle: “The greatest risk is not taking any risks at all.” Setting “ambitious OKRs… push beyond their comfort zones, leading to greater innovation and achievement.” “Harder goals correlate with higher performance, engagement, and motivation.”

  • Implementation: Google categorizes OKRs into “committed goals” (measurable, 100% attainment expected) and “aspirational stretch goals” (higher-risk, future-oriented, 60-70% attainment acceptable). Larry Page “embodies the principle of setting aspirational goals that seek exponential improvements.”

  • Example (Google Chrome Story): Sundar Pichai led the development of Chrome with a “stretch OKR of 20 million active users,” pushing teams “to achieve beyond perceived limits.” Even when missing initial targets, “the team adapted their strategies,” demonstrating the value of ambitious goals.

  • Example (YouTube Story): CEO Susan Wojcicki set a “bold goal: to reach one billion hours of daily watch time by 2016.” This “aspirational goal was not just ambitious but rooted in the broader context of global television consumption,” invigorating the organization.

2.2.4 3. Continuous Performance Management: OKRs and CFRs

  • Critique of Annual Reviews: “Traditional annual performance reviews are often ineffective, time-consuming, and do not fairly assess employee performance.” They can lead to “demoralized employees and high attrition rates,” as seen in the Adobe story.

  • Introducing CFRs: “Continuous Performance Management (CPM) using CFRs (Conversations, Feedback, Recognition) provides a more effective alternative.”

  • Conversations: “Authentic exchanges focused on driving performance.”

  • Feedback: “Bidirectional communication for ongoing improvement.”

  • Recognition: “Public acknowledgment of employee contributions.”

  • Synergy: “OKRs… and CFRs work together to enhance transparency and communication within organizations.” CFRs help “contextualize the achievement of goals, ensuring alignment.”

  • Example (Adobe Story): Adobe ditched annual reviews in 2012, introducing “Check-in,” a model involving “regular feedback, goal setting, and career development.” This “increased employee engagement and reduced voluntary attrition significantly,” promoting a “culture of continuous improvement.”

  • Example (Zume Pizza Story): OKRs and CFRs “promote high performance and growth,” enhancing “executive grooming, empower quieter contributors, and facilitate organizational improvement.” Zume Pizza implemented OKRs for “clear priority setting and alignment among employees” and “structured one-on-one meetings foster deep discussions about individual aspirations and work alignment.”

2.2.5

2.2.6 4. The Importance of Culture

  • Culture’s Role: “Culture is crucial in shaping a company’s identity and is a key focus for leaders and founders.” “Healthy culture and structured goal setting are interdependent.” Andy Grove noted that “corporate culture streamlines decision-making, reducing reliance on formal rules.”

  • Pre-requisite for OKRs: “Organizations may need cultural work before implementing OKRs.” Without addressing cultural issues, “the antibodies will be set loose and the body will reject the donor organ of OKRs.”

  • Key Elements of Healthy Culture (Google’s Project Aristotle):Structure and clarity: Clear goals and roles.

  • Psychological safety: The ability to take risks without feeling insecure.

  • Meaning of work: Personal significance of the work.

  • Dependability: Trust in team members to deliver quality work on time.

  • Impact of work: Belief that the work matters.

  • Example (Lumeris Story): Lumeris, a healthcare tech firm, initially had “superficial” OKR implementation due to “cultural barriers.” A “major reorganization aimed to unify diverse internal cultures and align goals across the organization,” establishing “a culture of ‘brutal transparency without judgment’.” This shifted them “from hero culture to team culture.”

  • Example (Bono’s ONE Campaign Story): Bono utilized OKRs to drive “cultural change” in the ONE Campaign, pivoting “from merely working on Africa to collaborating with African communities to empower them.” OKRs helped them “establish clear priorities” and maintain a “disruptive spirit” while ensuring “rigorous and effective” strategies.

  • Critical Thinking: The efficacy of OKRs is “heavily dependent on the organizational culture.” If the culture “does not support open communication, accountability, and a willingness to adapt objectives collaboratively, the OKR approach could backfire, leading only to a box-ticking exercise.”

2.2.7 5.Key Implementation Advice:

• Culture First: A healthy corporate culture, emphasizing psychological safety and clear goals, is crucial for OKRs to take root. Sometimes, cultural groundwork is needed before OKRs are implemented.

• Leadership Commitment: Leaders must invest time, provide clear direction, and model commitment to OKRs, as “people watch what you do more than what you say”.

• Separate from Compensation: Do not tie OKRs directly to compensation or performance reviews, as this can lead to “sandbagging” (setting easy goals) and stifle initiative. View OKRs as tools for growth and self-assessment.

• Patience and Adaptability: Implementing OKRs takes time and practice; it won’t be perfect initially. Perseverance and adapting the system to your organization’s needs are essential.

• Avoid “Goals Gone Wild”: Poorly managed goals can lead to negative outcomes like narrowed focus or unethical behavior, emphasizing the need for careful management.

2.2.8 6. The Future and Broader Applications of OKRs

  • Beyond Business: The potential of OKRs “extends beyond a mere organizational tool; they serve as a launch pad for entrepreneurial endeavors, capable of transforming diverse areas such as economic growth, healthcare, education, and social change.”

  • Empowerment: OKRs “inspire individuals, develop leaders, and unify teams, facilitating impactful projects across various sectors.”

  • Ultimate Vision: John Doerr “is convinced that if structured goal setting and continuous communication were to be widely deployed, with rigor and imagination, we could see exponentially greater productivity and innovation throughout society.” His “ultimate stretch OKR is to empower people to achieve the seemingly impossible together.”

2.3 Summary

2.3.1 A.Key Principles and Best Practices

Several overarching principles guide effective OKR implementation:

  • Less is More: Focus on a few well-chosen objectives (3-5) to avoid diluting efforts.

  • Bottom-Up Goal Setting: Encourage teams and individual contributors to have ownership over their objectives, fostering engagement and innovation.

  • Cooperative Framework: Goals should be negotiated and agreed upon collectively.

  • Flexibility and Adaptability: Modify objectives as circumstances change; OKRs are guardrails, not chains.

  • Push for Peak Performance: Set ambitious, even aspirational goals, to drive performance and innovation.

  • Separation from Compensation: Treat OKRs primarily as tools for growth and self-assessment, not rigid performance evaluations tied directly to pay, to avoid sandbagging and foster risk-taking.

  • Patience in Implementation: Recognize that embracing OKRs takes time and practice, and it won’t be perfect immediately.

  • Transparency: Make goals public to foster accountability, collaboration, and alignment.

  • Balance Quantitative with Qualitative: Pair key results with qualitative assessments to ensure a holistic evaluation of progress and prevent unintended negative consequences (e.g., Ford Pinto case).

  • Quarterly Cycles: Use frequent, often quarterly, cycles for timely feedback and agile adaptations in fast-paced markets.

  • Culture is Crucial: A healthy corporate culture, emphasizing psychological safety, clarity, meaning, dependability, and impact, is interdependent with structured goal setting. Cultural groundwork may be needed before OKRs are implemented.

  • Leadership Commitment: Leaders must invest time, provide clear direction, and model commitment to OKRs.

2.3.2 B.Practical Tools and Specific Methodologies

  • Cloud-based OKR Management Software: Enhances visibility, engagement, and connectivity across the organization, making goals accessible and relevant.

  • OKR Shepherds: Individuals designated to ensure universal adoption and accountability for OKRs within the organization.

  • Regular Check-ins: Weekly assessments or frequent discussions to prevent slippage, make timely adjustments, and strategize around obstacles.

  • Visual Aids: Utilizing visual tools in goal management to illustrate progress comprehensively and increase motivation.

  • OKR Scoring System: A method (e.g., 0.0 to 1.0 at Google) to evaluate what was accomplished, identify obstacles, and learn from experiences, fostering reflection and improvement.

  • “a.m.b. (as measured by)” Framework: Introduced by Bill Davidow at Intel, this clarifies how objectives will be assessed, turning abstract goals into concrete actions.

  • “Big Rocks Theory”: Used by YouTube, this metaphor emphasizes prioritizing the most critical objectives to ensure they are accomplished, focusing on top-level OKRs.

  • “10x Thinking”: Larry Page’s philosophy of setting goals that aim for exponential (ten times) improvements rather than incremental changes, driving radical innovation.

  • Disability-Adjusted Life Years (DALY): A metric used by the Gates Foundation to measure the impact of health interventions, facilitating data-driven decision-making for key results.

  • “Factivism”: Bono’s approach for the ONE Campaign, emphasizing an analytical and results-driven method to tackle global challenges, supported by OKRs.

  • “Selling Your Reds”: A practice at Lumeris where leaders openly share their at-risk objectives in meetings, promoting teamwork, problem-solving, and cross-departmental collaboration.

2.4 Conclusion

“Measure What Matters” powerfully argues for the adoption of OKRs and CFRs as indispensable tools for any organization aiming for “extraordinary growth.” By providing clarity, fostering alignment, ensuring accountability, and encouraging ambitious “stretch” goals, this framework helps transform ideas into impactful execution. However, the sources also critically emphasize that the success of OKRs is deeply intertwined with a supportive, transparent, and adaptable organizational culture.
