30  The Intelligent Investor

30.1 Executive Summary: The Core Principles of Intelligent Investing

The Intelligent Investor serves as the indispensable foundation for investment success, emphasizing intellectual framework and emotional discipline over high IQ or insider knowledge.

  1. Investment vs. Speculation: The foundational principle is the distinction between these two activities. Investment is defined as an operation which, upon thorough analysis, promises safety of principal and an adequate return; operations not meeting these requirements are speculative. The investor studies the underlying business and its fundamental value, whereas the speculator focuses on price action.
  2. Mr. Market: Graham’s brilliant allegory for the market’s fluctuating, irrational temperament. The intelligent investor must be a realist, refusing to let emotions be dictated by Mr. Market’s mood swings (greed or fear). The investor profits from folly rather than participating in it.
  3. Margin of Safety (MoS): This is the central concept of sound investment. It is a cushion—a favorable difference between the value of the business and the price paid for its shares—that allows the investor to survive losses if analysis proves incorrect. The MoS makes estimating the future unnecessary because the investment is secured by demonstrated figures and arithmetic.
  4. Know Thyself: The investor’s chief problem is likely to be himself. Success depends on temperament—rationality, discipline, and emotional control—rather than sheer brainpower. The intelligent investor must cultivate virtues like curiosity, skepticism, independence, and humility.
  5. Defensive vs. Enterprising: Investors must choose their role based on the time and “intelligent effort” they are willing to commit. The Defensive (Passive) Investor seeks safety and freedom from bother, relying on standardized, diversified portfolios. The Enterprising (Active) Investor is willing to devote significant time and care to selection to achieve better-than-average results.

30.2 What This Book Expects to Accomplish

The primary purpose of this book is to provide guidance on the adoption and execution of an investment policy suitable for laymen, focusing chiefly on investment principles and investors’ attitudes rather than detailed security analysis techniques. The goal is not to present a “how to make a million” guide, as there are no sure and easy paths to riches.

30.2.1 The Investor and the Speculator

The book is directed to investors, and its first task is to clarify and emphasize the distinction, now nearly forgotten, between an investor and a speculator.

  • Investment Definition: “An investment operation is one which, upon thorough analysis, promises safety of principal and an adequate return. Operations not meeting these requirements are speculative”.
  • Speculative Approaches: Most speculators are guided by charts or other mechanical means of determining when to buy (because a stock has gone up) and when to sell (because it has declined). This approach is the exact opposite of sound business sense and is unlikely to lead to lasting success.
  • Stocks vs. Businesses (Conceptual Difference):
    • Stocks are appraised by Price (precise to the penny) based on what someone will pay today, subject to market moods, changing up to thousands of times a day.
    • Businesses are appraised by Value (approximate range) based on the present worth of the total future cash generated, changing at most a few times a year.

30.2.2 The Investor’s Chief Problem

The investor’s main adversary is often himself. The investor’s chief problem—and even his worst enemy—is likely to be himself. By arguments, examples, and exhortation, the book aims to aid readers in establishing the proper mental and emotional attitudes toward investment decisions.

30.2.3 The Two Classes of Investors

The book addresses two basic kinds of investors, distinguished mainly by the amount of time and care they are willing to devote:

  1. Defensive (Passive) Investor: This investor places chief emphasis on avoidance of serious mistakes or losses. Their second aim is freedom from effort, annoyance, and the need for frequent decisions. This type of investor should stick to high-grade bonds and a diversified list of leading common stocks, bought at conservative prices.
  2. Enterprising (Active/Aggressive) Investor: This investor is willing to devote time and care to selecting securities that are both sound and more attractive than the average. This path is difficult and requires viewing security operations as equivalent to a business enterprise.

30.2.4 The Pitfalls of Growth Stock Investing

History shows that merely identifying obvious prospects for physical growth in a business do not translate into obvious profits for investors. Furthermore, experts do not have dependable ways of selecting and concentrating on the most promising companies in the most promising industries. The price paid is paramount; investors must remember to ask, “How much?”.

30.2.5 Framework: The Intelligent Investor’s Temperament

Investing successfully does not require a stratospheric IQ, but rather a sound intellectual framework and the ability to keep emotions from corroding that framework. Rationality and temperament are essential. The great money managers share seven indispensable virtues:

  1. Curiosity: A relentless quest to learn more, recognizing that current knowledge might be biased or incomplete.
  2. Skepticism: Recognizing that Wall Street sells hopes and dreams; being wary of opportunities that seem too good to be true.
  3. Independence: Thinking for oneself, relying on data and reasoning rather than the crowd’s approval.
  4. Humility: Recognizing that profit feels like proof, making one overestimate one’s own skill; realizing that luck is often bigger than oneself.
  5. Discipline: Adhering rigorously to a consistent plan.
  6. Patience: Knowing that financial success unfolds over years and decades.
  7. Faith: Believing in a better tomorrow for business, especially after times when this belief is hardest to hold.

30.2.6 Recap

  • Investment vs. Speculation: Investment requires thorough analysis, safety of principal, and an adequate return; speculation lacks these requirements.
  • Investor’s Focus: The investor studies the underlying business value; the speculator chases stock prices and market moods.
  • Defensive Investor Expectation: The defensive investor should aim for a sound, average overall return (estimated historically around 7.8% before taxes) by using a conservative policy.
  • Basic Policy: Defensive investors must maintain a significant part of their funds in bond-type holdings and a significant part in equities, typically divided 50-50, but ranging from 25% to 75% for either component.
  • Speculation Containment: If one must speculate, use a dedicated “mad money” account limited to a maximum of 5% of assets, segregated from the investment portfolio.
  • Superior Results: To achieve better-than-average results, the investor must follow policies that are inherently sound and promising, and not popular on Wall Street.
  • Buy Low, Sell Dear: The governing maxim for investors is to buy cheap and sell dear, directly contrary to the pernicious Wall Street maxim of buying because a stock has gone up.

30.3 The Investor and Inflation

30.3.1 The Problem of Inflation

Inflation (the shrinkage in the purchasing power of the dollar) is a major concern for investors.

  • Fixed Income: Holders of fixed-dollar income (like traditional bondholders) suffer directly when the cost of living advances.
  • Common Stocks: Stockholders have the possibility that inflation may be offset by advances in dividends and share prices, but high-quality stocks are not a better purchase than bonds under all conditions—especially if the stock market is priced high and dividend yields are low compared to bond yields.

30.3.2 The Inadequacy of Stocks as a Hedge

In the late 1960s/early 1970s, the stock market’s valuation (high price/earnings and low dividend yield) meant that the overall return expected from common stocks (about 8%) was barely above the return on bonds. If this expected 8% return were subjected to a continuing 3% annual inflation, the margin of protection would be insufficient.

  • Volatility: Earnings and average annual market value of a stock portfolio will not grow at a uniform rate; they “will fluctuate”.
  • Inflation Insurance: A stock component should carry more protection against inflation than a bond component, but there is no certainty that it will insure adequately.

30.3.3 Portfolio Policy and Inflation

The key conclusion remains constant: Just because of the uncertainties of the future, the investor cannot afford to put all his funds into one basket.

  • Defensive Stance: The conservative investor should seek to minimize risks. Despite the advantage of high bond yields, the defensive investor cannot afford to be without an appreciable proportion of common stocks (at least 25%) as insurance against large-scale inflation, even if stocks appear overvalued.
  • Recommended Action: Maintain a basic compromise policy divided between bonds and stocks (the 50-50 model, ranging from 25%-75%).

30.3.4 Application: Hedging Against Inflation

  • Human Capital: Augment your human capital (education, skills, career) to increase total income faster than the inflation rate. Do not concentrate financial capital in the same industry where human capital is concentrated.
  • Inflation-Protected Bonds (TIPS): Supplement a stock portfolio with a large allocation of inflation-protected bonds (like U.S. Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities, or TIPS) as the single best financial asset to protect against inflation.
  • Physical Capital: Owning a physical residence or property (physical capital) also provides a partial hedge against rising costs.

30.3.5 Recap

  • Inflation Risk: Inflation erodes the value of fixed dollar claims (bonds, cash).
  • Stocks Are Imperfect Hedges: Common stocks may offset inflation through rising dividends and prices, but only if they are not already priced too highly (low yield/high P/E ratio).
  • Balance is Key: Due to uncertainties, the investor must avoid placing all funds into one basket (neither all stocks nor all bonds).
  • The Stock Component: Despite overvaluation concerns, a stock component (minimum 25%) is necessary insurance against large-scale inflation.
  • Human Capital: Improving skills and income potential is a vital, personalized hedge against rising living costs.

30.4 A Century of Stock-Market History: The Level of Stock Prices in Early 1972

30.4.1 The Value of History

Prudence suggests that the investor should have an adequate idea of stock-market history, focusing on major price fluctuations and the varying relationships between stock prices, earnings, and dividends.

  • Long-Term Growth: The statistical record from 1871 to 1970 reveals a general picture of persistent growth across prices, earnings, and dividends, supplying encouragement for a consistent policy of common-stock investment.
  • Variability: The rates of growth in all three categories are quite variable, and historical performance (such as the superior gains since World War II) does not guarantee future percentage gains.

30.4.2 The Impossibility of Market Timing

An analysis of the Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA) reveals the difficulty of determining if the market level is “too high for sound investment”. Even experienced financial figures cannot reliably predict the magnitude or duration of market advances.

  • Stock Price Levels: At various high points in history (e.g., 1959, 1964), prices appeared dangerously high based on historical valuation metrics, yet the market often continued advancing.
  • The Long Run Illusion: The belief that “stocks always do better than safe investments” over five years or more is nonsense and distorts history. If an investor buys at a market peak (where prices fall drastically), “the long run might feel like eons”.

30.4.3 Application: A Cautionary Policy

When stock prices seem dangerously high (as in late 1964), the principles of investment call for the path of caution.

  • The Policy:
    1. No borrowing to buy or hold securities.
    2. No increase in the proportion of funds held in common stocks.
    3. A reduction in common-stock holdings to a maximum of 50 percent of the total portfolio (paying capital gains tax gracefully).
  • Dollar-Cost Averaging (DCA): Investors following a bona fide DCA plan may continue their periodic purchases or suspend them, but starting a new DCA plan during dangerously high market levels is strongly advised against, as many investors would lack the stamina to persist through an ensuing decline.

30.4.4 Recap

  • Historical Insight: The stock market shows persistent growth over the century, encouraging long-term stock investment.
  • Unpredictability: It is nearly impossible for even sophisticated analysts to consistently reach an informed, critical judgment of current market levels.
  • Investor Warning: The long-run reliability of stocks does not remove the risk of severe crashes or long recovery periods if purchased at high prices.
  • Cautionary Steps: During high market levels, investors should avoid margin, cap stock holdings at 50%, and be wary of initiating new accumulation plans.
  • Focus on Value: The work of a financial analyst falls somewhere between a mathematician and an orator, requiring precise analysis but accepting probable rather than strictly demonstrated conclusions.

30.5 General Portfolio Policy: The Defensive Investor

30.5.1 Return Based on Effort, Not Risk

The rate of return an investor should aim for is dependent not on the degree of risk they are ready to run, but on the amount of intelligent effort the investor is willing and able to bring to bear on the task.

  • Minimum Return: Goes to the passive investor, who wants both safety and freedom from concern.
  • Maximum Return: Would be realized by the alert and enterprising investor who exercises maximum intelligence and skill.

30.5.2 Framework: The Basic Bond-Stock Allocation

The defensive investor should divide funds between high-grade bonds and high-grade common stocks.

  • Guiding Rule (Range): The investor should never have less than 25% or more than 75% of their funds in common stocks, with an inverse proportion in bonds.
  • Standard Division: The implication is that the standard division should be an equal one, or 50-50.
  • Market Timing Dilemma: Increasing stock percentages in a bear market and reducing them in a bull market is difficult because it goes against human nature, which generates market excesses.

30.5.3 Framework: The 50-50 Formula (Rebalancing)

For most readers, an oversimplified 50-50 plan is recommended, where the guiding rule is to maintain as nearly as practicable an equal division between bond and stock holdings.

  • Mechanism: When the common-stock component rises to 55%, restore the balance by selling 1/11th of the stock portfolio and moving the proceeds to bonds. Conversely, if it falls to 45%, use 1/11th of the bond fund to buy more equities.
  • Psychological Advantage: This simple formula unquestionably aims in the right direction and, most importantly, will restrain the investor from being drawn more and more heavily into common stocks as the market rises to more and more dangerous heights.

30.5.4 Application: The Investment Policy Statement (IPS)

The hardest part of being a defensive investor is defending yourself from yourself. To counter emotional impulses, the investor needs a formal Investment Policy Statement (IPS)—a document that structures decision-making and acts as a constant reminder of what not to do.

IPS Component Detailed Explanation/Steps
Mission Statement

1. Define primary objective (e.g., long-term growth over X years).

2. Define secondary objectives (e.g., regular income).

3. Identify decision-makers.
Structure

1. Define target allocations (% in cash, bonds, US stocks, international stocks, etc.).

2. Maintain broad diversification within each allocation (minimum holdings specified).

3. Commit to never using margin or borrowed money. 4. Maintain cash to cover at least six months of expenses.
Implementation

1. Determine rebalancing frequency (e.g., quarterly or annually).

2. Commit to dollar-cost averaging (DCA).

3. Select funds based on the lowest cost, not highest return.

4. Set a maximum for total annual expenses (e.g., \(\le\) 1%).

5. All purchases/sales must conform to a checklist.

6. Segregate speculation in a “mad money” account (\(\le\) 5% of assets) and commit to never adding more money to it.
Monitoring

1. Update holdings value infrequently (e.g., no more than four times a year).

2. Measure performance against defined benchmarks (multiyear returns first).

3. Review the IPS annually for changes in circumstances.

4. Require written justification for any departure from the IPS.

30.5.5 Sound Bond Types

When allocating funds to bonds, the defensive investor should prioritize safety and unique advantages.

  • U.S. Savings Bonds (Series E/H): These combine
    • (1) absolute assurance of principal and interest payments,
    • (2) the right to demand full money back at any time, and
    • (3) a guarantee of at least a 5% interest rate for at least ten years. They protect against both high and low interest rates and allow for tax deferral.
  • High-Grade Corporate Bonds: Assure fixed interest and principal repayment. Safety is paramount, and bond prices fluctuate widely with changes in interest rates.
  • Preferred Stocks: Entitle the holder to a fixed dividend (which may be cumulative or noncumulative) but no specified principal due date.

30.5.6 Recap

  • Return Source: Investment return depends on the investor’s time and intelligent effort, not necessarily on the risk taken.
  • Allocation Rule: Defensive investors should maintain 25% to 75% in stocks, with the balance in bonds, favoring a 50-50 standard division.
  • Rebalancing Mechanism: A mechanical rebalancing plan (like the 50-50 formula) should be used to force the investor to buy low and sell high, controlling emotional urges.
  • Investment Policy Statement (IPS): A formal document outlining objectives, allocation, implementation (DCA, rebalancing), and constraints to maintain discipline.
  • Bonds: U.S. Savings Bonds offer unique safety, liquidity, and tax advantages; high-grade corporate bonds offer fixed interest and principal safety.

30.6 The Defensive Investor and Common Stocks

30.6.1 Investment Merits and Rules

The defensive investor includes common stocks because they provide a long-term satisfactory return, despite fluctuations. Stock selection should be relatively simple:

Rule Description
1. Diversification Adequate but not excessive: A minimum of ten different issues and a maximum of about thirty.
2. Size and Prominence Each company must be large, prominent, and conservatively financed.
3. Conservative Financing For industrial companies, common stock (at book value) must represent at least half of the total capitalization (including debt). For utilities, at least 30%.
4. Dividend Record The company should have a long record of continuous dividend payments.

30.6.2 Application: Dollar-Cost Averaging

Dollar-cost averaging (DCA) is a simple practice where the investor invests the same number of dollars each period (e.g., monthly or quarterly).

  • Mechanism: DCA ensures the investor buys more shares when the market is low than when it is high, leading to a satisfactory overall average cost.
  • Confidence: DCA is recommended as a strategy that can be used “with so much confidence of ultimate success, regardless of what may happen to security prices”.

30.6.3 The Concept of “Risk”

The terms “risk” and “safety” are often used confusingly. For the defensive investor, market price fluctuation does not equal a true risk of loss.

  • True Risk Definition: Risk should be applied solely to a loss of value which is either:
    • (1) realized through actual sale,
    • (2) caused by a significant deterioration in the company’s position, or
    • (3) the result of paying an excessive price in relation to intrinsic worth.
  • Long-Term Safety: If a well-selected, diversified group of common stocks shows a satisfactory overall return over many years, the group investment has proved to be “safe,” regardless of temporary price drops.

30.6.4 The Investor’s Personal Situation

The kind of securities purchased and the rate of return sought depend not on the investor’s financial resources, but on their financial equipment in terms of knowledge, experience, and temperament.

  • Inexperienced Investors: Those who are busy or lack training should avoid stocks entirely, confine themselves to bonds, or use the professional administration of investment funds.

30.6.5 Recap

  • Diversification: Hold 10 to 30 well-chosen issues.
  • Quality Stocks: Limit purchases to large, prominent, conservatively financed companies with long records of continuous dividends.
  • DCA: Dollar-cost averaging (investing fixed dollars periodically) helps mitigate market fluctuations and ensures buying low.
  • Risk: Market price fluctuation is not true risk; true risk comes from selling at a loss, corporate deterioration, or buying at an excessive price.
  • Temperament over Wealth: Investment policy depends on the investor’s financial equipment (knowledge, experience, temperament), not merely their wealth.

30.7 Portfolio Policy for the Enterprising Investor: Negative Approach

The enterprising investor, aiming for better-than-average returns, must adhere to crucial negative guidelines—what not to do—to avoid pitfalls that destroy wealth.

30.7.1 Avoiding Second-Grade Bonds and Preferreds

The enterprising investor must maintain a defensive stance against second-grade (lower quality) fixed-income issues.

  • The Lure: Second-grade issues offer higher yields (e.g., up to 8% or more historically).
  • The Danger: Buying second-grade issues at full prices (par or near par) is unwise. Even if higher yields ultimately offset principal losses in the long run (which is difficult for an individual to guarantee), the buyer will be worried and discommed when prices decline precipitously.
  • Rule of Thumb: Abstain from buying such securities around 100 if experience indicates they can probably be bought at 70 or less in the next weak market.

30.7.2 Warning Against Hot Common Stock Issues

The aggressive investor is tempted by new offerings and “hot” issues, which are often highly speculative and dangerous.

  • New Offerings (IPOs): The original sale of common stock of a previously unlisted company usually calls for intensive selling effort. The public’s capacity for enthusiasm and greed means they are often willing to pay heavily in advance for hoped-for plans.
  • Catastrophic Losses: History (e.g., AAA Enterprises, ZZZZ Best) is replete with examples of shares being sold to the public at inflated prices, soaring initially, and then collapsing to near zero.
  • The Moral: “For every dollar you make” as an enterprising investor chasing hot issues, “you will be lucky if you end up by losing only two”.

30.7.3 Application: Avoiding Alternative Investments

Enterprising investors are often tempted by “alternative” assets (e.g., hedge funds, private equity, private credit funds). Graham warns against these, as they are often highly speculative and poorly suited for individuals.

Risk/Drawback Description and Application
High Fees Total annual expenses can be extortionately high (e.g., 7.49%), guaranteed to reduce net returns.
Illiquidity Funds often lock up money for years, meaning you cannot withdraw cash when needed or when a better opportunity arises.
Leverage Risk These funds commonly use borrowed money (margin), increasing the overall risk of the investor’s portfolio, even if the investor didn’t personally borrow.
Institutional Advantages To invest successfully in alternatives (like Yale), an investor needs huge size (to access top funds), long time horizons, institutional expertise, and financial resilience to survive deep, permanent losses—advantages individuals lack.

30.7.4 Application: Avoiding Speculative Fads

Investors must protect against contemporary speculative fads like SPACs (Special-Purpose Acquisition Companies) or non-fungible tokens (NFTs).

  • The Illusion: Fads leverage celebrity endorsements or technological buzzwords, appealing to the desire for quick wealth. Large and quick profits are sufficient to dull the public’s critical faculty.
  • The Reality: These vehicles are riddled with complex conflicts of interest (insiders pay minimal amounts for control, managers are incentivized to push bad deals). The enterprising investor must commit significant time and energy to analysis before buying, seeking evidence, not fame.

30.7.5 Recap

  • Starting Point: The enterprising investor must start defensively with sound securities bought at reasonable prices.
  • Second-Grade Issues: Buy second-grade bonds or preferreds only at depressed prices, as purchasing them at par guarantees worry and potential losses.
  • Hot Issues/IPOs: Avoid new or “hot” common stocks; the potential for large and quick profits is far outweighed by the risk of catastrophic losses.
  • Alternatives: Avoid most alternative investments (hedge funds, private equity) due to high fees, illiquidity, and embedded leverage, which are structurally unfit for individual investors.
  • Discipline: To succeed, the enterprising investor must commit to intensive time and effort, realizing there is no shortcut to higher-than-average returns.

30.8 Portfolio Policy for the Enterprising Investor: The Positive Side

The enterprising investor aims for superior results through special opportunities. This effort must be viewed as a business enterprise.

30.8.1 Activities for the Enterprising Investor

The special activities for the enterprising investor in the common-stock field fall into four categories:

  1. Buying in low markets and selling in high markets (Market Timing/Formula Timing).
  2. Buying carefully chosen “growth stocks.”
  3. Buying bargain issues of various types.
  4. Buying into “special situations” (Work-outs).

30.8.2 1. Market Timing is Futile

Attempting to time general market movements (buying low and selling high) on a mathematical basis has not worked well since 1949. The market’s action has instead required a “special talent or ‘feel’ for trading” that cannot be taught. The 50-50 formula (rebalancing) remains the best specific, automatic formula for all investors.

30.8.3 2. The Growth-Stock Approach

Every investor wants stocks that outperform the average, but the selection of growth stocks (companies expected to grow faster than average) is complicated.

  • The Cost of Growth: If excellent prospects are fully recognized in the market, they are already reflected in a high price (e.g., a P/E ratio higher than 20), thereby eliminating the margin of safety.
  • Mutual Fund Performance: Even diversified investment in growth companies, handled by expert growth funds, yields no outstanding rewards compared with common stocks generally.
  • Unpopular Large Companies: A sound strategy is focusing on large, unpopular companies (like the low-multiplier issues in the DJIA list). Historically, these low-multiplier stocks have consistently outperformed the average.

30.8.4 3. Buying Bargain Issues (Value Investing)

A bargain issue is one that, on the basis of established facts by analysis, appears to be worth considerably more than it is selling for—at least 50% more than the price.

  • Net-Current-Asset Stocks: The most readily identifiable bargain issue is a common stock that sells for less than the company’s net working capital alone (current assets minus all liabilities). The buyer effectively pays nothing for the fixed assets (buildings, machinery, etc.).
  • Results: This strategy proved highly successful historically, yielding high average profits, provided the investor bought a diversified group and had the patience required for the stock to advance.

30.8.5 4. Application: SOYA Investing and Watchlists

The successful enterprising investor minimizes activity and maximizes analytical quality by adhering to a “SOYA” (Sit On Your Ass) investing philosophy.

  • Testing: Use a pretend portfolio for one year to test systematic methods of identifying undervalued businesses, not just chasing short-term gains. Judge success by whether the original rationale (documented via a checklist) was right.
  • Watchlists: Maintain a watchlist of healthy companies whose stocks have taken a dive but whose business is unimpaired. This helps override the instinctive, destructive tendency to buy high and sell low.
  • Memorialize Mistakes: Keep a “mistake wall” or detailed records of investment errors to shift the focus from the bad outcome to the flawed process (sins of commission or omission) and make it easier to learn and correct behavior.

30.8.6 No Middle Ground

There is no room in Graham’s philosophy for a middle ground between the passive (defensive) and aggressive (enterprising) investor. The investor cannot soundly become “half a businessman,” expecting half the normal business profits. If an investor seeks superior results, they must commit significant time, knowledge, and effort.

30.8.7 Recap

  • Market Timing: Attempting to time market fluctuations for profit is highly difficult and usually futile; stick to disciplined rebalancing.
  • Growth Stocks: Avoid paying high P/E ratios (e.g., over 20) for popular growth stocks, as the potential returns are often already discounted in the price.
  • Strategy: Focus on low-multiplier issues and healthy companies that are currently unpopular or neglected.
  • Bargain Issues: The deepest value is found in net-current-asset stocks (selling below liquid working capital), which offer a demonstrable margin of safety.
  • Discipline and Effort: Success requires viewing investment as a committed business enterprise, relying on careful analysis (checklists, watchlists) and discipline, rather than intuition or partial effort.

30.9 The Investor and Market Fluctuations

The investor must expect common stocks to fluctuate widely in value over the years. The key is how the investor reacts to these inevitable swings.

30.9.1 The Futility of Forecasting

A great deal of brainpower goes into market forecasting, but it is absurd to think that the general public can ever make money out of market forecasts. The average investor cannot anticipate market movements more successfully than the general public of which he is a part.

  • Investment Basis: Investment decisions should rest on established values and sound principles, not on market forecasts.
  • The Human Element: The investor must arm himself against the human tendency to become infected with the enthusiasm, overconfidence, and greed of the great public during bull markets.

30.9.2 Framework: The Mr. Market Parable

Graham introduced Mr. Market as a brilliant metaphor for the stock market.

  • The Partner: Imagine a business partner, Mr. Market, who shows up every day and offers to either buy your share or sell you an additional interest, based on what he thinks your interest is worth.
  • The Mood: Sometimes Mr. Market’s prices are plausible. Often, however, he lets his enthusiasm or fears run wild, offering prices that are “little short of silly”.
  • Investor’s Advantage: The intelligent investor is free to disregard Mr. Market’s quotations entirely, treating them as irrelevant unless they offer a significant advantage. The investor should buy from Mr. Market when he is gloomy (selling low) and sell to him when he is euphoric (buying high).
  • The Danger: The investor who permits himself to be stampeded or unduly worried by unjustified market declines is perversely transforming his basic advantage into a basic disadvantage.

30.9.3 The Paradox of Quality

There is a built-in contradiction in stock market quotations: the better a company’s record and prospects, the less relationship its price will have to its book value.

  • The Dilemma: The greater the premium above book value, the more the “value” depends on the changing moods and measurements of the stock market.
  • The Paradox: The more successful the company, the greater are likely to be the fluctuations in its price, meaning that in a very real sense, the better the quality of a common stock, the more speculative it is likely to be.

30.9.4 Application: Anchor Value to Tangible Assets

To maintain an independent and detached view of stock-market fluctuations, the conservative investor should concentrate on issues selling at a reasonably close approximation to their tangible-asset value (e.g., not more than one-third above that figure).

  • Justification: Purchases made near or below asset value can be logically regarded as having a justification or support independent of fluctuating market prices.
  • Master Game: With a portfolio anchored by sound book values, the investor can confidently ignore market swings and use the market’s “vagaries to play the master game of buying low and selling high”.

30.9.5 Framework: Mechanical Rebalancing

Some kind of mechanical method for varying the proportion of bonds to stocks is favored, primarily to counter the emotional urges of human nature.

  • Function: As the market advances, the formula dictates selling stocks and buying bonds; as it declines, the procedure is reversed.
  • Discipline: This approach provides an outlet for “otherwise too-pent-up energies” and ensures the investor’s operations are exactly opposite from those of the crowd.

30.9.6 Recap

  • Avoid Forecasting: The average investor cannot profit from market forecasts; success comes from acting on established value principles.
  • Mr. Market: Treat the market (Mr. Market) as an obliging but manic partner; use his irrationality to your advantage, selling to his enthusiasm and buying from his fear.
  • Investor Folly: Allowing market volatility to dictate action is the biggest mistake an investor can make.
  • Paradox: The highest quality, most successful growth companies are often the most speculative due to the high premiums paid above tangible assets.
  • Anchor Value: The conservative investor should choose stocks priced reasonably close to their tangible-asset value to ensure a justification independent of market price.

30.10 Investing in Investment Funds

30.10.1 Closed-End vs. Open-End Funds (Mutual Funds)

Investment funds offer a simple course for the defensive investor.

  • Mutual Funds (Open-End): Redeemable on demand at net asset value. They actively sell shares, often with a large sales load (premium/commission, generally 9%).
  • Closed-End Funds: Fixed capital structure; shares are traded on exchanges, often selling at a discount from their net asset value (NAV).

30.10.2 The Futility of Active Management

The mutual fund industry, as a whole, has failed to consistently outperform the general market averages (e.g., S&P Composite).

  • The Logic: Fund managers collectively administer so large a portion of common stocks that, mathematically, what happens to the market as a whole must happen approximately to the sum of their funds.
  • Performance Chasing: Looking for superior performance by selecting past winners is risky. Performance funds that show spectacular, short-term results often achieve them in unorthodox ways, leading to inevitable calamitous losses.

30.10.3 Application: Selection of Investment Funds

Graham provides one of the few clearly evident rules for selecting funds:

  • Rule: If you want to put money in investment funds, buy a group of closed-end shares at a discount (e.g., 10% to 15%) from asset value, instead of paying a premium (load) of about 9% above asset value for shares of an open-end company.
  • Reasoning: Assuming similar investment performance, buying at a discount ensures a significantly higher return for the money invested.

30.10.4 Framework: Low-Cost Index Funds/ETFs

The failure of most actively managed funds to beat the market means that the defensive investor can match the market average with little effort and nearly no cost by buying and holding an index fund. The advent of low-cost Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs) further simplifies this.

  • Index Fund Advantages: Provides the possibility of obtaining approximately average results on stock commitments, which is superior to the performance of most individuals who pick their own stocks.
  • Selecting an ETF (Efficiency, Tradability, Fit):
    1. Efficiency: Low annual expenses, preferably 0.10% or less.
    2. Tradability: High trading volume (e.g., \(\ge\) $250,000 daily in the U.S.); bid/ask spread 0.05% or less.
    3. Fit (Diversification): Broad diversification, such as a total market ETF covering the home country and another covering the rest of the world.
  • Warning: Avoid funds that are inverse, leveraged, or specialized (narrow niche ETFs), as these “di-worse-ify” the portfolio with risky clusters of weird assets.

30.10.5 Recap

  • Futility: Most actively managed investment funds fail to consistently outperform broad market averages over the long term.
  • Recommendation: The defensive investor should focus on low-cost index funds or broadly diversified ETFs to obtain average market results.
  • Selection Rule: Choose closed-end funds at a discount to net asset value, rather than paying a sales load/premium for open-end mutual funds.
  • ETFs: Select ETFs based on low Efficiency (low expenses), high Tradability (high volume, tight spread), and broad Fit (diversification).
  • Specialization: Avoid chasing performance or specializing in narrow fund niches, as these are often high-cost and high-risk.

30.11 The Investor and His Advisers

Investment in securities is unique because it is almost always based on advice from others. However, the investor must be wary of the nature and potential conflicts within the advisory structure.

30.11.1 The Problem of Advice

If the investor relies chiefly on the advice of others, they must limit themselves and their advisers strictly to standard, conservative, and even unimaginative forms of investment.

  • Independent Judgment: If seeking unconventional suggestions (the domain of the enterprising investor), the investor must have grown enough in knowledge and experience to pass independent judgment on the recommendations.

30.11.2 Types of Advisers

  1. Investment Counsel Firms & Trust Departments: These truly professional advisers are generally modest in their promises, relying mainly on normal investment experience. Their primary aim is to conserve principal value and produce an acceptable rate of income. Their chief value is shielding clients from costly mistakes.
  2. Financial Services: Offer information and guidance via uniform bulletins and statistical data. They often suffer from a pervasive attitude that stocks should be bought if near-term prospects are favorable, regardless of current price. The intelligent investor should use them only to supply information and suggestions, not as the sole basis for buying/selling.
  3. Brokerage Houses: Provide the largest volume of advice. Since their business is to earn commissions, their activities are often geared to day-to-day trading, meaning they try to help customers make money in a field (speculation) where they are mathematically condemned to lose in the end.

30.11.3 Framework: Choosing a Fiduciary Advisor

A good adviser can prevent the investor from being their own worst enemy (the inner con artist), but finding the right one is difficult.

  • Fees for Service (Avoid AUM): Most advisers charge based on Assets Under Management (AUM), which rewards them for how much money the client has, rather than the quality of personalized advice (e.g., financial planning). Look for an adviser who charges fees for service (by the hour, retainer, or subscription).
  • Fiduciary Status: Insist on an adviser who will act as a fiduciary, obligated to provide advice in the client’s best interest and disclose all conflicts.
  • Background Check: Use regulatory databases (FINRA, SEC) to check for disciplinary history, lawsuits, and criminal cases.
  • Warning Signs (Form ADV): Avoid advisers whose disclosures indicate frequent use of annuities, charting, tactical trading, or proprietary private funds.
  • Interview Questions: Ask critical questions, ideally in writing, covering fiduciary status, scope of advice (planning vs. management), compensation methods, and whether they invest in the same assets they recommend. Use a numerical rating system to score responses objectively, overriding gut feelings.

30.11.4 Recap

  • Reliance on Advice: If relying on external advice, stick to conservative, standard investments; otherwise, develop independent judgment.
  • Brokerage Conflicts: Brokerage house advice is often speculation-minded, designed to generate commissions, and should be treated cautiously.
  • Investment Counsel: Professional firms focus on principal conservation and careful, standard investment practice.
  • Fiduciary Focus: Seek an adviser who is a fiduciary (acting in your best interest) and charges fees for services rather than a percentage of assets (AUM).
  • Due Diligence: Conduct thorough background checks and interview candidates using a structured, objective process.

30.12 Security Analysis for the Lay Investor: General Approach

Security analysis involves the examination and evaluation of stocks and bonds. The nonprofessional investor should be equipped to distinguish between superficial and sound analysis.

30.12.1 The Valuation Paradox

The valuation techniques used by analysts often suffer from a troublesome paradox:

  • Inverse Reliability: The mathematical valuations have become most prevalent precisely in those areas (high-growth stocks) where they are least reliable. The more a valuation depends on future anticipation, the more vulnerable it is to miscalculation.
  • Simple Arithmetic: In four decades of experience, dependable calculations about common-stock values rarely went beyond simple arithmetic; higher mathematics often substitutes theory for adequate knowledge.

30.12.2 A. Bond Analysis: Safety Standards

Bond safety is determined primarily by earnings coverage.

  • Earning Coverage Test: The chief criterion is the number of times that total interest charges have been covered by available earnings over a period of years. Minimum standards (e.g., 5x pre-tax coverage for industrial bonds) were historically crucial for avoiding defaults (e.g., Penn Central collapse).
  • Stock/Equity Ratio: This ratio (market price of common stock relative to total debt) measures the “cushion” afforded by the junior investment, reflecting the market’s appraisal of future prospects.

30.12.3 B. Common Stock Valuation: The Growth Formula

Security analysis projects future earning power, but forecasts for individual companies often prove wide of the mark.

  • The Simplified Growth Stock Valuation Formula: Graham proposed a simple, illustrative formula to approximate the results of more complex calculations, intended for growth stocks expected to grow over the next seven to ten years: \[\text{Value} = \text{Current (Normal) Earnings} \times (\mathbf{8.5} + \mathbf{2} \times \text{Expected Annual Growth Rate})\]
  • Formula Interpretation: The base multiplier (8.5) represents a non-growth stock; the second term reflects the premium paid for future growth. Caution: This formula is for illustration only; projections have no high degree of reliability.

30.12.4 Framework: The Analytical Checklist

To succeed in the inherently “wicked” learning environment of the stock market (where feedback is delayed, inaccurate, and ambiguous), the enterprising investor must impose discipline through a checklist.

  • Rationale Recording: The checklist ensures documentation of rationale before buying, enabling the investor to learn whether they were right for the right reasons later.
  • Key Checklist Components (Focus on Business, Not Stock):
    1. Financial Health: Read at least the last three annual reports (10-K). Understand the balance sheet (debt danger), income statement (cost control), and cash flow statement (consistent cash generation).
    2. Competitive Advantage (Moat): Determine the company’s “moat”—its durable advantages (e.g., brand, monopoly, superior cost structure) that make it hard for customers to switch.
    3. Management Quality: Read the proxy statement. Is compensation fair relative to peers? Are executives buying shares with their own cash? Is incentive pay tied to sensible, long-term metrics (e.g., ROE growth over multi-year horizons)?.
    4. Premortem: Conduct a premortem: Imagine the company has filed for bankruptcy in one year—what went wrong?.
    5. Edge and Confidence: Define exactly what you understand that most other investors are missing, and state the probability (e.g., 80% certainty) you believe you are correct.
    6. Rule: If you haven’t followed all the steps, you cannot buy the investment.

30.12.5 Recap

  • Analysis is Difficult: The reliability of valuation decreases as reliance on future projections increases.
  • Bond Safety: Primary safety tests for bonds and preferred stocks rely on “coverage” (times charges earned) and the stock/equity cushion.
  • Growth Formula: The Value = Normal Earnings \(\times\) (8.5 + 2 \(\times\) Growth Rate) formula provides an approximate valuation framework for growth expectations.
  • Checklist Discipline: A formal, mandatory analytical checklist is essential for the enterprising investor to maintain discipline and ensure objective, consistent valuation based on the underlying business.
  • Value Focus: The checklist must refocus attention away from the stock price and onto the business fundamentals (cash flow, debt, moat, management).

30.13 Things to Consider About Per-Share Earnings

Earnings per share (EPS) figures receive major attention, but they abound in misleading possibilities. Investors must be educated about the booby traps in reported earnings.

30.13.1 Primary Warnings on Earnings

  1. Don’t take a single year’s earnings seriously: Base valuations on average earnings over a multi-year period (7–10 years) to smooth out business cycles and temporary anomalies.
  2. Beware of Booby Traps: Numerous accounting factors can impair the true comparability of EPS numbers.

30.13.2 Accounting Anomalies

Trap/Anomaly Explanation and Application
Dilution Potential issuance of new shares from convertible bonds, preferred stock, or warrants must be accounted for. The analyst must calculate earnings on a fully diluted basis, assuming all conversions/exercises.
Special Charges Companies often deduct losses (e.g., restructuring, asset write-downs) as “extraordinary” or “nonrecurring” to inflate primary earnings. These should typically be included in average earnings as they represent a part of the company’s operating history.
Tax Factors Past losses may result in temporary tax exemptions (e.g., loss carryforwards), where the company pays minimal income tax. True earning power should first be calculated on the basis of full income-tax liability.
Non-GAAP Reporting Companies frequently calculate “non-GAAP” figures (excluding charges like restructuring or acquisition costs) to cleanse their financial statements. The enterprising investor must determine if these non-GAAP numbers paint a fair picture or a fantasy.
Accounting Method Changes Changes in depreciation method (straight-line vs. accelerated) or inventory valuation (FIFO vs. LIFO) can significantly alter reported EPS, requiring careful scrutiny.

30.13.3 Framework: Use of Average Earnings

The historical practice of using average earnings over seven to ten years is recommended.

  • Purpose: This “mean figure” irons out frequent ups and downs of the business cycle and solves the problem of how to handle nearly all special charges and credits (by including them in the average).
  • The Calculation: Once average earnings are derived, these figures, along with ratings for growth and stability, provide a solid starting point for stock valuation.

30.13.4 Recap

  • Single-Year Trap: Never rely on a single year’s earnings; use a multi-year average (7–10 years) to determine true earning power.
  • Dilution: Always calculate earnings on a fully diluted basis to account for the potential issuance of shares from convertibles and warrants.
  • Special Charges: Include most special charges (e.g., restructuring costs) in the calculation of average earnings, as these represent real operational history.
  • Non-GAAP: Be skeptical of “non-GAAP” earnings, which often camouflage expenses; verify if these reports provide a fair picture or a fantasy.
  • Valuation: Dependable stock valuations are the exception, not the rule; for most investors, it is best to assure they are getting good value and let it go at that.

30.14 A Comparison of Four Listed Companies

This chapter demonstrates security analysis in operation by comparing companies that are superficially similar (listed next to each other on the NYSE) but fundamentally different.

30.14.1 The Shift from Superficial to Fundamental

The comparison highlights that the investor must look past the stock price (superficial) to the performance of the business (fundamental). The evaluation depends on six key dimensions:

  1. Profitability: Measured by earnings on book value (high return often indicates high growth potential) and operating income per dollar of sales.
  2. Stability: Measured by consistency of earnings (e.g., no decline in EPS against the average of the prior three years for 10 consecutive years).
  3. Growth: Measured by the historical rate of growth in revenue and earnings per share.
  4. Financial Position: Measured by working capital (current assets/liabilities) and total debt burden.
  5. Dividends: Measured by continuity and yield.
  6. Price History: Measured by price fluctuations (high prices entail high risk).

30.14.2 Core Observation: Value vs. Glamour

The chapter illustrates the dangers of “glamour” stocks—those with impressive profitability and growth but priced highly.

  • High Risk: High valuations entail high risks. A company like Emerson Electric (a “good-will giant” with enormous market value and high multipliers) is analogous to Zenith Radio, which had a brilliant record but saw its price collapse after profits fell.
  • Investor Preference: The fundamental reason for preferring value-type investments (lower multipliers, closer to asset value) to glamour-type investments (high multipliers) is the client’s considered conclusion that they prefer value to glamour.
  • Disengaging Attention: Disengaging attention from the stock price and focusing on performance is the best way to assess whether the next phase of a business will be distinction or extinction.

30.14.3 Recap

  • Focus on Business: Investment analysis must focus on fundamental business performance metrics, not just stock price.
  • Six Dimensions: Analyze companies across Profitability, Stability, Growth, Financial Position, Dividends, and Price History.
  • High Risk in High Value: High valuations (high P/E and price-to-book) entail high risk, as they are dependent on perpetually increasing earnings.
  • Value vs. Glamour: The soundest preference for an investor is for companies that offer underlying value (lower multipliers), regardless of the current market momentum of glamour stocks.

30.15 Stock Selection for the Defensive Investor

The defensive investor seeks a conservative portfolio of high-grade bonds and a diversified list of leading common stocks. The goal is satisfactory, not spectacular, results.

30.15.1 Two Approaches to Portfolio Construction

  1. The DJIA-Type Portfolio (Index Approach): The simplest way is to buy a true cross-section sample of the leading issues, which can be done by buying all thirty issues in the Dow-Jones Industrial Average (DJIA), or, more simply today, by buying shares of a representative investment fund (low-cost index fund).
  2. The Quantitatively-Tested Portfolio: Applying a set of objective, numerical standards to ensure minimum quality and minimum quantity (earnings and assets) per dollar of price.

30.15.2 Framework: Seven Quantitative Criteria for Defensive Stock Selection

The selection criteria emphasize protection against loss (safety) and purchasing stocks at a reasonable price (quantity/value).

Criterion Standard Purpose
1. Adequate Size Should be a major company (e.g., $100 million in annual sales for industrials, $50 million in assets for utilities). Ensures stability and prominence.
2. Strong Financial Condition Current assets \(\ge\) 2x current liabilities, AND debt \(\le\) net current assets. Ensures liquidity and limits exposure to financial distress.
3. Earnings Stability No deficit in the last ten years. Proves resilience across economic cycles.
4. Dividend Record Continuous dividend payments for at least 20 years. Demonstrates a long history of corporate responsibility and consistent profitability.
5. Earnings Growth Minimum one-third increase in EPS over the last 10 years, using a 3-year average at the beginning and end. Ensures the company is keeping pace with the national economy.
6. Moderate P/E Ratio Current price \(\le\) 15 times average earnings of the past three years. Limits the price paid for expected future growth, maintaining a factor of safety.
7. Moderate Price/Asset Ratio Current price \(\le\) 1.5 times book value. The product of the P/E ratio \(\times\) Price/Book ratio should not exceed 22.5 (e.g., \(15 \times 1.5\)). Anchors the price to tangible worth, avoiding excessive premiums for goodwill.

30.15.3 Conclusion: Diversification over Selectivity

Applying these stringent criteria reduces the pool of eligible stocks significantly (e.g., only five out of 30 DJIA stocks met all seven criteria in 1971).

  • Analyst Disagreement: Analysts widely disagree on the “best” stocks because current price often reflects all known factors, leaving selection largely to personal expectations.
  • Defensive Investor Advice: The advice to the defensive investor is to let individual selection alone. Emphasize diversification more than individual selection. The purpose of the criteria is to ensure the investor is getting his money’s worth, not guaranteeing superior market results.

30.15.4 Application: Combating the Inner Con Artist

Defensive investors must fight their natural tendency toward overconfidence, which is often fueled by generalizing from short-term market gains.

  • Evidence-Based Confidence: Force yourself to answer specific questions to ground confidence in objective reality:
    • How much is my portfolio up relative to the market?
    • What exactly do I know about this asset that most other people don’t?
    • Can I give three detailed reasons why this asset is still undervalued?.
  • Futility of Prediction: Conduct exercises in predicting the price of major indexes or commodities in a year to demonstrate the delusion of knowing how to predict markets.

30.15.5 Recap

  • Defensive Goal: Avoid serious losses and seek average results with minimal effort.
  • Stock Selection: Use a total market index fund (DJIA-type portfolio) or rely on the Seven Quantitative Criteria to ensure quality and value.
  • Key Criterion: Strict limits on price relative to earnings (P/E \(\le\) 15) and assets (P/B \(\le\) 1.5) are critical for maintaining the margin of safety.
  • Risk Mitigation: The criteria guarantee that the buyer gets their money’s worth, mitigating the risk of overpaying.
  • Final Word: The defensive investor should prioritize diversification over attempting to select individual “winners,” as successful selection is highly controversial.

30.16 Stock Selection for the Enterprising Investor

The enterprising investor aims to outperform the average, which is a difficult and potentially impracticable assignment, given that even top investment funds consistently fail to beat the market.

30.16.1 Graham-Newman Strategies for Superior Results

The historical success of Graham-Newman Corporation demonstrated that superior returns could be achieved by exploiting discrepancies between price and value in two main areas, requiring a specialized temperament:

  1. Unrelated Hedges: Buying stocks perceived as cheap and selling short stocks perceived as dear.
  2. Related Hedges (“Work-outs”): Buying convertible bonds/preferreds and simultaneously shorting the common stock to lock in a profit from a likely conversion/takeover.
  3. Net-Current-Asset (NCA) Issues (Bargains): Acquiring a diversified group of issues where the stock price is less than the net current assets alone (working capital).

30.16.2 Methodology: Finding Bargain Issues

The NCA strategy is the most effective approach for the enterprising individual, offering a huge margin of safety.

  • The NCA Rule: The price must be less than the current assets minus all liabilities (giving zero value to fixed assets like plant and machinery).
  • Patience and Diversification: This method requires buying a diversified group (to spread risk) and having patience, as it may take considerable time for the market to recognize the value.

30.16.3 Framework: The Stock Winnowing Process

The enterprising investor can apply simplified criteria to quickly screen the market for potentially undervalued stocks.

  • Step 1: Low Multiplier Screen: Start with a simple prima facie indication of cheapness, such as a low P/E ratio (e.g., \(\le\) 9 times recent earnings).
  • Step 2: Apply Quality Filters: Apply multiple, not overly severe, criteria to the screened list (similar to the defensive criteria but less stringent):
    1. Financial Condition: Current assets \(\ge\) 1.5x current liabilities, AND debt \(\le\) 110% of net current assets.
    2. Earnings Stability: No deficit in the last five years.
    3. Dividend Record: Some current dividend.
    4. Earnings Growth: Last year’s earnings must exceed those of four years prior (e.g., 1966).
    5. Price/Asset: Price \(\le\) 120% of net tangible assets.
  • Step 3: Diversify and Judge: This process (the “winnowing”) yields an ample list of stocks of at least average quality, purchasable at a low multiplier and near asset value, offering a good promise of satisfactory results.

30.16.4 The Danger of Goodwill Giants

Enterprising investors must be wary of “good-will giants”—companies combining great size, an excellent past record, and high public expectation of future growth (e.g., IBM).

  • Momentum: These issues often possess considerable momentum, which can carry them along indefinitely even if the price appears excessive by quantitative standards.
  • Price/Book: High prices for goodwill giants mean they sell for multiples far exceeding their book value, making them speculative (see Paradox of Quality in Chapter 8).

30.16.5 Recap

  • Investor Goal: Outperforming the market requires policies that are difficult and unpopular.
  • NCA Bargains: Focus analytically on Net-Current-Asset stocks (priced below net liquid working capital) as the clearest source of deep value and margin of safety.
  • Screening: Use quantitative filters (e.g., P/E \(\\le\) 9, strong financial health, modest P/B) to find overlooked, bargain-priced secondary companies.
  • Secondary Companies: Buy secondary companies only at a bargain discount to their value; their volatility makes purchasing at full price dangerous.
  • SOYA Investing: The enterprising investor maximizes probability of success by minimizing activity (“SOYA”), focusing intensive analysis on only a few opportunities.

30.17 Convertible Issues and Warrants

Convertible bonds and preferred stocks are hybrid securities that offer both the safety of a fixed claim and the opportunity to participate in stock appreciation.

30.17.1 Convertible Securities

In exchange for the conversion privilege (the option to trade the bond/preferred for common stock), the investor usually gives up something important, typically quality or yield, or both.

  • Structure: Convertibles are designed to appeal to a wider range of buyers (like institutions restricted from buying stocks) and allow companies to raise capital at a lower cost.
  • Call Provision: Convertibles often carry a redemption or “call” price, which allows the company to force conversion if the stock price rises high enough, effectively forcing the investor to take the common stock.

30.17.2 Warrants and Dilution

A warrant is a separate certificate granting the right to subscribe for (buy) a specified number of common shares at a fixed price.

  • Dilution: The existence of warrants (or convertibles) fundamentally dilutes the value inherent in an ordinary common share by creating the right for others to buy new shares.
  • Accounting Necessity: When calculating earnings per share (EPS), dilution must be quantified by calculating the adjusted EPS if all outstanding warrants or convertibles were exercised.
  • Financial Legerdemain: Warrants are often used to inflate the effective capitalization of a company or in financial maneuvers, contributing to the “speculative enthusiasm” of the market.

30.17.3 Application: Options and Hedging

Options (calls, the right to buy; puts, the right to sell) can be used judiciously by intelligent investors, primarily for hedging or contained speculation.

  • Hedging/Insurance: Options allow the investor to lock in a price range for a stock position, ensuring against a severe decline (by buying puts) or capitalizing on income (by selling calls). This can be used to protect a major investment goal (like a house down payment).
  • Mad Money: Options can be used for speculation within the dedicated “mad money” account (maximum 5% of assets). However, this incurs high costs and risks a 100% loss of the premium, demanding keen monitoring.

30.17.4 Recap

  • Convertibles: Hybrid securities offering fixed income stability plus stock participation, but they typically require concessions in quality or yield.
  • Warrants: Separate certificates that dilute the value of common shares, often arising from financial maneuvering.
  • Dilution Accounting: The investor must always calculate earnings on a fully diluted basis to reflect the potential increase in outstanding shares.
  • Options Use: Options are appropriate primarily for hedging existing positions or insuring against risk, but should be avoided for most speculative trading due to high complexity and risk.

30.18 Four Extremely Instructive Case Histories

Graham presents four case histories that exemplify the extremes, warnings, and pitfalls present on Wall Street in recent years.

30.18.1 1. Penn Central Railroad (The Under-analysis Warning)

The collapse of Penn Central demonstrated that the application of the simplest rules of security analysis would have revealed the fundamental weakness long before bankruptcy.

  • Warning: Despite appearances, Penn Central failed to meet conservative bond safety standards, as its interest charges were covered less than two times—far below the required minimum (historically 5x pre-tax).
  • Moral: Security analysts and conservative investors must not ignore fundamental safety standards, even if the company is large and prominent.

30.18.2 2. Ling-Temco-Vought (The Debt and Expansion Warning)

LTV exemplified head-over-heels expansion financed by massive debt.

  • Warning: LTV’s debt ballooned from $44 million to $1.865 billion, primarily relying on inflating “goodwill” assets (non-tangible value) to justify its high market price (22x tangible asset value). These paper assets were wiped out by losses.
  • Moral: Commercial banks (lenders) and investors should not aid and abet unsound expansions where earnings coverage is inadequate and the equity base is flimsy.

30.18.3 3. NVF Corp. (The Accounting Gimmick Warning)

NVF’s takeover of Sharon Steel used complex financial engineering (convertible debentures, warrants) to inflate results through obscure accounting.

  • Warning: The complex maneuvers reduced the company’s “real equity” substantially while temporarily manipulating earnings per share to appear low (giving a deceptively low P/E ratio of 2x).
  • Moral: The investor must be vigilant against complex accounting that masks the true financial position and dilution. A P/E ratio that is “too good to be true” usually is.

30.18.4 4. AAA Enterprises (The Public Greed Warning)

AAA Enterprises was a “hot issue”—an IPO based on little more than ambitious plans, which shot up in price before collapsing to worthlessness.

  • Warning: The stock soared to 28 based on enthusiasm and hope, wildly detached from its minuscule assets and non-existent earnings power.
  • Moral: The speculative public is incorrigible and will buy anything, at any price, if there seems to be “action” in progress (e.g., franchising, technology). The abuses in new offerings are largely the result of the public’s own heedlessness and greed, which regulation cannot fully prevent.

30.18.5 Recap

  • Fundamentals Over Appearances: Simple security analysis standards for debt coverage and solvency should never be ignored, regardless of a company’s size or fame (Penn Central).
  • Balance Sheet Scrutiny: Beware of empires built on excessive debt and non-tangible goodwill assets that vanish when operations deteriorate (Ling-Temco-Vought).
  • Accounting Scrutiny: Analyze complex financial structures (convertibles, warrants) that use accounting gimmicks to dilute equity or obscure losses (NVF).
  • Avoid Hot Issues: Never buy stocks based on hype, promises, or speculative fads, as such issues are almost mathematically doomed to fail for the typical investor (AAA Enterprises).

30.19 A Comparison of Eight Pairs of Companies

Graham compares eight pairs of similarly named, adjacent companies on the NYSE list to demonstrate the wide variation in corporate character and investment attitudes.

30.19.1 Core Conclusion: Price Determines Investment Quality

The comparisons reinforce the idea that there is no such thing as a good or bad stock; there are only cheap or expensive stocks. An experienced analyst should concede momentum to high-growth companies but must always question if the current valuation has already fully discounted all reasonably expected future growth.

30.19.2 Key Observations from Comparisons

Comparison Theme Example Graham/Zweig Conclusion
Simple vs. Complex National General vs. National Presto Industries National General used complex, heavily leveraged financial gadgets (warrants, convertibles) to obscure its true, risky valuation, while Presto had a simple, asset-backed structure. Simplicity is superior for the investor.

30.19.3 The Analyst’s Preferred Role

Most security analysts focus on selecting future winners. Graham is skeptical that this can be done with satisfactory results.

  • Graham’s Preference: The analyst should seek the exceptional or minority cases in which he can form a reasonably confident judgment that the price is well below value. This requires sufficient expertness to produce satisfactory average results over the years.

30.19.4 Recap

  • Value vs. Price: Investment quality is not inherent; it is determined by the price paid relative to the underlying business value.
  • Beware of Complexity: Complex capital structures (e.g., conglomerates, heavy warrant issuance) are often used to obscure poor underlying value and should be treated skeptically.
  • The Unpredictable: Overvalued stocks can remain overvalued longer than critics imagine (gambler’s fallacy), yet high valuations increase the risk of massive capital loss.
  • Analyst Focus: Analysts should prioritize identifying demonstrable undervaluations rather than predicting which quality company will become a future market darling.
  • Long-Term Law: Fundamentals always win. Manias, speculation, and hype (meme stocks, SPACs) inevitably detach from reality, leading to collapse.

30.20 Shareholders and Managements: Dividend Policy

Graham discusses the relationship between investors (as owners) and the management they employ, focusing on capital allocation decisions like dividends and share buybacks.

30.20.1 Investor-Management Relations

In principle, stockholders are the owners and “king”. In practice, however, shareholders are generally a “complete washout,” voting in sheeplike fashion for whatever management recommends.

  • Activist Role: Shareholders are justified in raising questions if results are unsatisfactory or poorer than competitors’. Individuals with enough conviction should scrutinize and possibly vote against demonstrably unproductive management.

30.20.2 Dividends and Stock Splits

Graham advocated for a clear and systematic dividend policy.

  • Stock Dividends: A proper stock dividend is tangible evidence of specific earnings reinvested in the business. Though academic critics argue they are “nothing but pieces of paper,” they are practically important: they allow the investor to cash out their share of reinvested profits by selling the new certificate without breaking up their original holding.
  • Pace of Change: Corporations should modernize their financial practices, including dividend policy, just as they modernize their facilities and products.

30.20.3 Management Accountability and Buybacks

The most important task of a CEO is to allocate capital to its most productive use.

  • Share Buybacks (Repurchases): Buybacks are only sound if the stock is genuinely undervalued. If management uses buybacks when the stock is overvalued, it is a catastrophic misuse of capital (e.g., Lehman Brothers, Citigroup, Bed Bath & Beyond).
  • Incentive Pay: Management compensation often seems excessive for unimpressive results. Directors should be held accountable by voting against their reelection.
  • Recommended Compensation Standard: Incentive pay should be based on sensible standards measured over multi-year periods, comparing performance against peers in the same industry (e.g., return on invested capital, net profit margin, and growth in sales and net income).

30.20.4 Recap

  • Shareholder Futility: Shareholders rarely exercise their rights, generally adhering passively to management’s wishes.
  • Dividends/Splits: Proper stock dividends (reflecting retained earnings) are psychologically and practically valuable to the investor as a way to partially cash out reinvested profits.
  • Capital Allocation: Share buybacks are only beneficial if the stock is substantially undervalued; otherwise, they destroy wealth.
  • Accountability: Investors should vote against directors who approve excessive or unjustified management compensation packages.
  • Performance Metrics: Management rewards should be tied to objective, long-term performance measures relative to industry peers (e.g., ROIC).

30.21 “Margin of Safety” as the Central Concept of Investment

The secret of sound investment can be boiled down to three words: MARGIN OF SAFETY. This concept runs through all investment policy and is the basis for achieving success while minimizing risk.

30.21.1 The Definition of Margin of Safety (MoS)

The margin of safety is the factor that ensures the viability of the enterprise and its securities, rendering unnecessary an accurate estimate of the future.

  • The Price: The margin of safety is always dependent on the price paid. \[\text{MoS} = \text{Value of the Business} - \text{Price of the Stock}\]
  • Demonstrability: A true margin of safety must be demonstrated by figures, by persuasive reasoning, and by reference to a body of actual experience. It is based on arithmetic, not optimism.
  • Protection: The MoS ensures that even if analysis is flawed or the business faces setbacks, the loss will be prevented or minimized.

30.21.2 MoS for Securities

  • Bonds/Preferred Stocks: MoS is measured by the number of times total interest/preferred charges are covered by available earnings. It is also measured by the excess of the total enterprise value (market value of stock) over the debt amount.
  • Common Stocks: MoS is provided by the excess of the stock’s earning power over the rate of interest on high-grade bonds. If the purchase is made at the market average over a span of years, the prices carry an assurance of an adequate MoS. A sufficiently low price can turn a security of mediocre quality into a sound investment opportunity.

30.21.3 Framework: The Four Business Principles

Investment is most intelligent when it is most businesslike. Graham outlines four business principles that encapsulate the MoS concept:

  1. Know your business: Do not seek profits in securities unless you know as much about security values as you would about merchandise you manufacture or deal in.
  2. Delegate with Care: Do not let anyone else run your business unless you can supervise their performance or have unusual reasons for implicit confidence.
  3. Base Decisions on Arithmetic: Do not enter an operation unless a reliable calculation shows a fair chance for a reasonable profit. Keep away from ventures in which you have little to gain and much to lose.
  4. Courage of Conviction: Have the courage of your knowledge and experience. If your judgment is sound, act on it, even if the crowd disagrees.

30.21.4 Framework: Internal Margin of Safety (Superforecasting)

The investor also needs an internal margin of safety to protect against self-deception and overconfidence.

  • Challenge Intuition: Convert forecasts from guesstimates to a formal, continuous process.
  • Steps for Superforecasting:
    1. Audit Sources: Seek a balanced cognitive diet, including skeptical and negative views that challenge your assumptions.
    2. Reference Class: Classify the company as a member of a category (e.g., all similar IPOs or all companies in the industry).
    3. Base Rate: Determine the average historical experience (base rate) for the reference class, using long-term data.
    4. Adjust: Adjust the base rate up or down based on the specific characteristics of the company (using the checklist).
    5. Be Probabilistic: Be specific and probabilistic in forecasts (e.g., “I’m 80% certain”) and record your reasoning.
    6. Seek Opposite Proof: Actively seek information that would prove your prediction wrong.
    7. Update and Revise: View a forecast as a continuous process, staying flexible and constantly adjusting views as new information arises (“People who are right a lot change their mind a lot”).

30.21.5 Recap

  • MoS Definition: Margin of Safety is the central concept, representing Value minus Price, and is essential for surviving flawed analysis.
  • MoS Source: MoS is created by arithmetic (e.g., buying stock where earning power exceeds bond interest yield) and is always dependent on the price paid.
  • Business Principles: Follow four core business rules: acquire knowledge, supervise delegates, rely on arithmetic over optimism, and have the courage of your knowledge.
  • Internal MoS: Build emotional discipline by practicing “superforecasting”—a formal, continuous process of prediction based on base rates, evidence, and constant revision—to counter overconfidence.
  • Ultimate Goal: Maintaining a margin of safety ensures the portfolio prospers from good decisions without being destroyed by bad ones.

30.22 Master Summary

The Intelligent Investor provides a timeless framework for financial success by establishing value investing principles and emphasizing emotional discipline. The core distinction lies between investment, which relies on thorough analysis promising safety of principal and adequate return, and speculation, which focuses on market price movement and timing.

The foundation of successful investment is the Margin of Safety (MoS). This is the difference between the true, conservatively calculated value of a business and the price paid for its stock. A sufficiently large MoS protects the investor from analytical errors and market vicissitudes.

The market is represented by Mr. Market, an irrational partner who fluctuates between euphoria and fear. The intelligent investor must possess a sound intellectual framework and temperament—including independence, skepticism, and humility—to ignore Mr. Market’s moods and profit by buying when prices are irrationally low and selling when they are irrationally high (buying cheap and selling dear).

Investors must choose their path: the Defensive Investor seeks safety and minimum effort, adhering to standardized, highly diversified portfolios (e.g., index funds and a strict 50-50 bond/stock allocation range) and using mechanical rules like rebalancing and dollar-cost averaging. The defensive investor should limit stock purchases to large, financially strong companies with continuous dividend records, bought at moderate price-to-earnings and price-to-asset ratios.

The Enterprising Investor commits significant time and effort to achieve superior results. This investor avoids speculative fads (hot IPOs, alternatives) and complex financial structures, focusing instead on deep value opportunities. The most promising strategy is acquiring Net-Current-Asset stocks—companies selling for less than their net liquid working capital—which offers a guaranteed margin of safety. Enterprising investors must use structured analysis (checklists, superforecasting) to test assumptions and counter their own inherent psychological biases.

Crucially, earnings and valuations must be treated with skepticism. Investors must base decisions on average earnings over a multi-year period (7–10 years) to smooth business cycles, and must diligently adjust for all forms of dilution and dubious non-GAAP accounting to ascertain true earning power. Ultimately, investment success hinges on emotional discipline and adhering to sound business principles: know your business, base decisions on arithmetic, and have the courage to act on verified knowledge.
