16  Emotional Agility

16.1 Executive Summary

Emotional Agility (EA) is a psychological framework defined as the ability to be flexible with your thoughts and feelings so that you can respond optimally to everyday situations. It is the key to well-being and success, allowing individuals to endure setbacks, tolerate high stress, and remain engaged while pursuing long-term goals aligned with their deepest values. Emotional agility directly counters emotional rigidity—the state of being “hooked” by thoughts, feelings, and behaviors that no longer serve us.

The book outlines the four essential movements necessary to achieve emotional agility, forming a continuous process for living intentionally:

  1. Showing Up: Facing into your thoughts, emotions, and behaviors willingly, with curiosity and kindness, rather than bottling them or brooding over them.
  2. Stepping Out: Detaching from and observing your feelings and thoughts to see them for what they truly are—mere information, not facts or directives—thereby creating the critical “space” between stimulus and response.
  3. Walking Your Why: Identifying your core values (your “why”) and using them as a compass to guide your daily actions and decisions, ensuring alignment with your most important aspirations.
  4. Moving On: Applying the Tiny Tweaks Principle and the Teeter-Totter Principle to generate lasting, values-based change. This involves balancing competence with challenge and continually making small, deliberate adjustments to mindset, motivation, and habits.

By embracing EA, individuals can cease fighting their inner world and instead turn their difficult emotions into sources of energy, creativity, and insight, leading to a truly authentic and thriving life.

16.2 Rigidity to Agility

The chapter introduces the foundational concept that human emotions (like fear, anxiety, or joy) are a neurochemical guidance system evolved to help us navigate complex life currents. These “embodied” physical responses aim to keep our inner state and outward behavior in sync with reality. However, emotions are not always reliable; they can dredge up painful past experiences, clouding judgment and leading to inappropriate responses.

Emotional Rigidity vs. Emotional Agility (EA) Rigidity occurs when people operate on emotional autopilot, reacting without true awareness, or when they expend too much energy suppressing or containing difficult emotions. This inflexibility often arises from “buying into the old, self-defeating story” or relying on mental shortcuts that no longer serve current needs (like the stubborn battleship captain who refused to change course for a lighthouse). Emotional rigidity is linked to psychological issues like anxiety and depression.

Emotional Agility is the key to well-being and success. It is about loosening up, calming down, and living with more intention by choosing how to respond to emotional signals. It is based on Viktor Frankl’s idea: “Between stimulus and response there is a space. In that space is our power to choose our response. In our response lies our growth and our freedom”. EA enables people to tolerate high levels of stress and setbacks while remaining engaged, open, and receptive, acting according to their most cherished values and pursuing long-term goals.

The Four Essential Movements of Emotional Agility: The process unfolds in four movements designed to move individuals away from habitual, negative patterns:

  1. Showing Up: Facing thoughts, emotions, and behaviors willingly, with curiosity and kindness. This includes accepting both valid feelings and distorted “old bits stuck in your psyche”.
  2. Stepping Out: Detaching from and observing thoughts and emotions to create a nonjudgmental space between feelings and reaction. This allows one to view oneself as the “chessboard, filled with possibilities, rather than as any one piece on the board” [31, 248n].
  3. Walking Your Why: After calming mental processes, focusing on core values and important goals. Values provide the compass for navigating choice points—the thousands of small decisions made every day.
  4. Moving On (Tiny Tweaks & Teeter-Totter): Implementing small, values-infused adjustments (Tiny Tweaks) for massive, lasting change, especially in routine parts of life. This also involves finding the perfect balance between challenge and competence (Teeter-Totter Principle) to maintain excitement and growth without being overwhelmed.

16.3 Hooked

The concept of being hooked means being caught by a self-defeating emotion, thought, or behavior. The human mind is a meaning-making machine that organizes sensory input into persuasive self-accounts or “scripts” that we accept as truth, often without questioning their veracity.

The Internal Chatterbox and Unreliable Narrator The voice of consciousness is a tireless chatterbox constantly barraging us with observations, comments, and analyses. This “unreliable narrator” is biased and often mixes factual observations with opinions, evaluations, and judgments, turning a simple thought (“I’m a slow writer”) into a debilitating judgment (“I’m done for”).

The Mechanism of the Hook Getting hooked is often reflexive. The hook is triggered when the Internal Chatterbox combines with Technicolor Thought Blending (thoughts accessorized with vivid images, symbols, and idiosyncratic interpretations), generating an Emotional Punch (a strong, visceral feeling). This high-intensity combination overrides objectivity and drives an autopilot response.

Thinking Fast and Slow Psychologist Daniel Kahneman describes two basic modes of thought:

  • System 1 Thoughts: Fast, automatic, effortless, associative, implicit, and carry heavy emotional weight, making them prone to getting us hooked. System 1 uses heuristics (rules of thumb) for quick judgments.
  • System 2 Thoughts: Slower, deliberative, effortful, flexible, and amenable to conscious rules. System 2 allows us to create the vital space between stimulus and response.

The Four Most Common Hooks:

  1. Thought-Blaming: Blaming thoughts for actions or inactions (e.g., “I thought I’d embarrass myself, so I didn’t mingle”).
  2. Monkey-Mindedness: Incessant, tireless internal chatter leaping from topic to topic, often resulting in “awfulizing” (imagining worst-case scenarios). It drains energy and takes you out of the moment.
  3. Old, Outgrown Ideas: Behaving based on strategies or narratives that were once functional (e.g., protecting oneself from childhood trauma) but now constrict behavior in adult life.
  4. Wrongheaded Righteousness: Hanging on too long to the need for vindication or proving you are right, often prolonging injustice and depriving oneself of valued things (the “cutting off your nose to spite your face” phenomenon).

16.4 Trying to Unhook

Humans have seven basic emotions, five of which (anger, sadness, fear, contempt, disgust) reflect the difficult side of life, confirming that challenging feelings are normal and useful. Trying to avoid or kill off these feelings prevents growth.

Flawed Strategies for Unhooking:

  • Bottlers: Push emotions aside, often rationalizing or immersing themselves in busy work to avoid discomfort. This strategy is associated with men more often than women [46, 250n]. Bottling is counterproductive; trying to suppress thoughts amplifies them (the “white bears” study). Suppressed emotions inevitably resurface in unintended ways (emotional leakage).
  • Brooders: Stew in misery, endlessly circling troubling feelings or obsessing over shortcomings. This strategy is associated with women more often than men [50, 251n]. Brooding, unlike bottling, involves feeling the feelings, but it gains strength like a hurricane, increasing aggression. Brooders are prone to co-brooding (venting with friends) and misery-about-misery (Type 2 thoughts). Brooding takes up massive intellectual energy and makes self-blame more likely.

The Teeter-Totter of Emotions Bottling (holding a stack of books at arm’s length) and brooding (crushing the books tight to the body) both lead to exhaustion and rigidity, preventing full engagement with the world.

Hooked on Happiness Aggressively pursuing happiness is fundamentally incompatible with the nature of happiness itself.

  • The Pursuit is Self-Defeating: Striving for happiness establishes impossible expectations that lead to disappointment (e.g., in studies, people told to value happiness felt less happy after a pleasant film).
  • The Wrong Kind of Happiness: Overly cheerful people may neglect important threats. High happiness levels can lead to cognitive rigidity, quick conclusions, reliance on stereotypes, and being less creative.
  • Isolation: The higher participants rank happiness on their goals, the more lonely they describe themselves as being.

The Upside of Challenging Emotions So-called “negative” emotions are vital messengers that promote insight and valuable life directions. They encourage:

  • Systematic Cognitive Processing: Leads to skepticism, attention to detail, and reliance on facts.
  • Improved Memory.
  • Perseverance: People in somber moods try to answer more questions on tests and get more right.
  • Generosity and Fairness.
  • Reduced Confirmation Bias: Anger can spur exploration of opposing views to tear them apart, ironically opening the door to persuasion.
  • Social Functions: Embarrassment and guilt foster cooperation and appeasement.

16.5 Showing Up

“Showing up” is the starting movement of EA—willingly confronting one’s inner world, including self-doubt and fear of failure, without having to slay or vanquish inner demons.

Key Concepts of Showing Up

  • Self-Acceptance as a Prerequisite for Change: We cannot change our circumstances until we accept what exists right now. Acceptance ends the “war” with ourselves, allowing constructive efforts to begin.
  • Self-Compassion: Treating ourselves with kindness and forgiveness, especially during rough patches. It is the antidote to shame (focusing on the self as bad) and promotes guilt (focusing on actions being wrong), which is more motivating for making amends. Self-compassion is linked to greater psychological health and motivation to improve.
  • Avoiding Social Comparison: Comparing oneself to others, whether celebrities or successful peers, damages self-image and leads to feelings of self-blame, guilt, and the need for external validation. The advice is to “Keep your eyes on your own work”.
  • Choosing Willingness: Making room in the heart for both joy and pain, and getting comfortable with being uncomfortable. This means seeing feelings as “just being” and choosing acceptance over struggling to control impulses. (Example: The car journey metaphor for cravings).

Framework: “What the Func?” A crucial practice in showing up is asking “What the func?” (short for function) to determine the purpose of an emotion.

  • Application: Guilt might signal that you value your family and need to realign priorities. Anger might signal a threat to a cherished value, like teamwork or job security.
  • Nuanced Emotional Vocabulary: Accurately labeling emotions (moving beyond vague terms like “stressed”) reduces painful, murky feelings to a finite experience, allowing for targeted communication and problem-solving. (Contrast: Alexithymia, meaning “no words for mood”).

16.6 Stepping Out

Stepping out is the movement of creating distance (or space) between the thinker and the thought, the feeler and the feeling. This shift in perspective is the meta-view—seeing oneself as the chessboard of possibilities rather than one restrictive piece.

Methods for Stepping Out

  • Expressive Writing (Pennebaker’s Method): Writing freely and frankly about emotionally significant experiences leads to improved physical health, mental well-being, and employment outcomes (e.g., in a study of laid-off engineers). The greatest benefits accrue when writers develop insight—using phrases like “I now realize” or “It struck me that”.
    • Pennebaker’s Writing Rules:
      1. Set a timer for twenty minutes.
      2. Write about emotional experiences (past week, month, or year).
      3. Write freely, without worrying about punctuation or coherence.
      4. Write just for yourself, not an eventual reader.
      5. Do this for a few days, then throw the paper away (the thoughts are “out of you and on the page”).
  • Mindfulness: Paying attention to the present moment, on purpose and without judgment. Mindfulness improves memory, creativity, mood, and relationships by increasing focus and reducing distraction. The opposite is mindlessness—autopilot behavior based on rigid rules.
    • Ways to Become More Mindful:
      1. Begin with the breath (focusing solely on inhales/exhales).
      2. Mindfully observe an object (seeing it as if for the first time).
      3. Rework a routine (focusing on each sight, sound, and texture of a daily task).
      4. Really listen (tuning into complex music without judging it).
  • Linguistic Stepping Out: Using language to distance oneself from fusion with a thought.
    • Decoupling Self and Emotion: Introducing the language: “I am having the thought that…” or “I am noticing that I’m feeling stress” instead of “I am stressed”. This immediately creates space.
    • Third-Person Self-Talk: Referring to yourself in the third person during stressful moments (like LeBron James did when making a difficult choice) allows you to transcend the egocentric viewpoint and regulate your reaction.
    • Repetition/Defusion: Repeating a highly charged negative phrase (like “I’m a loser”) until it loses its meaning and emotional power.
  • Letting Go: Holding thoughts and emotions lightly, choosing to abandon the battle (ending the tug-of-war by dropping the rope). This is an act of “vital engagement with the way things actually are,” not passive resignation.

16.7 Walking Your Why

“Walking your why” is the art of living intentionally by aligning your actions with your most cherished core values. If you do not actively sort out your values, you risk living someone else’s life, driven by cultural pressures or the effects of social contagion (mindlessly copying others’ choices).

Characteristics of Values:

  • They are qualities of purposeful action, not restrictive rules [120, 257n].
  • They are freely chosen, not imposed by others.
  • They are continuous and ongoing, not fixed, achievable goals.
  • They serve as a “psychological keel” to keep you steady.
  • They foster self-acceptance and freedom from comparison.

The Choice Point Every day is composed of thousands of choice points—small decision moments where you choose to move toward your values or away from them. Choosing moves toward your values, despite discomfort, makes life more vital and meaningful.

Identifying Your Values This involves asking deep questions to uncover inherent guiding principles:

  • What relationships do I want to build?
  • What kinds of situations make me feel most vital?
  • What did I do today that was actually worth my time?.
  • What do I want people to observe when they see me in a particular role (e.g., as a parent or colleague)?.

Handling Goal Conflicts What often appears to be a conflict between two values (e.g., career vs. family) is usually a goal conflict. Since values relate to the quality of action, one can commit to both (e.g., being a loving parent and a productive worker). When making a hard choice, accepting the loss inherent in the path not taken allows one to embrace the chosen decision with clarity.

Courage is Fear Walking. Acting in line with values requires courage—not the absence of fear, but the ability to walk directly into fear with values as a guide. This might mean pursuing a path that creates temporary stress or discomfort because it is ultimately more aligned with one’s “why”.

16.8 Moving On: The Tiny Tweaks Principle

Lasting, significant change is achieved not through massive revolutions, but through small, deliberate tweaks to everyday behaviors, which compound over time. The cost of failure for a small tweak is low, which reduces stress and boosts confidence, promoting creativity and persistence.

Tweaking Mindsets Mindsets are beliefs about whether core qualities (like intelligence or personality) are fixed or malleable.

  • Fixed Mindset: Believes qualities are fixed and cannot be changed. Leads to giving up when things get hard.
  • Growth Mindset: Believes qualities can be improved through learning and effort (malleable). Leads to persistence and resilience in rebounding from failure, promoting openness to new experiences.
  • Application: Tweaking mindsets (e.g., realizing that work activities count as exercise, or that aging does not necessitate total decline) has demonstrable physical and psychological benefits. Viewing change as a process, not an event, promotes a growth mindset.

Tweaking Motivations Motivation must be internal (from a “willing heart”) rather than external (from a “wagging finger”).

  • Have-to Motivations: Imposed by obligation, guilt, or shame (“I have to lose weight”). These motivations heighten temptation and are unstable.
  • Want-to Motivations: Freely chosen, reflecting intrinsic interest and core values. These motivations reduce the automatic attraction toward temptation, making goal attainment easier.
  • The Tweak: Repositioning an obligation (e.g., “I have to work today”) as a chosen action linked to values (“I want to spread the important message of emotional agility”).

Tweaking Habits The goal is to transform consciously chosen, intentional behaviors into durable, deeply ingrained habits.

Framework: Choice Architecture Tweaks:

  1. The No-Brainer: Switching up the environment so that the values-aligned choice is also the easiest one when you are stressed or rushed (e.g., using smaller plates, stocking healthy food).
  2. The Piggyback: Adding a new behavior onto an existing, entrenched habit (e.g., stashing your phone where you stash your keys upon entering the house).
  3. The Precommitment (“If-Then”): Anticipating potential obstacles and pre-planning a values-based response (e.g., “If I am tempted to sleep in, then I will immediately haul myself out of bed”).
  4. The Obstacle Course (Mental Contrasting): Combining optimism about achieving a goal with a realistic assessment of potential challenges. Fantasizing about success hinders follow-through, but mental contrasting links the desired future with the present reality, creating a pathway for change.

16.9 Moving On: The Teeter-Totter Principle

The Teeter-Totter Principle dictates that optimal development occurs in the sweet spot of creative tension between challenge and competence.

The Zone of Optimal Development (“Whelmed”)

  • Overcompetence: Leads to complacency, routine, rigidity, lack of growth, and boredom (one side of the teeter-totter stuck in the mud).
  • Overchallenge: Leads to stress and overwhelm, inhibiting creativity and responsiveness.
  • Whelmed: The optimal state where an individual is challenged but not overwhelmed, living at the edge of their ability.

The Curse of Comfort Our brains confuse familiarity and accessibility with safety, promoting avoidance of new or difficult experiences. This bias leads to sticking with the familiar route, even when it is inefficient or harmful. A deeper aspect is the brain’s priority for coherence—seeking a consistent self-image, even if that image is negative (e.g., self-sabotage by people with low self-esteem when they receive appreciation).

Choosing Challenge and Effortful Learning To break complacency, one must choose challenge over comfort. This means incrementally expanding:

  • Breadth: Acquiring new skills or exploring new avenues.
  • Depth: Improving the quality of engagement (e.g., the quality of listening). This growth requires effortful learning—mindful practice that continuously tackles challenges just beyond one’s current grasp. Effortful learning promotes neuroplasticity (the brain’s ability to grow new neurons and connect them).

Grit vs. Quit Grit is defined as passion and sustained persistence toward a long-term goal. Emotional agility helps develop constructive grit by ensuring persistence is aligned with core values. However, EA also provides the framework for the genuinely courageous decision to quit a path that no longer serves one’s values or goals.

Framework: Grit-or-Quit Assessment: When faced with a decision to persevere or change course, ask:

  • Overall, do I find joy or satisfaction in what I’m doing?
  • Does this reflect what is important to me—my values?
  • Does this draw on my strengths?
  • If I’m completely honest with myself, do I believe that I (or this situation) can really be a success?
  • What opportunities will I give up if I persevere with this (Opportunity Cost)?.
  • Am I being gritty, or am I being stupid?.

16.10 Emotional Agility at Work

The workplace is increasingly volatile due to constant change and demands for speed, which ironically pushes people toward rigidity, distraction, and simplistic solutions. EA is vital for resilience and effective leadership.

Workplace Hooks and Biases

  • Individual Hooks: Employees often revert to old, unhelpful personal narratives when stressed (e.g., insecurity leading to self-sabotage or overcommitment). Fixation on “the task” over the larger objective or human connection is another common hook.
  • Caring Too Much: When careers become bound to one’s sense of self, losing perspective and proportion is easy. Stepping out and “caring less” means focusing energy on things truly valued, not necessarily slacking off.
  • Group Hooks: Correspondence Bias is the tendency to attribute others’ behavior to fixed personality traits (“Jack is a phony”) while attributing our own similar behavior to circumstance (“I was under pressure!”). Contagion(secondhand stress and mood spreading) is rampant in groups, increasing the allostatic load (chronic, low-grade anxiety).

Applying EA to Workplace Stress

  • Reframing Stress: Denying or bottling stress is counterproductive. The key is adjusting one’s relationship to stress.
  • Linguistic Shift: Instead of saying “I’m stressed,” shift to “I am noticing that I’m feeling stress” to create space between identity and emotion.
  • “What the Func?”: Evaluate what the stress is signaling (e.g., exhaustion, misalignment with values, or simply the price of admission for a valued challenge).

Job Crafting When the dream job is unattainable, job crafting—creatively reconfiguring one’s current work circumstances—can make the job more fulfilling and engaging.

  • Change Tasks: Volunteer for projects or activities that align with your interests (e.g., a salesperson offering ideas to the marketing team).
  • Change Relationships: Seek out different people to interact with, possibly mentoring or creating internal programs.
  • Change Perception: Reframe the work’s meaning by connecting it to a higher purpose (e.g., an assembly line worker seeing the importance of her tiny, repetitive task in saving patients’ lives).

16.11 Raising Emotionally Agile Children

Modern parents are conscientious but often fall into overparenting (micromanagement and shielding children from adversity), which leads to high anxiety, low resilience, and contingent self-esteem (the belief that worth must be earned). EA provides a skill set—a “vaccine”—to inoculate children against being overwhelmed by life’s inevitable difficulties.

Modeling Emotional Agility The most effective way to teach EA is by practicing it yourself, especially during moments of stress or conflict with your child.

Parenting Steps for EA

  1. Show Up: Validate Feelings (“I See You”): Parents must acknowledge and accept their children’s full range of emotions without rebuke, even difficult ones like anger or frustration. Dismissing feelings (“It’s okay! It’s okay!”) sends a message to bottle it. Validation helps children feel loved (secure attachment) and reduces emotional intensity.
  2. Step Out: Restrain Behavior, Not Feelings: Help children label their emotions and put distance between the impulse and the action (e.g., “I see you’re annoyed, but you still need to restrain your behavior”).
  3. Walk Your Why: Encourage Autonomy:
    • Autonomy vs. Independence: Autonomous actions are fully owned and endorsed by the self, unlike mere independence (which may be driven by rebellion or peer pressure).
    • How to Encourage Autonomy: Honor children for who they are, give true choices when possible, provide rationale for decisions, and minimize extrinsic rewards.
    • Empathy and Morality: Autonomy is key to moral development; children given free choice (not coerced) are more generous and develop a stronger understanding of values like truth-telling.
  4. Moving On: Focus on Process and Tiny Tweaks: Focus on process (openness, experimentation, learning) rather than outcomes (pass-or-fail). Encourage children to brainstorm their own workable solutions. Courage is fear walking—helping children move forward in spite of their fear because the action aligns with what they want.

The Goal: The parent’s role is not to clip their children’s wings but to provide the emotional foundation so they can thrive—“that’s all”.

16.12 Conclusion: Becoming Real

The story of The Velveteen Rabbit illustrates the goal of emotional agility: to become “Real”. Becoming real means achieving authenticity—the absence of pretense and performance—so that actions emanate from core values and strength. This is achieved not through magic, but through a series of tiny steps over a lifetime.

Steps to Becoming Real (EA Recap):

  • Appoint yourself the agent of your own life and take ownership of your development.
  • Accept your full self—“good” and “bad” emotions—with compassion, courage, and curiosity.
  • Welcome your inner experiences and learn their contours without racing for the exit.
  • Embrace an evolving identity and release narratives that no longer serve you.
  • Let go of dead people’s goals, accepting that life involves getting hurt, failing, and making mistakes.
  • Abandon the idea of being fearless; instead, walk directly into your fears with your values as your guide.
  • Choose courage over comfort by engaging vitally with new opportunities to learn and grow.
  • Learn how to hear the heartbeat of your own “why.”
  • Finally, remember to “dance if you can”.

16.13 Final Comprehensive Summary

Emotional Agility (EA) is the mastery of psychological flexibility, enabling one to navigate life’s inevitable complexity, stress, and setbacks by choosing responses that align with deeply held values. This process is essential because the natural human tendency toward emotional rigidity (suppressing feelings or relying on fixed, habitual responses) is linked to unhappiness and prevents flourishing. EA provides the conscious choice, famously articulated by Frankl, to expand the “space between stimulus and response”.

The framework of EA is built upon four interconnected movements:

  1. Showing Up: This foundational step requires facing one’s inner experiences—including uncomfortable emotions, old traumas, and self-doubt—with curiosity and kindness. It is crucial to develop self-compassion, recognizing flaws and struggles as universally human, rather than falling into shame (the belief that one is bad). Showing up involves linguistically identifying the purpose of difficult feelings by asking “What the func?” and cultivating a nuanced emotional vocabulary, allowing one to choose willingness over compulsive control.
  2. Stepping Out: This movement creates critical distance from thoughts and emotions, allowing them to be viewed as information rather than directives. Techniques like expressive writing (Pennebaker’s method) and mindfulness help achieve this meta-view or “view from above”. A key application is linguistic stepping out, such as using the phrase, “I am having the thought that…” or employing third-person self-talk to depersonalize negative feelings. Stepping out culminates in the courageous act of letting go of fixed interpretations and resentments.
  3. Walking Your Why: This ensures all actions are intentional, serving one’s core values. Values are defined as ongoing qualities of action (e.g., honesty, creativity) and must be freely chosen, acting as a compass at every choice point. By connecting with a continuity of self (visualizing the future self), individuals resist external influences like social contagion and align current decisions with long-term aspirations. This movement also resolves apparent goal conflicts by focusing on the quality of action that aligns with a value, rather than the impossible constraint of limited time.
  4. Moving On (The Growth Imperative): This final movement governs the mechanism of change, which is achieved through persistence and optimal challenge.
    • The Tiny Tweaks Principle: Lasting change comes from small, values-infused alterations to habits, mindset, and motivation, which provide immense leverage over time. This requires shifting from a fixed mindset to a growth mindset, and repositioning motivations from obligatory “have-to” goals to internal “want-to” goals. Habits can be effectively established using choice architecture tweaks, such as the “no-brainer” or “if-then” precommitments.
    • The Teeter-Totter Principle: One must find the balance (“whelmed”) between stifling overcompetence and overwhelming overchallenge. This requires choosing courage over comfort, overcoming the psychological curse of comfort (the brain’s bias toward the familiar and coherent). While cultivating grit (passion and persistence) is vital, emotional agility dictates that one must also know when to quit unrealistic goals by assessing the opportunity cost over the sunk cost.

In application, EA transforms the workplace by mitigating the effects of contagion and emotional labor (surface acting). Leaders are encouraged to acknowledge their stress and promote flexibility through job crafting. For raising children, EA is taught by modeling compassion, validating emotions, and encouraging autonomy and intrinsic motivation, thus ensuring children develop the emotional resilience needed to thrive in an unpredictable world. Ultimately, emotional agility enables individuals to integrate their whole selves—“warts and all”—and live authentically and intentionally, choosing to “dance if you can”.
