20  Strategic Connections

20.1 Executive Summary

Strategic Connections serves as a guide for mastering advanced face-to-face networking skills necessary to thrive in the emerging Network-Oriented Workplace (NOW). The book establishes networking not merely as a “soft skill” or a tool for job seeking, but as an essential professional competency required to drive success for individuals, their careers, and their organizations by fostering collaboration, innovation, and competitiveness.

The core methodology revolves around The 8 Competencies for the Network-Oriented Workplace. These skills enable professionals to build trusting relationships, which the authors assert is the necessary foundation for collaboration. A central theme is the necessity of adopting a Strategic Networker Identity (Competency 1), moving away from common misconceptions, and actively managing one’s mindset.

Key frameworks and methodologies include:

  1. The Six Stages of Trust: Relationships move through stages (Accident, Acquaintance, Associate, Active, Advocate, Ally) based on demonstrated Character and Competence.
  2. The Four Nets: A framework (WorkNet, OrgNet, ProNet, LifeNet) used to analyze, balance, and leverage professional and personal contacts.
  3. The Networking Agenda: A preparation tool built on items you have To Give and To Get to ensure purposeful conversations, leveraging the Reciprocity Principle.
  4. Strategic Positioning Projects (SPPs): Large-scale, long-term networking efforts designed to achieve significant career or organizational goals.
  5. Best/Test Formula: A two-sentence framework used to answer “What do you do?” by stating your best talent and providing a brief testimonial (Test) to communicate expertise effectively.

By mastering these competencies, employees can shift their focus from running their organization to enabling their organization to run itself, leveraging collaboration to achieve enterprise-wide success in strategy execution, innovation, engagement, and competitiveness.

20.2 The Compelling Case for Face to Face

The emergence of the Network-Oriented Workplace (NOW) signals a new role for employees—from CEO to entry-level—requiring new opportunities for contributing to organizational success. The book emphasizes the necessity of mastering advanced face-to-face skills, arguing that trust is the fundamental ingredient for collaboration, and without it, success in today’s environment is unlikely. Since 1990, the authors have trained people in these state-of-the-art tools and techniques.

Collaboration is depicted as the ultimate goal in The Big Picture framework, which requires a progression: contacts must learn to trust you, leading to productive relationships, energized networks, and ultimately, connection, conversation, and collaboration. Leaders often mistakenly assume people instinctively know these steps, but specific skills are necessary to speed this progression.

The 8 Competencies for the Network-Oriented Workplace are the “interpersonal skills of collaboration” ranked highly by CEOs:

  1. Commit to a New Networker Identity.
  2. Take a Strategic Approach.
  3. Envision Your Ideal Network.
  4. Develop Trusting Relationships.
  5. Increase Your Social Acumen.
  6. Deepen Interactions.
  7. Communicate Expertise.
  8. Create New Value.

Networking efforts in the NOW must be directed toward impacting big organizational issues, providing substantial benefits: speeding strategy execution, prompting innovation, raising engagement, and streamlining competitiveness.

20.3 Commit to a New Networker Identity

This competency focuses on expanding and strengthening one’s networker identity to fully participate in the emerging NOW. It involves analyzing attitudes, shedding misconceptions, redefining networking, and managing one’s mindset.

20.3.1 The Nine Biggest Misconceptions About Networking

Most people (80%) hold beliefs that prevent them from networking effectively. Addressing these misconceptions is crucial for adopting a new identity:

  1. “My work should stand for itself.” Today, individual contributors must become collaborators, requiring networking, even in “quiet careers” like science or finance.
  2. “I’m not in sales.” In the new workplace, “business development is everybody’s business,” meaning everyone owns the organization’s success.
  3. “I rarely get anything out of networking events.” This usually stems from attending events without setting clear goals.
  4. “If I ask for help, won’t I seem incompetent? I don’t like the feeling of owing people.” Asking for help improves decisions; giving help increases job satisfaction. Strategic networkers give generously and do not “keep score” (moving beyond the tit-for-tat notion).
  5. “If I’m networking, people will think I’m job-hunting.” Networking is now encouraged for organizational benefit, not just career advancement.
  6. “Networking is manipulative.” Subterfuge and pushiness repel; the best networkers eliminate manipulation by teaching contacts to trust them.
  7. “Networking is just schmoozing.” Strategic networkers move beyond superficial chitchat into conversations that solve problems and access resources.
  8. “I’m an introvert. I’ll never be a networker.” Introverts, representing over half the population, excel at planning and listening, skills crucial for relationship building.
  9. “I don’t need face-to-face skills.” Face-to-face interaction builds trust faster than electronic communication; a blended approach is best.

20.3.2 The Definition of Networking

The strategic definition of networking is: “the deliberate and discretionary process of creating, cultivating, and capitalizing on trust-based, mutually beneficial relationships for individual and organizational success”.

This definition affirms that networking:

  • Is a process requiring time and intention.
  • Involves initiation, maintenance, and utilization of relationships.
  • Is founded on trust and offers mutual benefits.
  • Impacts both personal and organizational results.

20.3.3 Becoming a Strategic Networker

A strategic networker: chooses a positive mindset, learns a wide range of skills, and integrates the networker identity into their core self-concept. They immediately think, “Who do I know (or who could I know) who could/should be involved or who might help with this?” when facing a challenge.

20.3.4 Activate Your Networker Identity

Three principles should drive responses and actions when activating this identity:

  1. Refrain Networking: Shift the approach from “talking and taking” to “teaching and giving”. Teaching involves providing useful information about yourself and your organization, which feels professional and liberated for those who feel shy. Giving should be generous (“give first, give freely”) to aid the trust-building process and foster a collaborative culture over competition.
  2. Risk Reaching Out: Overcome fears about connecting (often rooted in adolescent angst) by taking a “good dose of reality.” Use preparation and practice (mastering the 8 Competencies) to reduce perceived risk. As trust builds, willingness to take risks and innovate increases.
  3. Reinforce the Collaborative Culture: Become a champion for the NOW by advocating for best networking practices, acting as a role model, and mentoring others.

20.3.5 Manage Your Mindset

Managing your mindset involves transforming negative internal narratives and adopting behaviors that promote confidence.

Tools for Mindset Management:

  1. Watch Your Language: Reject language that gives networking a negative, “chilly aura” (e.g., “cold shoulder,” “break the ice”).
    • Top 10 Language Turnoffs to Avoid [56–59]: “Schmoozing,” “30-second commercial,” “Pick your brain,” “Work a room,” “Elevator speech,” “Tricks of the trade,” “Favor bank,” “Power lunch,” “Business card exchange,” and labeling contacts as “Important people” (which encourages looking over shoulders for better contacts) [56–59].
  2. Catch Your Critic: The inner “Critic” sabotages connection efforts (e.g., “You never can remember people’s names”).
    • Action: Change your Critic into a Coach by reprogramming the inner voice with positive, supportive messages (“I’m well-prepared and eager to talk to people today”).
  3. Believe the Best About Yourself and Others: If people meeting for the first time believe they have a lot in common, they act like old friends, paying attention to conversational cues. Assume positive intentions and commonalities.
  4. Energize Yourself: Use techniques like imagining “entrance music” (a favorite song playing in your head) to feel upbeat and confident.
    • Power Pose Technique: Adopt an expansive and open “power pose” for two minutes before events (e.g., hands behind head, feet on desk) to change hormone levels, increase confidence, and ability to take risks.
  5. Find Role Models: Seek out comfortable and confident networkers to mirror, interview them, and learn about their development path. When modeling behavior, remember the All-or-Nothing Rule: If you do one thing well, people assume you do everything well; if you do one thing poorly, they assume you do everything poorly.

20.4 Take a Strategic Approach

Strategic networking overcomes the biggest mistake people make: being haphazard, scattershot, or passive. This competency ensures networking efforts are focused on specific outcomes related to job execution, enterprise-wide initiatives, and career advancement.

20.4.1 Networking in the Collaborative Culture

Strategic networkers are proactive—not reactive. Reasons for networking extend beyond personal gain to include team, department, and organizational benefits.

Applications: Job + N (Networking)

  • Getting the Job Done: Use networking to find resources, ideas, or contacts to solve project challenges and avoid reinventing the wheel.
  • Contributing to Enterprise-Wide Initiatives: Know the big issues and figure out how to contribute to them. Use six questions to explore key organizational goals:
    1. What’s up with my boss?
    2. What are the big goals? (General or specific)
    3. What’s in the company news? (Internal publications, website updates)
    4. What’s the scuttlebutt? (Water-cooler conversations exploring trends)
    5. What’s the industry intrigue? (Reading publications, monitoring pundits)
    6. What’s for lunch? (Informal lunches with other departments to share calendars/solve problems).
  • Creating Your Career: Career advancement requires special attention, including a grand plan, securing mentors/sponsors, and educating contacts about your value.

20.4.2 Put Some Purpose in Your Small Talk: The Networking Agenda

The Networking Agenda is a mental or written list of what you have To Give and what you want To Get. It transforms purposeless chitchat into strategic conversations, simplifying topic selection and increasing energy.

Methodology: Creating the Agenda

  1. Begin with the To Give Side: Focus on giving first, as this is the smart thing to do and leverages the Reciprocity Principle: If you give someone something, they will insist on giving you back even more.
  2. Define What You Have to Give: List accomplishments, skills, enthusiasms, interests, and resources. These become conversational topics that teach people what to count on you for.
  3. Define What You Want to Get: List what you want to find, learn, connect with, or understand (e.g., solutions, opportunities, resources).
  4. Use the Agenda: Use the Agenda daily (e.g., at breakfast) and especially before networking events to feel comfortable and capable during encounters.

20.4.3 Connect at ChoicePoints

ChoicePoints are spontaneous moments during the day when you choose whether or not to reach out (e.g., in the cafeteria, elevator, standing in line). Being ready with an Agenda makes these encounters valuable.

20.4.4 Connect in Groups

Joining groups strategically helps target the kinds of people you want to meet. Not all groups are equally useful; they range from highly structured referral groups to less network-focused civic/religious organizations.

Groups Arranged by Network Focus (Highest to Lowest) [44–47]:

  1. Customer Common Groups (members share clients).
  2. Special Purpose Networks (formed for one specific goal).
  3. Business Referral Groups (tightly focused on leads/referrals).
  4. Networking Organizations (main goal is relationship-building).
  5. Professional and Trade Associations (focus on development, career resources).
  6. Industry-Specific Organizations (connect peers across the industry).
  7. Chambers of Commerce (civic improvement, economic development).
  8. Civic and Service Organizations (community service, relaxed atmosphere).
  9. Volunteer Groups (blend passion with relationship building).
  10. Alumni Groups (shared camaraderie).
  11. Religious Organizations (relationships grow out of shared community).

Group Assessment and Participation:

  • Check It Out Before You Join: Assess the group’s mission, membership size, networking culture, leadership, and time commitment before joining [49, 124–127].
  • Avoid the 10 Biggest Mistakes Members Make: These include joining but not going, skipping the networking portion, waiting for others to initiate, or arriving without an Agenda.
  • Orchestrate Who Knows You: Use participation to demonstrate skills, showcase products, establish reputation, and attract new contacts. Leverage the All-or-Nothing Rule by performing brilliantly in group activities (e.g., treasurer).

20.4.5 Choose Your Goal: Strategic Positioning Projects (SPPs)

For far-reaching, long-term goals, create a Strategic Positioning Project (SPP). These efforts position you to be the “natural and only choice” when opportunity arises.

Methodology: SPP Planning (Pass at least four of these five tests):

  1. The Doorway Test: Position yourself in a “doorway” where your ideal contacts (customers, clients, employers) stream by, ensuring visibility.
  2. The All-or-Nothing Test: The SPP must be a vehicle for demonstrating your value and abilities; perform brilliantly so people assume you are good at everything.
  3. The Bottom-Line Test: Ensure the SPP is justifiable in terms of time and money investment, and confirm it moves you closer to your goal.
  4. The Five-Year Test: The project must set the stage for the specific, special phone call (promotion, recognition, opportunity) you want to receive in three to five years.
  5. The Pig-in-Mud Test: The project must represent a magnificent blend of your unique personal and professional interests and passions, making you genuinely happy and engaged.

20.5 Envision Your Ideal Network

This competency is about increasing the scope and balance of your contacts, moving beyond the question, “I know a lot of people. I just don’t know what to do with them”.

20.5.1 The Four Nets Framework

The Four Nets—WorkNet, OrgNet, ProNet, and LifeNet—provide a strategic mental model for visualizing contacts, spotting deficiencies, and leveraging resources from one Net to another.

Net Description Contacts Found: Contacts Help You: Key Challenges:
WorkNet Daily coworkers, team members, clients, vendors, contractors (assigned, not chosen). Inside Organization Get the job done, unsnarl tie-ups, access talent, solve problems. Balancing tasks with relationships, cultivating relationships, showing appreciation.
OrgNet People in other divisions, departments, or business units (entirely chosen by you). Inside Organization Contribute on a large scale, bolster bottom line, act like a leader, find new career opportunities. Being strategic in reaching out, finding opportunities outside your WorkNet, initiating contact with hierarchy.
ProNet Professional contacts outside the organization (associations, former jobs, vendors, virtual workers). Outside Organization Develop professionally, benchmark skills, provide valuable BringBack(trends/best practices), advance career. Choosing the right groups, making time and money commitment, introducing new ideas internally.
LifeNet Family, friends, leisure-time contacts, volunteers, religious organizations. Outside Organization Find joy and community, access diverse information, gain support, meet new useful contacts. Reinventing the Net through life stages, staying in touch amid work demands, avoiding “quarantining” personal/professional life.

The ultimate value lies in the synergy of the Nets, leveraging contacts and opportunities across them to multiply impact.

20.5.2 Your KeyNets

A KeyNet is a small group (typically 12 to 18 people) strategically chosen from across the Four Nets to help achieve one specific goal or project.

Methodology: KeyNet Selection and Development

  1. Start with a Goal: Write down the specific project or goal.
  2. List People: Choose people from any Net who have a stake, influence, resources, or can act as champions.
  3. Look for “Connection Gaps”: Determine who is missing (e.g., different gender, geography, role) and who might unintentionally impede the project if not engaged.
  4. Prioritize the People: Rank people (Low, Moderate, High) based on importance to the goal to determine who to network with first.
  5. Assess and Act: Decide how to treat the high-priority relationships:
    • Enrich: Deepen or reinforce a good relationship by showing appreciation and confirming eagerness to help.
    • Repair: Fix a strained relationship by acknowledging the situation, apologizing for your part, and seeking to understand their point of view.
    • Start or Rev Up: Initiate contact or renew a minimal relationship, seeking commonalities, asking good questions, and offering resources.

KeyNet Collaborator Roles Consider these supporting roles when selecting KeyNet members [191–195]: The Brain (expert), The Promoter (market savvy), The Creative (design/presentation), The Global Citizen (cross-cultural expert), The Influencer (knows everyone), The Sponsor (authorizes resources), The Wizard (innovator), The Critic (candid feedback), The Cheerleader (upbeat energy), The Guru (special wisdom), The Sarge (accountability/deadlines), The Wise Elder (experienced strategist), The Novice (fresh perspective), and The Heart (social cohesion/appreciation).

20.6 Develop Trusting Relationships

Trust is all-important; establishing it allows collaboration in meaningful ways. Trust is the expectation of continued, stable exchange value in a relationship.

20.6.1 Teach People to Trust You

Trust happens as you teach others about your Character and Competence and learn about theirs. Trust development is not instantaneous; Contacts Count research suggests it takes six to eight conversations (encounters) to prove trustworthiness and establish confidence.

Components of Trust:

  • Character: Positive personal qualities (e.g., reliability, fairness, going the extra mile) that assure people you are trustworthy.
    • Teaching Character: Show through actions (doing what you say you will do, meeting deadlines) and telling stories (anecdotes about how you handle situations).
  • Competence: Breadth and depth of expertise (e.g., proper credentials, staying at the leading edge, winning awards) that assures people you can be relied upon.
    • Teaching Competence: Show through actions (doing the job right the first time, following through) and telling stories (sharing accomplishments, expertise).

20.6.2 Move Through the Six Stages of Trust

Relationships are complex and grow through six stages:

  1. Accidents: Casual, unplanned, random encounters. Most often, nothing happens afterward.
  2. Acquaintances: People you met through others whom you could find again. They are valuable “untapped assets” and bridges to new circles. Requires intentional follow-up.
  3. Associates: People belonging to the same groups, organizations, or workplaces. There is implied permission to seek them out.
  4. Actives: People with whom you exchange valuable information, resources, or introductions. This stage requires action, swapping ideas, and demonstrating Character and Competence.
  5. Advocates: People who believe in your Character and Competence, will speak well of you, and actively seek out opportunities and information for you. This requires overcoming natural caution to “go out on a limb” for you.
  6. Allies: The people who are the “board of directors of your life.” They advise, support, and care about your success in all aspects of life (personal and professional). The relationship is based on deep trust and respect, often requiring confidentiality.

Relevance of Stages: The Stages help avoid asking for too much too soon, or too little too late. The goal is not to become Allies with everyone, but to be intentional about relationship development. Trust speeds responsiveness (e.g., Advocates respond in 48 hours; Allies in hours).

20.6.3 Renew Dormant Ties

Dormant ties (contacts lost touch with for three or more years) are surprisingly valuable. Research shows that contacting long-lost contacts often yields more new information than current contacts, and the trust previously built remains intact.

20.6.4 Next Steps: Consider the Risk and Value

Before taking the next step to deepen a relationship, assess the combination of risk (what one stands to lose) and value (what one stands to gain) for both parties.

Factors to Consider [232–233]:

  • Low-Risk Next Step: Asks for something the contact is likely ready and able to give; is easy to do; is not time-sensitive.
  • High-Risk Next Step: Asks for something difficult; requires significant time/money/effort; could put the contact in an awkward position.
  • Low-Value Next Step: Doesn’t significantly move the relationship toward the next Stage; provides no tangible outcome; might waste time.
  • High-Value Next Step: Teaches about your Character/Competence; favorably impacts your contact’s life/work; positions your contact favorably among colleagues.

Applications: Next Steps by Stage (Examples) [234–246]:

  • Accidents/Acquaintances: Find a reason to introduce yourself, propose another meeting (e.g., coffee), exchange contact information, or invite them to a group you belong to.
  • Associates: Ask a colleague for an introduction, join them in a group activity or committee, or send a note of congratulations on an achievement.
  • Actives: Teach them what to come to you for using brief stories, ask open-ended questions (e.g., “What are you excited about?”), look for ways to send them news or resources, or introduce them enthusiastically to others.
  • Advocates: Keep current on their skills to send them the right opportunities, refer them to others (e.g., your boss), or introduce them to beneficial contacts.
  • Allies: Recap the relationship and appreciate their support, express deep trust, and challenge them to grow/change.

20.7 Increase Your Social Acumen

Increasing social acumen involves managing meet-and-greet rituals with comfort and confidence, making the most of every interaction, and ensuring a positive first impression.

20.7.1 The Name Exchange

Since 97 percent of people report being bad at remembering names, slowing down the typically 4–5 second Name Exchange ritual is crucial.

Methodology: Learn Someone’s Name [259–261]:

  1. Repeat the first name: “It’s nice to meet you, Jennifer”.
  2. Ask for the last name again or confirm it: Ask them to say it distinctly to prevent mashing names together.
  3. Ask a question or make a comment about the name: This gives you a chance to say the name again (e.g., “Do you like to be called Jenny or Jennifer?”).

Methodology: Teach Your Name [261–264]:

  1. Give ’em a double dip: Say your first name twice: “I’m Bob, Bob… Schmidt”.
  2. Separate and articulate: Pause between your first and last name to ensure distinct pronunciation.
  3. Make your name memorable: Spell it out or give a tip to help others visualize/remember it (e.g., “It’s Mann with two ‘Ns’”).

Dealing Skillfully with Forgotten Names: Never say, “I’ve forgotten your name”. Instead, use high-energy techniques to prompt the other person:

  • Walk up confidently, shake hands, and give your name, banking on the ritual that they will reciprocate.
  • Mention the context where you met (e.g., “We met at the conference…”).
  • Ask their name with enthusiasm: “Tell me your name again. I remember you!”.
  • Ask a colleague for a quick reminder before approaching.

20.7.2 Join Groups Comfortably

Joining a group should be viewed as completing it until you showed up, transforming the mindset from “breaking in” to inclusion.

Steps for Joining a Group:

  • Signal commitment (e.g., gentle touch on an arm, strong eye contact).
  • Take a few seconds to listen to the topic.
  • When the conversation slows, introduce yourself to the person next to you.
  • If you feel excluded, examine these feelings, as they are often rooted in leftover adolescent angst (“They don’t think I’m cool enough”).

Groups to Avoid: Groups working on something (visibly busy and harried) or groups of people who know each other extremely well (e.g., college friends reuniting).

20.7.3 11 Tips for Avoiding Awkward Moments

  1. Be polite and positive (avoid topics about personal troubles unless they bring them up).
  2. Put enthusiasm into conversations by noticing or appreciating something about the other person.
  3. When catching up, ask general questions (“How’s your year been?”) to avoid missteps (e.g., asking about an ex-spouse or previous job).
  4. Go for the relationship, not the contract; build trust through casual conversation, then set up a separate time for business transactions.
  5. When doing business with friends, make your Agenda clear upfront to maintain boundaries and trust.
  6. Decide what to share and what to keep private (especially online).
  7. Take yourself seriously (avoid saying, “I’m just an intern”).
  8. Avoid secret language (jargon and acronyms) that confuses and puts off listeners outside your specialty.
  9. Don’t ask for free advice (ask, “Do I need to make an appointment?”).
  10. When you don’t know what else to say, say “Hi!”—but use a friendly, high-energy tone.
  11. Exile the electronics; give full attention to face-to-face contacts.

20.7.4 End with the Future in Mind

Many people find ending conversations difficult because there is no clear protocol. The goal is to leave the contact with the feeling of “To be continued…”.

Seven Ways to Make a Conscious Closing [294–302]:

  1. Center on your Agenda: Shift attention to where you are going (e.g., “I need to see two more people about board business before I leave”).
  2. Ask your contact for an introduction: Use this to transition while seeking relevant contacts (e.g., “Do you know anyone here who is involved with management training?”).
  3. Invite your contact to do something with you: (e.g., “Want to get a drink?”).
  4. Introduce your contact to others: Act as a “great connector,” intentionally linking people who might share a commonality or need.
  5. Play Concentration: Listen for a topic or interest mentioned by your contact and connect them with someone else you know who shares that detail.
  6. Sum up and appreciate: Shake hands and acknowledge the conversation and its value to you using specifics (e.g., “I learned so much from you about trends in our industry…”).
  7. Explain the next steps: State what you will do next or what you’d like them to do, signaling interest and building trust (e.g., “I’ll send you that article we talked about”).

20.7.5 The Likeability Link

Research shows that in relationship building, likeability often trumps competence. People choose to work with those they find likeable, even over those they view as more competent. Likeable people are easier to trust, and a little extra likeability goes a longer way than a little extra competence in making someone desirable to work with.

Principles of Likeability:

The authors cite Lovas and Holloway, asserting we like people who are:

  • Familiar to us.
  • Similar to us in some way.
  • Genuinely interested in our concerns and values.
  • Easy to like in return (trustworthy, positive, nonjudgmental, and real).

Likeability is made up of behaviors that can be learned. Likeable behaviors send positive messages (e.g., saving an article for someone says, “I know you”) and help progress through the Stages of Trust.

20.8 Deepen Interactions

Conversation is the crucial step between connecting and collaborating. Deepening interactions involves mastering five interconnected conversation skills.

20.8.1 1. Talk: Get into Dialogues That Build and Sustain Relationships

Dialogue skills help reach out and energize interactions.

Application: Dialogue Skills

  • Organize some openers: Prepare several conversation starters before an event.
  • Add something new: Contribute unique content to conversations to move beyond the superficial to the specific.
  • Appreciate other people: Give genuine, specific appreciation to build connection and trust.
  • Play a little: Use humor to see the light side and create camaraderie.
  • Look for differences: Explore differences in background, skills, and interests to gain new perspectives and expand thinking.
  • Engage with energy: Use facial expressions and vocal range to convey enthusiasm and avoid sounding bored.
  • Just say no: Say “no” to requests that are not a good fit; if you can’t say “no,” your “yeses” lose meaning.
  • Take clichés seriously: When hearing clichés (e.g., “Really busy!”), “bore in” by asking for details (e.g., “What’s been keeping you busy?”).
  • Explore the icebergs: Ask questions to uncover the submerged meaning behind vague “iceberg statements” (e.g., “What’s going on that’s crazy?”).
  • Accept the offer: Follow conversational cues and pursue topics your partner introduces.

20.8.2 2. Question: Ask Questions That Make People Stop and Think

Questions show respect, curiosity, and reveal your background. Adopting the mantra “Be Seriously Curious” helps move relationships quickly to the Active Stage.

Tool: The ASK Formula The ASK formula helps craft purposeful and engaging questions:

  • A = Add emotion: Ask how someone felt about a change or situation.
  • S = Seek an opinion: Ask what they think is the best improvement or what features might need customization.
  • K = Kick it around: Invite exploration of an idea or challenge.

20.8.3 3. Listen: Pay Attention to the Three Things That Are Important

Listening is an active skill that requires undivided attention and focus.

Focus for Active Listening [346–348]:

  1. Listen for evidence of Character and Competence: Pay attention to anecdotes that reveal reliability, ethics, and expertise.
  2. Listen for information about skills and talents: Identify what resources or expertise they possess that might be useful.
  3. Listen for clues as to what you can give: Listen generously for needs or challenges you can respond to with a resource, idea, or introduction, which advances the relationship to the Active Stage.

20.8.4 4. Give: Offer Resources and Ideas That Signal You Want to Connect and Collaborate

Giving is the heart and soul of success and a primary way to teach people to have confidence in you. Giving reveals your willingness to collaborate, your network range, and your generosity.

The Give: While having an Agenda (Give list) is helpful, also be receptive to cues others give about their needs (Gets). Giving should reflect the established Stage of Trust; over-giving prematurely (e.g., recommending a new neighbor for a job) can put your own reputation at risk.

Research on Giving (Adam Grant): The most successful Givers (as opposed to Takers and Matchers) are “otherish”—willing to help others while setting boundaries and expressing their own needs, which makes them more likely to find win-win solutions.

20.8.5 5. Reconnect: Follow Through and Stay in Touch in Ways That Build Trust

Follow-Through is carrying the networking motion to its natural completion, beginning with a good first conversation that uncovers the other person’s Agenda.

Goals of Follow-Through: Aim to teach your contact five specific things:

  1. Your name and how to reach you easily.
  2. Exactly what you do.
  3. Why they can trust you.
  4. What kinds of opportunities and referrals you are seeking and what you can refer to them.
  5. What kind of information and opportunities you are looking for.

Eight ReachBack Strategies (to dormant and current relationships) [366–371]:

  1. Set up the “annual” lunch (to save face after a long hiatus).
  2. Ask for feedback (e.g., send a blog post to review).
  3. Piggyback on other events (e.g., invite them to lunch before a shared meeting).
  4. Build a little sweat equity (suggest golf or a bike ride).
  5. Praise their publicity (send articles or respond to posts).
  6. Lend a book or CD.
  7. Have a bunch to lunch (select a mix of clients/contacts for mutual benefit).
  8. Find people to thank (look back over the week and express specific gratitude).

20.9 Communicate Expertise

This competency teaches how to talk about yourself, your team, or your company without bragging, primarily through effective responses to the common question, “What do you do?” and through strategic storytelling.

20.9.1 Answering “What Do You Do?”

Standard answers fail to build relationships or teach about your capabilities:

  • Cement: Giving your occupation (“I’m an attorney”)—misses the chance to make yourself unique.
  • Fog: Giving a complicated title—jargon-filled and unclear.
  • The Blob: Giving your industry (“I’m in real estate”)—puts you in a gray mass with everyone else.
  • The Flag: Giving your organization’s name (“I’m with Disney”)—wraps you in the company’s fame but hides your personal identity/talents.

Tool: The Best/Test Formula

The Best/Test formula is a two-sentence structure designed to put a clear picture in the listener’s mind:

  1. Your Best (Sentence 1): State the one talent or skill you want people to remember, keeping it short (around 15 words).
  2. Your Test (Sentence 2): Offer a brief, concrete example or testimonial (around 25 words) that backs up your talent by showing how you saved the day, solved a problem, or served the client.

Key Tips for Crafting Your Best/Test [164–165]:

  • Tell your talent, not your title.
  • Avoid acronyms and jargon.
  • Resist the ego trip (don’t rely solely on a prestigious company name).
  • Avoid four errors: Winging it, long sentences, extra words, and generalities.
  • Aim for the comment, “Tell me more,” instead of “Oh… nice”.

20.9.2 Why Storytelling Works

Stories bring experiences to life, are more convincing than bare facts, and stick in the listener’s mind, making you memorable. They create an emotional connection and allow contacts to accurately repeat your achievements when advocating for you.

Tool: The 5-S Story Formula This formula structures narratives to reveal Character and Competence:

  1. Segue: A transition sentence that signals you have a story and bridges from the previous topic (e.g., “I’ve been meaning to tell you…”).
  2. Situation: Briefly sets the scene (time, place, and who is involved).
  3. SNAFU: The challenge, problem, or difficulty that had to be overcome (Situation Normal, All “Fouled” Up).
  4. Solution: The dramatic highlight or turnaround—how the problem was solved.
  5. Significance: The positive impact the solution had on you, others, or your organization.

Tips for Story SUCCESS [415–417]:

  • S = Strategic: Ensure the story fits your goals and makes a specific point about you.
  • U = Unique: Helps you stand out from the crowd.
  • C = Clear: Eliminate all professional jargon.
  • C = Concrete: Provide specific details (e.g., “brick split-level” instead of “house”).
  • E = Exciting: Use vivid language and an upbeat, speedy delivery.
  • S = Short and Succinct: Use the 5-S formula and edit out unnecessary words.
  • S = Service-oriented: Mention the outcome and who your actions impacted (improved, earned, saved).

20.10 Create New Value

Constant change in the marketplace demands that employees put their networking skills to work to create new value, going beyond merely improving their own job performance.

20.10.1 Clarifying Collaboration

Collaboration is not the same as teamwork. While teamwork involves assigned roles, scheduled activities, and clear rules (like a baseball team), collaboration is fluid, voluntary, and spontaneous. Collaboration addresses a problem or opportunity, often involves fluid leadership, and requires a high level of trust to enable risk-taking and vulnerability.

When leaders call for collaboration, they are asking for innovation, outside-the-box thinking, breaking down internal barriers (silo smashing), better use of resources, and discretionary involvement.

20.10.2 Acting on the Three Principles

These principles guide the creation of new value:

  1. Reframing Networking (Focusing Beliefs):
    • “I am not my job title”: Be known for untapped talents and skills that cross boundaries.
    • “There is no ‘they’”: Stop using language that puts you on the sidelines; take ownership and engage directly with contacts.
    • “Ideas belong to everyone and to no one”: Collaborate to generate ideas faster, recognizing abundance.
    • “I seek out people unlike myself”: Intentionally diversify contacts by age, gender, geography, function, culture, and interests to gain new value, better solutions, and faster information flow.
  2. Risk Reaching Out (Creating Action):
    • Be a great connector: Make introductions for others, as this is an overlooked form of giving.
    • Make the investment pay off: Calculate the Return on Investment (ROI) for networking expenditures (dues, conferences, events) and devise a system to document the benefits accrued.
    • Find the BringBack: Take responsibility for gathering valuable information, resources, and business intelligence from the outside world and distributing it internally. This requires being alert, identifying recipients, and giving the information in an encouraging way.
    • Expand your OrgNet: Actively work across boundaries (silos) and hierarchical levels.
  3. Reinforce the Collaborative Culture (“Be the change you want to see”):
    • Act as if you own the organization, taking initiative to solve problems and acting strategically.
    • Ask: How can I cut expenses? Conserve resources? Eliminate redundancies? Invent new processes? Share what I have?.

20.10.3 Build Trust Online

Even in a technology-driven world, turning online connections (Associates) into trusting relationships (Actives, Advocates, Allies) requires applying face-to-face trust-building strategies.

Guidelines for Online Trust-Building [466–472]:

  • Show up human: Use language that reveals emotion (e.g., “I’m curious,” “I’m excited”) to compensate for the lack of body language.
  • Earn the right: Ensure you have helped an online contact before asking for a high level of commitment or time.
  • Keep agreements: Deliver on promises in a timely and accurate manner.
  • Share your contacts: Do homework to confirm quality before making online endorsements.
  • Give appropriately: Limit premature generosity to avoid overwhelming contacts.
  • Tell stories: Use short, upbeat stories (e.g., the 5-S formula) to teach about Character and Competence.
  • Show you’re listening: Ask about their projects or personal plans to signal attention.
  • Suggest a face-to-face meeting: Use video calls (FaceTime, Skype) if physical proximity is not possible to warm up the relationship.

20.10.4 Mentor and Model

Leaders and employees alike must model the behavior they want to see to encourage courage and collaboration in others.

Strategies for Mentoring and Modeling [473–478]:

  • Give special attention to those you know best to foster innovation (e.g., an internal “YOLO Lunch” to discuss big ideas, not current projects).
  • Convene a “coaching club” to brainstorm and critique Best/Test responses.
  • Offer “ride-alongs” on sales or prospect calls to junior staffers.
  • Encourage what you like by appreciating positive behavior in others.
  • Value asking as much as giving (ask for help as well as offering it).
  • Team up at events (agree to split up and cover more sessions, then introduce each other to relevant contacts).
  • Never go off duty; model the expected behavior at all times.

20.11 How to Develop and Support Your Network-Oriented Workplace

This chapter addresses organizational leaders, focusing on transforming a company from a command-and-control structure to one built on collaboration. The foundation is recognizing that trust-building is a learned skill set that must be taught to employees.

20.11.1 What’s Your Organization’s Networker Identity?

The organization’s identity is defined by two continua (see Figure 9–1): the organizational attitude toward networking (Discouraging \(\rightarrow\) Encouraging \(\rightarrow\) Committed) and the employee attitude (Unwilling \(\rightarrow\) Willing \(\rightarrow\) Committed).

Organizational Identity (Network Quadrants) [505–506] Description
Network-Averse Unwilling, unskilled employees in an environment that discourages networking (command-and-control mindset).
Network-Aligning Leaders are committed, infrastructure is in place (technologies, policies), but employees are dragging their feet due to lack of skills or networker identity development.
Network-Aspiring Employees are willing but organizational policies are unfriendly; networking is often “under the radar” and collaboration is an untapped resource.
Network-Actualized Individuals and organization commit to networker identities, employees are trained, and systems support networking, leading to full collaboration, growth, and profitability.

Achieving Network-Actualized status requires leaders to create connections, spark conversations, and foster collaboration.

20.11.2 Create Connections

Leaders must be catalysts for creating connections, which involves strategically managing the creation and growth of social capital.

Action Steps for Leaders [507–516]:

  • Bring Stakeholders Together: Form an internal KeyNet (from training, sales, HR, talent management, etc.) to set an enterprise-wide strategy for developing social capital.
  • Sync the Systems: Align infrastructure with the collaborative culture by revising hiring policies, job descriptions, performance evaluations, and compensation to reference networking.
  • Make Space: Create a physical environment that fosters “casual collisions of the workforce” (like Google’s campus design) where spontaneous meetings occur and ideas are generated. Block entrances or reroute foot traffic to increase random encounters.
  • Take Time: Provide “slack” in employees’ schedules and offer repeated reassurance that networking is valued, allowing employees to choose to interact at ChoicePoints.

20.11.3 Spark Conversation

Leaders must move the organization from monologues (“command and control”) to dialogues (“connect and collaborate”).

Action Steps for Leaders [517–525]:

  • Talk “With” Not “At”: Leaders must be visible and spend significant time asking questions, listening, and coaching employees up, down, and across the organization.
  • Improve the Quality: Foster conversation-like practices (like the World Café process) to achieve higher trust, improved efficiency, and better coordination between strategy and execution.
  • Tell the Story: Educate employees about the organization’s achievements and successes (business literacy). Since leaders cannot control external information flow, employees must be able to share the company’s story accurately when talking to outside contacts.
  • Insist on BringBack: Significantly increase the inward flow of valuable business intelligence from employees who attend outside events (conferences, associations), treating intelligence gathering as “everyone’s business”.

20.11.4 Foster Collaboration

This final step involves painting a clear vision, inviting participation, and continuously monitoring progress.

Action Steps for Leaders [526–536]:

  • Know Your Limits: Leaders cannot manage or control individual employee networks; interfering turns networking into a job assignment and removes the necessary discretionary nature of the relationship. Instead, provide trust-building training.
  • Answer “Why?” and “What?”:
    • “Why should we collaborate?”: Leaders must dramatize the Why (the benefits: increasing competitiveness, strategy execution, innovation, and engagement) and illustrate the pain that a lack of collaboration brings (e.g., duplication costs, poor product launches).
    • “What does collaboration look like?”: Highlight successful internal examples, find examples in the industry, and use employee ProNets to find best practices.
  • Elicit Buy-In: Recognize that networking is a discretionary act. Leaders must inspire employees to exert discretionary effort by creating an environment where people choose to share information and resources.
  • Monitor the Progress: Use a diagnostic flow chart (Getting to Collaboration) to determine why collaboration might be stalled. Check key areas: value creation, clear explanation of the Why, presence of employee skills, awareness of non-collaboration, negative consequences, environment of trust, and employee desire. If resistance persists despite all efforts, reassign or remove employees who lack the desire to collaborate.

20.12 Final Comprehensive Summary

Strategic Connections provides a comprehensive blueprint for transitioning individuals and organizations into the Network-Oriented Workplace (NOW), where collaboration, built on a foundation of trust, is the key driver of success. The book is grounded in The 8 Competencies for the Network-Oriented Workplace.

The first step requires an individual commitment to a Strategic Networker Identity. This means shedding the 80% of negative beliefs and misconceptions about networking (e.g., that it’s “schmoozing” or only for “salespeople”). Networking is redefined as the deliberate and discretionary process of creating, cultivating, and capitalizing on trust-based, mutually beneficial relationships, focusing on teaching and giving. Mindset management tools, like transforming the inner “Critic” into a “Coach” and using “power poses,” ensure the networker acts confidently.

Networking must be strategic (Competency 2), focusing efforts on specific goals relating to job execution (Job + N), career advancement, and enterprise-wide initiatives. This is enabled by the Networking Agenda (To Give/To Get) and large-scale Strategic Positioning Projects (SPPs), which must pass tests like the Doorway Test (positioning for visibility) and the Pig-in-Mud Test (aligning passion with purpose).

Contacts are organized and assessed using The Four Nets (WorkNet, OrgNet, ProNet, LifeNet) to ensure balance, diversity, and leveraging resources between professional and personal spheres. For specific goals, a small, diverse KeyNet(12–18 people) is assembled, utilizing collaborators for specific roles (e.g., The Brain, The Sponsor) and acting intentionally to enrich, repair, or start key relationships.

The core methodology for relationship development is through establishing trust via demonstrating Character and Competence across The Six Stages of Trust (Accident to Ally). Progress is determined by the evidence presented in six to eight encounters. Trusting next steps are planned by assessing the associated Risk and Value.

Effective face-to-face interaction requires increasing Social Acumen (Competency 5). Key rituals include mastering the Name Exchange (teaching and learning names thoroughly), comfortably joining groups, and gracefully ending conversations using clear, Agenda-based conscious closings. Maintaining high likeability is essential, as likeability often trumps competence in partner selection.

Interactions are deepened (Competency 6) through conversation skills: using the ASK formula (Add emotion, Seek opinion, Kick it around) for powerful questioning; actively listening for Character, Competence, and clues for giving; and providing generous Give. Relationships are sustained through consistent Follow-Through (or ReachBack) strategies to maintain ties and achieve the five goals of connectivity.

Expertise is communicated (Competency 7) effectively by avoiding conversational pitfalls (Cement, Fog, Blob, Flag) when answering “What do you do?”. Instead, the Best/Test formula is used to provide a talent statement (Best) and a concrete example (Test). Furthermore, expertise is showcased through storytelling, utilizing the 5-S formula (Segue, Situation, SNAFU, Solution, Significance) to ensure narratives are memorable, unique, and service-oriented.

Finally, employees must create new value (Competency 8) by clarifying that collaboration is a voluntary act distinct from teamwork, fostered by high trust. Value is created by acting on the three principles: Reframing (diversifying contacts, embracing that ideas belong to no one), Risking Reaching Out (being connectors, expanding OrgNets, gathering BringBack intelligence), and Reinforcing the Collaborative Culture. These face-to-face trust-building strategies are also crucial when building trust online.

Organizational leaders must actively foster the NOW (Chapter 9) by moving the company toward a Network-Actualized Identity. This involves strategically creating connections (making physical space for “casual collisions,” syncing systems), sparking conversation (moving from “at” to “with,” improving quality, and demanding BringBack), and fostering collaboration by answering the critical question, “Why should we collaborate?”. By integrating these strategic connections, organizations can realize the full potential of their human resources and thrive in the collaborative world.
