12  Business Development

12.1 Executive Summary

The primary aim of this textbook is to enable the market-oriented business developer to conceive, craft, and implement superior business plans through three interconnected modules. Business development is defined as the tasks and processes concerning the analytical preparation of potential growth opportunities, and the support and monitoring of their implementation, excluding strategic decisions or final implementation execution.

The core framework positions the business developer as an integrating generalist whose value lies in synthesizing specialist knowledge across functions—such as marketing, finance, and R&D—to manage the entire business operation. This role is essential for relieving senior management and overcoming cognitive limitations inherent in bounded rationality.

The book emphasizes that business is not an exact science; success requires a humble approach, focusing on basic concepts that work well in practice, rather than complex, unnecessary theories. Key methodologies revolve around defining a Business Model (narrative logic for making money) and substantiating it with a comprehensive Business Plan containing three major components: the Marketing Plan (delivering superior customer value), the Organization Plan(orchestrating resources and activities), and the Finance Plan (value appropriation and financing). The ultimate measure of success for a growth opportunity is whether the financial returns “beat the government bond”.

12.2 The Business Developer’s Mindset

12.2.1 Introduction and Default Positions

This introductory chapter establishes the foundational premises—or “default positions”—guiding the entire book, setting the stage for effective business development. The goal is to provide a mindset and basic tools to prevent persistent mismanagement and failure, recognizing that business theories and practices are often incongruent.

12.2.1.1 Key Concepts

Bounded Rationality Bounded rationality refers to the inherent cognitive limitations of decision-makers, constraining their ability to formulate and solve complex problems, and process information effectively, due to limits on available information and finite time. This realization is fundamental to understanding and doing business, as it drives human tendencies toward opportunistic behavior and overconfidence in decision-making. The concept, drawn from the works of Herbert Simon, Cyert, and March, underscores why idealized theoretical models often fail to capture actual business decisions.

The Integrating Generalist A good business developer is specialized as an integrating generalist, possessing a wide array of knowledge across different fields (e.g., strategy, marketing, finance). Unlike specialists, who focus on deep, detailed knowledge within a restricted field, the integrating generalist works at a higher level of aggregation, bringing together and synthesizing information across the entire business operation. This role is critical because specialists often become bogged down by unnecessary details, making the integrating generalist essential for steering the business towards overall value creation and appropriation.

Theory vs. Practice The business world is characterized by a significant gap between abstract theory (how activities ought to be carried out) and contextual practice (how activities usually are carried out). Business is not an exact science, and unlike physics, it lacks stylized facts or scientific regularities. Managers who fail to realize the limitations of general theories in complex, ever-changing business realities are likely to waste resources. Therefore, business knowledge provides general directions that help minimize mistakes, rather than offering predictable solutions.

12.2.1.2 Frameworks and Methodologies

Distinguishing Knowledge Types Human minds (and corresponding organizational personnel/functions) operate based on three capacities:

  1. Specialized Knowledge: Developed over time with experience in a specific, restricted field. Held by Specialists.
  2. Common General Knowledge: Knowledge needed to sustain daily life; holders are not experts in any area and cannot integrate specialist knowledge. Held by Common Generalists.
  3. Integrating General Knowledge: Wide knowledge across fields with the capacity to integrate specialized knowledge into general areas relevant to the entire business operation. Held by Integrating Generalists (Business Developers).
Concept Explanation
Business is not an exact science Management and marketing lack predictable scientific regularities, demanding a more humble, cautious approach.
Idealized Theories Frameworks presented in textbooks are merely beacons for improvement; they are not the actual state of affairs or clear, detailed answers to implementation.
Sunk Cost Fallacy Decision-makers often believe they are more rational than they are and fail to avoid “throwing good money after bad”.
Business Case Limitation Solving artificial business cases trains analytical skills but often fails to prepare practitioners for real-life constraints, resource issues, and implementation problems.

12.2.1.3 Recap (5 Bullets)

  • The book aims to enable the market-oriented business developer to conceive, craft, and implement superior business plans.
  • The business developer functions as an integrating generalist, synthesizing knowledge across specialized business functions.
  • Business is characterized by bounded rationality and the inevitable discrepancy between abstract theory and complex reality.
  • Avoid the trap of believing complexity equals truth; effective business development relies on state-of-the-art basics that are easily understood and implementable.
  • The overall structure comprises three modules: Mindset (How we think), Toolbox (What are our tools), and Crafting and Implementing (How we work).

12.2.2 Business Development

This chapter defines business development as a standalone construct, clarifying its core tasks, organizational placement, value proposition, and differentiation from related concepts like strategic management and entrepreneurship.

12.2.2.1 Key Concepts

Definition of Business Development (BD) BD refers to the tasks and processes related to the analytical preparation of potential growth opportunities and the support and monitoring of their implementation, but explicitly excludes decisions on strategy or the ultimate execution of implementation. BD is anchored theoretically in the Behavioural Theory of the Firm (BToF) and the Resource-Based View (RBV).

BD’s Value Proposition: Relieving Senior Management Business development creates value primarily by relieving senior management and the board from unnecessary analytical, planning, and monitoring activities related to new ventures with high growth ambitions. This addresses the managerial constraint on a firm’s growth rate known as the Penrose Effect. By taking over the preparation and implementation planning of business plans, BD allows senior executives to allocate their scarce managerial resources to core strategic issues and leadership.

The Good Business Developer A good business developer is characterized as a rare breed integrating generalist who possesses practical knowledge (technology, customer value, industry dynamics), the capacity for conceptual and abstract thinking, specialist knowledge from multiple functions, and experience working across both senior management and line functions. They focus on the overview of the entire business venture, acting as the organization’s in-house consultant.

12.2.2.2 Frameworks and Methodologies

Business Development Process (Overview) The BD process divides tasks into activities conducted before and aftersenior management’s executive decision to pursue a growth opportunity.

A. Tasks Before Executive Decision (Analytical Preparation):

  1. Search for Opportunities: Identifying profitable growth opportunities.
  2. Analysis (SWOT, PESTEL, P5F): Quantifying opportunities and compiling necessary data through external (market, industry) and internal (resources, activities) analysis.
  3. Due Diligence: Thorough investigation and validation of assumptions.
  4. Crafting the Business Plan and Model: Synthesizing data into the roadmap for the proposed venture.

B. Tasks After Executive Decision (Support and Monitoring):

  1. Support Implementation: Assisting line functions in putting the plan into action.
  2. Monitor Performance: Tracking progress against milestones and objectives.
  3. Update Business Plan: Continually revising the plan based on new market and operational knowledge.

Business Development vs. Other Functions

Function Focus Distinction from Business Development
Strategic Management Concerned with overall corporate and business strategy; determining competitive advantage and resource configuration (“Where/How to compete?”). BD assists senior management by providing analyses and monitoring implementation within the constraints of the decided strategy.
Entrepreneurship Combining means of production in novel ways, accepting the risk of profit or loss in one’s own business. BD is merely one critical skill on the entrepreneur’s palette, focused on structured preparation and planning.
Marketing/Sales Marketing is focused on delivering superior customer value; Sales is promoting customer purchase of a product or service. BD integrates these functions into the overall analysis and planning process (Marketing Plan).

Assessing BD Maturity BD in an organization is a matter of degree, not an either/or question. To assess organizational maturity, the book provides worksheets (e.g., Worksheets 1 & 2) that evaluate the extent to which analytical and implementation support activities are carried out. A score of at least 5 on a 7-point Likert scale (for both pre- and post-decision activities) is suggested as necessary for BD to add organizational value.

12.2.2.3 Recap (7 Bullets)

  • Business Development (BD) analytically prepares growth opportunities and supports/monitors implementation, but does not include strategy decisions.
  • BD is found either as a critical entrepreneurial skill, an assigned job function, or a separate staff function.
  • The primary value of BD is relieving senior management constraints (the Penrose Effect) by handling analytical and planning work on growth opportunities.
  • The good business developer is an integrating generalist possessing diverse functional experience and practical knowledge.
  • BD differs from Strategic Management, which sets the overall strategy, and Entrepreneurship, where BD is just one necessary skill.
  • A major component of BD is the Business Plan, which serves as a roadmap for analysis, feasibility study, and implementation manual.
  • Organizational BD maturity can be assessed based on the degree to which key pre- and post-executive decision tasks are formalized and implemented.

12.2.3 Market Orientation

Market orientation is the foundational mindset for the business developer, emphasizing superior customer value creation. This chapter explores what market orientation is, how it functions organizationally, and the distinction between reactive and proactive market approaches.

12.2.3.1 Key Concepts

Market Orientation Defined Market orientation is one of several business orientations (others include product, process, technology, or sales orientation). It involves the systematic organizational generation, dissemination, and action based on market intelligence regarding both current and prospective customers and competitors. Market orientation is typically associated with better business performance, primarily because it fosters continuous adaptation to environmental change.

Market-Oriented Information Processing Effective market orientation relies on three intertwined components:

  1. Generation: Acquiring market intelligence (customer needs, competitors’ capabilities) using various sources (internal, external, formal, informal).
  2. Dissemination: Sharing this information throughout the organization in a timely, systematic, and verifiable manner. This requires organization-wide awareness and incentives to share.
  3. Action (Responsiveness): Taking actions based on the processed information, intended to elicit a favorable customer response, either by lowering perceived costs or increasing perceived benefits.

Responsive vs. Proactive Market Approach A mature, effective market-oriented firm must achieve a dynamic balance between responsive and proactive market approaches.

  • Responsive Market Approach (Reactive): Identifying and satisfying stated customer needs (wants and demands). This approach is essential for meeting current market demands but can lead to incremental change.
  • Proactive Market Approach (Explorative): Discovering and meeting latent (unmet or unarticulated) customer needs that customers may not recognize yet. This approach is crucial for achieving radical innovation and long-term adaptation.

12.2.3.2 Frameworks and Methodologies

Barriers to Market Orientation Internal organizational factors can inhibit market orientation:

  • Organizational Structure: Centralized and formalized structures may inhibit information flows and responsiveness.
  • Incentive Systems: If rewards are based solely on individual short-term sales targets, long-term market intelligence sharing may suffer.
  • Dominant Business Orientation: When other orientations (e.g., technology or sales) overshadow market orientation, the firm may fail to define its purpose based on solving customer wants.

Developing Market Orientation A business developer can foster market orientation by:

  1. Ensuring Senior Management Commitment: Market orientation requires active endorsement and behavior setting from the top.
  2. Facilitating Cross-Functional Interaction: Breaking down organizational barriers to enable fluid information dissemination.
  3. Implementing a Comprehensive Market Information System (MIS): Systematically gathering, distributing, and acting upon market intelligence.

12.2.3.3 Recap (6 Bullets)

  • Market orientation is the set of tasks focused on creating and delivering superior customer value by systematically gathering, disseminating, and acting on market intelligence.
  • It is critical to gather information not just on customers, but also on competitors.
  • Information must be verifiable, disseminated through routine channels, and supported by incentives to share across the organization.
  • Firms must balance responsive (meeting stated needs) and proactive (meeting latent/unarticulated needs) market approaches.
  • Centralization, formalization, and inappropriate incentive systems can inhibit market orientation and responsiveness.
  • Successful market-oriented firms define their core problems as satisfactory solvers of customer wants, aligning with the scientific method.

12.2.4 Innovation, the Entrepreneur and Entrepreneurial Orientation

This chapter explores how ideas are converted into innovation, the specific characteristics and mindset of the entrepreneur, and how entrepreneurial activity is institutionalized within an organization (Entrepreneurial Orientation, EO).

12.2.4.1 Key Concepts

Invention vs. Innovation An invention is a unique product of the human intellect, such as a technological breakthrough or a literary work, that may have commercial value. An innovation is the successful introduction and commercialization of an invention in the marketplace; innovation is the successful outcome of the development process. The good invention should ideally be novel, involve an inventive step (non-obvious), and be capable of industrial application.

The Entrepreneur An entrepreneur is defined as someone who combines the means of production in novel ways in their own business and is willing to accept the risk of profit or loss. They are not defined by a profession or a lasting condition.

  • Traits and Biases: Successful entrepreneurs often exhibit overconfidence (overestimating their probability of being right) and representativeness (overgeneralizing from few observations). These biases can be necessary simplifying mechanisms when dealing with high uncertainty.
  • Opportunity Recognition: Entrepreneurs discover opportunities through their prior knowledge, which allows them to recognize the commercial potential of information others overlook.

Entrepreneurial Orientation (EO) EO refers to an organizational culture and process that encourages innovation, risk-taking, and proactiveness. EO is implemented through core processes that drive opportunity-seeking, link dispersed resources, and challenge existing beliefs (renewal process). Note that extreme entrepreneurial behavior (risk-taking, autonomy) may not always be optimal; moderated EO often leads to better performance.

12.2.4.2 Frameworks and Methodologies

Types of Innovation [117, 119–121, 133] Innovation can be categorized based on the degree of change required in two dimensions: technology and market structure.

  1. Incremental Innovation: Minor improvements to existing products/services using existing technology; dominant form of innovation.
  2. Radical Innovation: Creating new products/services that satisfy new customer needs using new technology.
  3. Architectural Innovation: Known technology/components reorganized in a novel way to redefine the product’s architecture.
  4. Modular Innovation: Significant changes to core components while maintaining the basic product architecture.

Open Innovation Open innovation is a paradigm that assumes valuable external knowledge should be explored and exploited to create and appropriate value, in contrast to the traditional closed model where R&D, development, and shipping must be done internally. This approach acknowledges the limitations of internal knowledge and seeks external partners.

Entrepreneurial Assessment (Worksheets) The book provides self-assessment worksheets to gauge an organization’s level of entrepreneurial orientation across characteristics such as proactiveness, autonomy, competitive aggressiveness, risk-taking, and innovativeness. The goal is to identify deviations from a preferred moderate level (e.g., þ1 on a –3 to þ3scale).

12.2.4.3 Recap (7 Bullets)

  • Innovation is the successful commercialization of an invention—a product of the human intellect with commercial value.
  • Successful entrepreneurs often demonstrate biases like overconfidence and representativeness, which aid decision-making under uncertainty.
  • Organizational learning should be directed toward taking advantage of the next good innovation, not predicting success.
  • Innovation types range from minor incremental changes to market-disrupting radical innovation.
  • Open innovation promotes exploring and exploiting external knowledge to capture value.
  • Entrepreneurial Orientation (EO) institutionalizes innovation, risk-taking, and proactiveness within the organization.
  • Success is often attributed not just to talent, but to extraordinary opportunity and avoiding basic mismanagement.

12.2.5 Business Models and Business Plans

This chapter introduces the two central tools for the business developer: the Business Model (BM) and the Business Plan (BP), which formalize the conception and feasibility of a growth opportunity.

12.2.5.1 Key Concepts

Business Model (BM) The BM is the organization’s logic or story detailing how it intends to make money now and in the foreseeable future from a given business opportunity. It is a simplified representation of the business logic, tying narratives to numbers. BMs are broadly categorized as either transaction-based (focus on content, structure, and governance of exchanges) or activity-based (focus on systems of interdependent tasks and processes).

Four Building Blocks of the Good Business Model A sound BM must address four critical components:

  1. Superior Customer Value Proposition: What value the business offers customers and how it addresses their “pain points”.
  2. Key Internal and Relational Resources: The necessary financial, physical, human, and organizational assets, including strategic alliances, required to deliver the value.
  3. Key Tasks and Processes: The interdependent activities (e.g., manufacturing, marketing, distribution) required to create and deliver value.
  4. Value Appropriation (Profit Formula): The underlying economic logic explaining how the firm delivers value at an appropriate cost and earns profit (revenue model and cost structure).

Business Plan (BP) The BP is a formal, written statement describing why, when, and how a growth opportunity will be implemented. It provides the analytical support and qualification for the Business Model. The BP’s length and detail depend on the complexity and scope of the venture.

Purpose: Uncertainty Reduction The primary purpose of the BP is to reduce uncertainty for stakeholders (investors, senior management, partners) regarding the opportunity’s viability and likelihood of success. This is essential due to the “liability of newness” and the prevalence of information asymmetries (adverse selection, moral hazard, hold-up problems).

12.2.5.2 Frameworks and Methodologies

Business Model vs. Strategy The BM focuses on how the components of the business fit together to create value, while strategy (business level) focuses on how to deal with competition and be different in a better way. Strategy is about competitive choices; the BM is the logic of how the firm operates.

BP Structure (Module III Roadmap) The BP provides a logical structure covering the entire business operation:

  1. Executive Summary: The pitch, including the Business Model.
  2. Company Overview: Corporate governance, start-up summary, location/facilities.
  3. Situation Overview: Market summary, SWOT analysis, competitors, key success factors.
  4. Market Strategy (Marketing Plan): Target market, positioning, and marketing mix.
  5. Organization Design (Organization Plan): Management team, structure, controls, and implementation.
  6. Financial Statements (Finance Plan): Assumptions, KPIs, breakeven analysis, projected statements, and performance ratios.

Causation and Effectuation

  • Causation: Processes that assume a specific effect (goal) and focus on selecting the means to achieve it (typical for banks).
  • Effectuation: Processes that take a set of means (resources/capabilities) as given and focus on selecting possible effects (opportunities) that can be created with them (typical for venture capitalists/angels).

The Narrative Test and Numbers Test To ensure a growth opportunity is viable, both the Business Model (narrative) and the Finance Plan (numbers) must be validated.

  • Pass/Pass (Good Potential): The business model is coherent, and the numbers add up, indicating readiness for implementation.
  • Fail/Fail (No Potential): The model is flawed, and numbers are unsupported, suggesting termination.
  • Pass Narrative/Fail Numbers (Uncertain Potential): The market need is present, but the financial viability is not yet proven.
  • Fail Narrative/Pass Numbers (Delusion): Numbers are superficially appealing but lack a credible underlying business logic.

12.2.5.3 Recap (7 Bullets)

  • The Business Model is the narrative logic answering “How do we make money?” based on four building blocks.
  • The Business Plan is the formal document providing analytical support and qualification (the numbers) for the Business Model.
  • The primary purpose of the BP is uncertainty reduction for internal and external stakeholders.
  • Good BPs follow a clear, simple standard format; deviation often signals a lack of professional capability.
  • Financial stakeholders often rely on the Causation mindset (banks) or the Effectuation mindset (VCs, Angels) when assessing risk.
  • The business developer must pass both the Narrative Test (BM) and the Numbers Test (Finance Plan) before proceeding to implementation.
  • Failure often stems from poor communication or delusion, emphasizing the need for honesty regarding capabilities and limitations.

12.3 The Business Developer’s Toolbox

12.3.1 Strategy and Strategic Management

This chapter defines strategy as commitment and details the strategic management process, providing the structure within which the business developer identifies and assesses growth opportunities.

12.3.1.1 Key Concepts

Strategy as Commitment Strategy is fundamentally about commitments: choices to dedicate specialized resources to investments that constrain the organization’s scope of future possibilities long into the future. Strategy is not merely setting goals (vision/mission) or improving efficiency (operational effectiveness). Commitments create strategic wedges that lead to performance differentials.

Four Criteria of Commitment

  1. Lock-in: Being stuck with an investment due to significant sunk costs (costs that cannot be recouped).
  2. Lock-out: Pre-empting competitors from pursuing the same strategic path.
  3. Lag: The time required for resource stock adaptation, creating lead time over competitors.
  4. Inertia: Resistance to change stemming from organization culture or specialized human resources.

Value Creation vs. Value Appropriation Strategy involves managing the tension between creating value for the customer and appropriating a share of that value for the firm (rent).

  • Value Creation: Processes like innovating, producing, and marketing products.
  • Value Appropriation: Processes like extracting profits through market power, property rights, or difficult-to-imitate resources.

12.3.1.2 Frameworks and Methodologies

The Strategic Management Process Strategic management is the set of analyses, decisions, and actions undertaken to create and sustain competitive advantages.

  1. Strategy Development (Analyses): Setting Vision, Mission, and Strategic Objectives. Performing SWOT analysis(Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats).
  2. Strategic Choice (Decisions): Determining Corporate-Level Strategy (“Where to compete?”) and Business-Level Strategy (“How to compete?”).
  3. Strategy Implementation (Actions): Organization design, structure, controls, and incentive schemes.

Vision, Mission, and Objectives

  • Vision Statement: A realistic, credible, and attractive answer to “Where are we going?” (future path, markets, technology focus).
  • Mission Statement: A brief statement indicating the organization’s raison d’être (present purpose, customer value, competitive advantage).
  • Strategic Objectives: Operationalize the mission by outlining specific, measurable targets (financial and non-financial). Objectives must be SMART (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound).

SWOT Analysis A critical synthesis tool for strategy development, drawing on sophisticated tools to feed the four boxes.

  • Opportunities (O) & Threats (T): Informed by analysis of the external business environment (e.g., PESTEL, Porter’s Five Forces).
  • Strengths (S) & Weaknesses (W): Informed by internal analysis of firm resources, capabilities, and activities (e.g., VRIO framework, Value Chain).

Strategic Choice

  • Corporate-Level Strategy (Where to compete?): Decisions on portfolio scope and boundaries, guided by Transaction Cost Economics. Choices include Integration (Vertical/Horizontal), Diversification, and Strategic Alliances.
  • Business-Level Strategy (How to compete?): Positioning the firm in a given market, guided by Porter’s Generic Strategies.
    1. Cost Leadership: Achieving the lowest cost position relative to competitors.
    2. Differentiation: Providing unique, value-added non-price features for which customers will pay a premium price.
    3. Focus Strategy: Targeting a narrow market niche based on cost or differentiation. Firms must avoid being “stuck in the middle”.

12.3.1.3 Recap (8 Bullets)

  • Strategy involves making commitments (e.g., sunk costs) that constrain future possibilities and are the source of competitive advantage.
  • Competitive strategy manages the trade-off between value creation (for customers) and value appropriation (for the firm).
  • The Strategic Management Process moves from analysis (Vision, Mission, SMART Objectives, SWOT) to decision (Strategic Choice) to action (Implementation).
  • SWOT analysis is a core tool, using sophisticated frameworks like PESTEL, P5F, and VRIO to inform the boxes.
  • Corporate strategy determines where to compete (Integration, Diversification, Alliances).
  • Business strategy determines how to compete (Cost Leadership, Differentiation, Focus), and must avoid being stuck in the middle.
  • Successful implementation requires anchoring strategy across the organization through organizational design, structure, and incentives.
  • The business developer’s role is to ensure growth opportunities align with the existing strategy, or suggest new strategic directions if necessary.

12.3.2 The Business Environment: Value Creation I

This chapter outlines the external analysis components of the SWOT (Opportunities and Threats), focusing on the three levels of the business environment: macro, industry/market, and value system.

12.3.2.1 Key Concepts

Levels of External Analysis

  1. Macro Environment: Broad external factors that influence the entire industry (analyzed using PESTEL). These are general and usually unmanageable by the firm.
  2. Market and Industry: The competitive structure and players (analyzed using P5F). The industry refers to firms offering close substitutes; the market includes all available substitutes.
  3. Value System: The interconnected network of firms (suppliers, buyers, distributors, complementors) involved in the co-creation of final customer value.

12.3.2.2 Frameworks and Methodologies

PESTEL Framework PESTEL analyzes the macro environment by breaking it down into six categories of factors that present opportunities or threats:

  1. Political (tax policies, trade barriers).
  2. Economic (GNP/GDP, inflation, interest rates, disposable income).
  3. Socio-cultural (demographics, social trends, customs).
  4. Technological (R&D efforts, innovation rate).
  5. Environmental (ecological issues, climate change, energy consumption).
  6. Legal (health/safety laws, IPR protection).

Porter’s Five Competitive Forces (P5F) Framework P5F assesses industry attractiveness (potential profitability) and provides strategic direction by analyzing five forces that erode industry profit.

  1. Competitive Rivalry: Intensity of competition among existing firms (influenced by industry growth, concentration, product differentiation, exit barriers).
  2. Potential Entrants: Threat of new firms entering the market (influenced by entry barriers like economies of scale, IPR protection, and government policy).
  3. Extra-Industry Substitutes: Products/services outside the industry boundary that fulfill the same customer need.
  4. Buyers’ Bargaining Power: The ability of customers to force down prices or demand higher quality/service (high when buyers are large or products are undifferentiated).
  5. Suppliers’ Bargaining Power: The ability of raw material/input providers to raise prices or reduce quality (high when suppliers are concentrated or inputs are specialized).

Value System and Gap Analysis The value system includes the network of suppliers, manufacturers, distributors, and customers co-creating value for the end-user. Analyzing the value system provides insights beyond industry competition.

  • Service Outputs: To identify opportunities, the business developer assesses the customer’s demands for service outputs across the value system (e.g., bulk-breaking, spatial convenience, waiting time, assortment, customer service).
  • Gap Analysis: This method identifies misalignment between customer demands (Service Output Demand) and the costs/benefits provided by the value system (Service Output Supply). Gaps signal opportunities (or threats) where value is either not created or created inefficiently.

12.3.2.3 Recap (7 Bullets)

  • External analysis covers three nested levels: Macro Environment (PESTEL), Industry/Market (P5F), and the Value System.
  • The PESTEL framework systematically assesses opportunities and threats from unmanageable broad factors (e.g., political, economic trends).
  • The P5F framework assesses industry profitability and competitive structure by analyzing rivalry, potential entrants, substitutes, and buyer/supplier power.
  • Industry analysis must include extra-industry substitutes (e.g., iPad vs. book) that fulfill the same basic customer need.
  • The Value System includes all interconnected players (including Complementors) necessary for co-creating superior customer value.
  • Gap Analysis identifies misalignments between customer requirements (Service Outputs) and the value system’s delivery, revealing opportunities for improvement or new ventures.
  • When conducting external analysis, avoid relying on qualitative lists; focus on quantifying the influence of relevant forces and defining the industry appropriately.

12.3.3 Resources and Organization Design: Value Creation II

This chapter details the internal analysis components of the SWOT (Strengths and Weaknesses), focusing on the firm’s resources, capabilities, and organizational structure required to create value.

12.3.3.1 Key Concepts

Resources and Capabilities

  • Resources: Tangible and intangible assets (e.g., machinery, knowledge, organizational processes) used to implement strategies. Resources are categorized into Financial, Physical, Human, Intellectual Property, Organizational, and Relational.
  • Capabilities: A firm’s ability to integrate resources to achieve a desired end. Capabilities are often specific to the firm or related to external partners (relational capabilities).
  • Resource-Based View (RBV): Theory focusing on firm-level, heterogeneous resources as the source of competitive advantage, assuming that only a subset of resources are strategically relevant.

Value Chain Analysis Porter’s Value Chain breaks down a firm’s activities into two general categories to identify sources of competitive advantage and cost reduction.

  • Primary Activities: Directly related to manufacturing and distribution: inbound logistics, operations, outbound logistics, marketing/sales, and (after-sales) service.
  • Support (Secondary) Activities: Infrastructure that supports the primary activities: procurement, technology development, human resource management (HRM), and general administration/infrastructure.

Organization Design and Control Organization design involves setting up the structure, processes, controls, and incentives necessary to exploit strategic resources.

  • Organizational Structures: Common types include the Simple Structure (entrepreneur-led), the Functional Structure (U-form) (departments segregated by function, efficient for narrow portfolios), and the Multidivisional Structure (M-form) (divided into specialized business units, preferred for diversified activities).
  • Control Mechanisms: Required to ensure employee behavior aligns with firm goals. These include formal rules, centralization/standardization, and objectives.
  • Incentives and Rewards: Systems (extrinsic/intrinsic) designed to motivate employees, which must be tailored to the generic strategy (e.g., differentiation requires flexible, creative rewards; cost leadership requires strict cost-focused rewards).

12.3.3.2 Frameworks and Methodologies

VRIO Framework The VRIO framework determines if a resource or capability is strategically relevant and a potential source of sustained competitive advantage.

  1. Valuable (V): Does the resource enable the firm to conceive or implement strategies that improve efficiency and effectiveness? (If no, competitive disadvantage).
  2. Rare (R): Is the resource controlled by only a small number of competing firms? (If no, competitive parity).
  3. Inimitable (I): Do competitors face a disproportionately high cost of imitating the strategy based on the resource? (If no, temporary competitive advantage). Inimitability stems from causal ambiguity, time compression diseconomies, or social complexity.
  4. Organization (O): Is the firm’s structure, procedures, and management designed to fully exploit the strategic resource? (If no, unused temporary competitive advantage).

VRIO Analysis Application Steps The business developer uses the VRIO framework to systematically assess the six resource categories across the four criteria.

  1. Identify Resources: List all tangible and intangible resources across the six categories (Financial, Physical, Human, IPR, Organizational, Relational).
  2. Assess V, R, I, O: For each resource, answer the four questions (Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, Organized).
  3. Determine Competitive Implication: Based on the scores, determine if the resource leads to competitive disadvantage, parity, temporary advantage, or sustained advantage.
  4. Inform SWOT: The outcome defines the firm’s true Strengths and Weaknesses (SW component of SWOT).

12.3.3.3 Recap (8 Bullets)

  • Internal analysis assesses the firm’s Strengths and Weaknesses based on resources, capabilities, and organization design.
  • The Resource-Based View (RBV) posits that competitive advantage comes from valuable, heterogeneous firm-level resources.
  • Resources are categorized as financial, physical, human, intellectual property, organizational, and relational (co-specialized assets with partners).
  • The Value Chain analyzes primary (logistics, operations, sales) and support activities (HRM, procurement) to identify cost or differentiation advantages.
  • Organization Design must align structures (U-form, M-form) and controls to efficiently exploit strategic resources.
  • Corporate Governance (Board, CEO) is essential, but boards must avoid being ceremonial or self-serving (e.g., friends/family).
  • The VRIO framework (Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, Organized) is the managerial tool for determining if a resource leads to competitive parity, temporary advantage, or sustained advantage.
  • A truly sustained competitive advantage (VRIO) is a rare phenomenon; temporary advantages are more common.

12.3.4 Value Appropriation and Property Rights

This chapter focuses on mechanisms to capture the value created by the firm, including market structure, strategic resources, and the legal framework of property rights.

12.3.4.1 Key Concepts

Value Appropriation (Rent Capture) Value appropriation is the process of extracting profits (economic rents) from the marketplace. Rents are returns that exceed opportunity costs.

  • Teece’s Framework: Appropriating value from an invention requires three components: difficult-to-imitate resources/capabilities, critical complementary resources, and an effective appropriability regime (or property rights environment).
  • Sources of Rent: Rents can be generated from industry structure (monopoly rents), unique firm resources (Ricardian rents), relational assets (relational rents), or superior foresight (entrepreneurial rents).

Legal Systems and Property Understanding the legal environment is crucial, especially for international business.

  • Civil Law: Based on general legal principles and written codes (e.g., Scandinavia, France, China). Contracts are generally shorter.
  • Common Law: Based on customary practices, past judicial opinions, and court decisions (e.g., UK, USA). Contracts are generally longer and more explicit.
  • Property: Differentiated into tangible (physical objects like machinery) and intangible (non-physical rights like patents, debt, goodwill).

Intellectual Property Rights (IPRs) IPRs protect firms’ creative efforts or commercial reputation. The legal protection of IPRs forms a key part of the appropriability regime.

  • Patents: A legal grant for an invention, providing the holder the exclusive right to make, use, or sell the invention for a limited area and time (typically 20 years) in exchange for full disclosure. Patents must be novel and non-obvious.
  • Copyrights: A property right covering expressions in literary, scientific, or artistic works, sound recordings, etc.. Protection exists once the work is fixed in a tangible form, without formal application.
  • Trade Marks: Signs (words, slogans, logos) used to distinguish a firm’s products or services and commercial reputation. Protection is granted via registration.
  • Design Rights: Protect the visual appearance (shape, configuration) of a product.

12.3.4.2 Frameworks and Methodologies

IPR Strategy: Thickets and Fences Firms often use strategic combinations of IPRs to maximize protection:

  • Patent Fences: Developing a portfolio of patents around a core invention to deter competition.
  • Patent Thickets: Surrounding a core technology with dense, overlapping IPRs, making it difficult for competitors to enter the field. This is common in complex industries like biotech.

Non-Disclosure Agreements (NDAs) NDAs are legal contracts used to protect confidential information when disclosure is necessary (e.g., presenting a high-tech concept to investors or partners). As there is no standard NDA, seeking legal counsel is always advised. NDAs typically define the confidential information, specify which parties can access it, and stipulate conditions for its return and termination.

12.3.4.3 Recap (8 Bullets)

  • Value appropriation focuses on extracting economic rents derived from sources like market power or unique firm assets.
  • The appropriability regime (property rights environment) determines how effectively a firm can capture profits from its innovations.
  • Legal systems differ globally (Civil, Common, Islamic), requiring business developers to seek local professional legal advice.
  • Patents grant exclusive rights for an invention in exchange for disclosure, lasting typically 20 years.
  • Copyrights protect the expression of literary or artistic works and exist without formal application once fixed in a tangible form.
  • Strategic use of IPRs includes building patent thickets or fences to discourage imitation and competition.
  • Strategic alliances are cooperative efforts to access complementary resources and capabilities, typically based on contractual or equity arrangements.
  • Non-Disclosure Agreements (NDAs) are critical when revealing core technology or business ideas to potential partners or investors.

12.4 Crafting and Implementing the Business Plan

This final module applies the mindset (Module 1) and tools (Module 2) to the practical tasks of crafting and implementing the detailed components of the Business Plan.

12.4.1 The Marketing Plan: Marketing Management and Market Research

The Marketing Plan is the foundation of commercial success, answering how superior customer value is created and delivered to a sufficiently large, profitable customer base.

12.4.1.1 Key Concepts

Marketing Plan Role and Content The Marketing Plan is often a stand-alone document and a detailed manual for the Chief Marketing Officer (CMO), outlining the tactics and operational details of getting to the customer. It contains the Situation Analysis (Market Summary, SWOT, Competitors) and the Marketing Strategy (STP, Marketing Mix).

Marketing Concepts

  • Needs, Wants, Demand: Needs are basic human requirements; wants are needs directed towards specific products. Demand refers to wants backed by the willingness and ability to pay, which is the business developer’s dominant focus.
  • Perceived Value: The consumer’s overall assessment of a product’s utility based on the ratio of what is received(benefits: functional, emotional) to what is given (costs: monetary, time, energy). Superior customer value is the core driver of the business model.
  • Positive Disconfirmation: Occurs when performance exceeds expectations, leading to satisfaction.

12.4.1.2 Frameworks and Methodologies

STP Process (Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning) STP provides the process for getting market access:

  1. Segmentation (S): Dividing the total market into distinct groups of customers who respond differently to competitive offerings. Segments must be Measurable, Accessible, Actionable, and Profitable.
  2. Targeting (T): Selecting the segment(s) that align best with the firm’s strengths and capabilities.
  3. Positioning (P): Identifying the superior value position (often a Unique Selling Proposition, USP) in the target market that differentiates the firm from competitors. Common positioning errors (under-positioning, confused positioning) must be avoided.
  4. Marketing Mix (4 Ps): Tweak the operational variables to elicit favorable response.

The Marketing Mix (4 Ps) The controlled parameters used to achieve positioning:

  1. Product: Features, quality, branding, packaging, warranty.
  2. Price: List price, discounts, payment terms.
  3. Place (Distribution): Channels, coverage, inventory, transportation.
  4. Promotion: Advertising, sales promotion, PR, direct marketing.

Market Research Market research provides the accurate and timely knowledge necessary to inform strategy. Data must be discerned from information, and primary data (collected for the specific problem) from secondary data (collected previously for another purpose).

  • Traditional Methods: Include Quantitative (large samples, testing hypotheses, causal predictions) and Qualitative (small samples, interviews, exploratory).
  • User-Driven Methods: Essential for new product development, as customers are poor assessors of novel solutions. Examples include Lead-User Method and Empathic Design, focusing on observing users in context.

Breakeven Analysis The breakeven analysis (part of marketing financials) determines the breakeven point (BEP) where total revenues equal total costs (operating income is zero). The profit-volume (PV) graph visually shows the impact of sales volume changes on operating income. The BEP is calculated by balancing sales forecasts (hardest part) with predictable expense forecasts (easiest part).

12.4.1.3 Recap (9 Bullets)

  • The Marketing Plan is the detailed manual explaining how to achieve a large, profitable customer base, serving as the foundation of the Business Plan.
  • The focus is on generating Demand, which is wants backed by the willingness and ability to pay.
  • Customer satisfaction results from performance meeting or exceeding Perceived Value (benefits minus costs).
  • The STP process (Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning) is the strategy for market access.
  • Positioning defines the firm’s unique advantage (USP) in the customer’s mind, guided by a chosen value strategy (e.g., More for More, Less for Less).
  • The Marketing Mix (4 Ps)—Product, Price, Place, Promotion—are the variables the firm controls to implement positioning.
  • Market research should utilize user-driven qualitative methods (e.g., Lead Users) for new products, rather than relying solely on traditional quantitative methods.
  • The Breakeven Analysis determines the volume needed to cover costs, revealing the minimum required sales forecast.
  • Numbers are meaningless without sound underlying assumptions based on credible market research.

12.4.2 The Organization Plan: Organizational Development and Governance

The Organization Plan explains how the firm orchestrates resources and tasks to deliver superior customer value, focusing on design, governance, controls, and managing organizational dynamics.

12.4.2.1 Key Concepts

Organization Plan Role The Organization Plan is typically integrated into the Business Plan. It consists of the Company Overview (architecture) and Organization Design (interior design). Its objective is to reduce stakeholder uncertainty regarding the team’s ability to implement the strategy by outlining responsibilities, structure, and control mechanisms.

Corporate Governance Corporate governance is the top-level control structure, encompassing decision rights, board composition, CEO appointment, and compensation. The board is responsible for strategic management and oversight, ensuring alignment with shareholder interests.

Principal-Agent Issues Conflict often arises because organizational members (agents) are boundedly rational, self-interested, and risk-averse, leading their actions to diverge from the interests of the principals (owners/shareholders).

  • Agency Costs: Costs incurred by principals to monitor agents and align incentives.
  • Managerialism: Empirical evidence suggests that CEO compensation is often tied more to firm size (managerial interest) than to firm performance (owner interest), demonstrating ongoing agency problems.

Organizational Development and Dynamics As a firm grows, management must transition from the fluid structure of a start-up to specialized functional or multidivisional forms.

  • Success Traps: Occur when people focus on previously successful strategies at the expense of exploring new solutions, making the organization obsolete when the market shifts.
  • Rich vs. King Dilemma: Entrepreneurs face the trade-off between maximizing personal financial return (rich) and maintaining control/power (king). Focusing on being “king” often hinders growth and financial viability.

12.4.2.2 Frameworks and Methodologies

Organization Design Components Organization design ensures the components of the organization are aligned to efficiently deliver value:

  • Strategy (Strategy, Vision, Mission)
  • Structure (Organizational Charts, Hierarchy)
  • Resources, Skills & Activities (Resources, Capabilities, Value Chain)
  • Controls & Incentives (Management Control Systems, Rewards)
  • Values, People & Leadership (Organizational Culture, HRM)

Skills Assessment The BP requires a rigorous assessment of required skills at three levels, typically rated High, Medium, or Low against industry standards:

  1. Board and Advisory Board: Assessing strategic, legal, and financial expertise.
  2. Daily Management (CEO/Line Officers): Assessing strategic management and industry knowledge.
  3. Employees: Assessing core capabilities and functional expertise. Deficiencies indicate a need for internal development or external alliances/acquisitions.

Designing Incentive and Compensation Schemes Effective systems motivate employees (agents) by aligning their interests with the owners (principals).

  • Motivation Factors: Satisfiers (Intrinsic) (e.g., achievement, recognition) motivate; Dissatisfiers (Extrinsic)(e.g., working conditions, pay) prevent dissatisfaction.
  • KPI Decomposition: Goals must be cascaded through the hierarchy (CEO KPIs \(\rightarrow\) Line Officer KPIs \(\rightarrow\) Employee KPIs) to ensure everyone works toward the primary goal of “beating the bond”.
  • Effective Systems: Should have clear objectives, link rewards to performance, provide clear feedback, and be perceived as fair and flexible.

12.4.2.3 Recap (8 Bullets)

  • The Organization Plan proves the firm is structurally and operationally capable of executing the marketing strategy.
  • Corporate governance is critical, managing principal-agent issues where self-interested agents may act against the owner’s best interest.
  • Empirical evidence supports managerialism, showing executive pay correlates more strongly with firm size than performance.
  • Organization design requires aligning five components: Strategy, Structure, Resources, Controls, and People.
  • A rigorous Skills Assessment (High, Medium, Low) is used to identify deficiencies in the Board, Management, and Employees that must be addressed.
  • Effective incentive systems must be carefully designed based on the firm’s strategy (e.g., cost leadership vs. differentiation) and link performance to clear rewards.
  • Growth often requires a transition from simple to functional (U-form) or multidivisional (M-form) structures.
  • Business developers must actively manage success traps and the founder’s “rich vs. king” dilemma, which can impede necessary organizational adaptation and wealth creation.

12.4.3 The Finance Plan: Financial Statements, Arrangements, and Valuation

The Finance Plan explains how operations are financed and how the firm appropriates value, focusing on projected financial statements, capital sourcing, and valuation.

12.4.3.1 Key Concepts

Finance Plan Structure and Goals The plan provides four major components: General Assumptions, Key Financial Indicators, Breakeven Analysis, Projected Financial Statements, and Performance Ratio Analysis. The goal is to provide adequate numbers, clarify assumptions, assess external capital needs, and use professional formats to reduce investor uncertainty.

Sources of Finance

  • Early Stage: Family/Friends, Banks (collateral focus), Public Seed Capital, Business Angels (BAs) (often focus on the entrepreneur’s credibility), and Venture Capitalists (VCs) (demand high returns, hard bargains).
  • Bootstrapping: Finding creative, internal ways to reduce costs or improve cash flow to delay or avoid external financing.

Contractual Covenants Investors use covenants to cope with information asymmetries, moral hazard, and adverse selection. These are protective clauses in financial agreements.

  • Staged Financing: Investments are injected in tranches dependent on achieving specific milestones. This is a “wait-and-see” approach that reduces risk.
  • Decision Rights: Investors (VCs) may take control over hiring/firing management, major acquisitions, or financing rounds.
  • Liquidation Rights: Priority rights specifying who gets paid first if the firm fails.

12.4.3.2 Frameworks and Methodologies

Financial Statements (Pro Forma)

  1. Profit-and-Loss (P&L) Statement / Income Statement: Shows revenues and expenses over a period, yielding Net Income. Key components include Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) and Operating Expenses (overheads).
  2. Balance Sheet: Shows assets, liabilities, and equity at a specific date (A = L + E).
  3. Cash Flow Statement: Analyzes activities (operations, financing, investments) that affect cash flow over a period. It determines the need for external working capital until the breakeven point is reached.

Performance Ratios Ratios derived from financial statements to assess performance in four areas:

  1. Profitability: How the firm earns money (e.g., Return on Assets, Gross Margin).
  2. Liquidity: Ability to meet short-term obligations (e.g., Current Ratio).
  3. Efficiency: How assets are used (e.g., Inventory Turnover).
  4. Stability: Management of long-term debt (e.g., Debt-to-Equity).

Breakeven Analysis (Working Capital Estimation) The breakeven analysis uses cumulative cash flow projections to determine the maximum cash requirement and the Breakeven Time (BET) needed for the venture to become self-financing. This figure is critical for investors, as it dictates the required working capital investment.

Opportunity Valuation Valuing new ventures is challenging due to uncertainty.

  • Discounted Cash Flow (DCF): Calculates the Net Present Value (NPV) of future cash flows. This method is difficult for new ventures due to unreliable long-term forecasts and inappropriate discount rates.
  • Payback Method: Evaluates projects based on the time needed to recover the initial investment. Considered simplistic because it ignores cash flows after recovery and the time value of money.
  • Real Options Logic: Views initial investments as the cost of an option to undertake further strategic steps (e.g., expanding, abandoning, waiting). This method is better suited for highly uncertain environments, as it captures the value of managerial flexibility (e.g., staged financing).

12.4.3.3 Recap (9 Bullets)

  • The Finance Plan explains how operations are financed, using financial statements to reduce stakeholder uncertainty.
  • Effective finance plans require clarifying assumptions based on the business model, as numbers are only as good as their origin.
  • The Cash Flow Statement reveals the need for external working capital and determines the Breakeven Time (BET).
  • Key sources of finance include VCs (high returns, control) and Business Angels (credibility focus).
  • Contractual covenants (e.g., milestones, decision rights) are used by investors to mitigate agency risks.
  • Opportunity valuation methods include DCF, Payback, and the preferred Real Options Logic, which values managerial flexibility and staged investment.
  • The pre-money valuation determines the entrepreneur’s non-cash contributions (patents, knowledge) and share ownership percentage before a new investment.
  • Dilution of shares refers to the diminishing percentage of ownership following a new investment.
  • The business developer must be skilled in identifying the interdependence of the financial statements and connecting them back to the business model.

12.4.4 Connecting the Dots: Implementing the Business Plan

This concluding chapter synthesizes the book’s lessons, focusing on the approval process, decision-making biases, organizational learning, and performance tracking necessary for successful implementation.

12.4.4.1 Key Concepts

The Main Dots for Implementation Implementation is the final, toughest phase of the BD process, which involves:

  1. Approval Process: Getting executive buy-in for the plan.
  2. Due Diligence: External assessment of the plan’s validity and team’s credibility.
  3. Implement Strategic Initiatives: Putting the plan into action, monitoring progress, and overcoming resistance to change.
  4. Performance Tracking: Measuring financial and non-financial progress.
  5. Report Progress: Updating the BP based on lessons learned and feeding back to stakeholders.

Decision-Making and Identity Decision-making is selecting a course of action to create and appropriate value. However, decisions often happen in a context of bounded rationality.

  • Identity: An individual’s or organization’s concept of self, organized into rules that determine which actions are appropriate in given situations. If the action matches the situation and identity, no deep thought is required.
  • Bias and Errors: Decision-makers are prone to oversimplification, recalling incomplete memories, focusing only on successes (selection bias), and active inertia (sticking to past successes).

Organizational Learning Learning occurs when an organization, through information processing, changes its range of potential behaviors. Market-oriented learning is foundational.

  • Exploration vs. Exploitation: Firms must balance exploration (searching for new knowledge and opportunities) and exploitation (refining and extending existing knowledge and capabilities). Too much of either leads to traps.
  • Absorptive Capacity: The firm’s ability to recognize the value of new, external information, assimilate it, and apply it commercially.

Resistance to Change Implementation success hinges on overcoming resistance. Key factors include:

  • Sense of Urgency: Leaders must create a compelling need for change, making it “painful” to maintain the status quo.
  • Anchoring Strategy: Ensuring the strategy is understood and supported throughout the entire organization, from executives down to the shop floor.
  • Decision Rights: Employees must have the autonomy and accountability to make local decisions necessary for implementation.

12.4.4.2 Frameworks and Methodologies

Approval Process and Due Diligence The approval process for external funding involves several stages:

  1. Preliminary Screening: Rapid assessment of the BP (often two minutes per plan).
  2. Presentation: Pitching the opportunity to investors.
  3. Agreement of General Terms: Establishing investment size, valuation, and milestones.
  4. Due Diligence: Rigorous, expert assessment of the BP’s validity (legal, technical, market, financial), the team’s credibility, and confirming critical assumptions.
  5. Negotiation: Finalizing terms based on due diligence findings.

The Balanced Scorecard A widely used performance tracking system that provides a multi-perspective dashboard for assessing progress and aligning strategy. It links mission and objectives to action points across four key perspectives:

  1. Financial (How do we look to shareholders?)
  2. Customer (How do customers see us?)
  3. Internal Business Process (What must we excel at?)
  4. Learning and Growth (Can we continue to improve and create value?)

Performance Reporting Progress must be reported accurately and frequently to stakeholders. Reports should focus on tracking performance against both financial and non-financial KPIs and milestones. The business developer’s responsibility is to update the business plan continually as new information is learned during implementation.

12.4.4.3 Recap (10 Bullets)

  • The implementation phase requires connecting the dots between planning, decision-making, action, monitoring, and reporting.
  • The Approval Process involves screening, presentations, agreeing on general terms, rigorous due diligence, and final negotiation.
  • Decision-making is heavily influenced by bounded rationality and organizational identity rules.
  • Business developers must be skilled in identifying poor meetings and flawed advice from non-experts.
  • Organizational learning requires balancing exploration (new knowledge) and exploitation (refining old knowledge).
  • Firms are prone to failure traps (cascade of failures leading to panic searching) and success traps (relying too long on past successes).
  • Successful implementation requires overcoming resistance to change by creating a sense of urgency and anchoring the strategy at all hierarchical levels.
  • The Balanced Scorecard is a key tool for performance tracking, linking strategic objectives to KPIs across financial, customer, internal, and learning perspectives.
  • Reports must be assessed critically to avoid biases like focusing only on success stories (selection bias).
  • The final stage involves determining whether to continue, adjust, or exit the growth opportunity based on monitored performance against original financial objectives (beating the bond).

12.5 Master Summary

“Business Development: A Market-Oriented Perspective” provides a unified framework for managing growth opportunities, centered on the market-oriented business developer—an integrating generalist skilled in synthesizing knowledge across functions. The book is structured around answering the fundamental question of how to conceive, craft, and implement superior business plans to generate wealth and “beat the government bond”.

The fundamental assumption is bounded rationality, meaning business is non-exact and unpredictable; thus, structured planning is essential for mitigating risk and managing agency conflicts. The core methodology involves validating the Business Model (the coherent narrative logic for value creation and appropriation) and supporting it with a comprehensive Business Plan (the analytical roadmap and feasibility study).

The planning process is divided into three modules:

  1. Mindset (How we think): Requires cultivating market orientation (systematic customer and competitor intelligence) and leveraging the entrepreneurial mindset (innovation, risk-taking, proactiveness).
  2. Toolbox (What are our tools): Utilizes established management frameworks for rigorous analysis:
    • Strategy: Defined by long-term commitments and managing the tension between value creation (using PESTEL, P5F, and Value System analysis to identify Opportunities/Threats) and value appropriation (using property rights, market power, and corporate/business strategies).
    • Internal Analysis (SWOT): Applying the VRIO framework (Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, Organized) and Value Chain Analysis to assess the firm’s specific strengths and weaknesses.
  3. Crafting and Implementing (How we work): Operationalizing the plan through detailed components:
    • Marketing Plan: Defines the STP process (Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning) and the operational Marketing Mix (4 Ps), informed by user-driven market research.
    • Organization Plan: Determines structure (U-form/M-form), corporate governance (managing principal-agent conflicts), assesses required skills, and designs alignment via controls and incentives.
    • Finance Plan: Provides projected financial statements (P&L, Balance Sheet, Cash Flow), calculates the required working capital via Breakeven Analysis, and uses models like Real Options to value opportunities and structure financing (e.g., staged funding with covenants).

Key Frameworks and Methodologies:

  1. External Analysis (OT): The macro environment is analyzed using PESTEL. Industry attractiveness and profit potential are determined by Porter’s Five Forces (P5F), emphasizing the threat of extra-industry substitutes. Opportunities are found by performing a Gap Analysis on Service Outputs within the Value System.
  2. Internal Analysis (SW): Strengths and Weaknesses are assessed using Porter’s Value Chain (analyzing primary and support activities) and the VRIO framework (determining if resources are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Organized). Inimitability is often protected via an effective appropriability regime utilizing IPRs (Patents, Trade Marks, NDAs).
  3. Market Strategy: Relies on a market-oriented culture that balances responsive (exploitative) and proactive(explorative) approaches. Implementation is driven by the STP Process (Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning/USP) and managed via the operational Marketing Mix (4 Ps). User-driven qualitative research (e.g., Lead Users) is prioritized for new product ideas.
  4. Implementation Structure and Finance: The Organization Plan must align resources, structures (U-form/M-form), and incentives/controls (managing Principal-Agent issues) with the overall strategy. The Finance Planrelies on projected statements (Cash Flow is critical) and utilizes Breakeven Analysis to determine capital requirements and the Breakeven Time (BET). In high-uncertainty environments, investors prefer Staged Financing based on the Real Options Logic, valuing managerial flexibility.
  5. Monitoring: Implementation is monitored via Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) that are decomposed through the hierarchy (“beating the bond”). The Balanced Scorecard (BSC) provides a multi-perspective dashboard for tracking progress against strategic objectives across Financial, Customer, Internal Process, and Learning perspectives. The final step requires continually revising the BP based on real-world feedback, avoiding biases like selection bias (focusing only on successes).

Successful implementation is the final hurdle, demanding that the business developer overcome common organizational dysfunctions (e.g., failure traps, active inertia, resistance to change) and track progress effectively using performance measures like the Balanced Scorecard. The business developer ensures continuous learning, anchoring the strategy across all organizational levels, and adapting the Business Plan based on real-world outcomes.
