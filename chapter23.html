23  Redesigning Work

23.1 Executive Summary

This document synthesizes the core arguments and frameworks from Lynda Gratton’s Redesigning Work, which posits that the global pandemic created a once-in-a-century opportunity to fundamentally rethink and reshape working practices. The central thesis is that organizations, having been thrust into a massive “unfreeze” state as described by psychologist Kurt Lewin, now have a unique chance to be bold and intentional in designing work that is more purposeful, productive, and fulfilling. The document outlines a practical, four-step design process—Understand, Reimagine, Model and Test, Act and Create—that guides organizations in developing a unique corporate “signature” rather than adopting a one-size-fits-all solution.

Key takeaways include the critical trade-offs between place and time. Reimagining place involves balancing the cooperation and serendipity of the co-located office with the energy and well-being derived from distributed homeworking. Reimagining time involves balancing the coordination benefits of synchronous, connected work with the need for focus in asynchronous, disconnected periods. The analysis emphasizes that successful redesign is driven by a deep understanding of productivity elements, human networks, and employee needs, which are themselves shaped by profound technological, demographic, and social trends like automation and longer lifespans. Ultimately, the success of any new model depends on its ability to be future-proofed, support technological transitions, and be perceived as fair and just by all employees, a process that requires active co-creation and engaged leadership.

23.2 The Opportunity to Redesign Work

The source material frames the current moment as an unprecedented opportunity for change, driven by a global disruption that broke deeply ingrained and often dysfunctional working habits. This period of transformation offers a chance to build on key lessons learned and consciously design a better future of work.

23.2.1 A Global “Unfreeze” Moment

The book applies Kurt Lewin’s “freeze, unfreeze, refreeze” model of institutional change to describe the impact of the pandemic.

  • Freeze State: Before 2020, most organizations were in a “frozen” state, with fixed cultures, structures, and processes. While some companies were beginning to experiment with flexibility in response to automation and demographic shifts, change was slow and small-scale.
  • Unfreeze State: The pandemic initiated a global “unfreeze” of unimaginable scale and velocity. It forced mass remote work, dismantling bureaucracy and challenging long-held assumptions about where and when work could be done. This created a collective experience of experimentation and imagination.
  • The Challenge: The current challenge is to leverage this “unfreeze” state to create lasting, positive change before organizations “refreeze” into new, potentially suboptimal habits. Leanne Cutts, former Group CMO at HSBC, described this stage as working with “wet clay,” emphasizing the need for imagination to “raise the bar.”

23.2.2 Key Lessons from the Pandemic Experience

The collective experience of the pandemic yielded several critical insights that form the foundation for redesigning work:

  • Accelerated Digital Skills: The workforce adopted new digital tools and skills with unprecedented speed, proving that rapid learning is possible.
  • Bureaucracy on the “Bonfire”: Organizations became more agile, shedding unnecessary processes and prioritizing ruthlessly. As Stanford professor Nicholas Bloom described it, this was “a bonfire of unnecessary regulations.”
  • True Flexibility is Possible: The period demonstrated that flexible work is viable for many roles, opening up opportunities to attract diverse talent and improve work-life integration.
  • The “Off” Switch is Critical: The shift to remote work highlighted the danger of an “always on” culture, which leads to longer hours and burnout. Data showed the average workday expanded by nearly 49 minutes.
  • Human Connections Matter: The experience exposed the vital, often-unseen role of human networks. While strong ties with close colleagues were maintained, the weaker ties that fuel serendipity and innovation eroded, particularly impacting the socialization of young employees.

23.3 A Four-Step Process for Redesigning Work

The book proposes a structured, four-step design process to help organizations create a unique work model—their corporate “signature”—that aligns with their specific purpose, values, and capabilities.

  1. Understand: Build a deep foundation by analyzing what truly matters for the business. This includes identifying the jobs and skills crucial for productivity, understanding how knowledge flows through human networks, and discovering what employees want and need from work.
  2. Reimagine: Use the foundation of understanding to creatively devise optimal work designs. This involves exploring the choices and trade-offs of place (office vs. home) and time (synchronous vs. asynchronous) to enhance productivity and well-being.
  3. Model and Test: Test the reimagined ideas against potential risks and key factors. A successful model must be future-proofed against demographic shifts, supportive of technological transitions, and perceived as equitable and fair by all employees.
  4. Act and Create: Embed the new model into the company’s practices and culture. This requires engaging leaders to shape the narrative, supporting the pivotal role of managers, and implementing co-creation processes that involve employees in the change.

23.4 Step 1: Understand

A successful redesign must be built on a deep, evidence-based understanding of the organization’s unique context. This involves analyzing productivity, networks, people, and the overall employee experience.

23.4.1 The Four Elements of Productivity

Productivity in knowledge work is multifaceted. Deconstructing jobs into their core tasks reveals four primary elements:

  • Energy: The vitality needed for dynamic tasks, such as managing high-pressure projects or engaging in intense meetings.
  • Focus: The deep concentration required for tasks like writing, data analysis, or strategic thinking, which demands a rested brain free from distractions.
  • Coordination: The efficient alignment with others on shared goals, including check-ins, feedback, and project management.
  • Cooperation: The participative sharing of ideas to generate new solutions and drive innovation, often in cross-functional teams.

23.4.2 Human Networks and Knowledge Flow

Work is embedded in networks of human connection through which knowledge and innovation flow. Any redesign must consider its impact on these networks.

  • Tacit vs. Explicit Knowledge: Explicit knowledge is easily codified (e.g., in manuals), while tacit knowledge (know-how, mental models) resides within trusted relationships and is harder to transfer.
  • Strong vs. Weak Ties:
    • Strong Ties: Relationships with trusted colleagues are excellent for exploiting existing tacit knowledge within a team. Proximity is a significant driver of their formation.
    • Weak Ties: Acquaintances and broader connections are crucial for distributing new information and creating “serendipitous” encounters that spark innovation. These were the connections most damaged by mass remote work.
  • Boundary Spanners: Individuals who connect otherwise separate networks play a vital role in synthesizing diverse knowledge and fostering exploration.

23.4.3 What People Want: Present and Future Needs

Understanding employee needs is critical. This can be achieved by creating “personas” (e.g., Julio, the experienced manager who loves remote work, and Mandy, the young new joiner who needs the office) and by analyzing universal trends shaping future aspirations.

  • To Stay Healthy: With people living longer, maintaining physical and mental health becomes paramount. Employees will seek work that allows for sufficient sleep, exercise, and social connection.
  • To Have a Balanced Family Life: The rise of dual-career couples and diverse family structures means employees need flexibility to manage complex personal lives.
  • To Learn (Faster Than Machines): The relentless pace of automation creates anxiety but also a powerful motivation to upskill and reskill. Organizations that support learning will attract and retain talent.
  • To Live a Multistage Life: The traditional three-stage life (education, work, retirement) is being replaced by a more flexible, multistage life where people may take sabbaticals, start side businesses, or re-enter education at various ages.

23.4.4 The Employee Experience of Work

The employee journey must be analyzed to understand “pain points” and “gain points.”

  • Case Study (HSBC): The global bank applied marketing tools to understand the employee experience. A multidisciplinary team mapped the employee journey, using data analytics and an Employee Net Promoter Score (eNPS) to identify and resolve frustrations, such as the crucial transition between graduate placements. The goal is to create an employee experience that is personalized, intuitive, and frictionless, mirroring modern consumer technology platforms.

23.5 Step 2: Reimagine

Based on a deep understanding, organizations can begin to reimagine work by making intentional choices about place and time, aiming to maximize upsides while mitigating inherent trade-offs.

23.5.1 The Core Choices of Place and Time

The fundamental design choices revolve around two axes, each with distinct benefits and drawbacks.

Axis Dimension Description Upside (+) Downside (-)
Place Co-located (e.g., office) Working together physically Boosts Cooperation through proximity and serendipity. Depletes Energy due to commutes and office distractions.
Distributed (e.g., home) Working from separate locations Boosts Energy by eliminating commutes and allowing for healthier routines. Reduces Cooperation by shrinking networks and chance encounters.
Time Synchronous Working and connected at the same time Boosts Coordination by making real-time alignment easier. Depletes Focus due to interruptions and an “always on” culture.
Asynchronous Working disconnected from others Boosts Focus by allowing for deep, uninterrupted concentration. Reduces Coordination as real-time collaboration becomes difficult.

The goal is to design work that optimizes for each element: the office for cooperation, the home for energy, asynchronous time for focus, and synchronous time for coordination.

23.5.2 Reimagining Place

  • The Office as a Place of Cooperation
    • Case Study (Fujitsu): In Japan, Fujitsu reimagined the office as a typology of spaces designed for different tasks:
      1. Neighbourhood Shared Office: Quiet, local cubicles for individual focus.
      2. Satellite Office: Suburban spaces for project teams to meet for coordination.
      3. Hub Office: Exciting city-center spaces designed to maximize cooperation and innovation.
    • Case Study (Arup): The design firm intentionally uses its Melbourne office to foster cooperation by playing with proximity (moving teams quarterly), reducing personal space to create collaborative zones, and acting as a community “node” that welcomes partners and hosts industry events.
  • The Home as a Source of Energy
    • Boundary Management: To be energizing, homeworking requires intentional “boundary work”—creating physical, cognitive, and temporal separation between work and personal life to prevent role spillover and distractions.
    • Case Study (BT & TCS): Veteran remote-work companies emphasize the need to manage performance by focusing on outcomes, not presenteeism. This builds trust and alleviates anxiety that remote workers will be overlooked for promotion.
    • Key Considerations: The redesign must address the deep gender dynamics of homeworking (where women often shoulder more domestic labor) and the complex impact on sustainability (where home energy use can offset commute savings).

23.5.3 Reimagining Time

  • Asynchronous Time for Focus
    • Human focus, unlike machine processing, is essential for future-oriented, imaginative, and creative tasks. It requires a rested brain and is compromised by multitasking.
    • Strategies: The redesign should include scheduling protected blocks of quiet time, promoting practices that support adequate sleep, and encouraging leaders to role-model unplugging to combat the “always on” culture.
  • Synchronous Time for Coordination
    • While vital, synchronous time is often overused, leading to meeting overload.
    • A Rhythm of Coordination: The most productive teams are not always connected but interact intermittently. Periods of “silence” allow for individual thought, while periods of “sound” allow for the pooling and synthesis of diverse ideas.
    • Case Study (TCS): The tech firm creates a rhythm through agile rituals like short, daily virtual “stand-ups” for immediate priorities and “retrospectives” at project ends to capture learnings.
    • Case Study (Telstra): The telecom company reinvented management by splitting the role into a “Leader of Work” (who manages scheduling and resources) and a “Leader of People” (who focuses on coaching and development), recognizing scheduling as a specialized skill.

23.6 Step 3: Model and Test

Any new work design must be tested against three critical factors: its long-term viability, its alignment with technological change, and its fundamental fairness.

23.6.1 Future-Proofing the Redesign

The model must be resilient to future demographic shifts and evolving employee aspirations.

  • Demographics: An aging and shrinking workforce in developed nations will intensify the war for talent, while a young and growing workforce in countries like Nigeria will create new talent pools. Models must support older workers and tap into global talent.
  • The Multistage Life: The model should accommodate the shift away from a linear three-stage life to a more flexible, multistage one by offering options beyond full-time, permanent employment.
  • Case Studies on Flexibility:
    • Unilever: Created “U-Work,” a “third way” employment model offering a retainer plus project pay, pension, and learning access, allowing the company to retain talent that desires more flexibility.
    • Open Banking: Utilized job-sharing for senior roles to retain top talent, improve work-life balance, and benefit from dual perspectives, which studies show can increase productivity.
    • CPP Investments: Pursuing a “golden model” to retain talent long-term, the firm offers sabbaticals and a “work from anywhere for three months” policy, aligning with its long-term investment purpose.

23.6.2 Supporting Technology Transitions

The model must facilitate the evolution of work alongside automation and AI.

  • Automation Pathways: Jobs will be affected differently by technology, with routine tasks facing substitution and non-routine analytical tasks seeing augmentation. Organizations must understand these pathways to guide employee development.
  • Case Study (NSW Public Commission): The Australian public service used data analytics to predict which of its 400,000 jobs would be automated, augmented, or newly created, allowing it to strategically realign its workforce around the customer journey and support regional development.
  • Building Human Skills: As machines handle routine tasks, uniquely human skills like empathy, context sensing, collaboration, and creative thinking become more valuable. The work environment must be low-stress to allow these skills to be developed and used.
  • Making Development Visible:
    • Case Study (IBM): The “Myca” chatbot helps employees see “escalator jobs”—roles that serve as gateways to higher-wage positions—and identifies the skills needed to make the transition.
    • Case Study (TCS): The “Knome” platform allows employees to get certified with digital “badges” for their skills and provides analytics on their internal network connections, making their collaborative capital visible.
    • Case Study (Microsoft): The company extends skill development into the community by partnering with local colleges to create data center academies, building a future talent pipeline in underserved areas.

23.6.3 Ensuring Fairness and Justice

For the redesign to succeed, it must be perceived as fair by all employees.

  • The Three Factors of Fairness:
    1. Distributive Fairness: Are the outcomes (e.g., flexibility, workload) equitable?
    2. Procedural Fairness: Was the process for making decisions consistent, unbiased, and representative?
    3. Interactional Fairness: Were people treated with respect, politeness, and honesty by their managers?
  • Addressing Inequity: The model must proactively address potential unfairness for different groups, such as primary carers, young workers, and employees whose roles do not permit remote work.
  • Case Study (Brit Insurance): To refresh its values and ways of working, the insurer created a design community by randomly selecting 10% of its workforce, from receptionists to senior underwriters. This co-creation process ensured procedural fairness, built deep knowledge across the firm, and generated employee-led ideas that had strong executive backing.

23.7 Step 4: Act and Create

The final step is to translate the tested model into reality by engaging managers, employees, and leaders.

23.7.1 The Pivotal Role of Managers

Good managers are the critical link in implementing the redesign, but they are often overloaded and under-supported. Success requires reinventing their role.

  • Managerial Mindset Shifts: Effective managers shift from being hierarchical controllers to team enablers, from hoarding to sharing talent, from managing intact teams to orchestrating fluid project work, and from overseeing presence to coaching for outcomes.
  • Case Study (Telstra): By splitting the manager’s job into “Leader of Work” and “Leader of People,” the company created space for dedicated coaching and development while ensuring work itself was expertly managed.
  • Case Study (Standard Chartered): The bank invested heavily in its 14,000 “People Leaders,” creating a community, providing development, and building a deep coaching culture by accrediting managers to coach their peers.

23.7.2 The Power of Co-Creation

Engaging employees in the design process builds buy-in and harnesses collective intelligence, proving more effective than top-down mandates or “burning platform” approaches.

  • Case Study (Ericsson): The tech multinational invited all 95,000 employees to a 75-hour moderated virtual conversation about company values and future work arrangements. This massive co-creation exercise democratized the conversation, built empathy across the organization, and surfaced grassroots experiments and champions for change.

23.7.3 The Leader’s Narrative

In times of uncertainty, the stories leaders tell are pivotal for creating meaning and direction.

  • The Nature of Narratives: Compelling narratives engage emotions, build connections, and help people visualize an achievable future. They are more powerful than data and facts alone.
  • Effective Narratives: Instead of issuing binary, final edicts, the best leaders frame the redesign as an experimental journey aligned with core values, acknowledging uncertainty and inviting co-creation.
  • Role-Modeling: The credibility of the narrative is tested by a leader’s everyday actions. Leaders must visibly model the flexibility, collaboration, and experimental mindset they wish to foster.

23.8 Case Study in Practice: The Sage Redesign Journey

The accounting-software company Sage provides a holistic example of the four-step process in action.

  1. Understand: The design team started with a data-driven approach, using employee surveys and performance data to build a case for change that aligned with the company’s strategic shift to a cloud-based model.
  2. Reimagine: They co-created with 200 employees to explore future work possibilities, leading to the establishment of four guiding principles: customer-centricity, fairness and trust, human connection, and experimentation.
  3. Model and Test: They developed four work archetypes (homeworker, flex worker, field worker, office worker) and boldly experimented with policies like a “work away” option allowing employees to work from another location for up to ten weeks.
  4. Act and Create: They created a managerial “playbook” to guide implementation, developed talking points to ensure leadership narratives were consistent, and empowered managers to create bespoke team-level agreements, all within a framework of continuous learning and iteration.
