4  Four Steps to Successful Strategy

4.1 Executive Summary

This document provides a detailed review of the “Customer Development” model for startups, contrasting it sharply with the traditional “Product Development” model. Based on the provided excerpts from Steve Blank’s “The Four Steps to the Epiphany” (2013 edition), this briefing highlights the critical importance of customer learning and discovery as a parallel, iterative process to product development. The core message is that startups fail not from a lack of product, but from a lack of customers and a proven financial model, and that a customer-centric approach is the repeatable path to success.

4.2 Main Themes and Core Concepts

4.2.1 The “Hero’s Journey” of Startups

Archetypal Path: Startups, like mythological heroes, embark on a quest with an unclear path and uncertain end. Joseph Campbell’s concept of the “Hero with a Thousand Faces” is applied to entrepreneurs, suggesting that while each journey feels unique, the underlying outline of success and failure is remarkably similar.
Hidden Roadmap: Serial entrepreneurs often recognize “an emerging pattern between our successes and failures. Namely, there is a true and repeatable path to success, a path that eliminates or mitigates the most egregious risks and allows the company to grow into a large, successful enterprise.” This book aims to chart that previously unwritten path.
Beyond Financial Resources: The entrepreneurial journey “tests more than financial resources. It tests their stamina, agility, and the limits of courage.”

4.2.2 The Flaws of the Traditional Product Development Model (The Path to Disaster)

The traditional product development model, while effective for established companies launching new products into known markets, is a “path to disaster” for most startups.

  • Focus on First Customer Ship (FCS) Date: The model forces sales and marketing to prioritize the FCS date, which is merely when “Product Development thinks they are ‘finished’ building the product.” It does not mean the company understands its customers or how to market/sell to them.
  • “Fire, Ready, Aim” Strategy: Focusing solely on FCS leads to a “Fire, Ready, Aim” approach, where companies build and ship without truly understanding who they are selling to or why customers will buy. This often results in discovering after launch that “their early customers don’t scale into a mainstream market, the product doesn’t solve a high-value problem, or the cost of distribution is too high.”
  • Emphasis on Execution Over Learning and Discovery: Startups often incorrectly assume prior experience is directly relevant, prioritizing “get it done, and get it done fast” over critical “learning and discovery activities” necessary to answer fundamental questions like “What are the problems our product solves?” and “Do customers perceive these problems as important or ‘must-have’?”
  • Lack of Meaningful Sales & Marketing Milestones: The Product Development model lacks “measurable, concrete objectives” for sales and marketing activities before FCS. Instead of focusing on deep customer understanding, it relies on execution-oriented metrics that often provide a false sense of progress.
  • Premature Scaling: Driven by business plans and revenue forecasts that assume success, startups scale their sales and marketing organizations prematurely, leading to a high “burn rate” before a viable business model is validated.
  • Death Spiral: Premature scaling, combined with a lack of customer understanding, can lead to a “Death Spiral” where sales miss targets, executives are replaced, and the company burns through cash trying to fix a fundamentally flawed strategy. Webvan, which “managed to burn through $800 million in three years,” serves as a cautionary tale.
  • “Build It and They Will Come” is a Prayer:This sentiment encapsulates the fatal flaw, highlighting that product alone is insufficient without a validated market and customer base.
  • Not All Startups Are Alike: The model ignores the fundamental truth that startups fall into four distinct Market Types (existing, new, resegmented low-cost, resegmented niche), each requiring “very different rates of customer adoption and acceptance” and dramatically different strategies and cash needs.
  • Unrealistic Expectations: The model leads to three unrealistic expectations: that it can guide non-product development activities (customer finding, market, business model), that Customer Development moves on the same schedule as Product Development, and that all startups achieve acceptance at the same rate.

Chapter 1, “The Path To Disaster: The Product Development Model,” argues that the traditional Product Development Model, while seemingly an obvious choice and widely used, often leads startups to disaster. This model, which emerged in manufacturing and spread to technology, is product-centric and assumes a well-defined market with known customers and competition. However, few startups actually fit these criteria, often not even knowing their market, yet they persist in using this model as a catchall for managing all activities, including finding customers and planning sales and revenue.

The sources highlight several critical flaws in relying solely on the Product Development Model for startups:

  • Ignores Customer and Market Development: The greatest risk for startups is not product development but customer and market development. Startups fail due to a lack of customers and a proven financial model, not a lack of product.
  • Focus on First Customer Ship Date: This model prioritizes the product launch date, leading sales and marketing to prepare for execution without first understanding customers or how to market and sell to them effectively. This can result in a “Fire, Ready, Aim” strategy where early customers don’t scale into a mainstream market, or the product doesn’t solve a high-value problem, leading to an expensive and ineffective sales organization.
  • Emphasis on Execution Over Learning: Startups using this model prioritize “getting it done fast” based on prior experience, often making faulty assumptions about customer problems and needs. This neglects the critical learning and discovery activities needed to answer fundamental questions about the product’s value and market. The process is linear, while customer and market discovery are iterative and recursive.
  • Lack of Meaningful Milestones: Unlike the clear milestones of Product Development (e.g., alpha/beta test, first customer ship), sales and marketing activities before product launch are often ad hoc, fuzzy, and lack measurable objectives. There’s no mechanism to stop and fix what’s broken in customer acquisition. True objectives should focus on a deep understanding of customer problems, willingness to pay, and a repeatable sales roadmap.
  • Premature Scaling: The pressure to staff sales and marketing to a business plan leads to premature scaling, burning through cash before customer understanding is achieved. This can result in a “Death Spiral” where missed sales targets lead to executive turnover and further financial drain.
  • Ignores Market Type Differences: The Product Development Model assumes all startups are alike, but different Market Types (existing, new, resegmented low-cost, resegmented niche) have vastly different customer adoption rates, sales/marketing strategies, and cash needs. This model is not only useless but dangerous for most startups, as it provides no guidance on how to uniquely sell or predict resources for each Market Type.
  • Unrealistic Expectations: It creates unrealistic expectations that the model can guide non-product development activities, that Customer Development will match Product Development’s schedule, and that all startups achieve acceptance at the same rate.

4.3 The Alternative: Technology Life Cycle Adoption Curve

Recognizing the failures of the Product Development Model, the sources discuss the “Technology Life Cycle Adoption Curve,” developed by Everett Rogers and popularized by Geoff Moore with the concept of “The Chasm,” as a potential alternative for startup sales and marketing.

4.3.1 This curve introduces five key ideas:

  1. Phased Adoption: Technology is adopted in phases by distinct groups: technology enthusiasts, visionaries, pragmatists, conservatives, and skeptics.
  2. Early vs. Mainstream Market: The first two groups (technology enthusiasts and visionaries) constitute the “early market,” while the next two (pragmatists and conservatives) make up the “mainstream market.”
  3. Bell Curve Shape: The overall market for any product approximates a bell curve, with the early market starting small and growing exponentially into the mainstream.
  4. Chasms Between Groups: There are “chasms” between each of these different groups, with the largest one occurring between the early and mainstream markets.
  5. Different Needs & Strategies: These chasms are caused by the distinct product needs and buying habits of each group. To cross the chasm into the mainstream market, completely new marketing and sales strategies are necessary, as early marketing lessons often cannot be leveraged.

4.3.2 Critique of the Technology Life Cycle Adoption Curve for Early-Stage Startups

Despite offering insightful distinctions between customer types, the sources argue that the Technology Life Cycle Adoption Curve does not provide a good roadmap for early-stage startups. The problems faced by new companies occur much earlier than any “chasm”. The curve can lead early-stage entrepreneurs to four “bad conclusions”:

  1. Premature Mainstream Dreams: It encourages dreams of mainstream market glory, which are best forgotten in the early stages. If the initial customer development isn’t handled correctly, a startup won’t survive to reach the mainstream.
  2. Misunderstanding Technology Enthusiasts: Technology enthusiasts, while depicted as an early customer segment, are often not “real” paying customers. They are part of a sales puzzle but rarely buy anything, making feedback from them potentially misleading for sales strategies.
  3. False Assumption of Smooth Adoption: The idea that a customer base will grow in a smooth, continuous curve leads to the dangerous notion that adoption is simply a sales execution problem. Even with the chasm concept, the transition between customer types is often a “step function” and highly dependent on the specific Market Type.
  4. Misplaced Focus on Execution: The curve and related literature emphasize “execution and adoption.” However, for early-stage startups, focusing on execution prematurely can lead to failure. Instead, a “learning and discovery” process is required to understand what to execute effectively.

Ultimately, the sources conclude that instead of fixating on how to “cross the chasm,” the initial step for a startup must be to focus on learning and discovery processes. This led to the invention of the “Customer Development” model, a parallel, customer- and market-centric process designed to complement Product Development and address its inherent flaws for startups.

4.4 The Customer Development Model (The Path to Epiphany)

The Customer Development model is presented as a “companion” to Product Development, designed to address its shortcomings by focusing on “customer learning and discovery.”

  • Core Premise: “Learning and discovering who a company’s initial customers will be, and what markets they are in, requires a separate and distinct process from Product Development.”
  • Four Iterative Steps: The model consists of four, recursive steps, emphasizing that progress is rarely linear:
    • Customer Discovery: “Finding out who the customers for your product are and whether the problem you believe you are solving is important to them.” This involves “getting ‘outside the building’” to test initial hypotheses about the product, customers, and market.
    • Customer Validation: “To build a repeatable sales roadmap for the sales and marketing teams that will follow later.” This step validates the business model by finding paying customers and a repeatable sales process.
    • Customer Creation: “Its goal is to create end-user demand and drive that demand into the company’s sales channel.” This step is where heavy marketing spending begins, after validation.
    • Company Building: “Where the company transitions from its informal, learning and discovery-oriented Customer Development team into formal departments with VPs of Sales, Marketing and Business Development.”
  • Iterative Nature (“It’s OK to Screw It Up”): Each step is circular, with “recursive arrows,” acknowledging that “finding the right customers and market is unpredictable, and we will screw it up several times before we get it right.” Going backward is “a natural and valuable part of learning and discovery.”
  • Low Cash-Burn Rate: The iterative nature of Customer Discovery and Validation keeps the startup’s “cash-burn rate low until the company has validated its business model by finding paying customers.”
  • Synchronization with Product Development: Customer Development and Product Development are “parallel processes” that “must remain synchronized and operate in concert.” However, unlike large companies where product specs are market-driven, in startups, “the marketing is product-driven,” with Customer Development seeking a market for the initial product vision.
  • Deliverables: Each step has “clear, concise deliverables” to provide “incontrovertible proof that progress is being made on the customer front.”

Within this context of iterative learning and adaptation, the sources explicitly state that the Customer Development model “shares many features with a U.S. war-fighting strategy known as the “OODA Loop” articulated by John Boyd and adopted by the U.S. armed forces in the second Gulf War”

4.5 Customer Discovery in Detail (Phase 1 of Customer Development)

Customer Discovery is the foundational first step, aiming to turn founders’ “initial hypotheses about their business model, market and customers into facts.”

  • Develop Product for the Few, Not the Many: Startups should focus on “early evangelists”—“a”“special breed of customer willing to take a risk on your startup’s product or service because they can envision its potential to solve a critical and immediate problem—and they have the budget to purchase it.”
  • Earlyvangelist Characteristics: These customers:
    • Have a problem.
    • Understand they have a problem.
    • Are actively searching for a solution and have a timetable.
    • Have cobbled together an interim solution (indicating high pain).
    • Have or can acquire a budget.
  • Start Development Based on Vision: The initial product specification comes from the founders’ vision. The Customer Development team’s job is to “take the product as spec’d and search to see if there are customers—any customers—who will buy the product exactly as you have defined it.” Feature requests are “by exception rather than rule.”
  • Customer Discovery Phases:
    • Phase 0: Get Buy-In: Ensure all stakeholders (board, execs) understand and commit to the iterative Customer Development process, Market Types, and the company’s mission and core values.
      • Startups that are entering an existing market
      • Startups that are creating an entirely new market
      • Startups that want to resegment an existing market as a low-cost entrant.
      • Startups that want to resegment an existing market as a niche player
  • Phase 1: State Your Hypotheses: Document initial assumptions for product, customer/problem, channel/pricing, demand creation, market type, and competition in one- or two-page briefs. These are “educated guesses” to be tested.
    • Product Hypotheses: Features, benefits, intellectual property, dependency analysis, delivery schedule, total cost of ownership/adoption.
      • Customer Hypotheses: Types of customers (end users, influencers, recommenders, economic buyers, decision-makers, saboteurs), customer problems (latent, active, vision), a “day in the life” of the customer, organizational/influence map, ROI justification, and minimum feature set.
      • Channel and Pricing Hypotheses: Initial distribution channel (direct, online, retail, etc.) and product pricing. Focus on “lifetime value” of a customer.
      • Demand Creation Hypotheses: How customers will learn about the product and key industry influencers.
      • Market Type Hypotheses: A provisional decision on whether the company is entering an existing, creating a new, or resegmenting an existing market (low-cost or niche).
      • Competitive Hypotheses: Analysis of existing competitors, their market share, and how the startup’s product is differentiated.
    • Phase 2: Test and Qualify Your Hypotheses: “Leave the safe confines of your office” to test assumptions with at least 50 potential customers. Use “reference stories” and a “problem presentation” to elicit feedback, not to sell. The goal is “in-depth customer understanding” and “market knowledge.”
    • Phase 3: Test and Qualify the Product Concept: Return to the company for a “reality check” with Product Development, then create a “product presentation” (focused on solution, not fluff) to reconfirm product-problem fit with customers. Identify potential advisory board members.
    • Phase 4: Verify: Summarize findings by “verifying the problem,” “verifying the product solution,” and “verifying the business model” based on customer feedback. This includes updating sales, revenue, and product plans.
  • Iterate or Exit: If the hypotheses are not sufficiently validated, the company must “iterate” (go back through phases, potentially changing product configuration or targeting different markets) or “exit” (pivot or shut down).

4.6 Customer Types in the Technology Life Cycle Adoption Curve

The Technology Life Cycle Adoption Curve, developed by Everett Rogers and refined by Geoff Moore, introduces entrepreneurs to five distinct groups that adopt technology in phases:

  1. Technology Enthusiasts: These are the first to adopt technology. However, for early-stage startups, they are often not “real” paying customers and feedback from them can be misleading for sales strategies. They are part of a “sales puzzle” but rarely buy anything.
  2. Visionaries: Along with technology enthusiasts, visionaries constitute the “early market”.
  3. Pragmatists: These, along with conservatives, form the “mainstream market”.
  4. Conservatives: They are part of the “mainstream market”.

Earlyvangelists: The Most Important Customers You’ll Ever Know

4.7 Customer Types in the Sales Process (Corporate and Consumer)

When forming customer hypotheses, the sources detail various “customer types” involved in a purchase decision, emphasizing that “customer” is often more complicated than a single individual. These types influence the sales process and exist for both corporate and consumer products:

  • End Users: These are the daily users of the product. While crucial to understand their needs, they may have the least influence in complex sales processes (e.g., children using a product but parents being the buyers).
  • Influencers: Individuals who have a stake in a product coming into their company or home (e.g., an IT expert or a child’s preferences for consumer products).
  • Recommenders: These individuals can “make or break a sale” by influencing product purchase decisions, differing from influencers in their stronger impact (e.g., a department head recommending a specific PC brand or a spouse’s strong brand preference).
  • Economic Buyer: The individual who holds the budget for the purchase and must approve the expenditure. This could be a corporate executive or a consumer with a specific budget (e.g., a teenager’s music budget or a spouse’s vacation budget). Identifying this person is critical.
  • Decision-maker: The person with the ultimate authority over a product purchase, regardless of other users, influencers, recommenders, or economic buyers. This could be a suburban parent or a Fortune 500 CEO, depending on the product.
  • Saboteurs: Individuals or organizations “wedded to the status quo” who may be threatened by a new product’s impact on their department’s stability, headcount, or budget. Startups need to predict who these might be and develop strategies to neutralize or even convert their influence.

For consumer products that are luxury items or don’t address a direct “problem,” identifying customer types involves understanding their emotional wants and desires and how to convince them of an “emotional payoff” for their discretionary spending.

4.8 Problem Recognition Scale for Customers

To understand customer problems, the sources introduce a “problem recognition scale” that categorizes customers based on their awareness and motivation:

  • Latent need: Customers have a problem but may not fully understand it, or they understand it but are not yet motivated to act beyond “papering over the symptoms”. These customers are typically “late adopters”.
  • Active need: Customers recognize a problem, are “in pain,” and are actively searching for a solution, but have not yet committed serious effort to solving it.
  • Vision: Customers have a clear idea of what a solution looks like, may have already “cobbled together a homegrown solution,” and are “prepared to pay for a better solution”. This indicates a “mission-critical” problem for businesses or a “must-have” need for consumers.

4.9 Key Takeaways and Actionable Insights

  • Customer-Centricity is Paramount: Startups must prioritize understanding customers and their problems before scaling operations or aggressively marketing.
  • “Get Out of the Building”: The facts about customers reside outside the office. Direct engagement is non-negotiable for validating hypotheses.
  • Embrace Iteration and Pivots: Expect to be wrong. The Customer Development model is explicitly designed for continuous learning, adaptation, and course correction (pivots).
  • Focus on Earlyvangelists: Identify and serve “earlyvangelists” who have a critical, immediate, and budgeted problem that your product can solve. Their feedback is invaluable for early iteration.
  • Low Burn Rate Until Validation: Conserve cash by deferring significant sales, marketing, and business development hires until the business model is validated through customer purchases.
  • Market Type Matters: Recognize which of the four Market Types your startup falls into, as this fundamentally dictates sales, marketing, and financial strategies.
  • Synchronization is Key: Foster continuous, constructive dialogue between Product Development and Customer Development to ensure the product evolves in concert with validated customer needs.
  • Problem-Solution Fit First: Don’t sell features; understand customer pain and confirm your product solves a “must-have” problem for which customers are willing to pay.
  • Quantify Value (ROI): Understand and articulate the quantifiable benefits (ROI for businesses, emotional payoff for consumers) your product delivers.

4.10 Introduction: The Customer Development Model

The core philosophy presented in these sources emphasizes that startups are not smaller versions of large companies. Traditional business models and marketing strategies are often detrimental to startups. Instead, a Customer Development Model is proposed, which prioritizes learning and discovery over execution in the early stages. This model comprises four main steps: Customer Discovery, Customer Validation, Customer Creation, and Company Building.

The critical insight is that “a startup needs to learn and discover as early as possible” and that “there should be no serious spending in marketing until the company has a proven and repeatable sales roadmap.”

4.11 Customer Validation: Proving Product-Market Fit

A. The InLook Case Study: A Cautionary Tale
The story of InLook, a startup that raised $8 million, serves as a prime example of the pitfalls of neglecting Customer Validation.

  • Premature Scaling of Sales: InLook hired an 11-person sales team and planned to double it without any revenue, based on a “phantom sales forecast.” As the text states, “In total, InLook had an 11-person team working on sales, and no revenue to date. Bob’s budget called for doubling the sales team by the end of the year.”
  • Lack of Customer Contact by CEO: Chip Stevens, the CEO, had stopped talking to customers, deferring to his VP of Sales, Bob. This led to a critical disconnect from customer reality.
  • Unvalidated Sales Forecasts: Bob assured Chip of “imminent orders” from beta customers, which turned out to be false. When Chip personally called these accounts, he learned the product “wasn’t mission critical enough for their company to justify the disruption.”
  • Absence of a Sales Roadmap: The sales team lacked a standardized process, and marketing efforts were inconsistent. Chip realized, “InLook still did not have a clue about how to sell Snapshot. Worse, there was no process in place to learn how to sell, just a hope that smart salespeople would ‘find their way.’”
  • Consequences: Chip dramatically slashed the burn rate by firing the VP of Sales and seven sales/marketing staff, then personally went “out to the field to discover what would make a customer buy.”

4.12 Core Principles of Customer Validation

  • Learning over Selling: “In the Customer Validation step, you are not going to staff a sales team. You are not going to execute to a sales plan… You simply do not know enough to do any of these things.” The goal is to find a scalable and repeatable sales process and business model, not merely generate revenue.
  • CEO/Founders’ Involvement: Delegating Customer Validation solely to the VP of Sales is a “fatal” mistake. “At a minimum, the company’s founders and CEO need to be out in front of customers at least through the first iteration of the Customer Validation step.”
  • Focus on Earlyvangelists: Startups must target “visionary customers” who are “willing to make a leap of faith and buy from a startup,” even an unfinished product. These “earlyvangelists” not only understand their problem but have actively sought and even tried to build their own solutions. They prefer to “see and hear the founders and the technical team.”
  • Validation through Purchase: “Giveaways do not prove customers will buy your product. The only valid way to test your assumptions is to sell the product.” Alpha and beta tests are engineering functions, not sales validation.
  • Sales Roadmap: A crucial output of this phase is a “proven and tested sales roadmap” that answers fundamental questions like
    • Are we sure we have product/market fit?
    • Who influences a sale?
    • Who is the decision-maker?
    • How many sales calls are needed per sale?
  • Pricing Strategy: For earlyvangelists, the goal is to sell for “as near to list price as you can.” Flexibility on terms (e.g., payment on delivery) is acceptable, but “be tougher on discounts” to validate the product’s perceived value.
  • Win/Loss Statistics: Understanding “why a customer said ‘no’ is more important in this step than understanding why a customer said ‘yes’.”

4.13 Phases of Customer Validation

  1. Get Ready to Sell: Articulate a value proposition, prepare preliminary sales materials (low cost, low volume), develop a preliminary distribution channel and sales roadmap, hire a “sales closer” (not a VP of Sales), align executives on product commitments, and formalize advisory boards (technical, business, customer, industry).

  2. Sell to Visionary Customers: Attempt to sell an unfinished product to 3-5 earlyvangelists to refine the sales roadmap and distribution channel plan. This phase is about learning, where “failures are as important as successes.”

  3. Develop Positioning: Based on customer feedback and market type, create initial positioning statements for both the product and the company. Present these to industry pundits and analysts for feedback.

  4. Verify: Confirm product-market fit, validate the sales roadmap and channel plan, ensure a profitable business model, and assess if enough has been learned to proceed to Customer Creation. If not, “Iterate, Return, or Exit.”

4.14 Customer Creation: Shaping Market Perception

4.14.1 The PhotosToYou Case Study: Branding Pitfalls in a New Market

PhotosToYou, an online digital photo printing service, serves as a cautionary tale of misaligned Customer Creation strategy.

  • Misunderstanding Market Type: The company believed it needed a branding strategy, typical for existing markets, but it was in a “new market” where digital camera and broadband adoption rates limited its customer base.
  • Premature, Expensive Campaigns: PhotosToYou embarked on a “national advertising campaign” and “large and expensive corporate identity program.” The text states, “PhotosToYou burned through its large pile of venture money in the first two years.”
  • Focus on Market Share over Adoption: The executives aimed to “drive end-user demand to their website and grab market share,” when the first-year goal in a new market should be “fostering market adoption, not market share.”
  • Consequences: High customer acquisition costs, customer churn, and ultimately a “cram-down” financing round where the company was valued at one-tenth of its previous value, leading to the replacement of the entire executive staff.

4.14.2 Market Type is Paramount

  • Different Strategies for Different Markets: “The types of appropriate marketing programs differ widely, depending on Market Type.” There isn’t “one-size fits all” for positioning, launch, or demand creation.
  • Four Market Types:
    • Existing Market: Clear competitors, known customer needs.
      • Strategy: “take as much market share as possible from incumbents,” focus on product differentiation (faster, cheaper, better).
      • Launch: Onslaught Launch (full-frontal assault using all demand-creation tools).
    • Resegmented Low End/Niche Market: Targeting a specific underserved segment within an existing market.
      • Strategy: educate customers about new issues, create demand while segmenting the market.
      • Launch: Niche Launch (all demand-creation dollars into acquiring a single identifiable market segment).
    • New Market: Customers couldn’t do something before your product existed. Strategy: “long-term customer education and adoption,” drive adoption of the market itself.
      • Launch: Early Adopter Launch (targeted, low-cost approach to prepare for mass market, gain “mind share” not market share).
  • New Lanchester Strategy: Provides rules for analyzing existing markets and the cost of entry.
    • For example, “If you decide to attack a market that has just one dominant player, be prepared to spend three times the combined sales and marketing budget of that dominant player.”
  • “First Mover Advantage” Fallacy: The idea that being first to market guarantees success is “simply not true.” Early market leaders (who enter early but not necessarily first) have greater long-term success.

4.14.3 Building Blocks of Customer Creation

  • Year One Objectives: Goals differ drastically by Market Type (e.g., market share in an existing market vs. market adoption in a new market).
  • Positioning: Both company and product positioning must align with Market Type.
    • Existing Market: Focus on being “different and credible” and differentiating along existing competition axes.
    • New Market: Communicate a “vision and passion of what could be” and solve a problem, rather than touting features.
    • Resegmented Market: Highlight the value of the market segment and the company’s innovation.
  • Launch: Three types of launches corresponding to Market Type.
    • Onslaught Launch: For existing markets, expensive, maximum exposure.
    • Early Adopter Launch: For new markets, targeted, low-cost, focuses on education and “tipping point.”
    • Niche Launch: For resegmented markets, all demand-creation into a single identifiable segment.
  • Demand Creation: Marketing activities (PR, advertising, trade shows) to drive customer awareness and desire. Goals must be “in sync” with sales goals.

4.14.4 Critical Components

  • Messengers: Identify and educate “Experts, evangelists, and connectors” (e.g., analysts, happy earlyvangelists, bloggers) to convey messages.
  • Memorable Messages: Craft “memorable and ‘sticky’” messages that resonate with the target audience and consider the “context” in which they are received.
  • Measurement: Establish specific success criteria for launches and demand creation efforts (e.g., leads, awareness, press coverage) and “course correct if necessary.”

4.15 Company Building: Scaling for Mainstream Success

4.15.1 The BetaSheet Case Study: Founder Misalignment

Mark, the founder and CEO of BetaSheet, a drug discovery company, faced challenges similar to InLook but at a later stage, highlighting issues in scaling management and culture.

  • Founder’s Strengths Become Weaknesses: Mark was a “passionate visionary,” successful in early sales, but struggled with scaling and process. He “thrived on chaos” but the company needed to “move beyond chaos.”
  • Resistance to Process: Mark “derisively rejected all of her proposals on how to put processes in place.”
  • Lack of Shared Vision: New hires were “hired guns” rather than committed owners, and Mark failed to evangelize his vision internally. His focus on “the next great vision” confused the sales team and product strategy.
  • Executive Exodus: The executive staff threatened to resign en masse due to Mark’s micromanagement and “new idea a day” approach, leading to his removal as CEO.
  • Consequences of Losing Innovation DNA: After Mark’s departure, the new, process-oriented CEO successfully grew the company for 18 months. However, BetaSheet later stumbled as competitors emerged and the company “lost the corporate DNA for innovation, and learning and discovery.” It ultimately shut down.

4.15.2 The Company-Building Philosophy

  • Beyond Chaos and Rigidity: A successful startup needs a “third alternative” to “startup chaos and corporate rigidity.”
  • Three Stages of Organizational Evolution:
    • Customer Development Team: Focused on learning and discovery (Customer Discovery, Validation, Creation).
  • Mission-Centric Organization: Scales up to cross the chasm, agile, entrepreneurial speed with more people (Company Building).
    • Process-Centric Organization: For large companies, builds repeatable and scalable processes.
  • Founder Continuity: Contrary to conventional wisdom, “The long-term success of a startup requires founder continuity long past the point when conventional wisdom says the founders should be replaced.” The challenge is for founders to adapt their management style.
  • Chasm Crossing: Geoffrey Moore’s “chasm” concept describes the gap in sales revenue between earlyvangelists and mainstream customers. Its width depends on Market Type. “Moore’s chasm-crossing strategy builds on what was learned from these early customers to develop a much larger, mainstream customer base.”

4.15.3 Phases of Company Building

  • Reach Mainstream Customers: Manage the transition from earlyvangelists, understanding how this differs by Market Type and how it shapes the sales-growth curve.
    • New Market: Widest chasm, “hockey-stick” sales growth (long flat period, then exponential). Strategies include “crossing the chasm” (niche markets) and “tipping point.” Requires “husbanding resources and passionately evangelizing and growing the market.”
    • Existing Market: Small or non-existent chasm, linear sales growth. Focus on “relentless execution and exploitation of the market,” positioning for differentiation. “Positioning can be considered successful when customers not only recognize the product or service but can recite its attributes.”
    • Resegmented Market: Moderate chasm, combining new and existing market challenges. Requires “branding and positioning to turn a small cadre of early evangelists into a mass market.”
  • Review Management and Build a Mission-Centric Organization: Assess if the current CEO and executive staff can scale. Develop a “mission-centric culture and organization.”
    • Board’s Role: Crucial for appraising the executive team and potentially guiding a transition for founders (e.g., Mark at BetaSheet).
    • Corporate Mission Statement: “For you and your company, not your customers.” Action-oriented, provides daily guidance, includes revenue and profit goals.
  • Transition the Customer Development Team into Functional Departments: Transform the mission-oriented culture into departments (Sales, Marketing, Business Development) aggregated by specific business functions.
    • Departmental Mission Statements: Each department crafts statements aligned with the corporate mission and Market Type (e.g., Marketing’s mission in an existing market: “to create end-user demand and drive it into the sales channel”).
    • Departmental Roles by Market Type: Roles shift from creativity/strategy to execution/scale. Business Development focuses on building the “whole product” through partnerships.
  • Build Fast-Response Departments: Implement decentralized decision-making and the OODA loop (Observe, Orient, Decide, Act) to maintain agility, speed, and responsiveness.
    • Observe: This stage involves information gathering and dissemination as a core part of the department’s culture.
      • Orient: This stage focuses on developing a culture within the department that promotes a deep understanding of the market, customers, and competitors. It includes a dispassionate process for reviewing both the company’s own products and those of competitors.
      • Decide: In this stage, individual managers and executives are empowered to make independent decisions. These decisions are made within the context of the overall corporate and departmental missions.
      • Act: This stage is about the efficient and immediate execution of tactical decisions. Actions are synchronized across departments to ensure mutual support for departmental missions and alignment with the corporate mission.
    • Mission Intention: Employees understand not just what to do, but why (the desired result).
    • Employee Initiative: Employees are empowered to act on their own authority within the mission context.
    • Mutual Trust and Communication: Essential for information flow, especially bad news, and for internal discipline.
    • “Good Enough” Decision-Making: Rapid decision-making with acceptable risk, rather than waiting for perfect solutions.
    • Mission Synchronization: Regular peer-wise, cross-departmental meetings to ensure alignment.
    • Information Gathering & Dissemination: Executives need first-hand knowledge, a bird’s-eye view (synthesized data), and the ability to see through the eyes of customers/competitors. Information, especially bad news, must be shared rapidly.
    • Leadership Culture: Managers inspire, guide, and support employees to exercise initiative and responsibility, potentially transforming “superstars” into coaches and role models.
