6  Primal Leadership

6.1 The Four Resonant Leadership Styles

The following four leadership styles—Visionary, Coaching, Affiliative, and Democratic—are powerful tools for creating resonance. When applied correctly, they build a positive emotional climate that boosts individual and team performance.

6.1.1 The Visionary Style

Generally the most effective of the six styles, the Visionary approach has the most strongly positive impact on emotional climate. Visionary leaders articulate a purpose that rings true for themselves and attune it to the shared values of the people they lead.
The Visionary style is powered by a combination of Emotional Intelligence (EI) competencies, including inspirational leadership, self-confidence, self-awareness, and empathy. The ability to sense how others feel and understand their perspectives allows the leader to articulate a truly inspirational vision. Transparency is also crucial; to be credible, leaders must genuinely believe their own visions. When it comes time to steer in a new direction, the change catalyst competence smoothes the transition.
Key behaviors of a Visionary leader include:

  • Moving people toward shared dreams. The leader articulates where the group is going but not how it will get there, setting people free to innovate, experiment, and take calculated risks.
  • Framing work within a larger vision. By showing people how their work fits into the big picture, the leader lends a clear sense not just that what they do matters, but also why.
  • Building team commitment. When individuals understand that everyone is working toward shared goals that align with their own best interests, they feel pride in belonging to the organization.

When Shawana Leroy took over a social work agency mired in bureaucratic rules, she didn’t start by changing procedures. Instead, she talked to employees one-on-one about the meaningfulness of their work and the compassion that drew them to it. By articulating a shared vision of helping poor families, she tapped into their dedication and sustained them through the difficult changes that followed, transforming the agency’s spirit.

Impact on Climate When to Use
Most strongly positive When changes require a new vision, or when a clear direction is needed.

6.1.2 The Coaching Style

The Coaching style involves a leader’s focus on helping people identify their unique strengths and weaknesses and tying those to their personal and career aspirations. Advertising legend David Ogilvy exemplified this style. When Shelley Lazarus (now CEO of Ogilvy & Mather) was a newcomer, Ogilvy spent time getting to know her life and career hopes. Decades later, she attributes her loyalty to the firm to the powerful personal bonds he forged.
This style exemplifies the EI competence of developing others, which lets a leader act as a counselor, exploring employees’ goals and values. It works hand in hand with two other competencies: emotional self-awareness and empathy. Emotional self-awareness creates leaders who are authentic and can give advice that is genuinely in the employee’s best interest. Empathy means leaders listen first, which allows the interaction to stay on target.
Core actions of a Coaching leader include:

  • Connecting personal wants with organizational goals. Through deep, one-on-one conversations, the leader establishes rapport and trust, helping an employee see how their personal aspirations connect to the company’s mission.
  • Identifying unique strengths and weaknesses. The coach helps people recognize their abilities and tie them to their career goals, encouraging them to establish long-term development plans.
  • Delegating “stretch” assignments. The leader gives employees challenging tasks that push them beyond their known abilities, tolerating short-term failure for the sake of long-term growth.
Impact on Climate When to Use
Highly positive To help an employee improve performance by building long-term capabilities.

6.1.3 The Affiliative Style

The Affiliative style revolves around creating harmony and building emotional bonds, which in turn fosters tremendous loyalty. A great example is Joe Torre, former manager of the New York Yankees. During a victorious World Series, he was credited with tending to his players’ psyches. He embraced players dealing with family tragedies and used the victory celebration to publicly praise players involved in contract disputes, signaling their value to the team’s owner.
The Affiliative style represents the teamwork and collaboration competence in action. Leaders are most concerned with promoting harmony and nurturing personal relationships. Empathy is also fundamental, as it allows a leader to care for the whole person, not just their work tasks. The style also relies on the conflict management competence when the challenge includes knitting together diverse individuals into a harmonious group.
Key characteristics of an Affiliative leader include:

  • Prioritizing emotional needs. The leader places less emphasis on tasks and goals and more on the feelings of employees, offering support during hard times.
  • Striving to keep people happy. By focusing on creating harmony, the leader fosters a positive emotional climate and improves team communication and morale.
  • Building loyalty and connectedness. By offering emotional support and recognizing employees as people, the leader strengthens relationships and builds a deep sense of belonging.

Despite its benefits, the Affiliative style should not be used alone. Its exclusive focus on praise can allow poor performance to go uncorrected, and employees may perceive that mediocrity is tolerated. Leaders who overuse this style often neglect to offer the corrective feedback that helps employees improve. Their avoidance of confrontation can derail a group, steering it toward failure. In crises or when people need clear direction, chummy but clueless leaders leave their followers rudderless.

Impact on Climate When to Use
Positive To heal rifts in a team, motivate during stressful times, or strengthen connections.

6.1.4 The Democratic Style

The Democratic style builds resonance by valuing people’s input and gaining commitment through participation. When Sister Mary was ordered to close the private Catholic school she managed, she didn’t simply lock the doors. She called meetings with teachers, staff, and parents, explained the financial crisis, and asked for their ideas. By allowing everyone to reach the difficult decision collectively, she avoided backlash and ensured that people, while sad, understood the outcome was inevitable.
This style builds on a triad of EI abilities: teamwork and collaboration, conflict management, and influence. The best democratic leaders are superb listeners who create a sense that they truly want to hear employees’ thoughts. They are true collaborators who work as team members rather than top-down leaders. Empathy also plays a role, especially with a diverse group, allowing the leader to attune to a wide range of people and avoid miscues.
Essential behaviors of a Democratic leader include:

  • Listening to concerns and ideas. The leader spends significant time listening to what employees have to say, making them feel heard and valued.
  • Building trust through involvement. By including people in the decisions that affect their work, the leader builds commitment and respect.
  • Keeping morale high. The simple act of asking for and listening to input lets people know their contributions are valued, which has a positive impact on the work environment.

Of course, the democratic style has its drawbacks. An overreliance on this approach can lead to endless meetings in which ideas are mulled over but consensus remains elusive, risking dithering and confusion. It is also wrong-headed in times of crisis, when urgent events demand on-the-spot decisions, or when seeking input from employees who are uninformed or incompetent.

Impact on Climate When to Use
Positive To build buy-in or consensus, or to get valuable input from employees.

While these four styles are proven resonance-builders, two other styles must be applied with caution, as they can easily create dissonance if used improperly.

6.2 The Two Dissonant Leadership Styles: (Apply with Caution)

The Pacesetting and Commanding styles are useful in very specific situations. However, they can easily crush morale and create dissonance if they are overused or applied poorly.

6.3 The Pacesetting Style

The Pacesetting style is defined by a leader who is obsessive about doing things better and faster, and asks the same of everyone. While this sounds admirable, it often poisons the climate.
But if applied poorly or excessively, or in the wrong setting, the pacesetting approach can leave employees feeling pushed too hard by the leader’s relentless demands… The result is that morale plummets…
Common behaviors of a Pacesetting leader include:

  • Exemplifying high standards. The leader is obsessive about doing things better and faster and asks the same of everyone around them.
  • Pinpointing poor performers. The pacesetter is quick to identify those not meeting their high standards and demands more from them.
  • Taking over for others. Believing they can do it better, the pacesetter often steps in when a subordinate’s performance slackens, showing a lack of trust and undermining employee confidence.

This style’s limitations are illustrated by the story of Sam, an R&D biochemist whose superb technical expertise made him an early star. Promoted to lead a team, his pacesetting style worked well with colleagues who were as competent and self-motivated as he was. But when promoted again to head the entire R&D division, his leadership failed. He didn’t trust his subordinates, refused to delegate, and micromanaged obsessively. He eventually returned to his old job, a star performer who had failed as a leader because his repertoire was too narrow.
The Pacesetting style is rooted in two key EI competencies: the drive to achieve and initiative. However, this drive can go awry when it arises in the absence of other crucial EI abilities. The most glaring lacks are often empathy and emotional self-management. This deficit leaves the leader oblivious to the rising distress of their team and manifests as impatience or micromanaging.

Impact on Climate When to Use
Because too frequently poorly executed, often highly negative To get high-quality results from a motivated and competent team.

6.4 The Commanding Style

The Commanding style is the least effective in most situations and can have a devastating impact on an organization’s climate, creating a “reign of terror” that erodes people’s spirits and commitment.
With a motto of “Do it because I say so,” such leaders demand immediate compliance with orders, but don’t bother explaining the reasons behind them.

Core characteristics of a Commanding leader include:

  • Demanding immediate compliance. The leader issues orders without explanation and often resorts to threats.
  • Seeking tight control. Rather than delegating authority, the leader monitors situations studiously and attempts to control every detail.
  • Focusing on negative feedback. The leader relies on criticism, rarely offers praise, and focuses on what people did wrong.

Despite its negative inclinations, the Commanding style can be used effectively to signal a major cultural shift. When one executive was brought in to change a money-losing division, he encountered a top management team that met in a formal, intimidating conference room with a marble table that “looked like the deck of the Starship Enterprise.” The setting stifled dialogue. To signal a shift toward openness, the new president had the room demolished—a clear command-style move with positive effects. His strong tactics worked because he attacked the old culture, not the people.
Effective execution of the Commanding style draws on three EI competencies: influence, achievement, and initiative. But to keep the style from going off track, a leader also needs emotional self-control to keep anger and impatience in check, as well as empathy to avoid running roughshod over people’s reactions. When a leader lacks these abilities, the style runs amok, and the dictatorial leader barks orders, oblivious to the emotional damage.

Impact on Climate When to Use
Because so often misused, highly negative In a crisis, to kick-start a turnaround, or with problem employees.

While these dissonant styles have their place, the key to great leadership is knowing that no single style is a silver bullet.

3. Conclusion: Building Your Leadership Repertoire
The most important lesson from this guide is that the best leaders do not rely on a single style. They are versatile, capable of adapting their approach to fit the needs of the moment.
The six leadership styles are like “the array of clubs in a golf pro’s bag.” Over the course of a match, the pro senses the challenge of each shot, swiftly pulls out the right tool, and puts it to work. High-impact leaders operate the same way, seamlessly switching between styles depending on the people and the business situation.
Research shows that leaders who have mastered four or more styles—especially the resonant ones of Visionary, Coaching, Affiliative, and Democratic—foster the very best climate and achieve the strongest business performance. By expanding your own repertoire, you can learn to lead with resonance and get powerful results.

6.6 Executive Summary

This document synthesizes the core principles of Primal Leadership, a model that posits the fundamental task of a leader is emotional. Great leadership operates through the emotions, creating “resonance”—a reservoir of positivity that frees the best in people. Conversely, ineffective leadership creates “dissonance,” undermining performance by driving emotions negatively. The vehicle for this is Emotional Intelligence (EI), a set of learnable competencies that are more critical to leadership success than IQ or technical skills.

The model is grounded in neuroanatomy, specifically the “open-loop” nature of the brain’s limbic system, which makes humans susceptible to emotional contagion. Leaders, being the center of group attention, have a disproportionate impact on the collective emotional state, or climate. Research indicates that climate can account for 20-30% of business performance, and that 50-70% of climate is attributable to the leader’s actions.

Effective leaders master a repertoire of six leadership styles—four resonant (Visionary, Coaching, Affiliative, Democratic) and two dissonant (Pacesetting, Commanding)—and deploy them flexibly according to the situation. Developing these styles and their underlying EI competencies is a process of self-directed learning, which involves five key “discoveries”: identifying one’s ideal self, understanding one’s real self, creating a learning agenda, practicing new behaviors, and leveraging supportive relationships.

Finally, creating a resonant organization requires extending EI beyond the individual to teams and the organizational culture. This transformation begins with confronting the group’s emotional reality and ingrained norms, then attuning people to a shared ideal vision. Sustainable change requires a systemic process, driven from the top, that rewires not just individuals but the entire organizational fabric.

6.7 Part I: The Core Principles of Primal Leadership

6.7.1 The Primal Task of Leadership: Managing Emotions

  • Resonance: When a leader drives emotions positively, they create resonance. This is a state of synchrony where people feel “in synch” or on the same emotional wavelength. Resonance amplifies and prolongs positive emotional impact, minimizes static in interactions, and builds the emotional glue that commits people to a team and an organization.
  • Dissonance: When a leader drives emotions negatively, they spawn dissonance. Dissonant leaders are out of touch with the feelings of a group and create an emotionally discordant environment. Dissonance leads to preoccupation with collective distress, undermining the foundations that allow people to perform at their best.

The emotional impact of a leader has tangible business consequences. A positive climate—driven by a resonant leader—correlates with higher morale, motivation, commitment, and retention of talent. For every 1% improvement in the service climate, there is a 2% increase in revenue.

6.7.2 The Neurological Basis: The Open-Loop Limbic System

Leadership’s emotional impact is rooted in the design of the human brain. The limbic system, the brain’s emotional center, is an open-loop system, meaning it depends on external sources—namely, connections with other people—for its stability. This contrasts with closed-loop systems like the circulatory system, which are self-regulating.

6.7.2.1 The Primal Impact of Leader’s Moods

The concept of Primal Leadership posits that the fundamental task of leaders is emotional. Success depends not merely on what leaders set out to do—such as creating strategy or mobilizing teams—but fundamentally on how they do it. The impact of a leader’s moods and styles is central to this paradigm, as they are the primary determinants of the organizational climate and, consequently, business performance.

  1. Emotional Contagion: The open-loop system means that individuals rely on connections with others for their own emotional stability, a process known as “interpersonal limbic regulation”. Because emotions spread irresistibly whenever people are near one another, a leader’s emotional state is contagious and influences the collective emotional “soup” of the group. Everyone watches the boss and takes their emotional cues from the top, causing a ripple effect throughout the company.
  2. Performance Driver: A leader has the maximal power to sway everyone’s emotions. Leaders “manage meaning” for a group, offering an interpretation of a situation to guide the collective emotional reaction.
    • If leaders drive emotions toward enthusiasm, performance can soar. Upbeat moods boost cooperation, fairness, and overall business performance.
    • Conversely, if leaders drive emotions toward rancor and anxiety, people are thrown off stride. Negative emotions, especially chronic anger or anxiety, powerfully disrupt work by hijacking attention from the task at hand.
    • Emotional climate, which is heavily influenced by the leader, can account for 20 to 30 percent of business performance. Specifically, roughly 50 to 70 percent of how employees perceive their organization’s climate can be traced directly to the actions of the leader.

This emotional impact determines whether a leader creates resonance or dissonance.

  • Resonance (Positive Impact): This occurs when a leader is attuned to people’s feelings and moves them in a positive direction, generating a “reservoir of positivity”. Resonance refers to “synchronous vibration” where people are emotionally “in synch” with the leader. Emotionally intelligent (EI) leaders naturally achieve this, leading to mutual comfort, emotional bonds, and engaged followers.
  • Dissonance (Negative Impact): This occurs when a leader fails to empathize or accurately read emotions, undermining the emotional foundation of the workplace. Dissonant leadership results in emotionally discordant groups where people feel off-balance and perform poorly. Leaders who spread bad moods are simply “bad for business”. Toxic leadership, such as from abusive tyrants or manipulative sociopaths, can poison the emotional climate of a workplace.

6.7.2.2 The Impact of Leadership Styles

A leader’s chosen style—a set of coordinated activities drawing on underlying emotional intelligence capabilities—directly affects the organizational climate and measurable financial results. The best leaders typically utilize a flexible repertoire of four or more styles.

6.7.2.2.1 Resonance-Building Styles (Apply Freely)

The sources detail four styles that consistently foster resonance and boost performance:

  1. Visionary: This style moves people toward shared dreams and has the most strongly positive impact on climate. Visionary leaders articulate where a group is going, allowing followers the freedom to innovate. It relies heavily on inspirational leadership, self-confidence, self-awareness, empathy, and transparency. It is especially effective when a clear direction or fresh vision is needed, such as during a turnaround.
  2. Coaching: This style connects an individual’s aspirations with organizational goals, leading to a highly positive impact. It involves deep conversations focusing on long-term development, delegating challenging assignments, and building trust. The underlying EI competencies are developing others, emotional self-awareness, and empathy.
  3. Affiliative: This style creates harmony and strengthens connections by valuing people and their feelings, often building loyalty. It has a positive impact on climate and is useful for healing rifts or boosting morale during stressful times. However, relying solely on this style can neglect necessary corrective feedback and tolerate mediocrity. Key competencies include collaboration and empathy.
  4. Democratic: This approach values people’s input, building buy-in and commitment through participation. It leads to high morale and a positive impact on climate. It is useful when a leader is uncertain of the best path or needs viable ideas for implementation. It relies on teamwork and collaboration, conflict management, and influence.
6.7.2.2.2 Dissonant Styles (Apply with Caution)

Two styles are noted for their potential to generate dissonance if misused or overused:

  1. Pacesetting: The pacesetting leader expects excellence and exemplifies it, focusing obsessively on doing things better and faster. Although it can yield brilliant results with highly competent and motivated teams, it often results in a highly negative climate when poorly executed. Excessive use leads to anxiety, burnout, and micromanagement, as the leader sets high standards but may be unclear about guidelines, leaving employees feeling distrusted and pushed too hard. It often suffers from a lack of empathy and emotional self-management.
  2. Commanding (Coercive): This style demands immediate compliance (“Do it because I say so”) and typically has the least effective, most highly negative impact on climate in most situations. It contaminates mood, erodes spirit, and undermines commitment. It is only appropriate in genuine emergencies, during an urgent turnaround, or when dealing with problem employees. Effective execution requires high emotional self-control to prevent harmful outbursts of anger or contempt.

6.8 Part II: The Four Domains of Emotional Intelligence

Emotional Intelligence (EI) is the set of competencies that enables leaders to create resonance. Intellect and technical skills are threshold capabilities—they get a person into the leadership door. However, EI competencies are the distinguishing factors that separate outstanding leaders from average ones. EI is comprised of four interconnected domains built upon a foundation of self-awareness.

6.8.1 Self-Awareness as the Foundation of Emotional Intelligence

Self-awareness is defined as having a deep understanding of one’s emotions, as well as one’s strengths, limits, values, and motives. It is the cornerstone upon which the entire structure of emotional intelligence—and thus resonant leadership—is built.

The four domains of emotional intelligence interact dynamically, and self-awareness facilitates both empathy (a key social awareness competency) and self-management. If a leader’s emotions are out of control due to a lack of self-awareness, their ability to handle relationships will inevitably suffer.

6.8.1.1 Key Components of Self-Awareness (Personal Competence)

  1. Emotional Self-Awareness: This involves reading one’s own emotions and recognizing their impact, and using one’s “gut sense” to guide decisions.
    • Leaders high in this competency are attuned to their inner signals, recognizing how their feelings affect their job performance.
    • They are attuned to their guiding values and can often intuitively grasp the best course of action, seeing the “big picture” in complex situations.
    • This awareness allows leaders to be candid and authentic, speaking openly about their emotions or with conviction about their guiding vision.
    • Emotionally self-aware leaders can spot rising anger as it crescendos, see the cause, and determine how to respond constructively, rather than letting anger build into an outburst. Leaders who lack this awareness may lose their temper without understanding why their emotions control them.
    • This competency also plays a crucial role in empathy: A person perpetually oblivious to their own feelings will also be tuned out to how others feel.
  2. Accurate Self-Assessment: This means knowing one’s strengths and limits.
    • Leaders with high self-awareness are realistic—neither overly self-critical nor naively hopeful—and are honest with themselves and others about their capabilities.
    • They exhibit gracefulness in learning where they need to improve and welcome constructive criticism and feedback.
    • This accuracy allows a leader to know when to ask for help and where to focus energy in cultivating new leadership strengths.
  3. Self-Confidence: This is defined as a sound sense of one’s self-worth and capabilities.
    • Knowing their abilities accurately allows self-confident leaders to play to their strengths and welcome difficult assignments.
    • They often possess a sense of presence, a self-assurance that lets them stand out in a group.

6.8.1.2 Self-Awareness, Values, and Decision Making

  • Clarity of Values: Self-aware leaders understand their values, goals, and dreams, knowing why they are headed where they are going. Since their decisions mesh with their values, they more often find their work energizing. Conversely, lacking self-awareness means a person is likely to make decisions that trigger inner turmoil by stepping on buried values.
  • Self-Reflection (The Telling Sign): A key sign of self-awareness is a propensity for self-reflection and thoughtfulness. Self-aware people routinely find time to reflect quietly, allowing them to think things over rather than reacting impulsively. Many outstanding leaders cultivate this thoughtful mode in their personal or spiritual lives, which allows them to act with the conviction and authenticity that resonance requires.
  • The Smart Guess (Intuition): Self-aware leaders access intuition—the essential ability to apply life wisdom in making business decisions. This is important because emotional memory banks help leaders judge information efficiently, showing that emotions are part of rationality, not opposed to it. Attuning to feelings helps leaders find meaning in data, leading to better decisions. The required inner attunement needed to sense this intuitive message is provided by self-awareness.

6.8.1.3 Self-Awareness in the Learning Process

  • The Emotional Reality Check: Discovering the real self involves taking inventory of one’s talents and passions—the person one actually is as a leader. This process requires self-awareness to overcome the “boiling frog syndrome” (the slow, invisible creep of complacency and habit).

  • Strengths and Gaps: The realization of one’s strengths and “gaps” (the difference between the ideal self and the real self) sparks the readiness to change. The ultimate goal of self-directed learning is to build on strengths and move closer to the ideal self, which requires self-awareness to honestly identify what to keep and what to change.

  • The Antidote to CEO Disease: Self-awareness, specifically knowing one’s strengths and gaps, is critical for leaders to break through the “information quarantine” (CEO disease) that surrounds them. Effective leaders actively seek out both negative and positive feedback to correct distortions in their self-perception.

6.8.2 Self-Management within Personal Competence

6.8.2.1 Key Competencies of Self-Management

  1. Emotional Self-Control: This is the ability to keep disruptive emotions and impulses under control. A key hallmark of this competency is a leader who stays calm and clear-headed under high stress or during a crisis. Self-control is essential for leadership because negative emotional surges (like anxiety or anger) can overwhelm the brain’s ability to focus on the task at hand. Leaders who exhibit strong self-control are able to maintain a confident, enthusiastic tone.
    This competency is crucial for mitigating the negative effects of the commanding and pacesetting leadership styles, as it allows a leader to channel potentially aggressive emotions. It helps a person override emotional impulses, freeing up mental energy for practicing new, more productive leadership habits.

  2. Transparency: This involves displaying honesty and integrity and maintaining trustworthiness. Transparent leaders “live their values” and possess an authentic openness about their feelings, beliefs, and actions. Integrity, at a primal level, hinges on impulse control (self-control) and ensures that a leader’s actions are consistent with their values.

  3. Adaptability: This is defined as flexibility in adapting to changing situations or overcoming obstacles. Adaptable leaders can juggle multiple demands without losing focus, are comfortable with organizational ambiguities, and are nimble in adjusting their thinking in the face of new data or realities. This is especially important in today’s ambiguous and rapidly changing business environment, where adaptability allows leaders to roll with changes and help the organization adjust.

  4. Achievement: This is characterized by a drive to improve performance to meet inner standards of excellence. Leaders strong in achievement strive constantly for performance improvements, set measurable yet challenging goals, and are pragmatic in calculating risks to ensure goals are worthy but attainable. This drive is internal, motivated by the need to meet high personal standards rather than external rewards like money or titles.

  5. Initiative: This is the readiness to act and seize opportunities. Leaders with strong initiative possess a sense of efficacy (that they can control their own destiny) and proactively seize or create opportunities instead of waiting for things to happen. This competency is required for pacesetting leaders, allowing them to exert an unhesitating, commanding tone when necessary, though it must be balanced by other EI skills.

  6. Optimism: This involves seeing the upside in events. An optimistic leader can “roll with the punches,” viewing setbacks as opportunities rather than threats, and expecting positive change in the future. Optimism is neuroanatomically linked to the left prefrontal area of the brain, which inhibits distress-driving areas (like the amygdala) and fuels motivated feelings, enabling the leader to persist despite inevitable failures and frustrations.

6.8.2.2 Self-Management and Leadership Effectiveness

  • Public Consequences: A leader’s emotions have public consequences. Because emotions are contagious, leaders’ primary tasks are the “emotional equivalent of good hygiene: getting their own emotions in hand”. Leaders cannot effectively manage emotions in others without first handling their own.
  • Creating Resonance: Leaders who master their impulses and feelings, remaining optimistic and upbeat, radiate the positive feelings that build resonance and create an environment of trust, comfort, and fairness. This self-mastery provides the mental clarity and concentrated energy demanded by leadership.
  • Impact on Change: Self-management, especially emotional self-control and adaptability, allows leaders to effectively lead through times of turbulence and change. Leaders who have mastered their emotions are better able to roll with changes and help the organization adjust.
  • The “Cool” Factor: The core capacity of self-management is linked to the historical concept of being “cool”—the ability to manage one’s turbulent feelings while allowing the full expression of positive emotions. The most meaningful act of responsibility a leader can perform is controlling their own state of mind.

6.8.3 Social Awareness as Attunement and Empathy

Social awareness supports the leader’s primal task of driving resonance. Resonance, which is a state of “synchronous vibration” where people are emotionally “in synch”, is naturally achieved by emotionally intelligent leaders. The domain of social awareness makes this possible through emotional attunement.

  1. Empathy (The Foundational Competency): Empathy is the ability to attune to a wide range of emotional signals, allowing leaders to sense the felt, but unspoken, emotions in a person or group. It involves sensing others’ emotions, understanding their perspective, and taking an active interest in their concerns.
    • Neurological Basis: Empathy stems from neurons in extended circuitry connected to the amygdala that read another person’s face and voice for emotion. This circuitry provides a steady stream of bulletins (“he’s getting a bit upset by that last remark”) that the prefrontal zone uses to fine-tune a leader’s response.
    • Limbic Resonance: This neural attunement is called “limbic resonance,” a “symphony of mutual exchange and internal adaptation” where two people harmonize their emotional state.
    • Impact on Leadership: Lacking empathy means a leader will unwittingly be “off-key,” speaking and acting in ways that trigger negative reactions. A person perpetually oblivious to their own feelings (lacking self-awareness) will also be tuned out to how others feel, confirming that self-awareness facilitates empathy.
  2. Attuned Leadership (Resonance-Building): Empathy allows leaders to tune into the emotional channels that create resonance and to fine-tune their message to keep it in sync.
    • Contextual Appropriateness: By being attuned to how others feel in the moment, a leader can say and do what is appropriate—whether that means calming fears, assuaging anger, or joining in good spirits.
    • Sensing Shared Values: This attunement also enables a leader to sense the shared values and priorities that can guide the group.
    • Empathetic Leaders are Effective: Leaders who grasp others’ feelings access a potent emotional guidance system that keeps what they say and do on track, making empathy the sine qua non (essential condition) of all social effectiveness in working life.

6.8.3.1 Competencies Associated with Social Awareness

  1. Organizational Awareness: This involves the ability to be politically astute, detecting crucial social networks and reading key power relationships. Such a leader understands the political forces at work in an organization, as well as the guiding values and unspoken rules that operate among people.
  2. Service: This competence involves recognizing and meeting follower, client, or customer needs. Leaders high in service foster an emotional climate so that people directly in touch with the customer or client keep the relationship on the right track, monitoring satisfaction to ensure needs are met.

6.8.3.2 The Business Case for Empathy

  • Talent Retention: Empathy is key to retaining talent because it allows leaders to develop and keep good people. Dissonant leaders—those who lack empathy—are one of the main reasons talented people leave and take the company’s knowledge with them.
  • Customer Relations: Empathetic people are superb at recognizing and meeting the needs of clients, customers, or subordinates.
  • Global Economy: In the growing global economy, empathy is a critical skill for getting along with diverse workmates and doing business with people from other cultures, acting as an antidote to cross-cultural misunderstandings.
  • Decision Making: Empathy means taking employees’ feelings into thoughtful consideration and then making intelligent decisions that work those feelings into the response.

6.8.3.3 Social Awareness and Leadership Styles

  • Democratic Style: Empathy plays a role in democratic leadership, especially when the group is diverse, as it prevents miscues.
  • Affiliative Style: This style, which focuses on emotional needs over tasks, relies on empathy to keep people happy by caring for the whole person.
  • Coaching Style: Coaching requires emotional self-awareness and empathy, ensuring that leaders listen first before giving feedback and that the advice is genuinely in the employee’s best interest.
  • Visionary Style: The ability to sense how others feel (empathy) is crucial for a visionary leader to articulate a truly inspirational message; a leader who misreads people simply cannot inspire them.

6.8.3.4 Role in Team Emotional Intelligence

In teams, the collective equivalent of empathy—Social Awareness—is the foundation that enables a team to build and maintain effective relationships with the rest of the organization. An emotionally intelligent (empathic) team identifies other key groups (internal or external) and proactively fosters a good working relationship with them, which is a powerful driver of organizational effectiveness.

6.8.4 Relationship Management as the Synthesis of EI

  1. Foundation of Self: Leaders must first have Self-Awareness of their own vision, values, and direction.
  2. Self-Control: Leaders must use Self-Management to maintain a positive emotional register and control impulses, ensuring authenticity and integrity in their interactions.
  3. Attunement: Social Awareness (Empathy) is essential to tune into the emotional channels of others, making the relationship management skills appropriate and “in synch” with the group’s feelings.

If a leader acts disingenuously or manipulatively, followers’ “emotional radar” will detect a note of falseness, dissolving the relationship into cynicism and distrust. The art of handling relationships, therefore, begins with authenticity, acting from one’s genuine feelings.

Relationship management is described not merely as friendliness, but as “friendliness with a purpose”: moving people in the right direction, whether toward agreement on strategy or enthusiasm for a new project.

6.8.4.1 Key Competencies in Relationship Management

6.8.4.1.1 Inspirational Leadership (Inspiration)

This involves guiding and motivating followers with a compelling vision or shared mission.

  • Inspirational leaders get people excited about a common mission and offer a sense of purpose beyond day-to-day tasks.
  • They embody what they ask of others and articulate a shared mission that rings true and inspires others to follow.
  • This competence strongly undergirds the effective Visionary leadership style, lending grand meaning to mundane tasks and maximizing buy-in for organizational goals.
6.8.4.1.2 Influence

This involves wielding a range of tactics for persuasion.

  • A leader’s power of influence ranges from finding the right appeal for a specific listener to building buy-in and a network of support for an initiative.
  • Leaders adept in influence are persuasive and engaging when addressing a group.
  • For example, one director, lacking formal team power, succeeded by convincing people through “appealing to their agenda”.
6.8.4.1.3 Developing Others (Coaching)

This competency involves bolstering others’ abilities through feedback and guidance.

  • Leaders adept at this show a genuine interest in those they help, understanding their goals, strengths, and weaknesses.
  • They are natural mentors or coaches who provide timely and constructive feedback.
  • This underpins the Coaching leadership style, which focuses on developing long-term capabilities and linking personal aspirations to organizational goals.
6.8.4.1.4 Change Catalyst

This involves initiating, managing, and leading in a new direction.

  • Change catalysts recognize the need for change, challenge the status quo, and champion the new order.
  • They are strong advocates for change, even in the face of opposition, and find practical ways to overcome barriers.
  • This is critical for Visionary leaders when they need to “smooth the transition” when changing direction.
6.8.4.1.5 Conflict Management

This relates to resolving disagreements.

  • Leaders who manage conflicts well draw out all parties, understand differing perspectives, and then find a common ideal that everyone can endorse.
  • They surface the conflict, acknowledge the feelings and views of all sides, and redirect the energy toward a shared ideal.
  • This skill is essential for the Democratic leadership style, where building consensus and quelling discord are key.
6.8.4.1.6 Teamwork and Collaboration

This competence focuses on cooperation and team building.

  • Collaborative leaders generate an atmosphere of friendly collegiality and model respect, helpfulness, and cooperation.
  • They draw others into active, enthusiastic commitment to collective effort and build team spirit and identity.
  • They intentionally forge and cement close relationships beyond mere work obligations.

6.8.4.2 Relationship Management and Leadership Styles

The effectiveness of the four resonant leadership styles (Visionary, Coaching, Affiliative, and Democratic) depends heavily on the visible skills of Relationship Management. For example:

  • Visionary leadership is fueled by Inspiration and the Change Catalyst competency.
  • Coaching relies on the Developing Others competency.
  • Affiliative leadership is characterized by Teamwork and Collaboration.
  • Democratic leadership uses collaboration, Conflict Management, and Influence to build consensus and buy-in.

6.9 Part III: The Leadership Repertoire: Six Styles and Their Impact

The most effective leaders master a repertoire of distinct leadership styles and switch between them as the situation demands. Four styles primarily build resonance, while two can easily create dissonance if used poorly or excessively.

Leadership Style How It Builds Resonance / Dissonance Impact on Climate When Appropriate
Visionary Moves people toward shared dreams. Most strongly positive When changes require a new vision or clear direction.
Coaching Connects a person’s wants with the organization’s goals. Highly positive To help an employee improve performance and build long-term capabilities.
Affiliative Creates harmony by connecting people to each other. Positive To heal rifts in a team, motivate during stress, or strengthen connections.
Democratic Values people’s input and gets commitment through participation. Positive To build buy-in or consensus, or to get valuable input from employees.
Pacesetting Meets challenging and exciting goals. (Often poorly executed, creating dissonance.) Often highly negative To get high-quality results from a motivated and competent team.
Commanding Soothes fears by giving clear direction in an emergency. (So often misused, it creates dissonance.) Highly negative In a crisis, to kick-start a turnaround, or with problem employees.

The Resonant Styles

  1. The Visionary Leader: Articulates where a group is going but not how it will get there, freeing people to innovate. This style frames tasks within a larger, shared mission, giving work meaning and building commitment. It is underpinned by the EI competencies of inspirational leadership, self-confidence, and empathy.

  2. The Coaching Leader: Focuses on personal development by exploring an employee’s life goals and career hopes, linking them to their daily work. This style builds rapport, trust, and motivation. It is the least-used style but delivers powerful results. It requires empathy, emotional self-awareness, and developing others.

  3. The Affiliative Leader: Prioritizes people and their feelings, striving to create harmony and build emotional bonds. This style is excellent for increasing morale, improving communication, and repairing broken trust. It relies heavily on empathy, collaboration, and conflict management competencies.

  4. The Democratic Leader: Spends time getting people’s ideas and building buy-in through participation. By listening to employee concerns, this style builds trust, respect, and commitment. It is most effective when a leader is uncertain of the best direction. It depends on collaboration, conflict management, and influence.

The Dissonant Styles (Apply with Caution)

  1. The Pacesetting Leader: Expects and exemplifies excellence, is obsessive about doing things better and faster, and demands the same from everyone. While it can work with highly motivated and competent teams, it often poisons the climate by leaving employees feeling pushed and untrusted. It can create dissonance if the leader lacks empathy and self-awareness.

  2. The Commanding Leader: Demands immediate compliance with orders and relies on threats, not explanations. This “Do it because I say so” style is the least effective in most situations, eroding morale and commitment. It should be used only with extreme caution in genuine emergencies or turnarounds, and when all else has failed with a problem employee.

6.10 Part IV: Developing Resonant Leaders

Emotional intelligence competencies are not innate talents but learned abilities. Leaders can change and expand their repertoire, but it requires a specific, self-directed process.

6.10.1 The Challenge: CEO Disease and the Failure of Traditional Training

Leaders often operate in an information vacuum, a phenomenon known as “CEO Disease,” where people withhold unpleasant but vital feedback. The higher a leader climbs, the less accurate their self-assessment is likely to be.
This is compounded by the fact that most leadership development programs fail to produce sustainable change. They often target the wrong part of the brain (the neocortex, for analytical skills) instead of the limbic system, where emotional habits reside. This leads to a “honeymoon effect,” where initial enthusiasm fades and old habits return.

6.10.2 The Five Discoveries for Sustainable, Self-Directed Learning

Sustainable change in EI competencies requires a process of self-directed learning that engages the emotional brain. This process is structured around five key discoveries.

  1. The First Discovery: My Ideal Self - Who do I want to be? Change begins by connecting with a powerful vision of one’s ideal self—a person’s dreams, values, and passions. This vision provides the motivation and energy required to undertake the hard work of changing ingrained habits. This contrasts with the “ought self,” which is based on others’ expectations.

  2. The Second Discovery: My Real Self - Who am I? This involves gaining an accurate picture of one’s current self, including strengths and gaps. Because of self-delusion and the CEO disease, this requires actively seeking out candid feedback from multiple sources (e.g., a 360-degree assessment) to get a full picture. The goal is to identify the balance of what to preserve (strengths) and what to change (gaps).

  3. The Third Discovery: My Learning Agenda - How do I get there? This is the creation of a practical, manageable plan for building on strengths and reducing gaps. An effective learning agenda is tied to a person’s own ideal, fits their learning style, and includes concrete action steps that can be integrated into daily life.

  4. The Fourth Discovery: Experimenting with and Practicing New Behaviors. To change habits, the brain must be rewired. This requires bringing bad habits into awareness and consciously rehearsing new, better behaviors at every opportunity until they become automatic. This implicit learning happens through extended practice in real-world situations and through mental rehearsal.

  5. The Fifth Discovery: Developing Supportive Relationships. Lasting change cannot happen alone. It requires a network of trusting relationships—mentors, coaches, peers, or friends—who provide a safe context to experiment, offer feedback, and affirm progress.

6.11 Part V: Building Emotionally Intelligent Organizations

The ultimate goal is to create a critical mass of resonant leaders, transforming the entire organization’s culture and climate.

6.11.1 From the Individual to the Collective

While leadership development for individuals starts with the ideal self, for groups and organizations, the process is reversed. Change begins when the group confronts its emotional reality and cultural norms. Groups are motivated to change only after they grasp, on a visceral level, the dissonance in how they function.

  • The Emotional Reality of Teams: Teams, like individuals, have an emotional intelligence, defined by their collective self-awareness, self-management, and empathy. The team’s EI is shaped by its norms—the unwritten ground rules that govern interaction. Emotionally intelligent teams have norms that support trust, cooperation, and effective conflict resolution. The leader’s role is to make these norms explicit and model them.
  • Organizational Transformation: A dissonant or “toxic” organization is often a product of leaders who fail to listen or who create a culture of fear. Transformation requires leaders to actively question the emotional reality and cultural norms. A process called Dynamic Inquiry—focused conversations with open-ended questions about feelings—is a potent tool for uncovering what people truly care about, what’s working, and what’s not.

6.11.2 Attunement, Not Alignment

Creating a resonant organization is not about mechanically “aligning” people with a strategy. It’s about attunement—a process that engages people’s hearts and minds, connecting them emotionally to a shared vision.
Emotionally intelligent leaders achieve this by:

  • Discovering the Emotional Reality: Respecting the group’s values, slowing down to listen, and engaging people at all levels in discovering the truth.
  • Visualizing the Ideal: Tuning into their own feelings to craft a vision, attuning others to it emotionally, and putting people first.
  • Sustaining the Change: Turning vision into action through modeling, creating systems (HR, rewards) that reinforce EI practices, and managing the organization’s myths and stories.

Sustainable leadership development must be a systemic process, not a one-off program. It must be a strategic priority driven from the top, engage the culture head-on, and build a language of leadership that permeates the entire enterprise.
