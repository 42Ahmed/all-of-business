26  Corportate Sustainability

26.1 Executive Summary

Corporate Sustainability (CS) and Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) have evolved from niche ethical considerations into central pillars of modern business strategy, largely driven by increasing investor demand for reliable Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) data and a rapidly developing regulatory landscape. This briefing document synthesizes a comprehensive analysis of the principles, measurement practices, reporting standards, and financial implications of corporate sustainability.

The foundational concepts of CS are rooted in business ethics and have expanded to encompass the “triple bottom line”—balancing economic, social, and environmental performance. The Circular Economy (CE) has emerged as a critical operational model for achieving these goals by replacing linear “take-make-dispose” systems with principles of reduction, reuse, and recycling.

To meet stakeholder demands for transparency, a sophisticated ecosystem of measurement and reporting tools has developed. ESG rating agencies (such as MSCI, Moody’s ESG Solutions, and S&P Global) and sustainability indexes (including the DJSI and FTSE4Good) have become influential market arbiters. However, their methodologies vary significantly, leading to a critical divergence in ratings that can confuse investors and dilute the market impact of sustainability initiatives. Concurrently, corporate disclosure has progressed from separate social and environmental reports to integrated frameworks like the Global Reporting Initiative (GRI) Standards and the International Integrated Reporting Council (<IR>) Framework. This evolution is now being formalized through regulations, most notably the EU’s proposed Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and the global standards being developed by the International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB), which aim to elevate sustainability reporting to the same level as financial reporting.

An empirical analysis of Italian listed firms (FTSE-MIB) from 2015-2019 reveals a complex and context-dependent relationship between sustainability and financial performance. The study found no statistically significant link between ESG performance and financial returns for the overall sample. However, for family-controlled firms, a negative relationship was identified, suggesting a prioritization of non-economic goals consistent with Socio-emotional Wealth (SEW) theory. This negative financial impact is significantly mitigated by “collegial leadership,” where multiple executive members on the board appear to foster more efficient and value-oriented sustainability investments. The key takeaway is that the financial benefits of sustainability are not automatic; they require strategic implementation and are deeply influenced by corporate ownership and governance structures.

26.2 The Foundations of Corporate Sustainability

The modern concept of corporate sustainability is a multifaceted philosophy built upon decades of evolving thought regarding the role of business in society. It integrates principles from business ethics, corporate social responsibility, and sustainable development, with the circular economy emerging as a key strategic framework for implementation.

26.2.1 From Business Ethics to Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)

The internationalization of markets has necessitated an integrated business approach that extends beyond profit-seeking to include ethical management. Business ethics, defined as the set of principles and values governing economic activities, provides the foundation for sustainable corporate conduct. This involves both:

  • Internal Ethics: Creating strong relationships with employees by recognizing their value and fostering parallel growth for both the individual and the company.
  • External Ethics: Pursuing the general interest alongside profit, creating moral cohesion and building trust with consumers, savers, and the market.

This ethical foundation gave rise to the more formalized concept of CSR. While lacking a single standard definition, CSR theory posits that a firm has responsibilities beyond its shareholders, encompassing legal, ethical, and philanthropic duties. The evolution of CSR can be traced through distinct phases:

  • CSR1 (1950s): Originated in the United States, based on the belief that companies should contribute to societal progress, though this was often seen as a compulsory duty.
  • CSR2 (1970s): Known as “Corporate Social Responsiveness,” this phase adopted a more managerial focus, developing tools like social audits and ethical codes.
  • CSR3 (1980s): Termed “Business Ethics” or “Corporate Social Rectitude,” this phase integrated the moral dimensions of CSR1 with the managerial aspects of CSR2.
  • Modern CSR (1990s-Present): Heavily influenced by R. Edward Freeman’s Stakeholder Theory, which posits that a firm’s long-term success depends on considering the legitimate expectations of all its interlocutors, not just shareholders.

26.2.2 Sustainable Development and the Triple Bottom Line

The concept of sustainable development, famously defined by the 1987 Brundtland Commission as development that “guarantees the needs of current generations without compromising the possibility that future generations will be able to satisfy their own,” is central to corporate sustainability. Its application in a corporate context is achieved through the triple bottom line approach, which calls for the joint maximization of three dimensions:

  1. Economic: Pertains to a company’s operating profit and the creation of economic capital (assets net of liabilities).
  2. Social: Relates to the management of social capital—the ability of people to work together for common purposes, based on values like honesty and trust. It involves behaving responsibly toward the full range of stakeholders.
  3. Environmental: Involves minimizing negative environmental impacts and operating according to established environmental standards.

26.2.3 The Circular Economy as a Sustainability Strategy

The Circular Economy (CE) provides a practical and strategic framework for operationalizing the principles of the triple bottom line. In contrast to the traditional linear economy (“take-make-dispose”), the CE is an economic system where the “end of life” concept is replaced by keeping products and materials in use for as long as possible.

  • Core Principle: The CE is based on the “3R” principle of Reduce, Reuse, and Recycle.
  • Link to Sustainability: The CE is considered a vital strategy for achieving sustainable development. It helps attain all three dimensions of the triple bottom line by enhancing resource productivity, creating economic value from waste management, and reducing environmental degradation. As stated, “The transition to a circular economy is a step towards sustainability.”
  • Business Benefits: Adopting a CE model can create a sustainable competitive advantage by generating cost savings, fostering innovation, creating jobs, increasing resource efficiency, and improving corporate reputation.

26.3 Measuring and Reporting Sustainability Performance

The demand for corporate sustainability has spurred the creation of a complex ecosystem for measurement, reporting, and standardization. This field is characterized by the rise of ESG ratings, the proliferation of disclosure frameworks, and a decisive push toward regulatory alignment with financial reporting.

26.3.1 The Rise of ESG Metrics, Ratings, and Indexes

The growth of Sustainable and Responsible Investment (SRI) created a powerful demand for reliable, standardized data on corporate non-financial performance. This led to the prominence of the Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) framework as the primary lens for analysis.

26.3.1.1 Key ESG Rating Agencies and Indexes

A number of specialized agencies and financial institutions have become key players in assessing and ranking corporate ESG performance.

Agency / Index Provider Key Characteristics
MSCI ESG Research A leading provider that grew rapidly through acquisitions (e.g., KLD, Innovest, RiskMetrics). Provides ESG ratings from leader (AAA) to laggard (CCC).
Moody’s ESG Solutions Group Formed after Moody’s acquired Vigeo Eiris, which itself was a merger of Vigeo (France) and EIRIS (UK). Provides ESG ratings on a scale of 0 to 100.
S&P Global Entered the ESG space by acquiring the ESG ratings division of RobecoSAM (SAM). Its Corporate Sustainability Assessment (CSA) is the basis for the Dow Jones Sustainability Indexes.
Dow Jones Sustainability Index (DJSI) Launched in 1999, it is one of the most recognized global sustainability benchmarks. It uses a “best-in-class” approach to select sustainability leaders within each industry.
FTSE4Good Index Series Created by the London Stock Exchange’s FTSE Russell group, it measures the performance of companies demonstrating strong ESG practices.

Other notable indexes include the STOXX Global ESG Leaders Index, the Euronext Vigeo Eiris indexes, and the UN Global Compact 100.

26.3.1.2 The Challenge of Rating Divergence

A critical issue within the ESG landscape is the significant divergence among ratings provided by different agencies. Studies show that homogeneity among ratings is low, which creates several problems:

  • It adds noise to the market, making it difficult for investors to identify truly sustainable companies.
  • It dilutes the impact of ESG performance on asset prices.
  • It allows companies to be rated differently by various agencies, undermining the credibility of the ratings themselves. This divergence stems from differing methodologies, subjective weighing of ESG criteria, and a lack of regulation in the rating industry.

26.3.2 The Evolution of Corporate Disclosure

Corporate reporting has evolved to integrate non-financial information, moving from voluntary, disparate documents to structured, comprehensive reports.

  1. Early Forms (1970s-1990s): Companies began issuing Social Reports (focusing on employees and community) and, later, Environmental Reports (focusing on pollution and resource use). These were often separate documents.
  2. Sustainability Reports (2000s): These reports integrate the three pillars of the triple bottom line. The Global Reporting Initiative (GRI), founded in 1997, became the de facto standard-setter. The GRI Standards provide a modular framework (Universal, Sector, and Topic Standards) for reporting on an organization’s economic, environmental, and social impacts.
  3. Integrated Reports (IR) (2010s-Present): The International Integrated Reporting Council (IIRC) promotes IR as a tool to explain how an organization creates value over time. The <IR> Framework links financial and non-financial information through the lens of six “capitals”: financial, manufactured, intellectual, human, social and relational, and natural.

26.3.3 The Push for Regulatory Standardization

Recognizing the need for comparability and reliability, regulators are moving to make non-financial disclosure mandatory and standardized.

  • Directive 2014/95/EU (NFRD): The EU’s Non-Financial Reporting Directive required large public-interest entities to disclose information on environmental, social, and governance matters. However, its limited scope and flexibility were criticized.
  • Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD): In 2021, the European Commission proposed the CSRD to strengthen and expand the NFRD. The CSRD will apply to approximately 50,000 EU companies (up from 11,000) and mandate more detailed reporting, including alignment with the EU Taxonomy for sustainable activities, with the ultimate goal of putting sustainability reporting on par with financial reporting.
  • International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB): In 2021, the IFRS Foundation established the ISSB to develop a comprehensive global baseline of sustainability disclosure standards focused on the needs of investors. Its first draft standards were released in March 2022.

26.4 The Link Between Sustainability and Financial Performance: An Empirical Analysis

The question of whether sustainability “pays off” has been a subject of extensive academic debate, with prior research yielding conflicting results. An empirical study of Italian listed companies provides specific insights into this relationship, particularly concerning the influence of family ownership and board structure.

26.4.1 Research Focus and Hypotheses

The analysis examines the impact of ESG performance on the financial performance of 40 firms listed on Italy’s FTSE-MIB index from 2015 to 2019. It specifically investigates how this relationship differs between family and non-family firms and the moderating role of board leadership.

  • Hypothesis 1: A positive relationship exists between a company’s level of sustainability and its financial performance.
  • Hypothesis 2: A negative relationship exists between sustainability and financial performance in family businesses, driven by the prioritization of non-economic goals (Socio-emotional Wealth).
  • Hypothesis 3: Collegial leadership (the presence of multiple executives on the board) positively moderates the sustainability-performance relationship in family firms.

26.4.2 Methodology Overview

  • Sample: 160 firm-year observations from Italian FTSE-MIB companies (2015-2019).
  • Sustainability Measure: ESG Rate provided by Standard Ethics, a rating agency that uses principles from the EU, OECD, and UN.
  • Financial Performance Measure: Return on Assets (ROA).
  • Key Variables: A firm was defined as a family firm if a family controls at least 25% of shares. Collegial leadership was measured as the ratio of executive members to board size.

26.4.3 Key Findings

The study’s results highlight a nuanced and context-specific relationship between ESG activities and financial outcomes.

  1. No Overall Link to Performance: For the full sample of firms, there was no statistically significant relationship between ESG ratings and ROA. This fails to support the general hypothesis that higher sustainability performance directly translates to better financial performance.
  2. A Negative Impact in Family Firms: When isolating family-controlled firms, the analysis revealed a negative and statistically significant relationship between their ESG rate and financial performance. This finding supports the Socio-emotional Wealth (SEW) theory, which suggests that family firms may pursue sustainability initiatives to preserve their reputation and legacy, even if these actions are not immediately profitable.
  3. The Moderating Role of Collegial Leadership: The most significant finding is that the negative ESG-performance link in family firms is positively moderated by collegial leadership. The three-way interaction between ESG rate, family firm status, and collegial leadership was positive and significant. This implies that in family firms where executive power is shared among multiple board members, investments in sustainability are managed more efficiently and are less detrimental—or potentially even beneficial—to financial performance. This governance structure may enhance the quality of strategic decision-making, ensuring that sustainability initiatives are better aligned with value creation.

26.5 Conclusions and Future Directions

26.5.1 Synthesized Conclusions

The pursuit of corporate sustainability is an increasingly integral part of corporate strategy, driven by stakeholder pressure and an evolving regulatory environment. The conceptual framework has matured to include the triple bottom line and the Circular Economy, while reporting has advanced toward integrated and standardized models under the guidance of bodies like the GRI, IIRC, CSRD, and ISSB.

However, the empirical evidence demonstrates that the financial benefits of sustainability are not guaranteed. The analysis of Italian firms indicates that the impact of ESG performance on financial returns is highly dependent on ownership structure and corporate governance. For family firms, sustainability initiatives may represent an investment in non-economic “socio-emotional wealth” at the expense of short-term profitability. This trade-off can be effectively managed through governance mechanisms like collegial leadership, which appear to foster a more strategic and value-oriented approach to sustainability.

26.5.2 Future Research Imperatives

The most pressing challenge identified is the divergence of ESG ratings. The lack of a standardized, reliable assessment methodology creates noise in financial markets, hinders effective capital allocation, and undermines investor confidence. Future research must address this gap. A primary objective should be to analyze the sources of this divergence in detail and work toward developing a unified, more accurate ESG assessment framework. A single, transparent rating system that overcomes the subjective biases and methodological inconsistencies of current providers would provide clearer signals to investors, enable more effective corporate benchmarking, and ultimately strengthen the contribution of sustainable investment to the real economy.
