17  Emotional Intelligence 2.0

17.1 Executive Summary

Emotional Intelligence 2.0 posits that Emotional Intelligence (EQ) is the critical factor distinguishing highly successful individuals from their less successful peers, far surpassing Intellectual Quotient (IQ) or experience. EQ is defined as the ability to recognize and understand emotions in oneself and others, and to use this awareness to manage behavior and relationships. While IQ is fixed, EQ is a flexible skill that can be learned and significantly improved through targeted practice (plasticity).

EQ comprises four core, learnable skills, grouped into two primary competencies: Personal Competence (Self-Awareness and Self-Management) and Social Competence (Social Awareness and Relationship Management).

The impact of EQ is immense: it accounts for 58% of job performance across all types of jobs. Ninety percent of high performers are also high in EQ, and every point increase in EQ correlates to a $1,300 increase in annual salary. The book provides a practical methodology, the Emotional Intelligence Action Plan, and 66 specific strategies designed to strengthen the neural pathway between the brain’s emotional and rational centers, thus enabling long-term improvement in emotional mastery.

17.2 The Journey

The journey toward understanding emotional intelligence begins with recognizing the innate conflict between human reason and feeling.

17.2.1 When Reason and Feeling Collide

The human brain is hard-wired to prioritize emotions. Everything we sense travels through the body as electric signals, which first pass through the limbic system (the area where emotions are produced) before reaching the frontal lobe(where rational, logical thinking occurs). This means that we experience things emotionally before our reason can engage.

The physical pathway for emotional intelligence involves constant, effective communication between these emotional and rational centers of the brain. Individuals high in EQ are able to use thought to guide their reactions, preventing emotions from hijacking behavior.

17.2.2 Your Journey: The Missing Link

Historically, intellectual horsepower (IQ) and experience were assumed to be the sources of success. However, research revealed a peculiar finding: people with the highest IQs outperform those with average IQs only 20% of the time, while people with average IQs outperform those with high IQs 70% of the time. Emotional intelligence (EQ) was identified as the critical variable explaining success above and beyond IQ.

The purpose of Emotional Intelligence 2.0 is to increase the reader’s EQ by providing time-tested strategies for lasting gains.

17.2.2.1 Methodology: Measuring Your EQ

The first step in the EQ journey is taking the Emotional Intelligence Appraisal® test to establish a baseline score. This score profile pinpoints the specific EQ skills needing the most improvement, guiding the selection of strategies. The process of measuring EQ and actively working on the skills is likened to learning a dance—practicing the steps with a partner yields exponential results.

17.3 The Big Picture

Emotional intelligence is your ability to recognize and understand emotions in yourself and others, and your ability to use this awareness to manage your behavior and relationships. Despite the importance of EQ, only 36% of people tested are able to accurately identify their emotions as they happen, meaning two-thirds are typically controlled by their emotions.

17.3.1 Triggers and Emotional Hijackings

Emotional Hijackings are moments when intense emotions take control of behavior, causing a person to react without thinking. These are induced when emotions are highly intense.

A Trigger Event is something that generates a prolonged emotional reaction in you. Reactions to triggers are shaped by personal history and experience. As EQ skills develop, individuals learn to spot their triggers and practice productive responses that eventually become habitual.

All emotions are derivations of five core feelings: happiness, sadness, anger, fear, and shame.

17.3.2 Sizing Up the Whole Person

EQ is distinct from both cognitive intelligence (IQ) and personality.

Quality Description Flexibility
IQ (Intellectual Quotient) Ability to learn; fixed from birth. Not flexible.
Personality Stable behavioral “style” (e.g., introversion/extroversion). Stable over a lifetime; does not change.
EQ (Emotional Intelligence) Ability to manage emotions and relationships. Flexible; can be learned and developed.

Measuring IQ, EQ, and personality together provides the most complete picture of an individual, as these qualities cover unique, non-overlapping ground.

17.3.3 The Impact of EQ

EQ is fundamental to a host of critical skills, including communication, time management, and decision-making. It is the single biggest predictor of performance in the workplace and the strongest driver of personal excellence and leadership.

  • EQ accounts for 58% of performance in all jobs.
  • 90% of high performers are high in EQ.
  • People with high EQs make an average of $29,000 more per year than those with low EQs.
  • The link between EQ and earnings is so direct that every point increase in EQ adds $1,300 to an annual salary.

17.4 What Emotional Intelligence Looks Like: Understanding the Four Skills

The four emotional intelligence skills combine under two primary competencies: Personal Competence (how you manage yourself) and Social Competence (how you manage relationships with others).

17.4.1 1. Self-Awareness (Personal Competence)

Definition: Self-awareness is the ability to accurately perceive your own emotions in the moment and understand your tendencies across situations. It involves developing a straightforward and honest understanding of what makes you tick, including what motivates you and what situations “push your buttons”.

Key Concepts:

  • Foundational Skill: Self-awareness makes the other three EQ skills easier to use.
  • Embrace Discomfort: Genuine understanding of emotions requires the willingness to focus on and tolerate uncomfortable or negative feelings.
  • Emotional Mistakes as Data: Having self-awareness means not being afraid of emotional “mistakes,” as they provide a steady stream of information necessary for growth.
What Self-Awareness Looks Like What a Lack of Self-Awareness Looks Like
Manages emotions; they don’t manage him. Stress and urgency are projected onto others.
Remains calm, cool, and collected, even when frustrated. Comes across as defensive or aggressive due to lack of awareness of tone and language.
Open and authentic at all times. Overwhelming personality; doesn’t notice when others are annoyed.

17.4.2 2. Self-Management (Personal Competence)

Definition: Self-management is the ability to use your awareness of your emotions to stay flexible and direct your behavior positively. It is revealed not only in resisting explosive behavior but also in the ability to tolerate uncertainty and explore options during difficult emotional situations.

Key Concepts:

  • Action, or Inaction: Self-management is what happens when you act—or choose not to act.
  • Delaying Gratification: Real results come from putting momentary needs on hold to pursue larger, more important goals, requiring commitment to self-management over time.
  • Preventing Hijackings: High self-management prevents emotional hijackings, allowing you to react nimbly to change and take initiative.
What Self-Management Looks Like What a Lack of Self-Management Looks Like
Epitome of patience and understanding during heated meetings. Responds too quickly, sharply, or disjointedly when stressed.
Ability to separate emotion from logic during a crisis. Emotional reactions “trickle” onto teammates, creating panic.
Handles confrontational situations well; never loses cool. Reveals stress to staff, setting a negative tone rather than diffusing it.

17.4.3 3. Social Awareness (Social Competence)

Definition: Social awareness is your ability to accurately pick up on emotions in other people and understand what is truly going on with them, even if you don’t feel the same way. This often means perceiving what others are thinking and feeling.

Key Concepts:

  • Outward Focus: Requires looking outward to learn about and appreciate others, tuning into their emotions.
  • Listening and Observing: These are the most important elements. To excel, you must stop talking, stop your inner monologue, stop anticipating responses, and stop planning what you will say next.
  • The Anthropologist Approach: Observing others in their natural state without letting your own thoughts or feelings disturb the observation.
What Social Awareness Looks Like What a Lack of Social Awareness Looks Like
Has a rare talent to read others’ emotions and adjusts to situations. Needs to allow others to feel good about their ideas, even if their own plan is better.
Good at acknowledging feelings when communicating difficult news. Hard to get a word in edgewise; talks over others when excited.
Uncanny ability to spot and address the “elephant in the room”. Misses the non-technical currents and the mood/evolution of opinions in meetings.

17.4.4 4. Relationship Management (Social Competence)

Definition: Relationship management is your ability to use your awareness of your own emotions and those of others to manage interactions successfully, ensuring clear communication and effective handling of conflict. It is the bond built with others over time.

Key Concepts:

  • Integrated Skill: This skill taps into all three other EQ skills: Self-Awareness (noticing your feelings), Self-Management (expressing feelings productively), and Social Awareness (understanding the other person’s needs).
  • Frequency and Quality: Relationships are a product of the quality, depth, and time spent interacting with another person.
  • Handling Conflict: Relationship management provides the skills needed to passively avoid problems or aggressively explode when frustrated.
What Relationship Management Looks Like What a Lack of Relationship Management Looks Like
Creates a safe and inviting forum for discussion; maintains an “open-door” policy. If they disagree with someone, they make it clear the relationship isn’t worth developing.
Empathetic listener; remains calm and answers difficult questions without alienating others. Dismisses others’ thoughts and fails to tailor communication style (e.g., always very direct).
Finds common ground and inquires about others’ interests. Minimizes a person’s point of view or experience; comes across as blunt and not empathetic.

17.5 Digging In: My Emotional Intelligence Action Plan

EQ skills are highly malleable due to the brain’s ability to change, a concept known as plasticity. Developing EQ involves strengthening the neural pathways between the rational and emotional centers of the brain. This process is gradual and requires tremendous effort and repetition until the new behavior becomes a habit. Measurable, lasting changes typically appear three to six months after beginning consistent practice.

17.5.1 Methodology: My EQ Action Plan Steps

To effectively improve EQ, focus your efforts using the following steps:

  1. Transfer Emotional Intelligence Appraisal ® scores onto Part One (My Journey Begins) of the action plan to establish a baseline.
  2. Pick one EQ skill to work on first. The human mind is most effective focusing on one skill at a time. While the Appraisal report recommends a skill, you may choose your own. However, if scores in all four areas are low (below 75), it is recommended not to start with Relationship Management.
  3. Pick three strategies to begin using for your chosen skill, drawing from the strategies chapters.
  4. Choose an EQ mentor. Find someone gifted in your chosen skill and ask them to provide feedback and guidance at regular intervals.
  5. Apply your chosen strategies, keeping these points in mind:
    • Expect success, not perfection. Pushing yourself means continuing to catch yourself when emotions interfere.
    • Practice, practice, practice. Quantity of practice in a variety of situations is the secret to increasing EQ.
    • Be patient. Lasting change takes a few months (3–6 months).
  6. Measure your progress. After sufficient progress, take the Emotional Intelligence Appraisal® a second time to complete Part Two of the action plan and determine the focus for your next skill development efforts.

17.6 Self-Awareness Strategies

Self-awareness is a continuous journey of understanding the “true essence” of yourself, requiring honesty and courage. Since hard-wired emotional reactions precede rational responses, being aware of the full range of feelings is essential for managing self and relationships.

Key Concepts and Applications (Selected Strategies):

1. Quit Treating Your Feelings as Good or Bad

  • Concept: Judging emotions (e.g., classifying guilt as “bad”) prevents you from understanding what caused the feeling. Suspending judgment allows the emotion to run its course and vanish.
  • Application: When an emotion surfaces, immediately notice it, refrain from labeling it good or bad, and remind yourself it is there to communicate something important.

2. Observe the Ripple Effect from Your Emotions

  • Concept: Your emotional “outpourings” are like a stone dropped in water, sending ripples in all directions and affecting everyone in your vicinity. This is critical because emotions are primary drivers of behavior.
  • Application: Watch how your emotions impact people immediately, and reflect on how they might affect a wider circle long-term. Ask others how your emotions affect them to understand the ripple effects more clearly.

Lean into Your Discomfort

  • Concept: The biggest obstacle to self-awareness is avoiding the discomfort that comes from seeing yourself clearly. Ignoring feelings is only a short-term fix; you must move toward, into, and through them to change productively.
  • Application: Consciously seek out feelings of mild emotional discomfort (boredom, confusion, anticipation). This helps discover personal “arrogances” or things you dismiss that you need to address to avoid repeating unproductive patterns.

Feel Your Emotions Physically

  • Concept: Emotions trigger physical sensations (e.g., stomach tightening, increased heart rate) due to the mind-body connection. Learning these physical cues allows you to spot emotions before you are mentally aware of them.
  • Application: Close your eyes and focus on your breathing, heart rate, and muscle tension. Think of positive and negative events and note the distinct physical changes that accompany those emotions.

5. Know Who and What Pushes Your Buttons

  • Concept: We all have triggers that irritate us. Pinpointing the specific people, situations, or conditions (like noisy offices) that trigger strong emotions allows you to maintain composure.
  • Application: Create a list of specific people and situations that push your buttons. Dig deeper to discover the source of the button (e.g., why a certain behavior reminds you of a past unresolved conflict) to help manage reactions.

7. Keep a Journal about Your Emotions

  • Concept: A journal provides objectivity, helping you develop perspective on your emotions and tendencies.
  • Application: Record events that triggered strong emotions and your response. Note patterns in which emotions get you down or pick you up. Describe the emotions felt daily, including the physical sensations that accompanied them.

14. Seek Feedback

  • Concept: Your internal lens is tainted by your experiences and moods, making true objectivity impossible. Self-awareness requires knowing yourself from the “inside out and the outside in,” meaning you need others’ perspectives.
  • Application: Ask for specific feedback from friends, coworkers, or mentors. Look for similarities in the information received, which helps you see how your emotions and reactions affect others.

17.7 Self-Management Strategies

Self-management is actively choosing what you say and do using emotional awareness. This skill builds directly on self-awareness: you can only choose how to respond productively if you are aware of the underlying feeling. Effective self-management ensures you achieve your goals and do not frustrate others.

Key Concepts and Applications (Selected Strategies):

1. Breathe Right

  • Concept: Shallow breathing deprives the brain (which uses 20% of the body’s oxygen) of the resources needed for complex functions like managing mood and maintaining focus. Deep, diaphragm breathing (making the stomach protrude) ensures maximum oxygen flow.
  • Application: During stressful moments, focus on slow, deep breaths, inhaling through the nose until the stomach swells, and exhaling gently through the mouth until the lungs are empty. This immediately engages the rational brain and promotes calmness.

2. Create an Emotion vs. Reason List

  • Concept: When your emotional mind conflicts with your rational mind over a decision, this tool helps clear your head.
  • Application (Step-by-Step):
    1. Draw a line down the middle of a page to create two columns.
    2. In the left column, write what your emotions are telling you to do.
    3. In the right column, write what your reason is telling you to do.
    4. Ask: Where are my emotions clouding judgment, and where is my reason ignoring important emotional cues?
    5. Use the list to decide whether the emotional or rational side should guide your decision.

4. Count to Ten

  • Concept: This simple technique stops rash action and interrupts the flow of anger or frustration, giving the rational brain valuable time to catch up with the overwhelmed emotional brain (limbic system).
  • Application: When feeling frustrated or angry, take a deep breath and silently count to ten. Even counting a few numbers can cool down the system and allow for a more constructive response.

9. Take Control of Your Self-Talk

  • Concept: The average person has about 50,000 thoughts daily, and this inner voice (“self-talk”) dictates emotional flow by turning the emotional heat up or down. Negative self-talk is unrealistic and self-defeating.
  • Application (Reframing Negative Self-Talk):
    • Turn “I always” or “I never” into “just this time” or “sometimes.”
    • Accept responsibility for your actions, but not someone else’s burden; similarly, stop always blaming others and take responsibility for your part.

10. Visualize Yourself Succeeding

  • Concept: Since the brain struggles to distinguish between what you see with your eyes and what you visualize, mental visualization is an effective way to practice new skills and form habits, particularly for situations that don’t occur often.
  • Application (Step-by-Step):
    1. Find a distraction-free room (before bed is ideal).
    2. Visualize yourself in a challenging situation where you typically lose control.
    3. Concentrate on the sights, sounds, and emotions until you feel them stirring.
    4. Picture yourself acting the way you wish to (e.g., remaining calm, saying the right things). Allow yourself to feel the satisfaction of success.

11. Clean Up Your Sleep Hygiene

  • Concept: Quality sleep is critical for self-management as patience, flexibility, and alertness are the first things lost when sleep is poor. Good sleep hygiene helps the brain move through necessary cycles to recharge.
  • Application (Sleep Hygiene Steps):
    1. Get twenty minutes of morning sunlight. This resets the inner clock, aiding evening sleep (must not be filtered by windows or sunglasses).
    2. Turn off the computer at least two hours before bedtime. Screen light tricks the brain, disrupting sleep quality.
    3. Keep your bed for sleeping. Avoid working or watching TV in bed so your body associates the bed with rest.
    4. Avoid caffeine, especially in the p.m. Caffeine has a six-hour half-life and severely disrupts sleep quality.

17.8 Social Awareness Strategies

Social awareness is achieved by looking outward to appreciate and understand others’ emotional cues, providing a more accurate view of your surroundings. To build this skill, you must ensure your focus is clear, tapping into both your five senses and your “sixth sense” (emotions).

Key Concepts and Applications (Selected Strategies):

1. Greet People by Name

  • Concept: Using someone’s name is a personal, meaningful, and influential social awareness strategy. It breaks down barriers and comes across as warm and inviting, helping you to remain connected to the person.
  • Application: Practice using a person’s name at least twice during an introduction or conversation. If a name is unusual, ask the person to spell it to help you remember it later.

2. Watch Body Language

  • Concept: Body language communicates nonstop and is an abundant source of information about how a person is really feeling. Professionals, like poker players, rely on reading cues (posture, eye contact, gestures) to determine sincerity and confidence.
  • Application: Perform a head-to-toe assessment: look at eye contact (maintained contact can show sincerity, shifty eyes suggest deception) and smiles (an authentic smile includes a crinkle of skin at the corner of the eyes).

5. Don’t Take Notes at Meetings

  • Concept: Multi-tasking (like taking notes while listening) sacrifices the quality of work because the brain cannot perform multiple activities at a high level simultaneously. By focusing on writing, you miss critical, non-technical clues like shifts in tone or body language.
  • Application: At your next meeting, refrain from taking notes. Instead, focus entirely on observing others’ facial expressions and making eye contact with the speaker to gather crucial emotional and social information.

7. Clear Away the Clutter

  • Concept: Internal distractions (mental chatter, planning responses while others are speaking) crowd out external focus, making social awareness impossible.
  • Application: When in conversation, do not interrupt until the other person is completely finished. Catch yourself planning a response, stop the thought, and physically lean toward the speaker to refocus on their words and face.

9. Go on a 15-minute Tour

  • Concept: When focused solely on destinations or tasks, you miss the people between points A and B. Touring your workspace helps you get in tune with others’ emotions and pick up on subtle social clues.
  • Application: Schedule 15 minutes twice a week to observe your surroundings. Look for the look and feel of workspaces, movement patterns, and who seeks interaction. On another day, tour for moods, noticing the overall atmosphere and how people’s feelings affect you.

11. Practice the Art of Listening

  • Concept: Listening is not just hearing words, but also analyzing the tone, speed, and volume of the voice for hidden messages. Focus is essential.
  • Application: Stop everything else when someone talks to you. When on the phone, don’t type an email. When meeting someone, close the door and sit near them to ensure full attention. These simple acts help you pick up on the cues they send.

14. Test for Accuracy

  • Concept: Even skilled individuals need to validate social observations, especially when there is interference or conflicting cues (e.g., someone says they are “fine” but looks sullen).
  • Application: Ask reflective questions to clarify observations. For example: “It looks like you are feeling down about something. Did something happen?”. This acknowledges the physical evidence you observe and shows interest.

15. Step into Their Shoes

  • Concept: Put away your own beliefs, emotions, and thinking patterns to try and experience a situation as the other person would. This is necessary to gain perspective and identify problems before they escalate.
  • Application: Ask yourself: “If I were this person, how would I respond to this question?” Use your history with them (past reactions, behavior) to answer, not your own default tendencies.

17.9 Relationship Management Strategies

Relationship management is necessary because all relationships take work. It requires the integration of all four EQ skills: self-awareness (noticing your feelings), self-management (acting accordingly), and social awareness (understanding the other person’s needs). Since you are half of any relationship, you hold half the responsibility for deepening the connection.

Key Concepts and Applications (Selected Strategies):

1. Be Open and Be Curious

  • Concept: Being open means sharing information about yourself, which helps prevent others from misinterpreting your behavior (e.g., explaining why you are punctual). Being curious means showing interest in the other person’s story to better understand their needs.
  • Application: Use your self-management skills to choose what information to share about yourself. Ask inquisitive, non-judgmental questions, choosing an appropriate setting and time.

3. Avoid Giving Mixed Signals

  • Concept: Confusion arises when your words, tone, and body language do not align (e.g., frowning while saying “great job”). People trust what they see over what they hear.
  • Application: Use self-awareness to identify your current emotions and self-management to decide how to express them. If strong emotions prevent appropriate expression in the moment, put the feeling on the back burner or explain the discrepancy (e.g., “If I seem distracted, it’s because I can’t stop worrying…”).

5. Take Feedback Well

  • Concept: Feedback is a unique gift for improvement, but the element of surprise can catch us off guard. Receiving feedback gracefully involves actively using self-awareness, social awareness, and self-management.
  • Application (Steps):
    1. Listen actively using social awareness, asking clarifying questions and asking for specific examples.
    2. Thank the person for their willingness to share, regardless of whether you agree.
    3. Use self-management to delay action. Take time to absorb the point and sort out your feelings before deciding what steps to take.
    4. Follow up with action to show you value their comments and solidify the relationship.

6. Build Trust

  • Concept: Trust takes time to build, can be lost instantly, and is critical for deepening connections. It is built through open communication, consistency in words and actions, reliability, and willingness to share.
  • Application: Be the first to lay some of yourself on the line (use self-awareness/self-management to share parts of yourself). Use social awareness to ask the other person what needs to happen to build their trust, and listen intently to their answer.

8. Only Get Mad on Purpose

  • Concept: Anger is a useful emotion if managed properly. To master it, you must be angry “with the right person, to the right degree, at the right time, for the right purpose, and in the right way”. Anger used purposefully can motivate others or communicate the gravity of a situation.
  • Application: Define your varying degrees of anger (from annoyed to rage) and write down specific examples. Determination when showing anger will actually improve the relationship, using social awareness to consider the other person’s likely response.

13. Explain Your Decisions, Don’t Just Make Them

  • Concept: When decisions are made without context, people feel “in the dark” and anxious. To support an idea, people need to understand the rationale. Transparency builds trust and makes people feel respected.
  • Application: When announcing a change, explain the “why” behind the decision, including alternatives considered, and acknowledge how the decision will affect everyone. Use social awareness to anticipate how the decision will be received.

15. Align Your Intention with Your Impact

  • Concept: A misalignment occurs when a good intention (e.g., trying to lighten the mood with a joke) results in a negative impact (offense or silence). These misunderstandings tarnish relationships.
  • Application (Analysis):
    1. Describe an incident, your intentions, your actions, and the resulting impact.
    2. Write down what you did not realize in the situation (missed cues, what you learned about others).
    3. Determine what you could have done differently to ensure your actions matched your positive intentions.

17. Tackle a Tough Conversation (Methodology)

  • Concept: Tough conversations are inevitable but can be navigated calmly by structuring the discussion and actively managing your reactions.
  • Steps:
    1. Start with agreement. Find common ground (e.g., agreeing the discussion is important, or a shared goal).
    2. Ask the person to help you understand his or her side. People need to feel heard. Focus on understanding their perspective to prevent frustration from rising.
    3. Resist the urge to plan a “comeback” or a rebuttal. Silence your inner voice (self-management) and direct attention fully to the person.
    4. Communicate clearly and simply. Avoid speaking in circles or code, especially when delivering difficult feedback.
    5. Move the conversation forward. Once perspectives are understood, reiterate common ground and discuss next steps for development or resolution (e.g., “I’m still invested in your development…”).
    6. Keep in touch. Check progress frequently to show continued investment in the relationship.

17.10 Just the Facts: A Look at the Latest Discoveries in Emotional Intelligence

Research conducted by TalentSmart® reveals several key trends regarding EQ scores across demographics and circumstances.

17.10.1 The Poles Are Melting: EQ Then and Now

Emotional intelligence is a skill set that can be learned—and unlearned.

  • Increase: Between 2003 and 2007, the collective EQ of the U.S. workforce steadily increased, demonstrating that the discussion and intentional practice of emotions help people become more skilled at identifying and understanding them.
  • Decrease (2008): In 2008, collective EQ scores dropped, linked directly to the economic recession and the resulting stress. Stress taxes mental resources, causing EQ skills to desert people when they are needed most.

17.10.2 The Battle of the Sexes: EQ and Gender

  • Self-Awareness: Men and women are statistically tied in their ability to recognize their own emotions.
  • Other Skills: Historically, women outperformed men in self-management, social awareness, and relationship management. However, men have recently caught up in self-management, a change attributed to shifting social norms that encourage men to pay more attention to their emotions.

17.10.3 It’s Lonely at the Top: EQ and Job Title

  • Trend: EQ scores tend to climb from entry-level positions upward toward middle management.
  • The Dip: Beyond middle management (Director level and above), there is a steep downward trend in EQ scores.
  • CEOs: CEOs, on average, have the lowest EQ scores in the workplace. This is often because leaders are promoted based on technical knowledge or tenure rather than on skills for managing people. Nonetheless, among executives, those with the highest EQ scores are the best performers.

17.10.4 The Generational Divide: EQ and Age

  • Trend: Self-management skills increase steadily with age (e.g., 60-year-olds scored higher than 50-year-olds).
  • Implication: Younger generations (Gen Y/X) often show deficient self-management skills not because of their upbringing but because they have had less time to practice managing their emotions. They have the capacity to soak up new information and can accelerate their EQ development through conscious effort, allowing them to lead like seasoned veterans sooner.

17.10.5 China’s Secret Weapon: EQ and Culture

  • Finding: Chinese executives averaged 15 points higher than American executives in self-management and relationship management.
  • Discipline: Executives in both countries show similar awareness of emotions (self-awareness and social awareness), but Chinese executives are better at putting that awareness into productive action. This advantage is linked to a culture where emotional outbursts and careless self-gratification are personally shameful, promoting emotionally intelligent behaviors like seeking feedback and following through on commitments.

17.11 Final Comprehensive Summary

Emotional Intelligence 2.0 establishes EQ as a dynamic skill set, crucial for high performance and success, distinct from fixed intellect (IQ) and stable behavioral style (Personality). The book’s core framework revolves around four interconnected skills:

  1. Self-Awareness: Understanding your emotions in the moment, achieved through strategies like journaling, seeking external feedback, and leaning into emotional discomfort.
  2. Self-Management: Using that awareness to direct behavior positively, often requiring the delay of immediate needs for greater goals. Practical tools include deep breathing (to oxygenate the brain) and creating an Emotion vs. Reason List.
  3. Social Awareness: Accurately perceiving and understanding the moods and motivations of others, demanding total focus on listening and observing non-verbal cues (e.g., watching body language, avoiding note-taking in meetings).
  4. Relationship Management: Successfully managing interactions and building lasting bonds by applying the three foundational skills. Key methodologies include taking feedback well, explaining decisions instead of merely making them, and strategically using emotion (“Only Get Mad on Purpose”).

The book emphasizes that EQ is highly susceptible to change—it can be developed through consistent practice (plasticity) but can also deteriorate under stress.
