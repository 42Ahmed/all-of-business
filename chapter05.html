5  Power of Emotional Intelligence

5.1 Executive Summary

The fundamental element of effective leadership is Emotional Intelligence (EI), which Goleman identifies as the sine qua non (essential condition) of superior leadership. While IQ and technical skills are necessary “threshold capabilities” for executive roles, EI—the ability to manage oneself and one’s relationships—is the discriminating competency that best predicts who among highly capable people will lead most ably.

Effective leadership is fundamentally a primal emotional task. Leaders must drive the collective emotions of their team in a positive direction, creating resonance (neural harmony that facilitates flow and performance) rather than dissonance(undermining emotional foundations).

The EI model crucial for leadership consists of four core abilities, which translate into learned emotional competencies that distinguish star performers:

  1. Self-Awareness (knowing one’s inner states, resources, and intuition).
  2. Self-Management (controlling one’s impulses and directing one’s emotions toward goals, including achievement motivation).
  3. Social Awareness (sensing others’ feelings and understanding organizational politics/networks).
  4. Relationship Management (handling relationships adeptly and building rapport).

The most impactful leaders do not rely on a single approach; they master and flexibly deploy multiple leadership styles depending on the situation, especially the authoritative, coaching, affiliative, and democratic styles. By effectively managing the organizational climate—the atmosphere employees feel—leaders can account for nearly a third of business results. Furthermore, developing EI is possible throughout life due to neuroplasticity, requiring a systematic learning process focused on consistent behavioral practice.

5.2 Chapter Summaries

5.2.1 A Surprising Synergy

This chapter introduces Emotional Intelligence (EI), also known as EQ, and details its crucial relevance in the business world.

5.2.1.1 The Importance of Emotional Intelligence in Business

Following the 1995 publication of Emotional Intelligence, the concept saw a significant surge in application within the workplace, particularly concerning leadership screening, selection, and development. EI abilities, rather than technical skills or traditional IQ, emerge as the “discriminating” competency that best predicts who will lead most ably among a highly intelligent group.

  • EI vs. IQ and Technical Skill: Indicators of IQ and technical skill drop toward the bottom of the list of competencies that define star performers the higher the leadership position.
  • EI Dominance at the Top: At the highest levels of leadership, competence models typically consist of 80 to 100 percent EI-based abilities. As one executive search firm head noted, CEOs are often hired for intellect and business expertise, but fired for a lack of emotional intelligence.

5.2.1.2 Framework of Emotional Intelligence

Goleman’s model of Emotional Intelligence focuses on the behavioral level, melding EI theory with decades of research on the competencies that separate star performers from average ones.

  • Four Generic Domains of EI: Most elements of emotional intelligence models fit within four core domains.
    1. Self-Awareness
    2. Self-Management
    3. Social Awareness
    4. Relationship Management

These core abilities determine an individual’s potential for self-mastery and relationship mastery, while emotional competence demonstrates how much of that potential has been mastered into on-the-job capabilities (e.g., customer service requires social awareness and relationship management).

5.2.2 Managing With Heart

This chapter focuses on the destructive effects of emotional deficiencies in the workplace and details the essential skill of giving constructive feedback (The Art of the Critique).

5.2.2.1 The Cost of Emotional Deficiencies

Emotional deficiencies in the workplace—such as intimidating or arrogant bosses, or miserable morale—result in tangible costs to the bottom line, including decreased productivity, missed deadlines, mistakes, mishaps, and employee turnover. Attitudes that prioritized being “emotionally aloof” for making “hard” decisions are outmoded in today’s competitive reality, which demands emotional intelligence. Emotional upset in the workplace, like individual agitation, hinders clear thinking, memory, learning, and decision-making; “stress makes people stupid”.

5.2.2.2 The Worst Way to Motivate Someone (Destructive Criticism)

Inept criticism is a primary cause of conflict on the job. Criticism voiced as personal attacks (ad hominem charges, disgust, sarcasm, contempt) leads to defensiveness, evasion of responsibility, and stonewalling. Destructive criticism, such as a blanket, generalized statement delivered angrily (e.g., “You’re screwing up”), leaves the recipient helpless and angry, devastating their morale and conviction in their capability. Managers often delay feedback until they “blow up,” delivering the criticism in the worst possible way, which only backfires and is received as an affront.

5.2.2.3 The Artful Critique: A Practical Handbook

An artful critique is a constructive message that focuses on actions and solutions, not character flaws. This approach holds out the hope of improvement rather than creating helplessness or anger.

Framework for the Artful Critique (The “XYZ” Statement):

  1. Be specific: Focus on a significant incident or pattern of deficiency.
    • Say exactly what the problem is, what’s wrong with it (or how it makes you feel), and what needs to be changed. Specificity is equally important for both criticism and praise.
  2. Offer a solution: The critique must point to a way to fix the problem; otherwise, it leaves the recipient frustrated and demoralized. It should suggest alternatives or sensitize the person to deficiencies needing attention.
  3. Be present (Face-to-Face and Private): Feedback is most effective when delivered face-to-face and privately, allowing opportunity for response or clarification. Memos make communication too impersonal.
  4. Be sensitive (Empathy): Be attuned to the impact of what you say and how you say it. Managers lacking empathy are prone to delivering hurtful put-downs, leading to resentment, bitterness, and defensiveness.

Emotional Counsel for Receiving Criticism:

  • See the criticism as valuable information for improvement, not a personal attack.
  • Guard against the impulse toward defensiveness; take responsibility instead.
  • If too upsetting, ask to resume the meeting later to absorb the message and cool down.
  • View criticism as an opportunity to work with the critic to solve the problem, rather than an adversarial situation.

5.2.3 What Makes a Leader? (The Components of EI)

Research confirms that emotional intelligence is twice as important as cognitive abilities (IQ) and technical skills for excellent performance across all job levels, becoming increasingly vital at senior ranks. This chapter details the four domains of EI and their associated competencies that define effective leaders.

5.2.3.1 Self-Awareness

Self-awareness is the first component, meaning a deep understanding of one’s emotions, strengths, weaknesses, needs, and drives. Self-aware leaders are honest with themselves and others.

  • Key Concepts:
    • Emotional Self-Awareness: Recognizing how one’s feelings affect job performance, self, and others. For instance, knowing that tight deadlines cause stress allows one to plan carefully.
    • Accurate Self-Assessment: Knowing one’s limitations and strengths, exhibiting a sense of humor about oneself, and welcoming constructive criticism.
    • Self-Confidence: A firm grasp of one’s capabilities, leading to calculated risks and playing to strengths.
  • Applications/Signs in the Workplace:
    • Candor and the ability to speak openly about one’s emotions and their impact on work.
    • Self-aware people make decisions that mesh with their values, finding work energizing.
    • In the hiring process, self-aware candidates are frank about admitting failures and often tell their tales with a self-deprecating sense of humor.

5.2.3.2 Self-Management (Self-Regulation and Motivation)

Self-management is the ability to control and channel biological emotional impulses in useful ways, operating like an ongoing inner conversation.

  • Key Concepts (Self-Regulation):
    • Self-Control: The capacity to manage disturbing emotions and impulses, remaining calm and clear-headed during crises.
    • Transparency: Living one’s values, openly admitting mistakes, and confronting unethical behavior, thereby ensuring integrity.
    • Adaptability: Comfort with ambiguity and change; the ability to juggle multiple demands without losing focus, adjusting flexibly to new realities.
  • Applications of Self-Regulation:
    • Creates an environment of trust and fairness, reducing politics and increasing productivity.
    • Allows people to “roll with the changes” during business upheaval (e.g., mergers, new technology adoption).
    • Enhances integrity; people with self-control challenge impulsive urges (like padding expense accounts) for long-term relational benefits.
  • Key Concept (Motivation):
    • Achievement Drive: A deep, embedded desire to achieve for the sake of achievement, beyond external rewards (salary, status).
    • Initiative: Leaders seize or create opportunities, cutting through red tape if necessary to forge better possibilities.
    • Optimism: Seeing opportunity, not threat, in setbacks; expecting the best of others.
  • Applications of Motivation:
    • People seek creative challenges, love to learn, and take great pride in a job well done.
    • They are restless with the status quo, constantly raising the performance bar (seeking to be “stretched”).
    • They track progress rigorously (“keep score”) using hard measures like profitability, maintaining optimism even when facing poor results.

5.2.3.3 Social Awareness

Social awareness involves sensing how people are feeling and understanding the dynamics of the broader environment.

  • Key Concepts:
    • Empathy: Thoughtfully considering employees’ feelings alongside other factors when making intelligent decisions. Empathy does not mean adopting others’ emotions or trying to please everyone.
    • Organizational Awareness: Being politically astute, detecting crucial social networks, and understanding power relationships, guiding values, and unspoken rules within an organization.
    • Service: Fostering an emotional climate that ensures people in touch with customers keep the relationship on the right track, monitoring client satisfaction closely.
  • Applications of Empathy:
    • Essential in team environments to sense and understand clashing agendas and viewpoints.
    • Crucial for globalization, as it allows leaders to be attuned to cross-cultural subtleties in body language and messages beneath words.
    • Key for talent retention, especially through effective coaching and mentoring relationships, where the leader senses how to give motivating feedback and when to push or hold back.

5.2.3.4 Relationship Management

Relationship management is the ability to manage relationships with others; Goleman defines social skill as “friendliness with a purpose”—moving people in a desired direction (e.g., agreement on strategy). It is the culmination of the other three dimensions.

  • Key Concepts:
    • Inspiration: Creating resonance and moving people with a compelling vision or shared mission, embodying what is asked of others.
    • Influence: Finding the right appeal for a given listener and building buy-in from key people and support networks.
    • Developing Others: Showing genuine interest in cultivating people’s abilities, giving timely and constructive feedback, and acting as a natural coach/mentor.
    • Change Catalyst: Recognizing the need for change, challenging the status quo, and advocating compellingly for the new order.
    • Conflict Management: Drawing out all parties, understanding differing perspectives, and redirecting energy toward a shared ideal.
    • Teamwork and Collaboration: Generating friendly collegiality, modeling respect and cooperation, and forging relationships beyond mere work obligations.
  • Applications of Social Skill:
    • Socially skilled people build rapport and wide networks, believing that nothing important gets done alone, ensuring they have a support structure when action is needed.
    • They are expert persuaders, combining self-awareness, self-regulation, and empathy to know whether an emotional plea or an appeal to reason will work best.
    • They often appear to be “idly schmoozing,” but they are actually building bonds across divisions and levels, anticipating future needs for help in a fluid environment.

5.2.4 Leadership That Gets Results (The Six Leadership Styles)

The single job of a leader is to get results. Research by Hay/McBer identified six distinct leadership styles, each rooted in different EI components, which directly impact the organizational climate and, in turn, financial performance.

5.2.4.1 The Concept of Organizational Climate

Climate refers to six key factors influencing the working environment, accounting for nearly a third of business results.

  • Flexibility: How free employees feel to innovate unencumbered by red tape.
  • Responsibility: Employees’ sense of responsibility to the organization.
  • Standards: The level of performance standards people set.
  • Accuracy of Feedback/Aptness of Rewards: The sense of accuracy about performance feedback and the suitability of rewards.
  • Clarity: Clarity regarding mission and values.
  • Commitment: The level of commitment to a common purpose.

5.2.4.2 The Six Leadership Styles

Only four of the six styles consistently have a positive effect on climate and results.

Style Description Core Emotional Intelligence Drivers Impact on Climate Best Use Case
1. Authoritative (Visionary) Mobilizes people toward a vision. States the end, gives freedom for the means. (Not explicitly detailed in source, but implied link to Inspiration, Initiative, and Self-Confidence) Most effective: Drives up clarity, commitment, standards, and flexibility. When a business is adrift and needs a new course.
2. Coaching Develops people for the future by helping them identify strengths/weaknesses and establish long-term goals. (Implied link to Developing Others, Empathy, and Self-Awareness) Markedly Positive: Improves flexibility, responsibility, clarity, and commitment through constant dialogue and investment. When employees are receptive, aware of weaknesses, and want to improve.
3. Affiliative Creates emotional bonds and harmony; people come first. (Implied link to Empathy, Relationship Building, and Communication) Markedly Positive: Increases communication, flexibility, and sense of belonging. To build team harmony, increase morale, improve communication, or repair broken trust.
4. Democratic Builds consensus through participation. (Implied link to Collaboration and Conflict Management) Positive: Builds trust, respect, and commitment. Drives up flexibility and responsibility. When a leader is uncertain about direction or needs fresh ideas for execution from able employees.
5. Pacesetting Expects excellence and self-direction; sets extremely high standards and exemplifies them. (Implied link to Achievement Drive and Self-Control) Highly Negative: Often destroys climate by creating overwhelmed employees and micromanagement. Flexibility and responsibility evaporate. 5.2.4.3 Leadership Flexibility

The most effective leaders master four or more styles—especially the authoritative, democratic, affiliative, and coaching styles—and switch flexibly among them depending on the demands of the situation. This fluid leadership relies on an exquisite sensitivity to the impact they are having on others.

Expanding the Leadership Repertory: If a leader lacks certain styles, they can either build a team whose members employ the missing styles (though this is less recommended) or, preferably, expand their own style repertories. This involves identifying the underlying emotional intelligence competencies they lack and working assiduously to increase their quotient of those competencies.

  • Example: A pacesetting leader who wants to use the affiliative style needs to improve empathy and potentially relationship building or communication.
  • Example: An authoritative leader seeking the democratic style needs to work on collaboration and communication.

5.2.5 The Group IQ

In the knowledge economy, productivity relies heavily on coordinated efforts within organizational teams, making emotional intelligence (the skills that help people harmonize) increasingly valued.

5.2.5.1 Group Intelligence and Social Harmony

Whenever people collaborate, they possess a group IQ, which is the sum total of the talents and skills of all members. However, how well they achieve their task is determined not by the average academic IQ, but by the group’s emotional intelligence.

  • The single most important factor in maximizing a group’s performance is internal harmony (social harmony).
  • In groups with high emotional static (fear, anger, rivalries), people cannot offer their best. Harmony allows a group to take maximum advantage of the creative and talented members’ abilities.
  • Individuals who are too eager, controlling, or domineering act as a drag on group performance.

5.2.5.2 The Power of Informal Networks

The productivity of high-IQ scientists and engineers (stars) at places like Bell Labs was found to depend not on academic IQ, but on their emotional IQ and their ability to motivate themselves and work their informal networks into ad hoc teams.

Star Performers Leverage Three Network Varieties:

  1. Communications Webs: Who talks to whom.
  2. Expertise Networks: Who is turned to for advice (technical excellence).
  3. Trust Networks: Who people trust with secrets, doubts, and vulnerabilities.
  • Star performers cultivate rapport with key people before a crisis, ensuring they get faster answers when they need help.
  • A manager may be high on expertise but low on trust, undermining their ability to manage and effectively excluding them from informal networks.
  • Organizational savvy and social skills—such as effective coordination, consensus building, persuasiveness, and taking initiative beyond the stated job—are key aspects of the emotional intelligence that define star performers.

5.2.6 Primal Leadership

Great leadership works through the emotions; it is the primal task of leadership to drive the collective emotions in a positive direction.

5.2.6.1 Resonance vs. Dissonance

Leaders act as the group’s emotional guide.

  • When leaders drive emotions positively (e.g., inspiring optimism), they create resonance, bringing out everyone’s best.
  • When leaders drive emotions negatively (e.g., inciting antagonism or hostility), they spawn dissonance, undermining the emotional foundations of performance.

5.2.6.2 The Open Loop and Emotional Contagion

The profound emotional impact of a leader stems from the open loop nature of the limbic system (our emotional centers). Unlike closed-loop systems, the open-loop system depends on external sources to manage itself, meaning we rely on connections with others for emotional stability.

  • Interpersonal Limbic Regulation: One person can transmit signals that alter hormone levels, heart rates, sleep rhythms, and immune function inside another person’s body.
  • Mirroring: During conversation, people’s physiological profiles (like heart rate) become remarkably similar, a phenomenon called mirroring or entrainment.
  • Contagion in Groups: Emotions spread irresistibly in groups, even nonverbally. The more cohesive the group, the stronger the sharing of moods.
  • The Leader’s Role: Everyone watches the boss; people take their emotional cues from the top. The leader adds the strongest “seasoning” to the “emotional soup” because their manner, voice, and facial expressions have special weight, making their emotions particularly contagious.

5.2.6.3 How Moods Impact Results

Moods (less intense, longer-lasting feelings) and emotions (intense, fleeting feelings) significantly affect work performance.

  • Positive Moods: Cheerfulness and warmth spread most easily and are highly beneficial. Upbeat moods boost cooperation, fairness, mental efficiency, creativity, decision-making skills, and willingness to be helpful. Laughter, in particular, signals trust, connection, and harmony, and is highly contagious due to specialized open-loop circuits in the brain. Optimistic leaders attract and retain talented people.
  • Negative Emotions/Hijacking: Chronic anger, anxiety, or futility powerfully disrupt work, hijacking attention from the task at hand (emotional hijacking). Distress erodes mental abilities, impairs empathy, and decreases social skills. Negative interactions with a boss are recalled with greater intensity, detail, and frequency than positive ones, making demotivation highly contagious.

Quantifying the Impact:

  • Leaders’ actions account for roughly 50 to 70 percent of how employees perceive their organization’s climate.
  • A direct correlation exists between positive climate and financial results; climate can account for 20 to 30 percent of business performance.
  • For every 1 percent improvement in service climate, there is a 2 percent increase in revenue.

5.2.7 The Social Brain

This chapter focuses on the neurological basis of interpersonal connection and rapport, stemming from the field of social neuroscience.

5.2.7.1 Relationship Management as Managing Brain States

“Managing relationships” means being responsible for how we shape the feelings of those we interact with—for better or for worse. In groups with power differences (like a work setting), the most powerful person is the emotional sender, setting the emotional state for the rest of the group. Emotional contagion operates automatically, instantly, and unconsciously through circuitry like the mirror neuron system.

5.2.7.2 The Ingredients of Rapport

Rapport reflects a physiological connection where emotions and body states move in tandem. It creates moments of “interpersonal chemistry” or “micro-flow” where things happen at their best.

Three Ingredients of Rapport:

  1. Paying Full Attention: Both individuals must tune in fully, putting aside distractions (e.g., turning off a cell phone or computer—the “Blackberry syndrome”).
  2. Non-Verbal Synchrony: When connecting well, people’s movements are almost choreographed, like a dance, regulated by specialized neurons called oscillators.
  3. Positive Feeling: A sense of connection, warmth, and closeness—an interpersonal high.

Having a “human moment” at work requires setting aside other tasks and paying full attention, opening the way to this emotional flow.

5.2.8 The Sweet Spot for Achievement

This chapter introduces the neurobiological conditions necessary for peak performance, summarized by the inverted concept.

5.2.8.1 The Inverted U: Stress and Performance

The relationship between mental adeptness/performance and the spectrum of moods forms an upside-down U. Joy, cognitive efficiency, and outstanding performance occur at the peak.

  • Low Stress (Left Leg): Too little challenge breeds disinterest and boredom; minds wander and motivation vanishes.
  • Peak Performance (Apex): This occurs where motivation and focus peak, at the intersection of a task’s difficulty and our ability to match its demand. In this zone, the prefrontal cortex (the high road/executive center) is highly active, enhancing creative thinking, cognitive flexibility, and information processing.
  • High Stress (Right Leg): When challenges begin to exceed ability, anxiety starts to erode cognitive efficiency (the “tipping point”).

5.2.8.2 Frazzle and Cognitive Dysfunction

Frazzle is a neural state where emotional upsurges (like those triggered by stress) hamper the prefrontal cortex.

  • When under stress, the amygdala (low road/emotional center) commandeers the prefrontal cortex, handing decision-making over to knee-jerk responses. This is called cognitive dysfunction.
  • High anxiety shrinks attention capacity, undermining the ability to take in new information, learn, or generate fresh ideas. Near-panic is the enemy of learning and creativity.

5.2.8.3 The Leader as a Secure Base

The essential task of a leader is redefined as: to help people get and stay in the brain zone where they can work at their best. This requires fostering a sense of psychological safety.

  • Secure Base: Leaders who act as a secure base are trustworthy, empathic, and connected, making employees feel calm, appreciated, and inspired.
  • Benefits of a Secure Base: Feeling secure allows a person to focus better on work, achieve goals, see obstacles as challenges (not threats), explore, be playful, take risks, and innovate.
  • Managing Pressure: A leader should act as a superb buffer, lessening overwhelming pressures from above and not making them worse. However, the leader should not protect employees from all discomfort, as resilience grows from a modicum of necessary pressure.
  • Emotional Support: The accumulation and frequency of positive versus negative daily interactions (e.g., a compliment after a setback) determine satisfaction and performance. Having even just one trusted person at work (a “best friend at work”) is one of the best predictors of job happiness.

Leaders who are socially intelligent help people contain and recover from emotional distress by listening with empathy and acknowledging their perspective, defusing toxicity.

5.2.9 Developing Emotional Intelligence

The capacity for emotional intelligence is not fixed; the brain has the capacity to grow and reshape itself throughout life, thanks to neuroplasticity and neurogenesis.

5.2.9.1 Steps for Developing Emotional Intelligence

Changing a deeply ingrained bad habit involves competing against strong existing neural circuitry. The learning process must be systematic and based on behavioral change.

Systematic Learning Process:

  1. Get Committed (Mobilize Motivation): Engage the motivating power of the left prefrontal areas. This involves drawing on the individual’s dreams and long-term vision for themselves.
  2. Gather 360-Degree Feedback: Use an instrument like the ESCI–360 (Emotional and Social Competence Inventory) to measure abilities. Ask trusted people to rate specific behaviors anonymously to determine which competencies to strengthen.
  3. Operationalize the Goal (Specific Behavior): Choose one or two specific behaviors to change, making the goal practical. Example: Breaking the “Blackberry syndrome” habit means creating a plan to turn off electronics and pay full attention every time someone walks into the office.
  4. Practice Consistently (Harness Neuroplasticity): Repeat the new, better habit over and over again to create new, strong neural circuitry that competes with the old default option.
    • Timeframe: It typically takes three to six months of using all naturally occurring practice opportunities for the new habit to become the new default choice.
  5. Use Mental Rehearsal: Mental rehearsal (imagining the action) activates the same neural circuitry as the real activity, counting as practice time and increasing performance ability when the actual moment comes.

5.3 Final Comprehensive Summary: Core Ideas, Frameworks, and Methodologies

Leadership: The Power of Emotional Intelligence asserts that Emotional Intelligence (EI) is the crucial ingredient that differentiates outstanding leaders from merely average ones, especially in senior executive roles. While intellectual and technical skills are necessary entry-level requirements, the core of leadership effectiveness rests on four domains of EI: Self-Awareness, Self-Management, Social Awareness, and Relationship Management.

5.3.1 Core Frameworks and Methodologies

5.3.1.1 I. The Primal Nature of Leadership and Resonance

Leadership is a primal emotional task defined by the leader’s ability to drive collective emotions. This emotional impact is due to the brain’s open loop design (interpersonal limbic regulation), which means emotions are highly contagious; everyone catches the leader’s mood, making the leader the group’s emotional guide.

  • Goal: Resonance: Effective leaders create resonance (neural harmony), which facilitates peak cognitive efficiency and performance (flow states).
  • Avoiding Dissonance: Leaders who spread bad moods, irritation, or anxiety create dissonance, impairing mental abilities, eroding empathy, and decreasing the group’s collective intelligence.

5.3.1.2 II. The Six Leadership Styles

The most effective leaders are not rigid but fluid, drawing on six distinct leadership styles based on the specific business situation, with the goal of maximizing organizational climate (flexibility, responsibility, standards, clarity, rewards, and commitment).

Positive Impact Negative Impact (Use Sparingly)
Authoritative: Mobilizing people toward a shared, compelling vision (most effective overall, especially when a business is adrift). Pacesetting: Setting extremely high standards and exemplifying them; often leads to micromanagement, overwhelmed employees, and lost initiative.
Coaching: Developing individuals by helping them identify strengths, set long-term goals, and providing instruction and feedback (least used style, but highly positive impact). Coercive: Demanding immediate compliance; destroys flexibility, responsibility, and commitment. Only suitable for genuine emergencies or turnarounds to break failed habits.
Affiliative: Creating emotional bonds and harmony; valuing people over tasks (excellent for repairing broken trust or increasing morale).
Democratic: Building consensus through participation and gathering ideas (good when the leader is uncertain or needs fresh execution ideas).

5.3.1.3 III. The Sweet Spot for Achievement (The Inverted U)

Optimal work performance occurs at the peak of the inverted U curve, which charts the relationship between challenge/stress and cognitive efficiency. Leaders must ensure employees remain engaged but not overwhelmed.

  • Frazzle: Excessive anxiety or stress causes cognitive dysfunction as the emotional centers (amygdala) sideline the thinking brain (prefrontal cortex), crippling abilities needed for learning, creativity, and focused attention.
  • Secure Base: A crucial leadership function is acting as a secure base, creating an environment of trust, support, and appreciation that allows employees to explore, innovate, and take necessary risks without being paralyzed by fear of failure or rejection.

5.3.1.4 IV. Practical Methods for Application

  1. The Artful Critique: Feedback should be specific, focus on actions (not character), offer a solution, and be delivered face-to-face with empathy. This prevents the destructive emotional backlash associated with generalized, angry attacks.
  2. Leveraging Group IQ: Since social harmony is the biggest factor in team success, leaders should foster cooperation and reduce emotional static. Star performers actively build three types of informal networks (communication, expertise, and trust) to coordinate efforts and solve unanticipated problems.
  3. Cultivating Rapport: High-quality social exchanges (rapport or “human moments”) require the leader to be fully present (paying attention), create non-verbal synchrony, and generate a positive feeling.
  4. Developing EI: EI competencies can be learned and improved through a systematic process leveraging neuroplasticity. This requires personal commitment, anonymous 360-degree feedback, operationalizing the goal into a specific behavioral change, and consistent practice (often three to six months) reinforced by mental rehearsal.
