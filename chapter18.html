18  Getting Things Done

18.1 Executive Summary

The Getting Things Done (GTD) methodology is a comprehensive, five-step system for achieving stress-free productivity. It is founded on the principle that the mind is for having ideas, not for holding them. To achieve a state of relaxed, focused control, all things considered unfinished—“stuff”—must be captured in a trusted system outside the mind.

The core framework involves processing everything that comes into one’s life through five distinct steps: Capture, Clarify, Organize, Reflect, and Engage. This process transforms ambiguous commitments into clearly defined desired outcomes (projects) and concrete next actions. A critical component of this framework is the identification of the “next physical, visible activity” required to move a commitment forward. This ensures that when time and energy allow, one can immediately choose an action without needing further deliberation.

Key operational standards include:

  • The Two-Minute Rule: If defining an action takes less than two minutes, do it immediately when it is defined, as it takes longer to track it than to complete it.
  • Next-Action Decision Making: Every commitment must have a defined next action; without this decision, items remain stuck (procrastinated).
  • The Weekly Review: This crucial practice ensures the system stays current, complete, and trusted, serving as the “master key” to maintaining focused control.

By consistently applying the GTD framework, individuals gain coherence across all their activities (horizontal control) and manage the successful development of individual topics and projects (vertical control), leading to clarity, confidence, and increased capacity for higher-level thinking (Horizons 3–5).

18.2 The Art of Getting Things Done

18.2.1 A New Practice for a New Reality

This chapter introduces the fundamental premise that traditional time-management models are insufficient for handling the volume and complexity of modern knowledge work. Work today often lacks clear edges or definitions of completion.

18.2.1.1 Key Concepts

The Need for Relaxed Control and Full Presence It is possible to function productively with a clear head and a positive sense of relaxed control, a state critical for successful professionals. This ideal state allows one to be fully present—whether working, socializing, or relaxing—with confidence that whatever is being done is exactly what should be done at that moment. The core methods presented aim to leverage existing basic skills (writing things down, deciding outcomes and actions, making choices) into new levels of effectiveness.

The Failure of Old Models Traditional organizational models, such as using calendars, daily to-do lists, and simplified priority coding (like “ABC” priorities), are inadequate for managing the high volume and variability of the modern workload. Similarly, focusing solely on the “bigger view” (values and major goals) fails if the day-to-day commitments are distracting or overwhelming, creating subconscious resistance to taking on larger, complex goals.

The Missing Piece: Action Management The key to managing “stuff” (anything unfinished or undetermined) is not managing time, information, or priorities, but managing actions. Things get stuck not because of a lack of time, but because the next physical step, or what “doing” looks like, hasn’t been decided.

Horizontal and Vertical Action Management Effective workflow requires two types of control:

  1. Horizontal Control: Maintaining coherence across the breadth of all activities and commitments in one’s life (e.g., managing the transition between work, family, social life, etc.). A good system must track many items and allow focus to shift quickly.
  2. Vertical Control: Managing thinking, development, and coordination within individual topics or projects. This is “project planning” in a broad sense, fleshing out ideas, details, and sequences required for a single endeavor.

The Major Change: Getting It All Out of Your Head There is an inverse relationship between how much something is on your mind and how much it is getting done. The goal is to capture and organize 100% of “stuff” in objective tools, not relying on the mind for storage. The short-term memory (like a computer’s RAM) has limited capacity, and incomplete items stored there reduce focus and performance.

18.2.1.2 Step-by-Step Breakdown: Dealing with Internal Commitments

Managing commitments requires specific activities and behaviors:

  1. Capture: Anything considered unfinished must be captured in a trusted collection tool outside the mind.
  2. Clarify: Determine exactly what the commitment is and decide the next action, if any, to make progress.
  3. Organize: Keep reminders of all required actions organized in a system reviewed regularly.
Practical Application: The Essence of Stuff
Stuff is anything allowed into your psychological or physical world that doesn’t belong where it is, but for which the desired outcome and next action have not been determined.
Stuff transforms into a clear inventory of meaningful actions, projects, and usable information once clarified.
The goal is to apply concentrated thinking to define desired outcomes and requisite next actions.

Recap

  • Traditional time management fails due to the volume and complexity of modern “knowledge work”.
  • The highest performance state is achieved through “mind like water”—relaxed, focused control.
  • Anxiety is caused by a lack of control, organization, preparation, and action.
  • The mind’s short-term memory (RAM) is not a storage place; relying on it for remembering incomplete tasks creates psychological load.
  • Managing all commitments requires clarity on the outcome, defining the next physical action, and tracking reminders in a trusted system.
  • Productivity comes from managing actions, not time, information, or priorities.
  • A bottom-up approach (getting control of the mundane) is often more effective than a top-down approach (vision setting).
  • Horizontal control manages the breadth of activities; vertical control manages project depth.

18.2.2 Getting Control of Your Life: The Five Steps of Mastering Workflow

Mastering workflow involves five discrete stages that must be executed consistently and separately, as the quality of the workflow management is only as good as the weakest link. Trying to execute all five steps simultaneously is a major reason people fail to get organized.

18.2.2.1 Framework: The Five Steps of Mastering Workflow

The horizontal management of one’s life is accomplished by moving through these phases:

  1. Capture: Collect all items that have your attention.
  2. Clarify: Define what each item means and what to do about it.
  3. Organize: Place the results into the correct categories and compartments.
  4. Reflect: Review the system contents to assess options.
  5. Engage: Execute the chosen actions.

18.2.2.2 1. Capture: Corralling Your Stuff

Core Concept: The goal is to eliminate “holes in your bucket” by collecting placeholders for 100 percent of the “incompletes”—anything, big or small, personal or professional, that you are internally committed to changing or deciding about. If it’s not in your trusted system, it is still resident in your mental space.

Methodology: Capturing Successfully

  • Get It All Out of Your Head: You must believe that you have truly captured everything for your mind to let go of the task of remembering.
  • Minimize the Number of Capture Locations: Use as many collection tools (in-trays, pads, digital devices) as you need to be ubiquitous, but as few as possible to make processing manageable.
  • Empty the Capture Tools Regularly: Collection tools must be emptied and processed promptly, otherwise they cease to function effectively.

18.2.2.3 2. Clarify: Processing Your Incompletes

Core Concept: Clarifying is the process of deciding the meaning of each captured item. This involves determining if an item is actionable and, if so, what the desired outcome and next action are.

The Clarifying Workflow (Processing “In” to Empty) For every item captured, ask:

  1. What is it? Identify the item or commitment.
  2. Is it actionable? Is something required to move this forward?
    • If No Action Required:
      • Trash: Throw it away, shred it, or recycle it.
      • Incubate: Put it on hold for later reassessment (Someday/Maybe list or tickler file).
      • Reference: File it so it can be retrieved later.
    • If Actionable (Yes):
      • What is the desired outcome (Project)? If more than one step is required, define the desired result (the project) and capture it on a Projects list.
      • What is the Next Action? Identify the next physical, visible activity required to move the item toward completion.

Next Action Decision: Do It, Delegate It, or Defer It

  • Do It: If the action takes less than two minutes (The Two-Minute Rule), do it immediately. This is the efficiency cutoff.
  • Delegate It: If the action takes longer than two minutes, ask “Am I the right person to do this?” If no, delegate it to the appropriate entity and place a reminder on a Waiting For list.
  • Defer It: If the action takes longer than two minutes and you are the right person to do it, defer it and track it on appropriate Next Actions lists.

18.2.2.4 3. Organize: Setting Up the Right Buckets

Core Concept: Organizing means that where something is located matches what it means to you. This step involves setting up the “right buckets”—the categories for reminders and materials resulting from the clarification process. These categories must be pristinely distinct from one another.

The Eight Organizational Categories

  1. Projects List: An index of every desired result requiring more than one action step to complete, committed to within a year. This list is primarily used for regular review, not daily execution.
  2. Project Support Material: Details, plans, and supporting information related to active projects, kept in separate folders, binders, or digital files.
  3. Calendar: Holds only time-specific actions (appointments), day-specific actions (like a call that must happen Tuesday), and day-specific information (like event directions or due dates). Crucially, it should not hold general daily to-do lists.
  4. Next Actions Lists: Holds all deferred actions categorized by the context required to perform them (e.g., location, tool, or person needed).
    • Common Categories: Calls, At Computer, Errands, At Office (miscellaneous), At Home, Anywhere, Agendas (for specific people or meetings), Read/Review (for longer reading tasks).
  5. Waiting For List: A list tracking delegated tasks or expected items from others, along with the date they were delegated or requested.
  6. Reference Material: Nonactionable information (paper or digital) kept for retrieval, organized by topic or area. The core tool is a fast, functional, and simple general-reference filing system (often single A–Z).
  7. Someday/Maybe List: A repository for ideas, projects, and fantasies that currently have no commitment to action, but which one may want to pursue in the future (e.g., “Learn Spanish,” “Get a sailboat”).
  8. Trash: Items to be discarded.

18.2.2.5 4. Reflect: Keeping the System Functional

Core Concept: Reflection is the act of stepping back and reviewing the system contents to keep the entire inventory—and one’s awareness—current and functional. This prevents the mind from taking back the job of remembering.

The Critical Success Factor: The Weekly Review The Weekly Review is the nonnegotiable practice necessary to maintain psychological equilibrium and trust in the system. It ensures that all commitments are assessed frequently enough to stay off the mind.

The Weekly Review Steps (Three-Part Drill)

  1. Get Clear (Processing Loose Ends):
    • Collect loose papers and materials (from pockets, desks, devices).
    • Get “In” to empty (process all notes, e-mails, and inputs to zero).
    • Empty your head (capture any remaining thoughts as new actions/projects).
  2. Get Current (Updating Maps):
    • Review Next Actions lists (mark off completions and add new next steps).
    • Review previous calendar data (capture remaining actions/info).
    • Review upcoming calendar (identify required preparation actions).
    • Review Waiting For list (follow up as needed).
    • Review Projects list (ensure every project has a current next action).
    • Review relevant checklists (including Areas of Focus).
  3. Get Creative (Generating New Ideas):
    • Review Someday/Maybe list (pull items forward into active projects if motivated).
    • Review Goals and Vision (Horizons 3–5) to generate new ideas, projects, and commitments.

18.2.2.6 5. Engage: Making the Best Action Choices

Core Concept: Engaging is the execution phase—choosing the optimal action in the moment. If the previous four steps are maintained, action choices can be made intuitively, based on current reality.

Choosing Actions in the Moment: The Four-Criteria Model

  1. Context: What location, people, or tools are available to you right now? (e.g., At Phone, At Computer, At Home).
  2. Time Available: How much time do you have before your next commitment? Use “weird time” (small, random windows of time) productively by matching actions to available minutes.
  3. Energy Available: How much mental or physical energy do you have? Match high-energy tasks with high-energy times, and use low-energy times for simple tasks (e.g., filing, reading, organizing).
  4. Priority: Given the first three constraints, what action from your lists is the most important to accomplish?.
Application: The Threefold Model for Daily Work
1. Doing Predefined Work: Working from your Next Actions lists and Calendar (executing tasks previously clarified).
2. Doing Work as It Shows Up: Handling unexpected actions, interruptions, or crises, including two-minute tasks (This is often reactive).
3. Defining Your Work: The process of capturing and clarifying new inputs, organizing the results, and breaking down projects.

Recap

  • The GTD framework is a five-step continuous process: Capture, Clarify, Organize, Reflect, and Engage.
  • Clarifying involves determining if an item is actionable (resulting in a next action or project) or nonactionable (resulting in trash, incubation, or reference).
  • The Two-Minute Rule dictates immediate action to maintain efficiency.
  • Deferred actions are organized by context (e.g., @Calls, @Errands) to ensure reminders appear when they can be acted upon.
  • A Project is any desired outcome requiring more than one step to complete.
  • The Calendar is sacred territory, reserved only for time- or day-specific obligations, not general to-dos.
  • The Weekly Review is essential for maintaining trust, cleanliness, and completeness of the entire system.
  • Engagement involves choosing actions based on context, time, energy, and priority.

18.2.3 Getting Projects Creatively Under Way: The Five Phases of Project Planning

While horizontal focus (the five steps of workflow) is often sufficient, complex projects require structured, creative thought—vertical focus. This informal planning is often called “back-of-the-envelope planning”.

18.2.3.1 Framework: The Natural Planning Model

The human brain naturally follows a five-step process whenever it seeks to execute a desired outcome:

  1. Defining Purpose and Principles: Clarifying the “Why”.
    • Purpose: The intended function or goal (e.g., satisfying hunger, signing a business deal). Clear purpose creates decision-making criteria, aligns resources, and expands creative options.
    • Principles: The standards, values, or behaviors that define the boundaries of the plan (e.g., affordability, quality, ethical conduct).
  2. Outcome Visioning: Imagining the successful result.
    • This is the “What”—a clear mental picture of what success will look, sound, and feel like. This focus affects perception and performance, acting as a filter for relevant information (like a computer’s search function).
  3. Brainstorming: Generating all potential ideas, details, and components related to the vision.
    • Rules for effective brainstorming: Don’t judge, challenge, evaluate, or criticize; go for quantity, not quality; put analysis and organization in the background. Capturing these ideas externally (like mind mapping or listing) prevents the mind from having to hold them.
  4. Organizing: Structuring the gathered ideas by components, sequences, and/or priorities.
    • This is where natural structure emerges from the raw brainstormed data. The result often involves hierarchical outlines or flowcharts.
  5. Identifying Next Actions: Determining the specific physical steps required to move the project forward, for every active component.

18.2.3.2 Application: When to Plan More Deeply

Most projects require only in-head planning to identify the next action. The Natural Planning Model (NPM) should be deliberately applied when a project is new, stuck, or ambiguous.

  • Need More Clarity? Shift thinking up the planning scale. If brainstorming is fuzzy, re-examine the outcome/vision; if the outcome is unclear, return to the core purpose.
  • Need More to Be Happening (Action)? Shift thinking down the planning scale. If there is enthusiasm about the purpose but no action, the vision may be unclear. If brainstorming is done but action is stuck, rigor is required to organize deliverables and define next actions.

Recap

  • Vertical focus (project planning) is necessary for complex situations.
  • The Natural Planning Model (NPM) mirrors the brain’s instinctual process for achieving outcomes.
  • The five phases of NPM are Purpose, Vision, Brainstorming, Organizing, and Next Actions.
  • Purpose (the “Why”) defines criteria and aligns resources.
  • Vision (the “What”) focuses the mind’s resources to generate solutions (teleological function).
  • Brainstorming should prioritize quantity of ideas and suspend judgment.
  • Projects are sufficiently planned when every active component has a next action assigned.
  • If a project is still on your mind, more thinking (planning) is required.

18.3 Practicing Stress-Free Productivity

18.3.1 Getting Started: Setting Up the Time, Space, and Tools

Successful implementation of the GTD methodology is supported by setting up the physical and digital environment optimally.

18.3.1.1 Setting Up Time and Space

  • Time Commitment: An ideal initial implementation takes two whole, contiguous days. This minimizes interruption, which can double the time needed for the intensive front-end cognitive work of capturing and clarifying.
  • Work Environment: A dedicated, individual, self-contained workspace (at home, at work, and even in transit) is critical.
    • The basics require a writing surface, room for an in-tray, and core digital tools.
    • Most professionals need sufficient filing space (at least two drawers) for general reference and project support materials.
  • Office in Transit: An efficiently organized micro-office (briefcase/pack) is needed to take advantage of short “weird time” windows and moments between locations.

18.3.1.2 Step-by-Step Breakdown: Essential Tools

The necessary tools are either low-tech (paper/physical) or high-tech (digital).

Tool Category Essential Items Success Factors/Notes
Physical Processing Paper-holding trays (at least three), plain letter-size paper, Post-its, clips, stapler, tape, rubber bands. Trays should be stackable and side-facing for easy retrieval. Plain paper is used for capturing one thought/idea per sheet for individual processing.
Organization/Tracking An automatic labeler, file folders, a calendar, wastebasket/recycling bins. The Labeler is a “surprisingly useful tool” that makes filing easy and attractive. The calendar holds the “hard landscape” of commitments—things that must happen.
Reference System General-reference files (physical and digital) are crucial. The filing system must be fast, functional, and fun. If it takes longer than one minute to file, it will be resisted. Recommended: A single A–Z system for general reference to minimize ambiguity about where something is filed. File drawers should be kept less than three-quarters full to reduce unconscious resistance to filing.

Recap

  • Implementation works best with an initial block of uninterrupted time (ideally two days).
  • Setting up a dedicated, functional workspace (the “home base”) minimizes unconscious resistance to processing.
  • A dedicated general-reference filing system is critical to prevent nonactionable clutter from clogging the workflow.
  • A single A-Z filing system is recommended for general reference to simplify searching.
  • The automatic labeler should be treated as a basic, indispensable tool.
  • A key operational trick is to “Put It in Front of the Door”—using an external trigger to ensure important items are remembered at the right time.

18.3.2 Capturing: Corralling Your “Stuff”

This chapter details the physical and mental gathering process necessary to achieve the “empty head” state.

18.3.2.1 Step-by-Step Breakdown: The Capture Process

The capture phase is complete only when one can easily see the outer edges of the inventory of everything that has attention.

  1. Preparation: Before starting, handle any urgent existing commitments so the mind is free to focus entirely on the capture task.
  2. Boundary Definition: Identify items that can stay where they are because they require no action: Supplies, Reference Material, Decoration, and Equipment. Everything else needs to be captured.
  3. Physical Gathering: Systematically move through all locations (desktop, drawers, countertops, files, shelves, etc.).
    • If an item is too large (e.g., a piece of equipment), write a note to represent it and put the note into the in-tray.
    • If a stack is too large for the in-tray, create stacks around it, clearly delineating them as “in”.
    • Avoid the “Purge-and-Organize Bug”—the primary goal is capturing, not cleaning or organizing, which happens later in the process.
  4. Mental Gathering (Mind Sweep): Once the physical environment is clear, capture all remaining items lingering in your mental space.
    • Use plain paper to write out each thought, idea, or project on a separate sheet. This creates discrete placeholders for later processing.
    • Use the Incompletion Triggers list (covering professional, personal, financial, planning, systems, meetings, and waiting for) to jog the memory and ensure completeness [307–310].
  5. Final Inventory: The final inventory of “in” includes all physical papers/notes, voice mails (transferred to paper notes), task lists (printed out), and digital messages staged in the communication software “in” area.
Note on Stuff and Attention
Resistance to Capture: People resist capturing items because they often seem “not that important,” but these items still nag at the consciousness and take up disproportionate energy.
Pack Rat vs. Action Management: The GTD method is not necessarily advocacy for minimalism. One can keep twelve boxes of old journals, provided they are where they want them to be and any required actions about them (e.g., “organize journals”) are captured and processed.

Recap

  • The Capture phase should include every single incomplete commitment, personally and professionally, to ensure the mind fully trusts the external system.
  • Items requiring no action (supplies, reference, decoration, equipment) should remain in place.
  • If an item is too big for the in-tray, a note representing it should be captured instead.
  • The “Mind Sweep” is used to extract lurking mental commitments, with each thought written on a separate note.
  • The goal is to get everything into the funnel quickly, postponing in-depth clarification and organizing until the next step.

18.3.3 Clarifying: Getting “In” to Empty

This is the central process of deciding what an item is and what to do about it. It requires fast, hard thinking about the next action.

18.3.3.1 Processing Guidelines

  • Process the top item first: Start at one end and move in order. Avoid emergency scanning or picking and choosing items, as this undermines the system’s integrity.
  • Process one item at a time: Do not let the eyes wander to other items. Focused attention forces the decision needed to move forward.
  • Never put anything back into “in”: Once an item is touched, it must be determined what it means and where it needs to go, even if the decision is to defer action.

18.3.3.2 Step-by-Step Breakdown: The Clarifying Process

  1. Trash/Reference/Incubate: If the item is nonactionable, dispose of it, file it in the reference system, or stage it for future review (incubate).
  2. Determine Next Action: If the item is actionable, identify the next physical, visible activity required to move the situation toward closure.
    • Example: For “Clean the garage,” the next action might be “Call John re: refrigerator in garage”.
    • If the next step is “deciding what to do,” this is not an action. There is always a preceding physical activity (e.g., “Draft ideas for the decision,” or “Call Fred to get input”) to facilitate the decision.
  3. Execute the 3 D’s: Decide whether to Do it, Delegate it, or Defer it.
Methodology: The Two-Minute Rule
Rule: If an action will take less than two minutes, it should be done at the moment it is defined.
Rationale: It’s more efficient to do the task now than to spend time capturing, clarifying, organizing, and reflecting on it later.
Flexibility: The two-minute benchmark is a guideline. In focused processing sessions, it may be extended to five or ten minutes. When rushing, it might be shortened.
  1. Tracking Delegation: If delegated, the item must be tracked on a Waiting For list. The date of delegation should be recorded, as follow-up responsibility now rests with the delegator.
  2. Tracking Deferral: If the action is the user’s to do and takes longer than two minutes, it is deferred to the appropriate Calendar (if time-sensitive) or Next Actions list (if as-soon-as-possible).
  3. Identifying Projects: After identifying next actions, check if the defined action will complete the commitment. If more than one step is required, define the desired result as a Project (outcome requiring more than one action step) and add it to the Projects list.

Recap

  • Processing requires focused effort, moving through items one at a time from top to bottom.
  • The core difficulty in clarifying is determining the absolute next physical step.
  • The next action decision releases mental energy by creating a concrete, achievable task instead of an overwhelming, amorphous goal.
  • The two-minute rule should be applied primarily when engaging with new input.
  • Delegated tasks must be tracked on the Waiting For list to manage the commitment of others.
  • The process culminates in generating the Projects list, ensuring every commitment has a defined outcome.

18.3.4 Organizing: Setting Up the Right Buckets

Airtight organization requires that categories are kept pristinely distinct (hard edges). The organizational system is composed entirely of lists and folders.

18.3.4.1 Organizing Action Reminders

Actions are grouped by the most appropriate reminder structure: Calendar (for fixed time/day) or Next Actions Lists (for as-soon-as-possible).

Category Purpose and Application
Calendar For time-specific actions (appointments) and day-specific actions/information (e.g., directions, due dates, or reminders that must surface on a certain day).
Next Actions Lists (Context-Based) The best way to be reminded of an “as soon as I can” action is by the particular context required. Sorting by context maximizes productivity by leveraging windows of time and focused energy (e.g., consolidating all calls).
Agendas Lists Running lists of items to discuss with specific people (bosses, partners, assistants, advisors) or in standing meetings. This minimizes interruptions and maximizes communication efficiency.
Read/Review For items requiring more than two minutes of reading time. Physical materials are often managed in a separate tray; digital content is managed in a separate digital folder.
Waiting For List Inventory of delegated or expected items. This must be reviewed regularly to follow up on the commitments of others.

18.3.4.2 Organizing Nonactionable Data (Reference and Incubation)

Nonactionable items fall into three categories: Trash, Reference, and Incubation.

  1. Reference Materials: Information kept for later use.
    • Goal: To have a simple library of data, easily retrievable.
    • Systems: General-reference filing (single A-Z system for paper and ad hoc digital items); Large-category filing (for complex topics needing 50+ folders); Contact managers.
    • Principle: Once distinguished from actionable items, reference material adds no psychological weight, allowing one to keep as much as space permits.
  2. Incubation: Items staged for later reassessment.
    • Someday/Maybe List: Inventory of projects/ideas that are optional or not currently actionable (e.g., hobby ideas, future trips). Reviewing this list periodically is crucial for harnessing creative imaginings.
    • Tickler File (Bring-Forward File): A 43-folder system (31 days, 12 months) used to hold physical reminders (or notes) that need to surface on a specific future date. This is a unique executive function: deciding not to decide until a last responsible moment.
Methodology: Setting Up a Physical Tickler File
1. Acquire 43 file folders: 31 labeled 1-31 (days), 12 labeled with months.
2. Arrange the folders in a container:
3. Place the daily files in front, starting with the next day’s date, until the 31st.
4. Place the 12 monthly files behind the daily files, starting with the next month.
Daily Routine: Every day, check the daily file. The contents are pulled out and processed in the in-tray. The emptied file is moved to the back, labeled with the following month’s correct date.

18.3.4.3 Application: Checklists

Checklists are “recipes of potential ingredients” used to organize procedures, evaluate potential steps, or ensure nothing is missed in an area of responsibility.

  • Purpose: To extend the number of important operations one can perform without thinking, conserving mental energy.
  • Application: Used for blueprints of work (e.g., Job Areas of Responsibility), regular maintenance procedures (e.g., Weekly Review steps), or in novel situations where control is required.
  • Principle: They provide structure until a procedure becomes automatic (like training wheels), allowing the mind to focus on exceptions rather than routines.

Recap

  • Organization hinges on separating action from nonaction and keeping categories distinct.
  • The next-action decision is crucial, as it determines which context-based list receives the reminder.
  • Next actions should be grouped by context (tool, location, or person) to maximize efficiency when one is in that mode.
  • A single A-Z general-reference system is preferred over multiple project-based filing systems for flexibility.
  • Incubation stages non-urgent ideas (Someday/Maybe list) or date-specific reminders (Tickler File).
  • The Tickler File is a low-tech “personal postal service” for ensuring physical items surface on a future date.
  • Checklists document procedures and areas of accountability, supporting focus and allowing operations to advance without constant deliberation.

18.3.5 Reflecting: Keeping It All Fresh and Functional

Consistent reflection is the prerequisite for clarity and stability.

18.3.5.1 What to Look At, When

  • Calendar: Most frequently reviewed item. This handles the “hard landscape” of the day’s fixed commitments.
  • Next Action Lists: Reviewed whenever one has discretionary time and the corresponding context (e.g., access to a phone).
  • Projects, Waiting For, and Someday/Maybe Lists: Reviewed only as often as needed to prevent the user from wondering about their status. For most people, this means during the Weekly Review.

18.3.5.2 Step-by-Step Breakdown: The Weekly Review (Expanded)

The Weekly Review ensures that all three requirements for relaxed control are met: all “in” is processed, all systems are updated, and new creative ideas are captured.

1. Get Clear

  • Collect all loose papers, notes, and receipts from all locations (pockets, briefcase, digital inboxes) into the in-tray.
  • Process all “In” to empty (notes, e-mails, etc.), applying the clarifying process to each item.
  • Empty Your Head (mind sweep) to capture any remaining commitments not yet externalized.

2. Get Current

  • Review Next Actions Lists (check off completed, identify new next steps for old items).
  • Review Previous Calendar (scan last 2-3 weeks for missed actions or information, and ensure the calendar is archive-ready).
  • Review Upcoming Calendar (look ahead for preparations needed for upcoming meetings, travel, or deadlines).
  • Review Waiting For List (follow up on stagnant items or check off received items).
  • Review Projects List (check status of each project, ensuring a next action is defined for all active projects).
  • Review Checklists (Roles, Goals, etc.).

3. Get Creative

  • Review Someday/Maybe List (pull forward items that are now relevant or exciting).
  • Review Horizons of Focus (Horizons 3–5) to ensure alignment and trigger new objectives or projects.

18.3.5.3 Practical Application: Scheduling the Review

The Weekly Review is highly effective because it mimics the pre-vacation clear-out.

  • Ideal Time: Block out two hours early in the afternoon of the last workday (e.g., Friday).
  • Rationale: The week’s events are fresh, and there is still time to reach colleagues for necessary actions before the weekend.
  • Customization: The review time should be tailored to one’s personal lifestyle (e.g., long commute, Saturday morning coffee shop).

Recap

  • Consistent reviewing is required for the mind to trust the external system and remain clear.
  • Most day-to-day review is an accumulation of brief moments of checking the calendar and context-based Next Actions lists.
  • The Weekly Review is the master key for maintaining system integrity and keeping up with the “whirlwind of activity”.
  • The three components of the review are Get Clear, Get Current, and Get Creative.
  • During the review, the Projects List is scanned to ensure a next action is defined for everything in motion.
  • Reviewing the Someday/Maybe list is the primary component of the “Get Creative” phase.
  • The ideal time for the Weekly Review is typically two hours on a Friday afternoon.

18.3.6 Engaging: Making the Best Action Choices

Engaging is the process of making appropriate choices about what to do at any point in time, supported by three different priority frameworks.

18.3.6.1 1. The Four-Criteria Model (In-the-Moment)

This model, is used for choosing which task to execute from the Next Actions lists.

  • Context: Defines what can possibly be done where you are, with the tools you have. Organizing lists by context automatically grounds actions in reality.
  • Time Available: Match actions to the precise window of time available. Using “weird time” productively requires lists to include short, simple actions.
  • Energy Available: Match actions to current vitality level. Use low-energy times for non-demanding tasks (e.g., filing, organizing, light reading).
  • Priority: After the first three constraints are considered, use the remaining two frameworks (Threefold and Six-Level Models) to determine the relative importance of the remaining options.

18.3.6.2 2. The Threefold Model for Evaluating Daily Work

This defines the three kinds of activities competing for attention on a daily basis:

  1. Doing Predefined Work: Working through context-based Next Actions lists and the Calendar.
  2. Doing Work as It Shows Up: Handling unplanned interruptions, communications, or crises. There are no interruptions—only mismanaged inputs; if the system is current, responding to surprises is a conscious choice.
  3. Defining Your Work: Processing the in-tray, clarifying new inputs, and breaking down projects.

18.3.6.3 3. The Six-Level Model for Reviewing Your Own Work

This provides the hierarchical perspective (altitude) necessary to assign priority and meaning to actions. Priorities flow from the top down.

Horizon Level (Altitude) Focus & Definition Application & Review Cycle
Horizon 5: Purpose and Principles Ultimate intention, core values, and raison d’être. Ongoing criteria for monitoring and correcting activities. Why does your organization/life exist?.
Horizon 4: Vision Long-term (3–5 year) desired outcomes and ideal scenarios. Reviewed as often as needed to ensure the immediate work is pointing in the right direction.
Horizon 3: Goals Mid- to long-term (1–2 year) objectives and outcomes. Provides criteria for assessing life direction and setting new projects.
Horizon 2: Areas of Focus/Accountabilities Roles and responsibilities that must be maintained at a certain standard (e.g., staff development, health, finances). Used as a checklist to ensure all necessary commitments have associated projects. Reviewed every 1–3 months.
Horizon 1: Current Projects All outcomes requiring more than one action step to complete within the next year. The core of the Weekly Review, ensuring a next action is active for every commitment.
Ground: Current Actions The accumulated inventory of all next actions (on context lists). Reviewed daily, moment-to-moment, using the Four-Criteria Model.

Working from the Bottom Up: While priorities logically flow top-down, GTD suggests starting “bottom-up” (Ground and Horizon 1). Gaining control of the current, mundane details clears the inner decks, allowing the mind to focus constructively on the more meaningful and elusive higher levels without resistance.

Recap

  • Action choices are governed by the Four-Criteria Model: Context, Time, Energy, and Priority.
  • Organizing lists by Context is crucial because it ensures that only runnable actions are visible when the resources are available.
  • Daily work consists of pre-defined work, emerging work (surprises), and defining new work.
  • The Six-Level Model (Horizons of Focus) provides the hierarchical framework for strategic prioritization.
  • Horizons 2 (Areas of Focus) helps generate projects to maintain standards and roles.
  • Operating from the bottom up (Ground and Horizon 1) provides the foundation of control and trust necessary before focusing effectively on higher goals (Horizons 3–5).

18.3.7 Getting Projects Under Control

This chapter addresses the methods for deep, or vertical, thinking on projects that require more planning beyond simply defining the next action.

18.3.7.1 Planning Steps for Projects

When a project is stuck or requires more clarity, planning actions must be defined and executed.

  1. Brainstorming Action: If the Next Action is “Draft ideas re X,” this action is placed on the @Computer or @Anywhere list, depending on the preferred medium (digital vs. paper mind map).
  2. Organizing Action: If notes are collected, the next action may be “Organize Project X notes,” placed on the @Office or @Anywhere list based on location of files.
  3. Information Gathering: The next action may be to gather information needed before planning can begin (e.g., “Surf Web for vendor options”).

18.3.7.2 Tools and Structures to Support Deep Thinking

Informal, external tools are key to sustaining creative thought beyond the mind’s limited focus time (a few minutes).

  • Writing Tools: High-quality pens and perforated paper should be universally accessible to capture ideas quickly.
  • Whiteboards/Easels: Provide large visual space for brainstorming, helping anchor thinking and fostering ad hoc visual communication.
  • Software Tools: Mind-mapping and outlining applications are highly useful for capturing and structuring informal project plans digitally.
  • Project Files: Separate file folders or loose-leaf pages should be created for new topics immediately, even if only containing raw notes.
Practical Application: Using Ideas Effectively
Clear the Deck: Before a deep-thinking session, clear the environment and minimize distractions.
Focused Engagement: Use the Natural Planning Model (NPM) steps as a structure for the session: start with Purpose, move to Vision, then capture all related ideas (Brainstorm).
Translate Thinking: Translate the resulting plan into the first Next Action and ensure that thought fragments are filed as Project Support Material.

Recap

  • Most projects require minimal planning, but the most complex 5% may need a deliberate application of the Natural Planning Model.
  • More informal, back-of-the-envelope planning is required more often than formal project management.
  • The next action for an ambiguous project is often “Draft ideas re X” or “Organize Project X notes”.
  • External tools like whiteboards, pens, and paper pads help anchor thinking and are essential for focused engagement over long periods.
  • The goal of vertical planning is to move a project from the stuck state to a clear next action, enabling horizontal movement.

18.4 The Power of the Key Principles

18.4.1 The Power of the Capturing Habit

The discipline of capturing 100% of “stuff” creates profound personal and organizational benefits.

18.4.1.1 Personal Benefits of Capturing

The initial experience of a full capture is often described as feeling “so bad, and yet so good”. The bad part is acknowledging the full, overwhelming inventory; the good part is the immediate relief and clarity from externalizing it.

  • Integrity and Trust: When agreements with oneself are externalized, they can be renegotiated consciously. It is impossible to renegotiate agreements you cannot remember making, yet the subconscious holds you accountable for them, creating stress.
  • Saying No with Integrity: Maintaining a complete inventory makes it easier to decline new commitments because one has an objective view of the current workload.
  • Eliminating Psychological Drag: Items held in the head take up disproportionately more or less attention than they deserve. Capturing everything eliminates this “dull sameness” of pressure and frees the mind to focus on the task at hand.
  • Radical Departure: Traditional time management errs by suggesting that unimportant tasks are not worth tracking. In GTD, everything is tracked—because if it is on the mind, it is consuming energy that must be released.

18.4.1.2 Organizational Benefits

When a culture adopts the 100% capture standard, communications tighten, and trust increases.

  • Reducing “Interrupt-itis”: Organizations with poor capture systems suffer from constant interruptions because people cannot trust putting communications into the system. Timely responses via the trusted system (e.g., emptying an e-mail inbox) reduce the perceived need for face-to-face follow-up meetings.
  • Facilitating Renegotiation: A complete capture system allows for continuous, healthy renegotiation of commitments, which is critical in a world where everyone has more to do than they can manage.
  • Tight Ship: Collective adoption of the capture standard creates a foundation of efficiency and focus, even if the strategic direction (Horizons 3–5) needs adjustment.

Recap

  • Capturing 100% of commitments (open loops) is necessary for the mind to fully relax.
  • The subconscious part of the mind holds all commitments in the present tense, creating failure and stress if they cannot all be done immediately.
  • Capturing allows conscious, objective renegotiation of agreements, strengthening integrity.
  • In a capture-focused culture, trust in the system increases, minimizing unnecessary interruptions and follow-up.
  • Uncaptured incompletions create a “psychological drag” that limits clarity and focus.

18.4.2 The Power of the Next-Action Decision

The commitment to defining the next action as a standard operating query is transformative, leading to increased energy, clarity, accountability, and productivity.

18.4.2.1 Why Next Action is Critical

The next-action technique (pioneered by Dean Acheson) forces the necessary front-end thinking before a situation turns into a crisis.

  • Creating the Option of Doing: Most “stuck” projects are stuck because the specific next physical step (e.g., “E-mail Fred for info re: the garage” vs. “Get a tune-up”) has not been defined.
  • Intelligent Dumbing Down: Deciding the next action intelligently “dumbs down” the overwhelming picture of a complex project (e.g., taxes) into a single, concrete, doable task. This shifts focus from the paralyzing negative image to a positive, winnable step.
  • Procrastination: Bright, sensitive people procrastinate most because their creative minds easily visualize all the potential negative complexities and failures of a project (e.g., getting audited), causing immediate resistance. Defining the next action circumvents this resistance.

18.4.2.2 Organizational Value of a Next-Action Standard

  1. Clarity: It forces discussions to end with a clear conclusion about whether an action is needed, what it is, and who owns it, rather than a vague sense of decision.
  2. Accountability: By defining who is responsible for the next physical step, accountability is immediately established.
  3. Productivity: It shifts behavior from avoiding thinking until the last minute (when things “blow up”) to proactively defining actions on the front end.
  4. Empowerment: Taking small, defined steps builds momentum and belief in the ability to achieve results.

Recap

  • “What’s the next action?” should become a standard query to force clarity and execution.
  • Projects get stuck because the next physical, visible step is undefined.
  • Defining the next action “intelligently dumbs down” overwhelming problems, replacing negative imagery with positive, achievable tasks.
  • Sensitive people often procrastinate because they over-visualize the complex difficulties of a task.
  • The organizational benefits of the next-action standard include clarity, immediate accountability, and increased momentum.

18.4.3 The Power of Outcome Focusing

Focusing on the desired outcome (project definition) ties high-level vision to mundane daily activity and is essential for productivity.

18.4.3.1 Significance of Applied Outcome Thinking

Defining the successful outcome is necessary before defining the next action; they are interconnected.

  • Identifying Incompletes: Everything experienced as incomplete must have a reference point for “complete”.
  • Multilevel Outcome Management: The key questions—“What does this mean to me?” and “What do I want to be true about this (desired outcome)?”—must be answered for every item, linking high-level purpose to the smallest detail.
  • The Fast Track: By applying GTD principles in the trenches, individuals accelerate their professional and personal development because they demonstrate a reliable ability to execute.
  • Productivity at its Best: Defining specific projects and next actions that address real quality-of-life issues (e.g., spending time with family) is a high manifestation of productivity.

18.4.3.2 The Power of Natural Planning

The Natural Planning Model provides an integrated, flexible framework that connects intention to execution.

  • Purpose: Healthy and mature to challenge the “why” of any activity.
  • Vision/Outcome: Being comfortable creating visions of success before the methods are clear is a phenomenal trait.
  • Brainstorming: Willingness to generate and capture all ideas without judgment accesses creative intelligence.
  • Organizing: Mental discipline required to hone ideas into components and sequences.
  • Next Action: Moving on something in the physical world is the essence of productivity.

When outcome focusing becomes an organizational standard (asking “What do we want to accomplish with this?”), it replaces negativity and resistance with focused direction.

Recap

  • Outcome focusing links high-level vision to the concrete execution required in the short run.
  • Project definition and next action definition are inseparable; one informs the other.
  • The ability to execute effectively at the operational level (GTD fundamentals) moves individuals faster toward higher career and life goals.
  • Defining desired outcomes for subtle concerns (e.g., quality of life, relationship issues) is productivity in its highest form.
  • The Natural Planning Model is a cohesive model for thinking through situations, ensuring consistency from purpose down to action.

18.4.4 GTD and Cognitive Science

Since the first edition, scientific research has validated the efficacy of GTD principles, providing a foundation for why the methods produce greater clarity, control, and focus.

Scientific Validation Category GTD Correlation
Distributed Cognition / External Mind Research confirms the mind is poor at holding (remembering) ideas but excellent at having (pattern detection) them. GTD provides an external brain (the trusted system) to store triggers, freeing the brain for creative thinking.
Relieving Cognitive Load of Incompletions Unfinished goals consume mental capacity (cognitive load). Research confirms that completion is not required to relieve the burden—only a trusted plan that ensures forward engagement is needed. Defining the next action is a sufficient “plan” if the reminder is parked in a trusted place.
Flow Theory GTD practices, such as clearing the mind and focusing attention on one task, enable individuals to find the state of “flow” (optimal experience) more easily. Completing tasks and tracking progress (feedback) also support flow.
Self-Leadership Theory GTD supports all three categories of self-leadership strategies (behavior-focused, natural reward, constructive thought patterns). The system provides self-cuing (physical artifacts spurring future action) and helps shift the mind-set from overwhelmed to motivated (constructive thought).
Implementation Intentions This research shows that connecting an action to a specific context (a cause-and-effect link) ensures the behavior is automatically enacted. GTD lists (e.g., “When I am @Home, I will [Action]”) function as optimal implementation intentions.
Psychological Capital (PsyCap) GTD builds the four components of high PsyCap (Self-efficacy, Optimism, Resilience, and Hope). Systematically defining next actions and achieving small wins builds confidence and control (Self-efficacy). The ability to recalibrate the system quickly during stress builds Resilience.

Recap

  • Cognitive science provides a formal validation for the effectiveness of GTD principles.
  • GTD functions as an “external brain” (distributed cognition), freeing the mind from memory storage.
  • The cognitive burden of unfinished tasks is relieved by simply defining the next action and placing the reminder in a trusted system.
  • GTD practices enhance the ability to achieve the psychological state of “flow” by promoting clear goals and singular focus.
  • GTD lists function as “implementation intentions,” linking specific actions to contexts for automatic execution.
  • The methodology supports positive psychological capital (PsyCap) by boosting self-efficacy, optimism, resilience, and hope.

18.4.5 The Path of GTD Mastery

GTD mastery is a lifelong process of learning and integration, not a final state of perfection. Mastery involves consistently engaging in productive behaviors to achieve clarity and stability, especially when confronted with change or ambiguity.

18.4.5.1 The Three Tiers of Mastery

Tier Focus Hallmarks
1. Mastering the Basics Getting control of the machine; awkward but effective application of fundamentals. Consistent daily use of the 5 steps; regular processing to empty the in-tray; rigid adherence to next-action definition; consistent Weekly Review.
2. Integrated Life Management Extending the horizon of control (week-to-week, month-to-month); focus shifts from the system mechanism to leveraging the system. A complete, current, and clear Projects list; a working map of roles (Horizon 2); dynamic customization of the integrated system; challenges/surprises trigger system use instead of throwing it off track.
3. Focus, Direction, and Creativity (Postgraduate) Utilizing clear internal space to optimize experience and generate novel value. Utilizing freed-up focus to explore higher commitments (Horizons 3–5) and values; leveraging the external mind to produce self-created contexts and triggers for creative ideas (e.g., using A-list checklists for creative engagement).

18.4.5.2 Key Maturity Milestones

  • Project List as Driver: At Tier 2, the Projects list becomes the “heartbeat” of the operational system. Maturity is recognizing anything that has one’s attention (concerns, worries) and translating it into an achievable outcome (project).
  • Functional Awareness: At Tier 2, the system is no longer a collection of lists but a cohesive “control room”. The user understands the essence of the model and can customize it on demand (functional awareness).
  • Leveraging External Mind: At Tier 3, the user creates structures (checklists, periodic reviews) that proactively bring valuable or inspirational topics into conscious focus, leveraging the mind’s pattern-detection ability.

Recap

  • GTD Mastery is a dynamic, lifelong path of integrating productive behaviors for clarity and stability.
  • The first tier involves achieving competence in the five fundamental steps (Capture, Clarify, Organize, Reflect, Engage).
  • The second tier (Integrated Life Management) focuses on mastering Horizons 1 and 2—making the Projects list the central driver and coordinating roles/accountabilities.
  • A key signpost of mastery is translating ambiguous tensions (e.g., neighbor boundary issues) into defined projects with clear next actions.
  • The third tier involves using the distraction-free mind to proactively generate and capture high-level creative and directional value.

18.5 Master Summary

The Getting Things Done (GTD) methodology, developed by David Allen, is a complete system designed to achieve a state of relaxed, focused control (or “mind like water”) by managing actions and commitments externally. It reframes personal management from struggling with time and priorities to effectively managing the results of one’s own behavior.

The core framework involves processing 100% of “stuff” (anything incomplete or undecided) through five interconnected phases:

  1. Capture: Gather all internal and external inputs into trusted collection tools (in-trays).
  2. Clarify: Process each item to determine if it is actionable (requiring a next action and outcome/project) or nonactionable (trash, reference, or incubation). This step includes the vital Two-Minute Rule.
  3. Organize: Place the results into specific, clearly separated categories: Calendar (fixed time/day), Next Actions Lists (grouped by context, like @Calls or @Home), Projects List (outcomes requiring multiple steps), Waiting For List, and Reference/Incubation.
  4. Reflect: Regularly review the contents of the system. The non-negotiable anchor is the Weekly Review, ensuring the entire inventory remains current, clean, and trusted.
  5. Engage: Choose what action to take in the moment based on the Four-Criteria Model (Context, Time, Energy, Priority).

When facing complex or stuck situations, Vertical Focus is engaged through the Natural Planning Model (Purpose, Vision, Brainstorming, Organizing, Next Actions). This ensures clarity of outcome and defined next actions are established before execution begins.

The power of GTD is validated by cognitive science, which confirms that externalizing and defining clear next steps relieves the cognitive burden of unfinished loops, thereby enhancing psychological capital and freeing the mind for higher-level strategic thinking (Horizons 3–5). Mastery of the system enables continuous learning and adaptation, moving the user from merely surviving daily inputs to proactively leveraging their cleared focus for creative, directional activity.
