28  Integrated Management

28.1 Dynamic Performance Frontier (DPF)

28.1.1 Defining and Visualizing the Performance Frontier

The DPF is conceptualized as the maximum value an enterprise can create at a given cost using the best available technologies, skills, and management techniques. For integrated management, this frontier is dynamically viewed by mapping performance along two dimensions: economic value creation/destruction and natural/social value creation/destruction.

This relationship is often illustrated in four quadrants:

  1. Quadrant 1 (Upper Right): Represents enterprises simultaneously creating both economic value and environmental/social value. This is the ideal location for strategically integrated enterprises.
  2. Quadrant 2: Shows where economic value is created while environmental/social value is destroyed.
  3. Quadrant 3: Involves the destruction of both economic and environmental/social value.
  4. Quadrant 4: Illustrates destroying economic value while creating environmental/social value.

Integrated management aims to strategically position an enterprise in Quadrant 1, pushing the frontier outward through innovation.

28.1.2 The Role of Innovation and Trade-offs

A core insight derived from the DPF model is the necessity of innovation in achieving simultaneous improvements in both financial (economic) and environmental/social (ESG) performance.

  • Necessity of Innovation: In the absence of substantial innovation, the DPF suggests that a firm’s financial performance may decline as its ESG performance improves. To achieve the goal of integrated management—improving both aspects at once—firms must invent new products, processes, and business models.
  • Avoiding Trade-offs: Trade-offs are typically experienced by firms operating directly on the performance frontier. However, firms located inside the frontier (often referred to as being toward the bottom-left of the performance curve) can avoid these trade-offs by adopting existing best practices and technologies to catch up to the current performance norms. Disruptive innovation—such as that categorized as “beyond greening”—pushes the entire frontier outward, offering trade-off-free solutions.

28.1.3 Pushing the Frontier Through Integrated Management

Integrated management provides the tools and strategic alignment necessary to move the organization toward and beyond the current performance frontier.

1. Strategic Alignment and Materiality: Key practices for pushing the DPF outward involve identifying material ESG issues and aligning them with strategy. The integration of sustainability is driven by the realization that ESG factors and innovation have substantial implications for financial performance.

  • Organizations like the Sustainability Accounting Standards Board (SASB) contribute to this movement by creating “materiality maps” for 88 industries. These maps help management accountants determine which ESG issues are likely to have the biggest financial impact, thereby transforming these issues into integrated value creation opportunities.

2. New Valuation Metrics: Integrated management involves challenging traditional “first cost” mentalities and incorporating environmental and social valuation into financial decision-making.

  • New measures like Integrated Future Value (IntFV), Integrated Rate of Return (IntRR), and Return on Integration (ROInt) are proposed to assess capital expenditures based on accrued environmental and social impacts and benefits, going beyond myopic costs and single bottom-line cash flows.
  • The use of internal carbon pricing or the Social Cost of Carbon Dioxide (SCC) in financial analysis enables firms to value negative externalities (waste) and the benefits of avoiding those impacts, supporting projects that reduce GHG emissions and foster innovation.

3. Adoption and Organizational Change: Firms can be categorized along an adoption curve: Innovators, Early Adopters, Early Majority, Late Majority, and Laggards.

  • The greatest difficulty lies in crossing the “chasm” between Early Adopters and the Early Majority. Innovators and Early Adopters (those already closer to the frontier) formally integrate sustainability, gaining specialized assets and a more dynamic performance frontier that creates obstacles for competitors to catch up.
  • The failure to integrate sustainability strategically often results in a widening integration chasm regarding technological competence, awareness, and institutional alignment. The integrated management movement, supported by boundary-spanning change agents, aims to close these chasms by leveraging systems and focusing on long-term goals like the UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs).

28.2 Building Shared Understanding

Key Focus Areas of Building Shared Understanding

The overarching purpose of this section is to promote awareness and facilitate a shared vision of the integrated management opportunity across all enterprise functions. This process involves recognizing that sustainable and successful enterprises must manage multiple forms of capital beyond just financial concerns, including human, intellectual, manufactured, social, and natural capital.

The sources detail this shared understanding across three core areas:

1. Integration Across Disciplines (Chapter 4): Integrated management requires working across normal lines in business to address big, systemic problems. The traditional structure of companies often suffers from too many functional silos, which limits communication to only the language of financial capital (cash).

  • Overcoming Silos and Obstacles: The importance of cross-functional inputs to decision-making is highlighted. Functional managers often look at the same problem from their narrow perspective, solving only the symptoms rather than the root cause. Specialists can become obstacles to generalists who possess a larger system perspective. Integrated management aims to overcome these obstacles by leveraging existing metrics across functions rather than introducing complex new ones, thereby simplifying performance management.
  • The Need for New Roles: The integration movement calls for boundary-spanning individuals and change agents (like Chief Sustainability Officers or even any employee acting as a “Chief Integration Officer”) to link internal functional systems with external information and foster collaboration and innovation.
  • Functional Integration Opportunities: Integration must become part of enterprise functions, moving beyond being “bolted-on to one function” to being “baked-in across the enterprise”. The sources detail how integrated management opportunities exist within key functions:
    • Accounting and Finance: These functions are crucial for valuing and recording ESG performance. They are key enablers of integrated reporting, helping to identify top-line growth and bottom-line savings by valuing social and environmental capital (IntVA).
    • Marketing: This function can influence positive change by identifying customer desire for sustainably developed products, using sustainability to build brand strength, and ensuring transparency.
    • Operations: Focused on efficiency and creating products/services without violating sustainability principles, operations management utilizes standards (like ISO 14000, 20400, 26000, and 50000) and systems (like SOS) to maximize efficiency and asset effectiveness.

2. Value Creation for Stakeholders and Shareholders (Chapter 5): The sources stress the renewed energy in understanding value creation for both shareholders and stakeholders. This is critical because value creation serves as the common language for the entire enterprise.

  • Materiality as a Priority Tool: Materiality is presented as a firm-specific social construct essential for prioritizing integration projects. Material issues are those that impact an enterprise’s ability to create, preserve, or erode economic, environmental, and social value. By focusing on material issues, managers can identify high-impact integration opportunities and uncover leverage points within the system.
  • Shareholder Value Map: The concept of increasing shareholder value is broken down into four major, interconnected areas where integrated management can contribute value simultaneously: revenue growth, operating margin, asset efficiency, and expectations.
  • The Business Case for Integration: Integrated management provides a compelling business case by driving up revenue, reducing costs and risks, and building intangible value. Examples include productivity gains, cost avoidance, lower energy expenses, and reduced hiring/attrition expenses.

3. Strategic Approach and Action Learning: Building shared understanding leverages a collaborative, problem-based, and iterative learning approach.

  • Action Learning: The process of discussing real problems, clarifying different perspectives, and generating consensus is key to building trust and shared understanding across organizational groups. The initial ambiguity in understanding sustainability is overwhelming for some, but the chance to offer input to a shared vision of the future is a welcome opportunity for reflection and innovation for others.
  • Common Language: To succeed with integrated management, executives must translate ESG practices into established core business language, such as performance improvement, operational excellence, cost reduction, and revenue.

28.3 Assessing the Current Reality (As Is)

28.3.1 Design Thinking: A Human-Centered Approach to Innovation

Design Thinking is described as a human-centered methodology for innovation that integrates the needs of people, the possibilities of technology, and the requirements for business success. It functions as an applied approach to systems thinking that helps organizations disrupt markets and solve complex problems.

Key Characteristics and Process:

  • Definition: Design Thinking investigates ill-defined problems, gathers information, analyzes data, and proposes human-centered solutions.
  • Goal: The process seeks innovative products, processes, and services where viability, desirability, feasibility, and sustainability converge.
  • The Process (Invent, Test, Bring Integrated Management to Life):
    • Discovery (Catalyst): This phase recognizes a problem or opportunity and frames it within constraints (e.g., price, technology, or adherence to the Strategic Sustainable Development (SSD) sustainability principles). Observation and field-based research are crucial here to develop empathy and a robust understanding of the problem.
    • Ideation: This involves generating, developing, and testing multiple ideas, using primary and secondary research. Collaboration across multidisciplinary groups is essential to enhance the probability of achieving a truly innovative idea.
    • Implementation: This stage translates the project into reality through prototyping, which helps the team identify implementation obstacles and unintended consequences. The final step is developing a communication strategy to inform stakeholders and differentiate the new value proposition.
  • Integrated Solutions: Ideally, Design Thinking leads to user-centered, innovative solutions to “legitimate” business problems. An integrated solution, as achieved through this methodology, is enlightened, imaginative, multifaceted, and can be transformational in nature by reimagining roles, systems, and structures.

28.3.2 Life Cycle Assessment (LCA): A Tool for Operationalizing Design

LCA is the tool that supports Design Thinking and the high-level operational goals of “Zero” (such as Net Zero Energy, Net Zero Waste, and Net Zero Water) by providing granular, systems-level data on product impacts.

Definition and Scope:

  • Purpose: LCA is a technique used to measure and track a product’s resource use and environmental impacts from cradle to grave (raw material extraction to end-of-life) or to closed-loop systems (cradle-to-cradle).
  • LCA and Systems Thinking: LCA fits well with a macro level of analysis involving systems thinking, as it helps identify and manage sustainability risks and waste reduction opportunities throughout the interconnected supply chain, production, and delivery systems.
  • Transparency and Data: LCA increases transparency by generating data that can be used for Environmental Product Declarations (EPD) and displayed online, allowing customers to see the environmental and social aspects of products, including the carbon footprint of each stage of the life cycle. This level of detail empowers decision-makers to set goals with upstream and downstream partners.

The Four-Phased Approach (ISO Standards):

LCA aligns with International Organization for Standardization (ISO) standards (ISO 14040 and 14044), which provide guidelines for a structured, phased assessment. This process is applied using problem-based learning (PBL) principles to build multidisciplinary skills.

  1. Goal and Scope Definition: This involves defining the purpose of the LCA, the decision criteria, the functional unit, and the boundaries of the system (e.g., cradle to gate, gate to gate, or gate to grave).
  2. Life Cycle Inventory Analysis: This phase classifies events, determines mass and energy inputs and outputs, and collects relevant data, often resulting in flow charts and supply chain maps that reveal complexity and material location.
  3. Life Cycle Impact Assessment: This phase evaluates the environmental significance of the inventory data, rolling up inputs and outputs into impacts such as global warming potential (measured as \(\\text{CO}\_2\)), acidification, and human toxicity.
  4. Interpretation: This final phase focuses on identifying multidisciplinary opportunities for improvement, including evaluating the original goals and targeting processes with large impacts. It allows for “what if” scenarios (e.g., alternative materials or transportation modes) to see how the impact assessment changes.

LCA Outcomes and Integrated Management:

LCA is crucial for integrated management because it enables various organizational functions and strategic activities:

  • Design and Innovation: It helps to sustainably design products and supply chains to minimize negative environmental and social impacts. The earlier sustainability is incorporated into design, the easier it is to identify value-creating improvements.
  • Cross-Functional Collaboration: LCA requires multidisciplinary collaboration, linking operations, supply chain managers, engineers, and designers to capitalize on system improvement opportunities.
  • Value Chain Management: It identifies opportunities to improve environmental aspects across the supply chain, revealing that impacts often reside largely with suppliers (e.g., Puma found 94% of their environmental impacts were from Tier 1 through 4 suppliers).
  • Decision-Making: LCA provides measurable metrics for decision analysis (e.g., GHG emissions) and supports the identification of \(\\text{CO}\_2\) output, which can be valued using tools like the Social Cost of Carbon (SCC) to inform investment choices.
  • Reporting and Marketing: It assists in the selection of relevant environmental performance indicators for Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) and sustainability reporting.

28.3.3 Enterprise Systems Assessment

28.3.3.1 The Role of Management Systems (EMS) in the “As Is” Assessment

Traditionally, management systems focused on internal control and compliance, but they are now viewed as key enablers of integrated management.

  • Ubiquity and Formalization: Nearly every enterprise, regardless of whether it has a formal, International Organization for Standardization (ISO) registered system or an informal waste removal process, already possesses some form of management system for sustainability (EMS).
  • ISO Standards as Frameworks: The ISO standards provide a systematic approach to management. ISO 14001, the core standard for EMS, provides the basic framework for establishing an EMS, which includes developing an environmental policy, planning, implementation, monitoring, corrective action, and continuous improvement (following the Plan-Do-Check-Act cycle). Other relevant ISO standards include 50001 for energy management and 26000 for social responsibility.
  • Benefits of EMS: The benefits associated with adopting and certifying to standards like ISO 14001 include proactive environmental management, resource and cost efficiency, enhanced reputation, improved communication, and a capacity to move beyond ad-hoc approaches toward more integrated performance management. Research has shown that EMS positively affects product design, waste reduction, and recycling.
  • Integration with Supply Chains: EMS standards, even though voluntary, are becoming an “order qualifier” in some regions (like the European Union) and have been mandated by large original equipment manufacturers (OEMs), such as those in the auto industry, for their suppliers. This systematic pressure drives integrated management throughout the value chain. Suppliers that integrate these standards internally gain specialized benefits, while those who see certification only as a compliance game miss the full advantages.

28.3.3.2 The Evolution to Sustainable Operating Systems (SOS)

The evolution from EMS to SOS signifies a crucial change in mindset and scope, aligning operational management with strategic sustainable development.

  • Beyond Environmental Focus: While traditional EMS are often associated with environmental health and safety (EH&S) and focused on reducing negative impacts, SOS expands the scope to manage environmental, social, and governance (ESG) data, knowledge management, and real-time visibility of Integrated Bottom Line (IBL) performance.
  • Strategic Alignment: SOS are designed to support the dynamic goals and integration strategies of an enterprise. They align operating systems, business management, and supply chain design with a proactive approach to managing environmental and social issues. Successful SOS link sustainability initiatives directly to the overall enterprise strategy, gain top management support, and adopt a long-term perspective.
  • Enabling Value Creation: SOS help identify integration opportunities and the resulting benefits, which include cost reduction, increased competitive advantage, increased profits, enhanced innovation, and risk mitigation.
  • Boundary Spanning and Change Agents: This evolution calls for boundary-spanning individuals and change agents to leverage management systems to drive innovation and integrate IBL performance into strategic planning. These systems allow managers across functional disciplines to address complex problems and support organizational change toward sustainability.

28.3.4 Strategic Frameworks

28.3.5 Key Strategic Frameworks for Enterprise Systems Assessment

The following frameworks are highlighted as critical tools for integration:

28.3.5.1 1. SWOT Analysis (Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats)

The SWOT analysis is considered a basic, yet versatile strategic planning tool and an effective starting point for integration assessment and team discussions.

  • Function: It is used to systematically identify the internal attributes (Strengths and Weaknesses) and external conditions (Opportunities and Threats) relevant to a specific business situation.
  • Integrated Focus: The analysis helps benchmark current practice and find gaps between the current (“as is”) and future (“to be”) state. When conducting a SWOT analysis, managers should specifically consider where the enterprise is already integrating environmental or social sustainability (Strengths), what parts of the business are not very sustainable (Weaknesses), how systems thinking and design thinking can enhance products or services (Opportunities), and what competitors are reporting as their sustainability strengths (Threats).
  • Systems Thinking: The process of performing a SWOT analysis should utilize systems thinking to gain a cross-functional and big-picture understanding of current operations. The information compiled is used to identify new strategies and goals.
Quadrant Focus Area Integrated Management Questions
Strengths Internal attributes that aid objective achievement. What specialized knowledge does the enterprise possess? Where is the enterprise already integrating environmental or social sustainability?
Weaknesses Internal attributes that hinder objective achievement. In what areas does the enterprise need improvement, and what resources are lacking? What parts of the business are not very sustainable?
Opportunities External conditions that will help achieve objectives. How can the enterprise include social and environmental performance for existing customers? How can systems thinking and design thinking enhance products or services?
Threats External conditions that could negatively affect performance. What obstacles does the enterprise face? What are competitors reporting as their sustainability strengths? What is occurring in the economy and industry (e.g., related to sustainability trends)?

28.3.5.2 2. PESTLE Analysis (Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, Environmental)

PESTLE is presented as a mechanism for external assessment and is described as forming a “much more comprehensive version of a SWOT assessment”.

  • Function: This tool is used to track the broader business environment, find trends, and help plan the launch of a new product, service, or project.
  • Strategic Alignment: PESTLE informs strategic management, defining what a company should do while also accounting for its integration goals and strategies. The analysis specifically includes assessing environmental concerns for the industry, such as climate change.
Factor Strategic Questions
Political What is the political situation of the country and how can it affect the industry?
Economic What are the prevalent economic factors?
Social How much importance does culture have in the market and what are its determinants?
Technological What technological innovations are trending and can affect the market structure?
Legal Are there any current legislations that regulate the industry or can there be any change in the legislations for the industry?
Environmental What are the environmental concerns, e.g., climate change for the industry?

28.3.5.3 3. GE-McKinsey 9 Cell Matrix

This framework is a portfolio screening tool designed to help decentralized corporations determine the optimal allocation of cash resources.

  • Function: It assesses potential growth and profitability by measuring Industry Attractiveness against Business Unit Strength (Competitive Strength). The visualization assists managers in aligning operating systems and finding integrated management opportunities by directing strategic decisions regarding growth.

28.3.5.4 4. BCG Growth Share Matrix

This matrix is a well-known tool for portfolio analysis and is positioned within the general toolkit of strategic frameworks alongside SWOT, PESTLE, Ansoff’s Matrix, and Porter’s Five Forces. It uses a two-axis model (Relative market share and Market growth rate) to categorize business units as Stars, Question marks, Cash cows, or Dogs.

28.3.5.5 5. Ansoff’s Matrix (Growth Strategies)

Ansoff’s Matrix is another framework used for strategic assessment that focuses on identifying growth opportunities based on markets and products (Market Penetration, Market Development, Product Development, and Diversification).

28.3.5.6 6. Porter’s Five Forces

This framework is used to analyze an industry’s competitive environment to determine its attractiveness and likely profitability.

  • Function: It encourages organizations to look beyond direct rivals and examine five forces that can erode profitability: Competitive Rivalry, Threat of New Entry, Threat of Substitution, Supplier Power, and Buyer Power.
  • Strategic Outcome: The analysis informs the industry analysis format for developing competitive advantage, helping the enterprise determine what it must do well to maintain or improve its competitiveness.

28.3.6 Strategic Application and Outcome

Collectively, these strategic frameworks support the core mission of Enterprise Systems Assessment by:

  • Uncovering Strategic Opportunities: They help managers extend their analysis beyond basic assessments to uncover higher-level strategic opportunities.
  • Understanding System Needs: The frameworks facilitate an understanding of existing operating system needs and align supply chains with organizational goals.
  • Continuous Assessment: These tools should be used as part of a recurring process to continuously benchmark current practice, identify gaps, and update strategies.

The insights derived from applying these frameworks—often combined with the review of sustainability standards (ISO, GHG Protocol) and management systems (EMS evolving to SOS)—form the foundation for integrated decision-making and planning the actions required to close the gap between the enterprise’s current reality and its vision for a sustainable future.

28.3.7 sustainability standards

I. International Organization for Standardization (ISO) Standards

The ISO standards provide a systematic and globally recognized approach to management systems, which are foundational for enterprise assessment and integration.

A. Role and Scope of ISO Standards:

  • Foundation of Management Systems: ISO standards provide a structured approach for developing and implementing management systems. Nearly every enterprise has some form of Environmental Management System (EMS), whether formal (like an ISO-registered system) or informal.
  • Evolution toward Integrated Systems (SOS): ISO standards help facilitate the evolution of Environmental Management Systems (EMS) into Sustainable Operating Systems (SOS), which manage environmental, social, and governance (ESG) data and real-time visibility of Integrated Bottom Line (IBL) performance.
  • Global Acceptance: The ISO 14001 standard for EMS is internationally accepted, with over 300,000 certifications worldwide in over 171 countries. Its adoption has been mandated by large original equipment manufacturers (OEMs) and government entities, making it an “order qualifier” in some regions, thereby driving integrated management throughout the value chain.

B. Specific ISO Standards for Assessment and Management:

The sources detail several ISO families relevant to enterprise assessment and sustainability:

  • ISO 14000 Family (Environmental Management): This family gives requirements for EMS.
    • ISO 14001 (EMS): This is the core set of standards providing the basic framework for EMS, including the Plan-Do-Check-Act cycle. Certification to this standard offers benefits like resource and cost efficiency, enhanced reputation, and improved communication.
    • ISO 14040–14044 (Life Cycle Assessment - LCA): These standards set the principles and framework for LCA. LCA, in turn, supports Design Thinking by quantifying a product’s environmental impacts (e.g., carbon footprint) from cradle to grave or closed-loop systems.
    • ISO 14064 (GHG Emission Quantification and Reporting): This standard aligns with the GHG Protocol to set principles and requirements for quantifying and reporting GHG emissions at the organizational level.
  • ISO 26000 (Corporate Social Responsibility): This standard provides guidance (not certification requirements) to clarify social responsibility and translate principles into effective actions globally.
  • ISO 50001 (Energy Management Systems): This standard provides a framework for organizations to develop a policy for more efficient energy use and manage energy consumption.

28.4 Greenhouse Gas (GHG) Protocol

The GHG Protocol is described as the most widely used international accounting tool for government and business leaders to understand, quantify, and manage greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions.

  • Standardized Measurement: Developed by the World Resources Institute (WRI) and the World Business Council for Sustainable Development (WBCSD), the GHG Protocol was created to establish a uniform standard for corporate GHG accounting and reporting.
  • Scope Definitions: The Corporate Standard classifies emissions into scopes to determine organizational responsibility:
    • Scope 1: Direct emissions from sources owned or controlled by the company (e.g., owned vehicles or boilers).
    • Scope 2: Indirect emissions from the generation of purchased electricity.
    • Scope 3: All other indirect emissions occurring in the value chain.
  • Enabler for Strategy: The protocol provides the framework for nearly every GHG standard globally (including the ISO 14064). Its use enables organizations to set goals, differentiate themselves, reduce waste, and manage the associated Social Cost of Carbon Dioxide (SCC), which informs investment decisions.
  • Data for Stakeholders: The protocol is leveraged by organizations like the Carbon Disclosure Project (CDP) to collect and centralize data on corporate climate change and water management policies, serving a worldwide network of investors.

28.4.1 III. WELL Building Standard

The WELL Building Standard is presented as a certification that focuses on the integration of human health and wellness into the built environment.

  • Integrated Management in Buildings: The WELL Building Standard is a voluntary standard used for assessing high-performance buildings, alongside others like LEED and Passive House.
  • Focus on Human Capital: This standard highlights the importance of human capital integration by certifying buildings that promote the health and productivity of employees. The sources note that improving employee productivity is a key integrated benefit, with some research showing estimated human savings (health and productivity gains) that can be valued alongside financial and environmental savings from investments in the built environment.

28.5 Brainstorming Actions to Close the Gap (To Be)

28.5.1 Strategic Context and Methodology

Section IV builds directly on the Strategic Sustainable Development (SSD) framework, particularly the ABCD planning process (Awareness, Baseline, Creative solutions, Decide and prioritize), serving as the “C-step”.

  • The Gap/Chasm: Brainstorming is necessary because there is typically a gap or “chasm” between the strategic vision developed in Section I and the current operational reality assessed in Section III. This gap represents the required effort to move an organization toward a more sustainable and value-creating future.
  • The Funnel Metaphor: This phase is illustrated by the Funnel Metaphor, where the narrowing walls represent the constraints of an unsustainable society. Brainstorming actions must work within these constraints to prioritize available integrated management options.
  • Creative Solutions (Step C): This step involves generating a list of possible actions, investments, and collaborative efforts required to cross the chasm. The goal is to generate innovative solutions grounded in business viability and human desirability, ensuring that viability, desirability, feasibility, and sustainability converge. These actions should involve both short-term and long-term opportunities.
  • Informing Prioritization (Step D): The resulting list of actions serves as the input for Step D (Prioritization), where options are analyzed based on their flexibility, pace of progress, and potential return on investment (ROI), aligning with the enterprise strategy and vision.

28.5.2 Core Areas for Brainstorming Actions

The brainstorming phase relies on reviewing established, innovative trends to inspire actionable change that pushes the enterprise onto the expanded Performance Frontier. Actions should focus on eliminating or mitigating negative impacts related to multiple forms of capital (human, intellectual, natural, manufactured, social, and financial).

1. Embracing Innovative Trends and Closed-Loop Systems (Chapter 8): Actions should leverage best-practice trends that create financial, environmental, and social value simultaneously.

  • Valuing Natural Capital: Actions can involve implementing practices derived from Natural Capitalism, such as dramatically increasing the productivity of natural resources and moving toward biologically inspired production models.
  • Closed-Loop Innovation (C2C/IE): Brainstorming focuses on developing Cradle-to-Cradle (C2C) products or engaging in Industrial Ecology (IE) to reduce waste and utilize byproducts. This includes material substitutions, new product/service design, shifting to new energy types, and developing closed-loop systems.
  • High-Performance Assets: Actions related to High-Performance Buildings (such as Net Zero Energy Buildings) are crucial, as they simultaneously reduce energy consumption and \(\text{CO}\_2\) emissions (waste) while increasing human health and productivity, offering a strong business case for integrated investment.

2. Integrating Finance, Measurement, and Reporting (Chapters 9 & 10): A significant focus is placed on actions that integrate environmental and social performance into the financial decision-making process, often by valuing previously “hidden” externalities.

  • Social Cost of Carbon (SCC): A key action is the adoption of an internal or “shadow” Social Cost of Carbon Dioxide (SCC) in financial analysis. This transforms waste (\(\text{CO}\_2\) emissions) into a measurable value (cost avoided), informing investment choices and differentiating firms.
  • New Valuation Metrics: Brainstorming includes the proposal and application of new integrated valuation metrics for projects, such as Integrated Future Value (IntFV), Integrated Rate of Return (IntRR), and Return on Integration (ROInt). These metrics stress-test investments by including environmental and social value created or destroyed, going beyond myopic single-bottom-line cash flows.
  • Reporting: Actions include adopting formalized reporting systems like the GHG Protocol and the Global Reporting Initiative (GRI), or pursuing Integrated Reporting () to increase transparency and communication of value creation.
  • New Business Models: Actions might involve creating hybrid structures, like Benefit Corporations (BCorps), designed to pursue both profit and societal benefits.

28.5.3 Implementation and Prioritization Considerations

Action items generated during brainstorming should be relevant to the enterprise and captured in a strategy statement.

  • Materiality and Strategic Alignment: The actions brainstormed must address issues that are material to the organization (those impacting its ability to create, preserve, or erode value for itself and its stakeholders).
  • Multidisciplinary Approach: Successful brainstorming necessitates a multidisciplinary approach, moving people beyond their functional silos to collaborate and reflect on their initial assumptions (double and triple-loop learning).
  • Leverage Points: The ultimate goal is to identify leverage points—small shifts within the system that produce big changes—to accelerate progress toward sustainability. Tools like Multiple-criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) can be used to assess and prioritize competing options identified in this phase.
  • Global Goals: Actions should align with large, shared goals, such as the UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), which provide 17 strategic opportunities for enterprises to map their value creation activities into global efforts.

28.6 Prioritization and Action (The Future)

28.6.1 Strategic Integrated Enterprise (IntEnt)

Defining the Strategic Integrated Enterprise (IntEnt)

A Strategic Integrated Enterprise (IntEnt) is an organization that has successfully integrated Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) performance into its core strategy, operations, and decision-making processes. The IntEnt is able to operationalize goals for sustainable development by aligning decisions with strategic environmental and social guidelines, adhering to timelines of action that move the enterprise toward a sustainable society, and leveraging tools and management techniques to guide change.

The defining features of the Strategic Integrated Enterprise include:

  • Holistic Management of Capital: IntEnts are successful because they manage multiple forms of capital beyond just financial concerns, including human, intellectual, manufactured, social, and natural capital. This holistic perspective ensures value creation across all dimensions.
  • Strategic Alignment and Systems Thinking: They move beyond narrow, functional, and reductionist approaches by incorporating a systems perspective into strategic initiatives. The goal is to ensure that performance measurement systems are aligned with the total success of the system, rather than the local optimization of a single function.
  • Dynamic Performance: IntEnts are constantly looking for innovative ways to create value and thrive while contributing to a sustainable future. They operate closer to the ideal upper-right quadrant of the Dynamic Performance Frontier by generating both economic and environmental/social value simultaneously.

28.6.2 The Vision of “What Could Be”

The Strategic Integrated Enterprise represents the “What Could Be” future where a “no-compromises” approach to integrated management is the norm. This vision is necessary to overcome the historical mindset of the industrial revolution, which assumed unlimited resources, unchecked waste, and profit as the sole measure of success.

The Integrated Management Strategy Statement of an IntEnt explicitly links economic success, reduction of supply chain impacts, and collaboration with communities, aligning long-term goals toward sustainability. For IntEnts, success means achieving ambitious goals, such as:

  • Goals of Zero/Net Positive: Aiming for Net Zero waste, Net Positive operations and buildings, and valuing \(\text{CO}\_2\) as waste.
  • Full Capital Valuation: Valuing both natural and social capital within a financial statement (e.g., an Integrated Bottom Line (IBL) profit and loss statement).
  • Integrated Valuation: Utilizing metrics like Integrated Future Value (IntFV) and Return on Integration (ROInt) to stress-test investments and move beyond myopic, single-bottom-line cash flows.
  • Global Alignment: Mapping their actions into large, shared goals like the UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), which provide 17 strategic opportunities for value creation.

28.6.3 Prioritization and Action for IntEnts (Section V)

Section V focuses on how to prioritize and execute the brainstormed actions (Section IV) to successfully transform into an IntEnt. This process requires managers to:

  1. Utilize the ABCD Planning Process: The structure of the entire book mirrors the ABCD planning process (Awareness, Baseline, Creative solutions, Decide and prioritize). Section V focuses heavily on Step D (Decide and prioritize) to ensure chosen actions are flexible, strike a balance between the pace of progress, and offer a positive return on investment.
  2. Prioritize Materiality and Global Goals: Actions must address issues that are material to the organization (those impacting its ability to create, preserve, or erode value) and align with global frameworks like the SDGs.
  3. Benchmark Against Leaders (Evaluators): IntEnts are often benchmarked and highlighted by corporate evaluators, such as Forbes Top Sustainability Rankings and Newsweek’s Green Rankings. These rankings signal the importance of specific ESG metrics (e.g., carbon emissions, energy, water, waste, and leadership diversity) and prove that integrated management is already driving success in leading multinational corporations (MNCs). These ranked companies serve as models for integrated management in action.
  4. Embrace Corporate Scale as Agents of Change: Corporations, especially MNCs, are crucial agents of change because their revenue and influence often exceed that of entire countries. Their size and ability to scale integrated management options allow them to set industry norms and significantly impact global systems. IntEnts leverage this scale to tackle large, complex problems like climate change and social inequity.

28.7 The Future: Call to Integrated Management Action

28.7.1 I. The Necessity of the Call to Action and the No-Compromises Approach

The sources stress that the movement requires decisive action driven by a fundamental shift in mindset.

1. Challenging Conventional Thinking: The call to action is necessary to challenge the conventional mindsets inherited from the industrial revolution, which assumed unlimited resources, unchecked waste, and profit as the sole measure of success. This old thinking fostered systems that are wasteful (e.g., wasting over half of all energy produced, or throwing away 99% of manufactured goods within nine months). The authors emphasize that lack of integrated decision-making is the root problem, and there is “no place for lack of awareness and continued waste”.

2. A No-Compromises Vision (What Could Be): The ultimate vision for integrated management is a “no-compromises, no-apologies approach to the integrated management of sustainability”. This means actively pursuing innovative solutions that avoid the traditional trade-offs between economic performance and environmental/social value.

Specific actions for this “no-compromises” future include:

  • Utilizing the Strategic Sustainable Development (SSD) planning process to establish goals of zero toxic chemicals in products and supply chains.
  • Ensuring that water leaving facilities is cleaner than the water coming in.
  • Viewing \(\text{CO}\_2\) as waste and placing an economic value on it (e.g., using the Social Cost of Carbon (SCC)).
  • Designing Net Positive operations and buildings that generate more electricity and water than they consume.
  • Valuing natural and social capital within financial statements, such as a profit and loss statement.
  • Adopting global reporting standards like the Integrated Reporting () framework and aligning actions with the UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs).

28.7.2 II. The Role of Innovative Solutions and Measurement

The call to action relies on leveraging existing solutions, emergent measurement practices, and embracing innovation.

1. Leveraging Existing Solutions and Trends: The information presented throughout the sources provides an “evidence based, practical look” at successful practices. The call urges decision-makers to utilize these known solutions, such as Natural Capitalism, Cradle-to-Cradle (C2C), Industrial Ecology (IE), and high-performance building strategies, which have proven successful for innovators and early adopters. Furthermore, large corporations are viewed as critical agents of change because their revenue and influence often exceed that of entire countries, allowing them to scale integrated management options and significantly impact global systems.

2. New Valuation Practices: Integrated management requires moving beyond single bottom-line metrics and challenging traditional financial analysis, which focuses narrowly on first costs. Key actions include adopting and utilizing new integrated valuation metrics such as:

  • Integrated Future Value (IntFV): To evaluate the future value of assets and risks accrued and compounded over time, valuing both impacts and benefits.
  • Integrated Rate of Return (IntRR) and Return on Integration (ROInt): To stress-test investments by including environmental and social value created or destroyed, going beyond myopic single bottom-line cash flows.
  • Integrated Bottom Line (IBL): Defined as the valuation, analysis, and disclosure of financial, social, and environmental assets and liabilities to internal and external stakeholders of a firm.

3. Alignment with Global Goals (SDGs): A primary action point is aligning organizational strategy and performance with the UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). The SDGs provide 17 strategic opportunities for enterprises to map their value creation activities into global efforts, ensuring that actions contribute to a sustainable and prosperous society by 2030.

28.7.3 III. Personal and Organizational Commitment

The call to action is directed at both enterprises and individual decision-makers, emphasizing that integration is everyone’s job.

1. Organizational Transformation: Integrated management requires organizational change management that addresses organizational systems, including leadership, vision, employees, and policies, moving beyond mere technical efficiency changes. Companies must be ready to adopt emerging technologies (e.g., AI, big data, dashboards, and Blockchain) and take a systems perspective to solve complex problems. Failure to adapt leads to a widening institutional chasm regarding the purpose of the firm.

2. The Personal Vision and Action Plan: The sources assign the reader a final, reflective task: to prepare a Vision and Action Plan. This personal plan should describe how the individual will integrate business and sustainable development in their own career and enterprise. Specifically, it requires thinking about actions related to:

  • The personal point of view on the role of business in the world for the new millennium.
  • A personal action plan for using commerce as a tool to foster a more sustainable world.

The goal of this assignment is to create a “touchstone” document to be revisited annually, ensuring continuous reflection and action toward integrated sustainable development. The authors conclude that the integrated management movement provides hope, reinforcing the belief that the systems and enterprises of tomorrow need a vision of innovative solutions.
