13  Sales Acceleration Formula

13.1 Executive Summary

The Sales Acceleration Formula details a methodology for engineering predictable and scalable revenue growth, transforming sales from a traditional “art form” into a science grounded in metrics, repeatable processes, and technology. The approach is defined by four foundational components: the Sales Hiring Formula, the Sales Training Formula, the Sales Management Formula, and the Demand Generation Formula.

The most critical takeaways and core concepts include:

  • Engineering Success: The core mission is “scalable, predictable revenue growth,” achieved by ensuring consistency across hiring, training, management, and lead quality.
  • Metrics-Driven Approach: Subjectivity is removed by relying heavily on data and analytics (regression analysis, funnels, service level agreements) to diagnose performance, drive strategic shifts, and iterate on processes.
  • The Modern Salesperson: With today’s empowered buyer, aggressive, high-pressure salespeople are ineffective. The most successful salespeople are intelligent, helpful, curious, and coachable.
  • Passive Sourcing: World-class sales hiring requires a passive recruiting strategy, as truly great salespeople rarely apply for jobs.
  • Sales/Marketing Alignment (SMarketing SLA): Sales and Marketing must be aligned and accountable to quantified Service Level Agreements (SLAs). Marketing is put on a revenue quota (implied dollar value of leads), and Sales is held accountable for effective lead coverage.
  • Inbound Focus: Demand generation must pivot from interruptive “outbound marketing” (cold calls, unsolicited emails) to “inbound marketing,” helping buyers find the company through quality content and social media participation.
  • Coaching Culture: Sales management should prioritize metrics-driven coaching, focusing on diagnosing and improving one specific skill area at a time rather than overwhelming the salesperson with feedback.

13.2 The Sales Hiring Formula

The Sales Hiring Formula focuses on leveraging metrics to predictably hire the same successful salesperson profile every time, customized to the company’s specific buyer context.

13.2.1 Uncovering the Characteristics of a Successful Salesperson

The foundational premise is that world-class sales hiring is the most important driver of sales success. Investing energy in recruiting top performers wins the “war,” while focusing on coaching mediocre reps or closing big deals only wins the “battle”.

13.2.1.1 The Context-Dependent Ideal Hire

The ideal sales hiring formula is unique for every company because sales success depends on aligning a salesperson’s unique strengths (e.g., consultative selling, high activity, great presentation skills) with the company’s unique sales context (e.g., transactional vs. complex, target audience, market maturity). For example, high-activity salespeople excel in transactional sales contexts but often fail in evangelistic sales environments where educational skills are necessary.

13.2.1.2 Methodology: Engineering the Sales Hiring Formula

Although the ideal traits differ by company, the process to engineer the formula is the same. This process provides a measurable, objective way to hire salespeople with the highest probability of becoming top performers.

Step 1: Establish a Theory of the Ideal Sales Characteristics

  • List the characteristics believed to correlate with success (e.g., intelligence, aggression).
  • Document a clear definition for each characteristic.
  • Define a 1 to 10 scoring rubric for each characteristic to ensure consistency.
  • Summarize results on an Interview Scorecard.

Step 2: Define an Evaluation Strategy for Each Characteristic

  • Determine practical methods for evaluation (e.g., behavioral questions, role-plays, exercises, reference checks).

Step 3: Score Candidates against the Ideal Sales Characteristics

  • Maintain discipline in filling out the Interview Scorecard after every interview, documenting findings and learnings.

Step 4: Learn and Iterate on the Model

  • Review scorecards of top performers to identify common, consistent characteristics, and increase their weight.
  • Decrease the weight of, or eliminate, characteristics that do not predict success.
  • Identify missing characteristics and add them to the scorecard.
  • Application: After accumulating sufficient data (e.g., hundreds of hires), run formal regression analysis to correlate hiring characteristics with post-hire sales success, eliminating subjectivity.

13.2.1.3 Insight on the Modern Buyer

Early HubSpot regression analysis revealed that characteristics traditionally associated with sales (e.g., aggression, objection handling) had the worst correlation with success. Statistics suggest salespeople who are intelligent and helpful, rather than aggressive and high-pressure, are most successful with today’s empowered buyer who will no longer tolerate being strong-armed into a purchase.

13.2.2 Five Traits Great Salespeople Have and How to Interview for Them

For HubSpot’s specific buyer context (rapidly evolving industry requiring education), five traits correlated most significantly with sales success.

13.2.2.1 1. Coachability: The ability to absorb and apply coaching.

Coachability was found to be the most significant influencer of the hiring decision.

Evaluation Methodology (Three Steps):

  1. Set Up a Role-Playing Exercise: Model the company’s specific buyer context.
  2. Evaluate Ability to Self-Diagnose: Ask the candidate to self-assess their performance, looking for reflection, analysis, and specific proposals for improvement.
  3. Evaluate Ability to Absorb and Apply Coaching: Provide one piece of positive feedback and one area for improvement, then coach the candidate (e.g., using a whiteboard). Request they redo the role-play, looking for effort, not perfection, in applying the feedback.

13.2.2.2 2. Curiosity: The ability to understand a potential customer’s context through effective questioning and listening.

Great salespeople ask questions that are thought-provoking, build trust, and seek to understand customer goals, aspirations, and struggles.

Evaluation Opportunities:

  • Initial Greeting: Observe if the candidate begins the interview with a question, demonstrating research and interest.
  • Role-Play: Assess if the candidate leads with great, contextual questions related to the prospect’s interests (e.g., the eBook they downloaded), or if they rely on a generic, boring elevator pitch (“show up and throw up”).

13.2.2.3 3. Prior Success: A history of top performance or remarkable achievement.

This is the most objectively measurable trait.

  • Direct Sales Experience: Look for candidates who rank in the top 10 percent of a reasonably sized sales force.
  • Non-Sales Experience: Evaluate success through other activities: academic performance (class rank, standardized scores), standout performance on varsity sports (captain, championship contribution), or differentiating achievement in former roles.

13.2.2.4 4. Intelligence: The ability to learn complex concepts quickly and communicate those concepts in an easy-to-understand manner.

Intelligence is a strong predictor of success, especially in rapidly evolving industries.

Evaluation Methodology:

  • Interview Training: Test intelligence by effectively commencing sales training during the interview.
  • Information Retention: Expose the candidate to new information (e.g., training materials on SEO) early on, and then reference those materials later in a role-play. Assess how well they understood the concepts and communicated them simply back to the interviewer.

13.2.2.5 5. Work Ethic: Proactively pursuing the company mission with a high degree of energy and daily activity.

Evaluation Techniques:

  1. Observations during the Interview Process: Note how quickly the candidate returns calls, turns around deliverables, and if they push the pace of the interview process.
  2. Reference Checks: Ask former supervisors to rank the candidate’s core characteristics (coachability, curiosity, intelligence, work ethic) from strongest to weakest, followed by why.
  3. Behavioral Questions: Inquire about the rigor of their typical work day or work week, and their “must-do” activities.

13.2.3 Finding Top-Performing Salespeople

Sourcing great candidates is identified as the hardest part of the hiring process. Great salespeople never need to apply for a job; they have multiple offers at all times and are passive candidates.

13.2.3.1 Build a Recruiting Agency within Your Company

Reliance on external recruiting agencies is unpredictable and non-exclusive. The recommended approach is to hire high-performing, aggressively motivated agency recruiters internally and pay them with a lower base salary and meaningful performance bonuses based on fill rates and long-term success. This “recruiting agency within your corporation” operates with internal metrics on candidate touches, connections, and hires, providing a predictable, scalable process.

13.2.3.2 Finding Quality Passive Sales Candidates on LinkedIn

LinkedIn is an exceptional source for strong passive candidates.

Step 1: Leverage the Search Capability

  • Use advanced filters: Zip code, Job title, School (correlated with intelligence), and Company.
  • Tactic: Search for former employees of local companies known for quality sales training, then ask the Head of Sales (via networking favor) for recommendations on who was a standout performer.

Step 2: Screen the Search Results

  • Look for indicators of sales excellence (rankings, quota attainment).
  • Look for longevity (3, 4, 5+ years) at high-performing firms.
  • Assess alignment between the prospect’s current buyer context and your company’s context.
  • Evaluate school/major (correlated with intelligence/prior success).
  • Check profile quality (a weak profile is a red flag for social selling importance).

Step 3: Engage with the Prescreened Candidates

  • Prioritize mutual connections for warm introductions.
  • If cold-contacting, guess the corporate email format (more valuable than LinkedIn InMail).
  • Keep the email brief, referencing shared context (e.g., [Current employer/undergraduate school] in the subject line).
  • The “Ask”: Ask for a referral rather than going directly for the job offer, making the approach less confrontational and more genuine.

13.2.3.3 Find Quality Passive Sales Candidates through Your Team: The “Forced Referral”

The Forced Referral is the most effective technique for finding talent, especially as the team scales.

  • The manager connects with all salespeople on LinkedIn.
  • The manager reviews the salesperson’s network for potential fits.
  • The manager presents the salesperson with a short list of 18 potential candidates, and the salesperson identifies which ones are top performers and is comfortable introducing.

13.3 The Sales Training Formula

13.3.1 Setting Up a Predictable Sales Training Program

The traditional “ride-along” training approach, where a new hire shadows a top performer, is dangerous because it is neither scalable nor predictable. Every top performer succeeds in her own unique way (“superpower”) and may expose the new hire to bad habits.

13.3.1.1 Defining the Sales Methodology

A well-defined sales methodology (the “best-practice blueprint”) enables sales training to be scalable and predictable.

The Three Elements of the Sales Methodology:

  1. The Buyer Journey: The steps a company takes when purchasing a product (e.g., discovering a problem, educating oneself, evaluating solutions). This is the starting point for methodology design to ensure the buyer’s needs remain central.
  2. The Sales Process: The actions Sales takes to support the customer along the buying journey. Stages must be aligned with the buyer journey (for helpfulness) and must be inspect-able (clear and easy for managers to audit status, e.g., “Discovery verified” instead of “Influencer bought in”).
  3. The Qualifying Matrix: Defines the information needed from a buyer to determine if the company can help them and if the buyer wants help.
    • Example: BANT (Budget, Authority, Need, Timing) is an old-school start point. Iterating beyond this requires keeping criteria short, simple, and specific to the evolving buyer context.

13.3.1.2 Create a Training Curriculum

The curriculum structure follows the methodology creation process: start with the Buyer Journey, then introduce the Sales Process, and finally create deep-dive sessions for each stage of the Sales Process (e.g., prospecting, connect call, discovery call). Top salespeople can be empowered to teach specific classes aligned with their “superpowers,” serving as professional development.

13.3.1.3 Adding Predictability with Exams and Certifications

Exams and certifications add predictability to the training process and provide a platform for iteration.

Tool Purpose Evaluation Focus
Exams Test factual information, such as product knowledge and acumen (e.g., 100-question test). Quantifiable factual results.
Certifications Test qualitative skills, such as the ability to navigate sales process stages (e.g., Discovery Certification). Role-playing scenarios evaluated against a quantifiable rubric (scores of 1, 5, 7, 10) that outlines specific expected behaviors.

Note: Certification evaluators should not be the trainee’s hiring manager to ensure accurate performance assessment and avoid conflicts of interest.

13.3.1.4 Constant Iteration on the Sales Process

The structured training methodology provides a measurable baseline from which to constantly evolve the sales process.

  • Six-Month Feedback Forms: Ask salespeople (once on the job for six months) to rank training sessions by value and suggest missing subjects.
  • Correlations between Training Performance and Sales Performance: Analyze if high scores on the product exam or certifications predict success in the field. Strong correlations affirm the formula; weak correlations necessitate iteration.
  • Desired Behavior Changes: Use the structured formula to easily adjust training when the business evolves (new products, observations in the funnel).

13.3.2 Manufacturing Helpful Salespeople Your Buyers Trust

In the modern marketplace, where buyers have all the control, salespeople must prove their worth by transforming into genuine consultants and trusted advisors. Modern selling should feel less like a seller/buyer dynamic and more like a doctor/patient relationship.

13.3.2.1 Training Salespeople to Understand the Buyer’s Day-to-Day

Salespeople need a high degree of business acumen to understand their buyers’ goals and translate generic product messaging into customized stories. The best-trained salespeople have experienced the day-to-day job of their potential customers.

  • Application (HubSpot): Sales hires spent their first few weeks developing their own websites, blogs, and social media presences. By living through the marketer’s pain points (their target customer), they gained authentic empathy and credibility, enabling them to genuinely advise prospects.

13.3.2.2 Building a Personal Brand with Social Media

Salespeople should leverage the Internet to build authoritative status as thought leaders. Social media presents an opportunity for all salespeople to be perceived as trusted advisors by their buyers.

Application: Social Media Tactics

  • Reallocate Time: Salespeople should take time normally spent on low-yield prospecting (e.g., cold calls, Chamber of Commerce meetings) and reallocate it to social media participation.
  • Engage with Influencers: Find people on Twitter that prospects follow. Retweet their posts and offer genuine help. Build relationships and eventually ask them to promote high-quality, relevant content.
  • Participate in Groups: Join LinkedIn groups where prospects congregate. Answer questions intelligently (without promoting the product) to demonstrate knowledge. People will click through to the company profile.
  • Blog Contribution: Write blog articles that answer common questions prospects ask. This establishes the company as an authority on industry pain points.
  • Networking Rule: Use a 2:1 ratio for social media—one-third of messages should be about your company, and two-thirds should be about other people (i.e., adding value).

13.4 The Sales Management Formula

13.4.1 Metrics-Driven Sales Coaching

Effective sales coaching by sales managers is the most important lever to drive sales productivity. A common mistake is overwhelming the salesperson with too many simultaneous feedback points.

13.4.1.1 The Metrics-Driven Coaching Model

The best sales managers identify the one skill that will have the biggest impact on a salesperson’s performance and customize a coaching plan around developing only that skill, using metrics to diagnose the deficiency.

13.4.1.2 Implementing a Coaching Culture (The Monthly Cadence)

A regular, metrics-driven cadence ensures accountability and buy-in.

  • 1st of Month: Sales Managers meet 1:1 with salespeople to review metrics and co-create a personalized coaching plan.
  • 2nd Morning: Directors meet with Managers to inspect their coaching plans.
  • 2nd Afternoon: VPs meet with Directors to inspect their plans.

13.4.1.3 Creating the Coaching Plan Together (Co-Creation)

The plan is created with the salesperson to maximize buy-in and empower them to become their own independent coach.

  • The manager uses a sequence of questions: self-assessment, qualitative reflection, and then reviewing quantitative metrics (e.g., lead volume, connect rates) compared to peers.
  • The crucial question: “Which skill do you think we should work on this month, and what’s the best way that I can help you with that skill?”.
  • The manager then schedules follow-up coaching sessions to focus on that single skill.

13.4.1.4 Framework: “Peeling Back the Onion”

This methodology uses deeper metrics to properly diagnose the root cause of a high-level performance issue.

  • Example: If a salesperson has a low Leads Worked-to-Demos Booked ratio, the manager peels back the funnel by looking at two new ratios: Leads Worked-to-Connects (diagnoses prospecting frequency/personalization issue) and Connects-to-Demos Booked (diagnoses trust development/pitch issue). This saves time and ensures the manager focuses on the correct area.

13.4.1.5 Measuring Coaching Success

Coaching success is measured by comparing an individual salesperson’s funnel activity month-over-month (Figure 7.4). This allows managers to look back at prior coaching plans and verify if the targeted skill metrics improved.

13.4.2 Motivation through Sales Compensation Plans and Contests

The sales compensation plan is the most effective tool for the CEO and VP of sales to drive business strategy.

13.4.2.1 Criteria to Evaluate a New Commission Plan (The Three Factors)

A compensation design should be evaluated against three criteria to ensure optimal impact:

  1. Simple: Salespeople must not need a spreadsheet to calculate their earnings. Complexity causes confusion, leading salespeople to ignore the plan and revert to old habits.
  2. Aligned: The plan must be properly aligned with the most important company goal at that time (e.g., customer count, profitability, customer success, new market penetration).
  3. Immediate: Success or failure should be reflected in the paycheck immediately to maximize the plan’s impact on behavior.

13.4.2.2 Evolution of Sales Compensation (Contextual Planning)

There is no perfect compensation plan; the appropriate plan depends on the stage of the business. Strategic goals are executed by adjusting the plan:

  • Plan 1: The Hunting Plan (Goal: Customer acquisition velocity). Paid high upfront commission ($2 per $1 MRR) with a four-month claw-back. Side Effect: High churn in Month 5 (when claw-back expired).
  • Plan 2: The Customer Success Plan (Goal: Retention). Commission rate adjusted based on individual salesperson’s customer churn rate (stack-ranked into quartiles). Top quartile doubled commission; bottom quartile rate was cut. Result: Churn dropped by 70%.
  • Plan 3: The Customer Commitment Plan (Goal: Long-term commitment). Commission payout was delayed (50% paid first month, 25% paid at six months, 25% paid at 12 months), unless the customer prepaid annually, in which case the full commission was paid immediately. This aligned commission with behavior (securing prepayment) that correlated with high customer success.

13.4.2.3 Promotion Tiers: Removing Subjectivity

Promotional tiers offer a formalized, quantifiable career growth path for individual contributors.

  • Quantifiable Criteria: Promotions are based on achieving measurable goals (e.g., accumulating $60K in MRR install base, acquiring $5K new MRR per month, securing six months average paid up front).
  • Alignment: Criteria are aligned with desired business behaviors.
  • Meritocratic: Tiers take the subjectivity and politics out of compensation increases and are not reliant on tenure.

13.4.2.4 Sales Contests

Sales contests are an effective tool (Robin to the compensation plan’s Batman) to drive short-term behaviors and build team culture.

Best Practices for Contest Design:

  1. Align with Short-Term Behavior: Target behaviors difficult to drive through the compensation plan (e.g., boosting summer activity).
  2. Make Team-Based: Competing in teams (e.g., four teams of three) significantly boosts team culture and encourages high performers to help others.
  3. Make Prize Team-Based: Rewards should be experienced together (e.g., golf outing, sailing trip) to maximize the positive impact on culture.
  4. Send Updated Standings Every Night: Daily visibility is critical for engagement.
  5. Choose Time Frame Wisely: Monthly contests are ideal; daily is too short, quarterly is too long.
  6. Avoid Contest Fever: Run only one simultaneous contest per group to prevent dilution.

13.4.3 Developing Sales Leaders—Advantages of a “Promote from Within” Culture

The strategy adopted was to develop the sales management layer from within, promoting people who knew the product, buyer persona, and internal sales system. The focus should be on building leadership skills rather than general sales management skills.

13.4.3.1 Leadership Development Methodology

A formal leadership curriculum (e.g., 12 weeks) is used to develop the leadership bench.

Training Components:

  • Defining and Developing Leadership Style (e.g., authentic leadership, forceful/enabling leadership).
  • Providing Positive and Negative Feedback.
  • Successful Mentoring and Coaching.
  • Managing Conflict (with direct reports, peers, and boss).
  • Managing through Change and Building/Developing the Team.

Curriculum Execution:

  1. Preparation Document: Candidates complete a prep document on scenarios (e.g., conflict management) to test natural instincts.
  2. Reading: Candidates complete assigned readings on best practices.
  3. Role-Play: Candidates meet in person to role-play the scenarios, evaluating their ability to absorb and apply the best practices.

13.4.3.2 Prerequisites for Leadership Consideration

Entry into the leadership curriculum must be earned and is evaluated across three skill areas:

  1. Performance: Consistent goal attainment (e.g., exceed sales targets for six months in a row).
  2. Sales Skills: Demonstrated well-roundedness across the entire sales methodology, ensuring they do not rely only on a single “superpower”.
  3. Leadership Potential: Demonstrated contribution to the team while still an individual contributor (e.g., mentoring new reps, leading training classes, insightful contributions in meetings).

13.4.3.3 From the Classroom to the Real World

Before formal promotion, qualified leadership candidates are given the opportunity to hire their own salesperson, mentor them through training, and manage them for the first two months while still carrying their individual quota responsibilities. This temporary double-duty is a safe environment to practice time management and evaluate if management is the right fit.

13.4.3.4 Common Potholes from New Sales Managers

New sales managers frequently fall into three traps:

  1. Weak Time Management: New managers often miscalculate how much coaching time is available (realistically 3–4 hours/week per person). Success depends on efficient coaching execution.
  2. The Manager as a Glorified Salesperson: Managers step in to run demos or close deals for reps. This spoils the reps, erodes their confidence, and prevents the manager from scaling their team effectively.
  3. Giving Up on a Salesperson Too Early: Managers, especially inexperienced ones, often write off new hires who just need effective coaching and patience for a few months before they “click”. If a rep demonstrates coachability (applies feedback the next day), they should be retained and developed.

13.5 The Demand Generation Formula

13.5.1 Chapter 10: Flip the Demand Generation Formula—Get Buyers to Find You

Today’s buyer is empowered by the Internet (Google, social media) and actively researches problems. Interruptive “outbound marketing” (cold calls, unsolicited mail/email) is disliked by buyers and is increasingly ineffective, as buyers use technology (DVRs, spam blockers, Do Not Call lists) to avoid it. Companies must adopt an “inbound marketing” strategy, helping buyers find them.

13.5.1.1 How to Rank at the Top of Google

Google’s ranking algorithms focus on two key authority signals:

  1. Inbound Links: Quality and quantity of hyperlinks from other websites (a link from the Wall Street Journal is exponentially more valuable than a link from a personal blog).
  2. Social Media Influence: High levels of engagement (retweets, followers, fans) on social media channels.

Successful inbound marketing comes from two tactics:

  1. Continual quality content production (e.g., blogs, ebooks, webinars).
  2. Frequent online participation in social media where your target buyers are already conversing.

13.5.1.2 Methodology: Create a Content Production Process

Commitment is key; results do not happen overnight. Executives must focus on creating a sustainable process, delegating the writing to specialists, rather than trying to handle the tasks themselves.

Content Production Framework:

  • Hire a Journalist: Find a specialist with great journalism skills. They do not need to be a domain expert, as their job is to extract knowledge.
  • Form a Thought Leadership Committee: Assemble people who understand the industry and customers (executives, engineers, salespeople). Salespeople are valuable as they hear the questions buyers ask early in their journey.
  • Define the Process: Schedule regular, niche interviews (e.g., 1 hour per week per committee member).
  • Content Extraction and Scheduling: That one interview yields an ebook, 3-4 blog posts, and dozens of social media messages. Content should be released over time, driving readers to landing pages where they provide contact information for free content (e.g., an ebook).

13.5.1.3 Framework: Long-Tail Theory

The Long-Tail theory suggests that a large percentage of overall data/revenue is represented by a multitude of small data batches far down the curve.

  • Application to Content: Focus content on “long-tail” topics (niche, specific phrases, e.g., “IT for pharmaceutical companies”) rather than generic “head” phrases (e.g., “IT consultant”). Long-tail topics are less competitive, and the prospects searching for them are much more qualified for your business.

13.5.2 Converting Inbound Interest into Revenue

13.5.2.1 Marketing’s Role in Converting Interest

The first, most common mistake Marketing makes is passing all inbound leads to Sales. Inbound leads are high on “pain” but low on “fit” (e.g., PhD students doing research). Passing unqualified leads overwhelms Sales and causes them to hate the entire batch.

13.5.2.2 Framework: Buyer Persona/Buyer Journey Matrix (Buyer Matrix)

Complicated lead scoring algorithms (e.g., “once a lead exceeds 50 points”) are often flawed and pass irrelevant leads to Sales. The Buyer Matrix is a better framework.

  • Y-axis: Buyer Personas: Static attributes (e.g., Small Business, Mid-Market, Enterprise).
  • X-axis: Buyer Journey Stages: Changing stages (e.g., Problem Education, Solution Research, Solution Selection).
  • Customization: Each of the resulting “buyer states” receives customized marketing collateral and sales engagement.
  • Pass-to-Sales Threshold: The matrix is used to define when a lead is qualified for Sales based on persona and journey stage (e.g., Enterprise leads might be passed at Problem Education; Small Business leads might be nurtured until Solution Selection).
  • Optimization: Measure lead conversion rates for leads passed at different stages. If conversion is high, consider passing the lead sooner (moving threshold left). If conversion is low, require Marketing to nurture the lead further (moving threshold right).
  • Microsegmentation: Refine the matrix by segmenting rows or columns (e.g., segmenting the Enterprise row by influencer roles: IT, Finance, Marketing end user) to further customize the buyer experience.

13.5.2.3 Sales’ Role in Converting Interest

Sales must adjust their strategy when dealing with context-rich inbound leads:

  1. Scrap the Elevator Pitch—Lead with Context: Traditional, repetitive voicemails are torture and negate all of Marketing’s inbound work. Salespeople must treat the contact sequence like a dialogue, referencing specific actions (e.g., “I noticed you downloaded our ebook on Facebook marketing…”) and offering helpful suggestions. The “going negative” voicemail (“I’ll assume this is no longer a priority”) often generates the highest callback rate.
  2. Call Low, Then Call High: Do not ignore an inbound lead just because the contact is an intern.
    • Call Low: Engage the initial contact with a relevant, low-level value proposition and provide free consulting. Transform the contact into a “coach” who provides internal context about organizational needs and priorities.
    • Call High: Use the internal context gathered from the “coach” to leave a highly customized, relevant voicemail for the decision maker that maximizes the likelihood of earning time.
  3. Prioritize Prospecting by Level of Engagement: Salespeople should use technology to know in real time when a lead engages (e.g., visiting a pricing page, opening a sales email). The engagement level is the best criterion for prioritization, replacing outdated methods like alphabetical order or rigid touch cadences.
  4. Specialize Sales by Inbound versus Outbound: Specializing teams resolves conflicts over lead quantity and quality. Inbound teams handle inbound leads exclusively, while Outbound teams handle cold calling and business development. This works well from $0 to $100M.

13.5.3 Aligning Sales and Marketing—The SMarketing SLA

The dysfunctional relationship between Sales and Marketing is the “kiss of death” in a buyer-driven world. The Sales and Marketing Service Level Agreement (SLA) replaces subjective friction with quantifiable goals and clear accountability.

13.5.3.1 The Marketing Service Level Agreement (SLA)

The Marketing SLA holds Marketing accountable to Sales for both quality and quantity of leads.

Putting Marketing on a Revenue Quota: The SLA should not be based on a raw number of leads, as different lead actions (e.g., demo request vs. collateral download) indicate different buyer journey stages and have different closing rates.

  • Marketing is held accountable to an aggregate implied lead dollar value.
  • Calculation: For each buyer state (persona/journey stage), calculate the implied dollar value: (Conversion Rate to Customer) \(\\times\) (Average Purchase Price).
  • Result: Marketing is incentivized to focus on high-value calls to action (e.g., free trials) that increase the implied lead value, aligning them with Sales’ revenue goals.

13.5.3.2 The Sales Service Level Agreement (SLA)

The Sales SLA ensures Sales is accountable to Marketing for effectively working the leads produced.

Quantification of Effective Lead Working:

  • Time to Call: Leads called within minutes convert at an exponentially higher rate. The SLA mandates new leads must be called within a specific time (e.g., one hour of converting).
  • Optimal Call Frequency: Data analysis is used to determine the optimal number of times to call a lead (e.g., 5 calls for Small Business, 12 calls for Enterprise) to maximize profitability while balancing organizational time investment.
  • Enforcement: A daily report called the “Do Not Be on It” dashboard tracks any salesperson violating the Sales SLA (e.g., free trial lead is one week old but called fewer than three times).

Daily Management: Daily reports updating both Sales and Marketing on the SLA performance allow leadership to manage the Sales and Marketing machine on a daily, proactive basis.

13.6 Technology and Experimentation

13.6.1 Technology to Sell Better, Faster

Historically, sales technology was built for the leader (forecasting, pipeline review) and forced administrative work on the salesperson, leading to poor adoption and incomplete data. Modern sales technology must focus on two goals:

  1. Sell Faster: Accelerate the sales process by eliminating admin work and mundane tasks.
  2. Sell Better: Create a better buying experience by capturing buyer context (e.g., website visits, email opens) and making it readily available, enabling salespeople to engage helpfully.

13.6.1.1 Accelerate Lead Sourcing with Technology

Technology must automate the historically time-consuming process of verifying a lead and entering data into the CRM.

  • Application: Tools (like Sidekick) overlay a sidebar on a prospect’s website during a Google search, instantly providing data (revenue, territory, key executives, CRM history). This allows salespeople to source 10 times more leads in the same amount of time.

13.6.1.2 Accelerate Sales Prospecting with Technology

Technology must eliminate manual log entries and inform the salesperson’s actions.

  • Real-Time Context: Salespeople are informed immediately when a prospect engages (e.g., opened a sales email, visited the website), allowing them to engage contextually.
  • Automation: Technology automates dialing, voicemail logging, email logging, and task scheduling (according to the Sales SLA’s optimal cadence), eliminating all admin work for the salesperson.

13.6.1.3 Accelerate Lead Engagement and Reporting

Technology ensures the sales process stages are driven by buyer actions, not the salesperson’s subjective gut feeling.

  • Opportunity Status: Opportunities automatically move forward or backward based on captured buyer actions (e.g., prospect opened the proposal, VP attended demo).
  • Collateral Alignment: Technology recommends the most appropriate blog article, case study, or ebook based on the prospect’s specific Buyer Persona and Buyer Journey stage.
  • Automated Reporting: Technology that helps the salesperson increases adoption, which in turn leads to accurate, automated data capture for sales executives (forecasting), marketers (lead effectiveness), and salespeople (activity comparison).

13.6.2 Running Successful Sales Experiments

A core philosophy of continual improvement through a culture of experimentation is a key ingredient to sales acceleration.

13.6.2.1 Generating Ideas for Experiments

HubSpot used a “bottom-up” innovation funnel, recognizing that the most groundbreaking ideas come from the front line (employees interacting daily with prospects). The executive role is to create the environment for this innovation.

  • Hackathons: Internal hackathons focused on specific problems (e.g., competitor offering, culture issue) are used to brainstorm solutions and design experiments.
  • Transparency: Sharing monthly financials, executive priorities, and NPS survey results with all employees empowers them to act as “CEOs” of their functional area.
  • Innovation Career Track: Establishing an attractive, defined career path for employees passionate about breaking new ground (the “innovation track”) gives culture credibility.

13.6.2.2 Best Practices of Experiment Execution

Following a specific formula ensures experiments are efficient and effective.

Execution Formula:

  1. Define Clear Goal/Measure of Success: Set a clear objective (thesis) and quantify what success and failure look like before beginning.
  2. Design Execution: Determine the fastest and lowest-cost way to test the thesis.
  3. Choose a Leader: Select a leader with passion, functional knowledge, and professional goals aligned with the experiment.
  4. Assemble the Team: Use multiple top performers for the first phase of the experiment. This ensures that if the experiment fails, the result is a “true negative” (meaning the idea itself was infeasible, not that execution was flawed).
  5. Establish Routine Check-ins: Hold monthly experiment board meetings with senior leadership to review data, provide guidance, and decide whether to continue funding, expand, or discontinue the project.

13.6.3 HubSpot’s Most Successful Sales Experiments

13.6.3.1 1. The HubSpot Value Added Reseller (VAR) Program (Go-to-Market Experiment)

Despite advice against launching a partner channel early (due to management difficulty and loss of product feedback cycle), a persistent salesperson (Pete Caputa) received approval to test a VAR program.

  • Execution: Pete ran the initial test as a nights and weekends effort while still meeting his quota (120%). Success was defined as five new customers acquired through VARs who logged into the platform at least once a week.
  • Outcome: The experiment was successful, leading to Pete’s full-time focus on scaling the channel. The contained, cheap bet yielded enormous gains, becoming a significant revenue generator.

13.6.3.2 2. GPCT (Goal, Plan, Challenges, Timeline) (Sales Methodology Experiment)

HubSpot’s initial matrix, BANT, failed to ensure proper “Need” discovery. GPCT was developed to improve the discovery process.

  • GPCT Definition:
    1. Goal: A quantified and implicated business goal (e.g., increase leads by 20%; implication if failure occurs).
    2. Plan: The buyer’s existing strategy to achieve the goal (allowing Sales to assess realism and redirect ineffective plans).
    3. Challenges: Gaps in resources (staff, budget, software) that will hinder the plan.
    4. Timeline: The specific date for goal achievement and the consequences of missing it.
  • Execution: The shift was rolled out as an experiment, taught to five top salespeople led by the best coach. This ensured a “true negative” could be determined if necessary.
  • Outcome: The team crushed the metrics. Crucially, adoption happened organically across the sales floor as peers overheard the top performers using the successful GPCT framework, providing efficient buy-in far superior to a mandatory “annual kickoff” rollout.

13.7 Final Comprehensive Summary: The Sales Acceleration Formula

The Sales Acceleration Formula is a blueprint for achieving predictable scale by treating sales execution as a science built on quantifiable processes, technology, and iteration. The system is built upon four interdependent formulae:

1. The Sales Hiring Formula (Part I): World-class hiring is the primary driver of success. The ideal profile depends entirely on the company’s unique buyer context, but the process for engineering this profile is universal. Through metrics and iteration, HubSpot identified the five core traits for the modern buyer (Coachability, Curiosity, Prior Success, Intelligence, Work Ethic). Hiring requires a passive recruiting strategy (since top reps never have to apply) and using techniques like building an internal recruiting agency and executing the “Forced Referral” via LinkedIn to source high-quality, passive talent. The ideal first hire is an Entrepreneur who can accelerate the company toward product/market fit, rather than an experienced SVP who is prone to delegation.

2. The Sales Training Formula (Part II): Training must move beyond unstructured “ride-alongs” to become scalable and predictable. This is achieved by first defining the Sales Methodology (Buyer Journey, Sales Process, and Qualifying Matrix). Predictability is enforced using Exams and Certifications that quantify the learning of both factual knowledge and qualitative selling skills. The goal of training is to manufacture helpful salespeople who are trusted advisors (a “doctor/patient relationship”). This is done by training them to experience the day-to-day job of the customer (e.g., building their own website/blog) and enabling them to build a personal thought leadership brand via social media.

3. The Sales Management Formula (Part III): Sales management is primarily about Metrics-Driven Sales Coaching. Managers must adopt a consistent monthly cadence to diagnose one critical skill deficiency at a time (avoiding feedback bombardment). They use techniques like “Peeling Back the Onion” (diving into granular metrics) and co-creating coaching plans with the salesperson for maximum buy-in. Business strategy is implemented through the sales compensation plan, which must be Simple, Aligned, and Immediate. Motivation is reinforced using quantified Promotion Tiers (removing subjectivity from career growth) and Team-Based Contests (driving short-term behavior and culture). Leaders should be promoted from within, following a formalized curriculum and demonstrating well-rounded sales skills and leadership potential before official promotion.

4. The Demand Generation Formula (Part IV): Demand generation must be “flipped” to focus on inbound marketingstrategies (getting buyers to find you through Google and social media) instead of interruptive outbound tactics. Success requires continual, quality content production (leveraging a journalist and a thought leadership committee) focused on Long-Tail keywords that attract highly qualified prospects. Sales and Marketing must be aligned via the SMarketing SLA. The Marketing SLA puts Marketing on a revenue quota (based on the implied dollar value of leads), while the Sales SLA holds reps accountable for effective lead coverage (e.g., calling leads within one hour, following optimal call frequency). Sales must adapt by leading with customer context, prioritizing leads by engagement, and employing a “call low, then call high” strategy.

5. Technology and Experimentation (Part V): Technology is vital for acceleration by helping reps Sell Better(capturing buyer context for helpful engagement) and Sell Faster (automating administrative logging and lead sourcing). This high adoption, in turn, yields the accurate, automated data needed for effective management. Finally, the organization must foster a culture of continual improvement through disciplined experimentation, following a clear formula (defining goals, assembling top performers, measuring results) to efficiently test innovative ideas (like the GPCT methodology or the VAR program) and drive strategic shifts.
